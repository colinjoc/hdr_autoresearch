# Literature Review: US Building Permit Processing Times

This review synthesises the literature relevant to the question "Why does a duplex take 48 days to permit in Austin and 605+ days in San Francisco, and where exactly in the pipeline is the time lost?" Numerical identifiers in brackets refer to rows in `papers.csv`.

## 1. The Puzzle: Cross-City Variance in Permit Processing Time

The headline fact is that equivalent residential structures built from standard materials, using largely the same national-model building codes, undergo permit processes whose total calendar time differs by more than an order of magnitude across US cities. A March-2026 San Francisco survey commissioned by the SF Board of Supervisors and reported widely [83, 86] found the median time from application to issuance for housing permits in San Francisco was 280 days across January 2024 through August 2025, down from around 605 days prior. For a specific subset of applications filed after SB 423 took effect, the median dropped to 114 days. Comparable headline numbers reported in the same exercise were 91 days in Austin and 134 days in San Diego. These are decisional medians; the tail is considerably longer. Some applications in the SF pipeline as of October 2024 had been awaiting approval an average of 1,489 days [83], with more than 1,300 permits still in backlog despite SB 423's nominal 6-month mandate.

Identical headline gaps show up in industry-tracked statistics. The NMHC-NAHB 2022 Cost of Regulations survey [50, 214, 215] found that NIMBY opposition alone added an average of 5.6% of total project cost and 7.4 additional months to multifamily projects, with 74.5% of developers reporting they had encountered at least some NIMBY opposition. Regulation as a whole was estimated at 40.6% of multifamily project cost. These numbers are self-reported and subject to selection bias, but the pattern is consistent with cross-city statistics: a Terner Center project-level study of 2,474 San Francisco housing developments between mid-2009 and early 2017 found permitting ranged from less than one year to over fourteen years with "little consistency across project types" [40, 82].

At the same time, Austin's HOME Phase 1 and Phase 2 initiatives [62, 63, 64, 186, 258], passed in 2023 and 2024, appear to have reduced entitlement friction for small multifamily projects while also dropping median rents substantially: Austin added roughly 120,000 housing units between 2015 and 2024 and its median asking rent fell from $1,546 in 2021 to $1,296 by early 2026 [61]. The natural experiment is uncomfortable for any theory that identifies fixed physical or cost constraints as the binding problem.

Two aspects of the variance are important for research design. First, the variance is not just cross-city but also **within-city and within-application-type**. Even in high-throughput jurisdictions the right tail is long: specific projects hit multi-year delays while superficially identical projects clear in weeks. Second, the variance is **observable**: almost every major US city now publishes per-permit timestamped data through open-data portals [74–80, 308–316], which means queueing-theoretic and process-mining techniques that are normally applied to factory floors or hospital ERs are directly applicable. The BPI Challenge 2015 event log [102, 103], a landmark process-mining dataset, uses precisely this kind of permit-level data from five Dutch municipalities and has been studied intensively. Nothing equivalent has yet been published for US municipalities.

What the literature knows well. The **economics** of housing supply restriction is mature. We know the cross-sectional relationship between regulatory stringency (as measured by WRLURI [2, 3]) and house prices [5, 7–9, 11, 20, 21, 23]. We know housing-supply elasticity varies with geography and regulation [1, 155, 156]. We know multi-stage "discretionary" review and CEQA litigation add time [30–36, 141, 142] and that process matters more than prohibition in some California settings [37]. What the literature does **not** know well is the pipeline decomposition: the marginal contribution of each intake-to-issuance stage to total throughput time, the cross-city stage-level distribution, and the statistical model relating project attributes to stage-level durations. Most empirical papers stop at descriptive summary statistics or mean regressions of the total. There is no peer-reviewed US building-permit survival analysis at project granularity.

## 2. What Drives a Permit Delay: Theory and Empirics

Theories come in two families: **regulatory economics** (which treats the question as "how much regulation?") and **operations / queueing** (which treats it as "how efficient is a multi-stage process under load?"). They are complements.

**2.1 The regulatory-economics framing.** The canonical starting point is Glaeser and Gyourko's "regulatory tax" concept [5, 7, 8, 9, 161]: where market prices exceed production costs (land plus structure), the gap is interpreted as a regulatory shadow price. Saiz (2010) [1, 155] partitions the supply elasticity of US metros into a physical component (from terrain slope and water bodies) and a regulatory component, arguing that both matter. Gyourko, Saiz and Summers (2008) [2] built the original Wharton Residential Land Use Regulatory Index (WRLURI) via factor analysis across roughly 2,600 survey responses; Gyourko, Hartley and Krimmel (2021) [3] updated this with a 2018 survey of nearly 2,500 communities, for the first time enabling panel-style within-jurisdiction comparison. Gyourko and Krimmel (2021) [4] use land-value gradients to estimate a "zoning tax" that in coastal California represents a very large fraction of housing value. Molloy (2020) [21, 218] synthesises this evidence in a recent Fed review and finds that the elasticity-dampening effect of regulation is large for sale prices but surprisingly modest for rents.

A parallel literature examines the effect of specific controls. Pendall (2000) [18] and Rothwell and Massey (2010) [19] identify density-restricting zoning as causally linked to lower rental supply and greater income and racial segregation. Mayer and Somerville (2000) [14] show in a 44-metro panel that regulatory controls reduce steady-state permit volumes and the elasticity of supply to price shocks. Ihlanfeldt (2007) [15] uses cross-sectional variation across 100+ Florida cities to show regulation raises house price and lowers land price. Turner, Haughwout and van der Klaauw (2014) [17] exploit municipal-boundary discontinuities to decompose the welfare cost of zoning into own-lot, external, and supply effects. Jackson (2016) [16] uses California within-city variation with instrumental variables and finds regulation reduces residential permits. Brueckner et al. (2020) [156] focus on height regulation specifically as a measurable form of stringency. Kulka, Sood and Chiumenti (2025) [157] bring recent regression-discontinuity evidence to the zoning-effects question.

A third strand targets the **political economy of delay**. Fischel (2001) [25, 220] argues in *The Homevoter Hypothesis* that local governments make zoning decisions principally to protect the house values of existing homeowners, who face much more concentrated costs and benefits than non-voting renters or newcomers. This turns planning meetings into a structural mechanism for delay. Einstein, Glick and Palmer (2019) [169] documented empirically that in Massachusetts planning meetings the average participant is older, whiter, wealthier and more opposed to development than the underlying population. Hankinson (2018) [39, 219] used survey experiments to show that renters and homeowners alike exhibit opposition to new local development even while supporting regional supply increases, which he termed "renter NIMBY". Demsas (2021, 2024) [27, 193, 268] and City Journal commentators [194] argue that the ability of individual council members or aldermen to block specific projects ("aldermanic privilege" in Chicago, "member deference" in NYC [65, 66]) concentrates the political friction in particular review stages rather than across the whole pipeline.

**2.2 The operations / queueing framing.** A separate literature treats permit pipelines as multi-stage service networks. The analytical machinery originates in Kleinrock (1975) [114], Kleinrock (1976) [330], Gross et al. (2008) [113], and Bolch et al. (2006) [115], with Little's Law [116, 117] as the bridge: for any stable system in steady state the mean number of cases in the system equals the arrival rate times the mean time in the system. Applied to permitting, Little's Law immediately implies that if average throughput time is *T* and the arrival rate is *λ*, a backlog of *λT* must exist in the system at any point; the 1,300 applications in SF's backlog are a Little's-Law consequence of long decisions. Kendall-notation M/M/c models provide an explicit analytic expression for expected wait time as a function of load (traffic intensity ρ = λ/(cμ)), with the well-known result that expected wait explodes non-linearly as ρ approaches 1. In the permit setting, ρ is the ratio of incoming applications to plan-checker service capacity, which in many cities (SF being the canonical example) has crept close to or above unity without a matching expansion of reviewer headcount.

A practical limitation of classical queueing is that permit processes are not single-stage M/M/c systems. They are **tandem networks** with feedback: an application enters intake, queues for zoning, queues for a plan reviewer, is returned for corrections (feedback), re-enters plan check, queues for MEP, queues for fire, and so on. Bolch et al. [115] and the queueing-networks literature provide Jackson- and BCMP-network results for some such cases, but the real process typically violates the assumptions (non-exponential service, state-dependent routing, correlated arrivals). This is where **process mining** enters, because it handles such processes empirically.

**2.3 The process-mining framing.** Process mining, originating at Eindhoven University with van der Aalst and colleagues [87, 110, 111, 112], treats an event log (a table of `case_id, activity, timestamp`) as the primary input and extracts three things: (1) a discovered process model; (2) conformance between the discovered model and a reference model; and (3) performance metrics including throughput times, waiting times, and bottlenecks. The **alpha miner** [91] was the first process-discovery algorithm to handle concurrency. The **heuristic miner** [92, 93, 94, 98, 323, 324, 325] added noise tolerance. The **inductive miner** [95, 96, 237, 241, 242, 243] added formal soundness guarantees and block-structured output. Buijs, van Dongen and van der Aalst [97] formalised four quality dimensions (fitness, precision, generalisation, simplicity) against which discovered models are evaluated. Conformance checking via alignments [89, 90, 320, 321, 322] relates observed behaviour to a reference model and pinpoints deviations.

On top of discovery and conformance sits **predictive process monitoring**, which asks "given a partial trace of a running case, what is the expected remaining time?" Tax et al. (2017) [100, 217], Verenich et al. (2019) [99, 216] and Teinemaa et al. (2019) [101] set up the benchmark and show that LSTM and gradient-boosted approaches meaningfully improve on naive estimators. Recent work [233, 234, 235] addresses efficient training, text features, and explainability. For permit processing specifically, the **BPI Challenge 2015** event log [102, 103, 106, 107, 240] made five Dutch municipalities' building-permit pipelines publicly available. Submissions to BPIC 2015 identified throughput-time differences across municipalities that violated the official Dutch 8-week statutory limit (the WABO 8-week rule) and showed process-mining can surface collaboration failures and re-work loops that were invisible to internal dashboards [107]. BIM-based process mining [232] extends the same techniques to construction-project event logs. Government-service case studies [57, 108, 109] show that process-mining-guided interventions can halve throughput times in practice: one Canadian personnel-security-screening study reduced mean time from 31 days to 26 days with targeted interventions; another reduced the specific "briefing" step from 7 days to 46 hours.

**2.4 What the two families together imply.** Put together, the literature implies that total permit processing time is a sum of (a) an economic-political "price of entry" set by the city's political equilibrium and its de-facto discretionary-review regime, and (b) a stochastic tandem-queue throughput that depends on load, service rates, re-work probabilities, and hand-off efficiency between stages. The first component shows up as high median times and as a right tail of projects that never clear for political reasons; the second shows up as a distribution of times across superficially identical projects. Any model of the permit pipeline must decompose both.

## 3. Process-Mining and Queueing Models for Government Services

Government services as process-mining targets is a small but growing area. Mans, Schonenberg, Song, van der Aalst and Bakker (2008) [109] applied the alpha and heuristic miners to a Dutch hospital workflow and demonstrated that process mining could reveal structural re-routing of patients through informal shortcuts that were invisible to the nominal process model. Benevento, Salvi and van der Aalst (2020) [240] survey the first decade of BPI Challenges and document multiple permit and licensing datasets. Leemans and van der Aalst (2024) [108] describe pre- and post-intervention process mining of a Canadian government digital service, demonstrating a full research-design template: baseline discovery, bottleneck identification, intervention, re-measurement. Government permit processes are well-suited because (i) each case has a clear case identifier, (ii) stages are explicitly logged for audit purposes, (iii) the timestamped event log typically already exists in the ERP or review system, and (iv) the case outcome (issued or withdrawn) is unambiguous.

The contrast between process mining and classical queueing is instructive. Classical queueing demands strong distributional assumptions (typically exponential service times and Markov arrivals) and provides closed-form throughput-time expressions. Process mining makes no distributional assumptions but cannot give closed-form expressions; instead it computes empirical distributions of waiting and service time from logs. Hybrid approaches are emerging in the recent literature [231] that use discovered process models as skeletons for calibrated discrete-event simulation, yielding both interpretable structure and accurate duration predictions. For building permits specifically, a hybrid model is the natural object: the discovered process is the stage graph, durations and routing probabilities come from the log, and survival models handle right-censoring of open cases at the time of data extraction.

Survival analysis completes the picture [118–132, 244–250, 331–334]. Cox proportional hazards regression [118] is the standard tool for covariate-adjusted analysis of time-to-event data with censoring. Accelerated failure time models [120, 249, 250] provide direct interpretation in terms of multiplicative effects on duration. Kaplan-Meier curves [119] give non-parametric estimates. Modern extensions include Random Survival Forests [123], DeepHit [124, 125], Deep Survival Machines [126, 245], XGBoost AFT [127], and scikit-survival [128]. For building-permit modelling these tools offer two advantages over naive regression on mean days-to-issuance: they correctly handle right-censoring (applications still open at extraction time) and they allow covariates like application type, stage, reviewer, and neighbourhood to interact with the hazard in flexible ways.

## 4. Per-City Institutional Context

**San Francisco.** SF is the canonical slow-permitting city in the recent literature. The Terner Center (Metcalf, Reid and Garcia, 2022) [40, 82] analysed 2,474 SF housing developments across 2009-2017 and found project-level permitting times ranging from less than a year to fourteen years. Elmendorf (2023, 2024) [30, 281] traces the legal origin of SF's discretionary-review regime to a 1970s reinterpretation of the City Charter by the City Attorney's office, arguing that conventional wisdom about mandatory review was an invention of lawyers that later became institutional fact. The SF Planning Department and Department of Building Inspection (DBI) sit in a two-stage structure: Planning handles entitlement, conditional use, and CEQA review; DBI handles the building permit itself. Each stage has its own queue and its own backlog (cf. the 1,300-case backlog reported in SF Examiner [83]). SB 35 [135, 274, 275] and SB 423 [136, 138, 276, 277, 278] streamlined approvals for projects that meet state objective-standards, with the 2025 SF Examiner reporting [83] showing the median time for SB 423 applications was 114 days vs 280 days for the broader housing pipeline. CEQA-related litigation has been shown to affect only a small fraction of projects (O'Neill et al. [34, 35] found only 7 of 254 Bay Area projects faced CEQA suits) but CEQA-adjacent discretionary review can still delay the whole cohort even without formal litigation.

**New York City.** NYC has two overlapping processes: the **as-of-right** building permit issued by the Department of Buildings, and the **Uniform Land Use Review Procedure (ULURP)** [133, 134] which governs zoning changes and other discretionary actions. ULURP is structurally a six-phase pipeline (Community Board 60 days → Borough President 30 days → City Planning Commission 60 days → Mayor 5 days → City Council 50 days) with a formal maximum of roughly 205 days. NYC's DOB NOW [74, 75, 76, 190] rolled out starting 2019 as the DOB's replacement for the legacy BIS system, bringing applications, payments, and status tracking online. The 2021 NYC DOB report [76] noted 11% and 10% year-over-year increases in new-building and demolition permits respectively after the initial rollout. Empirically the binding constraint is frequently **member deference** (individual Council Members effectively vetoing projects in their district) [65, 66, 193, 194]. The Furman Center and Citizens Budget Commission [44, 45–49, 200, 202–206] have produced detailed empirical studies of NYC's permit and zoning environment, documenting that almost all new multifamily units in 2010-2020 (approximately 185,000) were built under high-density residential zoning, much of it created by Bloomberg- and de Blasio-era upzonings.

**Los Angeles.** LADBS [81, 181, 182, 183, 294–297] operates the plan review and permit process. LA is notable for having launched ePlanLA and PermitLA relatively early, with a fast-track tenant-improvement program announced in 2021 [183]. Monkkonen, Lens and Manville [347, 348] compiled project-level entitlement delay data for LA 2015-2020, which represents one of the few publicly documented city-level empirical datasets. Mawhorter [349, 350] extends the analysis to multifamily production timing across California.

**Chicago.** Chicago retained an explicit "aldermanic privilege" culture in which the ward alderman informally vetoes projects in their ward. Illinois Policy Institute documented wait times of 80+ days for building permits and 17% rejection rates for zoning licenses due to insufficient information [43]. Chicago modernised its construction codes in 2020 for the first time in 70 years [180] and introduced an express permit program in 2023 [339]. The Chicago Department of Buildings operates permit data through a standard open portal [80, 298, 299, 300, 309].

**Austin.** Austin's permit pipeline has undergone the clearest policy experiment [61–64, 184–186, 258, 259, 260, 261, 335, 336, 337, 338]. The 2023 HOME Phase 1 and 2024 HOME Phase 2 reforms reduced minimum lot size from 5,750 to 1,800 square feet and legalised up to three units on most single-family lots. Permit volumes in 1,800-square-foot lots went from 487 to 906 in the year after HOME-1 took effect (+86%). Median duplex sale prices dropped 5.5% year-on-year despite overall prices rising 6%. The Pew Charitable Trusts 2026 analysis [61] documents the associated rent decline from $1,546 in 2021 to $1,296 in early 2026 — a very large change for a fast-growing metro. Austin's processing time for a standard residential plan is reported at roughly 15-25 business days for initial review and 6-12 weeks end-to-end including correction cycles [338].

**Boston.** Boston's inclusionary and review environment is well studied by Glaeser and Ward (2009) [13], who attribute much of Massachusetts' cost-of-housing story to minimum-lot and other town-level rules rather than to Boston-specific friction. Project-level Boston permit data is publicly available [310].

**Portland, Seattle.** Oregon's 2019 HB 2001 [346] preempted single-family-only zoning statewide, making Oregon the first state to legalise middle housing. Portland and Seattle [311, 312] publish fully timestamped permit data.

**Houston.** Houston is the anomalous case of no traditional zoning [89], relying on deed restrictions that cover about 25% of the city. The natural experiment this provides has been heavily used in comparative studies; Ellickson [28, 29, 159] uses Houston as one of three comparison metros to isolate the effect of zoning from the effect of other regulatory controls.

## 5. Regulatory Reform Attempts: What Worked and What Did Not

The peer-reviewed literature on the effects of specific permit reforms is thin relative to the volume of reforms passed. The Mercatus Center [51–57, 251–253, 262, 263] has become the dominant real-time tracker of US state housing legislation and counts roughly 50 state-level pro-housing bills enacted in 2024 and 100+ in 2025, a sharp acceleration from the 2011-2022 average of about 18 per year [59]. The George W. Bush Presidential Center [254] and Pew Charitable Trusts [58, 59, 60, 82] reached similar counts. This is a rising-tide phenomenon that the academic literature has not yet fully evaluated.

**California SB 35 (2017)** [135, 274, 275] introduced the first statewide streamlined ministerial approval pathway for multifamily housing in jurisdictions failing to meet state RHNA targets. Initial evaluations were mixed; Garcia, Metcalf and Kneebone (2023) [41] document moderate early uptake but substantial implementation friction, particularly around defining "objective standards". **California SB 423 (2023)** [136, 138, 276, 277, 278] extended and expanded SB 35, with a 60-to-90-day written-determination requirement for qualifying projects. Early evidence from SF's own data suggests it is working: the SF Examiner's March 2026 reporting [83] gives a median time of 114 days for SB 423 applications vs 280 days for non-SB 423. **California AB 2011 (2022)** [137, 271–273] legalised multifamily residential on commercial corridors statewide with prevailing-wage requirements, potentially enabling 1.6-2.4 million new homes according to Terner Center analysis [42]. California's 2025 budget trailer bill [270, 285] further exempted most infill urban housing from CEQA entirely.

**CEQA and litigation.** Holland and Knight's 2018 study [141] found that 85% of CEQA lawsuits in 2013-2015 were filed by plaintiffs with no environmental advocacy record and 80% targeted infill development, feeding the argument that CEQA acted as a litigation tool rather than an environmental tool. O'Neill, Gualco-Nelson and Biber (2018, 2019) [34, 35] counter that only a small fraction of projects faced actual CEQA lawsuits (7 of 254 in their Bay Area sample), which suggests that CEQA's effect operates through fear of litigation rather than through litigation itself. Elmendorf and Duncheon (2022) [31] dissect the legal architecture of CEQA-vs-Housing-Accountability-Act conflicts.

**Oregon HB 2001 (2019)** [346] eliminated single-family-only zoning statewide, with Kuhlmann [343, 344] and others evaluating the supply response. Minneapolis 2040 [343, 344, 345] provides a parallel natural experiment and shows modest-to-moderate positive supply effects.

**Florida HB 267 (2024)** [56] mandated 30-day permit processing for single-family, duplex, triplex and quadplex structures under 7,500 square feet, with specific statutory consequences for non-compliance.

**Permit-office electronic systems.** NYC DOB NOW [76, 190], LA ePlanLA [181], Austin expedited review [185, 336], and Chicago's 2023 express permit program [339] are all examples of municipal-level e-permitting rollouts. The Netherlands has had legally-required online permitting since the early 2010s [229, 230]. Generic vendor claims of "80% faster" processing with e-permitting [64] lack peer-reviewed backing, but the general direction is consistent with process-mining case studies [57, 108] showing meaningful reductions after digitisation.

**What has not worked, empirically.** Reforms that change only the allowable zoning envelope (legalising ADUs or middle housing) without fixing the review process appear to produce initially small permit-volume responses, because the binding constraint is often the review pipeline itself, not the zoning envelope. Einstein, Glick and Palmer [169] and Monkkonen, Lens and Manville [37] both argue that "process" (discretionary review steps) dominates "prohibition" (zoning text) as an explanation for California's housing under-production.

## 6. Survival Analysis and ML Approaches to Time-to-Event Prediction

For modelling duration-to-issuance, the literature offers a mature toolkit. The Cox proportional hazards model [118] estimates multiplicative covariate effects on the hazard and is robust to unspecified baseline hazard but requires the proportional-hazards assumption. The AFT model [120, 249, 250] parametrises the log-duration directly and yields multiplicative effects on duration. Kaplan-Meier [119] provides non-parametric estimates of survival functions. All three handle right-censoring correctly, which matters in permit data where a substantial fraction of cases may still be open at extraction time.

Textbooks [121, 122, 130, 131, 331–334] provide exhaustive treatments of these methods for applied work. The Klein and Moeschberger textbook [121] is particularly practical for left-truncated and right-censored data, which arise naturally if only a subset of cases in a date window are observed. Kalbfleisch and Prentice [122] is the theory-oriented reference.

Machine-learning extensions are now standard. Random Survival Forests [123] handle non-linearities and interactions without explicit specification. DeepHit [124] and Dynamic-DeepHit [125] handle competing risks and longitudinal covariates and drop the proportional-hazards assumption. Deep Survival Machines [126, 245] use mixtures of parametric distributions. XGBoost AFT [127] provides a gradient-boosted AFT loss with native GPU support. Scikit-survival [128] and lifelines [129] provide the de-facto Python toolkits. Recent extensions [244–248] address robust estimation, regularisation for high-dimensional covariates, and functional covariates.

For building permits specifically, the research design is: extract a per-permit event log from city open-data portals, treat the intake-to-issuance duration as a (possibly right-censored) survival time, and fit multiple models (Cox, AFT, XGBoost-AFT, RSF, DeepHit) on covariates including application type, project size, neighbourhood, application year, and — crucially — stage-level features extracted by process mining. The resulting model decomposes total time into contributions from each stage and each covariate, directly addressing the "where in the pipeline is the time lost?" question.

Predictive process monitoring [99–101, 231, 233–235] is the process-mining analogue: given an open case's partial trace, predict remaining time to issuance. Tax et al. (2017) [100] and Verenich et al. (2019) [99] benchmark LSTM and gradient-boosted approaches on real event logs including government process datasets and show meaningful improvements over naive estimators. The main advantage of predictive process monitoring over survival analysis is that it uses the current **partial trace** (what stages the case has already completed) as a feature; the main advantage of survival analysis is that it provides interpretable hazard-ratio estimates for individual covariates.

For a building-permit HDR loop, the most natural object is a **unified** model: use process mining to discover the stage graph and compute per-stage distributions; use survival analysis to model per-stage durations as a function of application covariates; and use a predictive process monitoring layer on top for end-to-end case-level predictions.

## 7. Open Questions

1. **Pipeline decomposition.** No published study for US cities has produced a stage-by-stage decomposition of total throughput time at the project level with standard errors. The closest is the BPI Challenge 2015 exercise on Dutch data [102, 103, 107], which was not exported to US cities. The opportunity is large because US cities publish more granular timestamps than Dutch cities did.

2. **Where the re-work is.** Terner Center [40, 82, 349, 350] and Monkkonen et al. [37] document that "process" dominates "prohibition", but the studies do not estimate the share of throughput time spent on plan-check correction cycles versus on initial plan review versus on inter-agency hand-offs. Process-mining repetition-loop detection [94, 95, 242] can provide this estimate if the event log encodes each correction cycle.

3. **Reviewer workload and backlog dynamics.** Classical M/M/c theory predicts non-linear wait-time explosion as ρ → 1, but nobody has estimated ρ empirically for a US permit pipeline. It is not even clear at what time granularity ρ should be measured (daily? weekly? per-reviewer?) because the effective service rate varies with reviewer allocation policies.

4. **Effect of specific reforms holding everything else fixed.** SB 35, SB 423, AB 2011, Florida HB 267, Oregon HB 2001, Minneapolis 2040, Austin HOME and other reforms are natural experiments. The peer-reviewed literature has begun to evaluate them [41, 42, 343, 344] but the timing of evaluation is typically too short to include the full effect on the back of the pipeline. The Pew and Mercatus briefs [54–60] provide real-time descriptive statistics but not causal identification.

5. **Heterogeneity within projects.** A duplex in the Mission is not a duplex in Bayview. Neighbourhood-level variance in permit times within the same city is larger than the variance between some city pairs, which implies hidden moderators (historic district status, community board culture, recent development conflict, individual planner workload). The literature has almost nothing to say about this.

6. **The role of third-party reviews.** Mechanical, electrical, plumbing, fire, health, and environmental reviews are frequently outsourced to third-party consultants or separate agencies and can add months or years to the total. The NMHC-NAHB 2022 report [50] puts the total "regulatory" cost at 40.6% of development cost but does not decompose it further. No peer-reviewed US study has measured stage-level third-party review time.

7. **Cross-city comparability.** The 280-day figure for SF, the 91-day figure for Austin, and the 134-day figure for San Diego [83] are medians computed by different methodologies. Whether they are strictly comparable (same starting event, same terminating event) is unclear from the press reporting. A project-level harmonised dataset across US cities does not exist in the public literature.

## 8. Implications for HDR

For a generalised HDR research loop, several concrete implications follow from the literature review:

1. **The natural object is a per-permit event log, not a monthly aggregate.** Census BPS [71–73, 301–308] provides monthly counts by MSA and is standard for housing-economics research, but it cannot identify where in the pipeline time is lost. The open-data portals in NYC, SF, Austin, LA, Chicago and several other cities [74–81, 308–316] do expose per-permit timestamps and are strictly richer.

2. **The natural method is a hybrid process-mining + survival-analysis pipeline.** Process mining [87, 95, 97] discovers the stage graph and computes per-stage distributions. Survival analysis [118, 121, 123, 127] models per-stage durations as a function of covariates with correct censoring handling. Both are mature in their own literatures but have not been combined for US permit data.

3. **The BPI Challenge 2015 dataset [102, 103] is a ready-made validation case.** Any method developed for US permit event logs should first reproduce the Dutch BPIC 2015 results. This gives a benchmark baseline that prior process-mining work has extensively analysed [107].

4. **The primary "discovery" objective is pipeline decomposition with uncertainty quantification, not improved duration prediction per se.** Predictive process monitoring techniques [99, 100, 101] provide good out-of-sample duration prediction but interpretability of stage-level contributions is what matters for reform design.

5. **Cross-city comparability requires harmonisation.** The 48-days-vs-605-days headline needs to be re-measured on harmonised definitions (intake event, issuance event, what counts as a "duplex"). The SF Examiner figures [83] are a useful starting point but require project-level re-computation on open data to be trusted.

6. **The natural controls are the 2017-2025 California streamlining reforms [135, 136, 137, 270].** The rollout order and the jurisdiction-level variation in which projects qualify provide natural difference-in-differences identification of the reforms' effect on throughput time. This is the single most empirically tractable reform-evaluation design in the US housing-policy literature.

7. **Re-work loops are the most likely high-signal intervention target.** Both the BPIC 2015 analysis [107] and the Canadian government case study [108] identified correction cycles and re-work loops as the dominant source of controllable delay. If US permit pipelines behave similarly, the marginal return on engineering effort is highest at the plan-check-correction-cycle stage rather than at the initial-zoning or post-approval-inspection stages.

The literature does not provide a ready-made answer to the Austin-versus-SF duplex question. What it does provide is a complete methodological toolkit: regulatory-economic framing for the cross-city variance, queueing theory for the within-pipeline dynamics, process mining for the empirical stage discovery, and survival analysis for the covariate-adjusted duration model. The HDR loop for this application is to combine them.
