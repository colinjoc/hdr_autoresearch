# Literature Review: Asset Correlation Regimes

**Project type:** exploratory (compact Phase 0 lit review — ~45 citations, 3 themes)

**Core question:** In the last 20 years, when do gold, bitcoin, and commodities decouple from equities, and is the 2024–2026 behaviour (high positive correlation with equities under heightened risk) unusual once conditioned on macroeconomic regime?

This review assembles 45 citations across three themes. It is deliberately less exhaustive than a publication-target review: the goal is that the reader understands the mainstream findings, the major methodological tools, and the open debates well enough to design and interpret a rolling-correlation panel across SPY / GLD / BTC / WTI / BCOM / DXY conditioned on Fed, inflation, dollar, and geopolitical regimes.

---

## Theme 1: Asset correlation regimes — the empirical literature on time-varying cross-asset correlations

The starting observation of this literature is that cross-asset correlations are not constant. Longin and Solnik (2001, P002) showed using extreme-value theory on decades of international equity returns that correlations are materially higher in the joint lower tail of the return distribution than in the upper tail — an asymmetry invisible to simple rolling-window Pearson estimates. The companion classic is Forbes and Rigobon (2002, P001), which warned that the naive correlation spikes observers attribute to "contagion" during the 1997 Asian, 1994 Mexican, and 1987 US crises are in large part a heteroskedasticity artefact: when the variance of the common factor rises, unconditional correlations mechanically rise too. Their unbiased estimator found that once this bias is removed, there is "no contagion, only interdependence" in those episodes. For our project this is a methodological caution: the naive 90-day rolling Pearson we will compute is subject to exactly this bias, and any 2024–2026 spike we observe in SPY-GLD or SPY-BTC correlation needs a Forbes-Rigobon style robustness check.

The workhorse parametric tool of the field is Engle's DCC-GARCH (Engle 2002, P003). It models each asset's variance as a univariate GARCH and then parameterises the conditional correlation matrix with a two-parameter (α, β) recursion so that correlations mean-revert toward a long-run unconditional level. Empirically α is small and β is large, so that DCC-estimated correlations are highly persistent — daily autocorrelation of correlation is often near 0.95. This has two implications for us: (i) short-horizon (30-day) rolling correlations will be noisy; the signal lives at 90-day and 250-day horizons. (ii) If the 2024–2026 correlation regime is truly novel, DCC will show the conditional correlation drifting to a level not visited before in the sample.

Ang and Bekaert (2002, P008) were the first paper to formalise regime-switching in international correlations: they estimated a two-regime Markov-switching model where the bear regime has high vol AND high correlations, showing that "when you most need diversification, it's not there." Their framework descends from Hamilton (1989, P009), whose regime-switching autoregression is still the textbook mechanism for identifying unobserved discrete states from continuous time series. Pesaran and Timmermann (2007, P010) give the corresponding estimation guidance: in the presence of structural breaks, pre-break data can still improve out-of-sample forecasts if the sample reduction from a post-break-only window inflates estimation variance more than the bias from mixing regimes. In our context this informs window-length choice: a purely post-COVID window would be far too short.

The safe-haven sub-literature is built on Baur and Lucey (2010, P004), who define a hedge as an asset uncorrelated with stocks on average, and a safe haven as one that is uncorrelated or negatively correlated during stock-market extremes. They find gold is both, but its safe-haven function lasts only about 15 trading days. Baur and McDermott (2010, P005) extend this across 30 years and many countries and find gold's safe-haven role is concentrated in developed Europe and the US but notably absent for BRICs, Japan, Australia, Canada — so the result is not universal. Reboredo (2013, P006) uses copulas and finds gold is a safe haven against USD tails, and Bredin, Conlon and Potì (2015, P007) use wavelets to show gold's safe-haven role exists at all time horizons during crises, but that longer-horizon investors can actually get higher downside risk from gold in recessions. The methodological lesson: the answer depends on (a) which equity market, (b) over what horizon, and (c) in what period. Akhtaruzzaman et al. (2021, P016) find gold's safe-haven properties weakened during the COVID-19 crisis itself.

For bitcoin the same questions have been asked but later. Dyhrberg (2016, P011) fit GARCH volatility models and found BTC behaved somewhere between gold and USD — a hedging claim that read plausibly in 2015 but was subsequently contradicted. Bouri et al. (2017, P012) gave the most-cited careful answer: BTC is a diversifier, weakly a safe haven only against certain Asian indices, and not a convincing hedge. Shahzad et al. (2019, P013) introduced a quantile-based distinction between "weak" and "strong" safe havens and found BTC's safe-haven role is time-varying and index-dependent — it is sometimes a weak safe haven but almost never a strong one. Smales (2019, P014) argues that BTC's volatility, liquidity, and transaction costs make it structurally unsuitable as a safe haven. The decisive negative result is Conlon and McGee (2020, P015): during the March 2020 COVID-19 crash, BTC fell in lockstep with the S&P 500 and a portfolio allocation to BTC materially increased downside risk. Selmi et al. (2018, P017) had earlier shown that any safe-haven role of BTC is conditional on market state — a finding the COVID episode dramatised. Cheah and Fry (2015, P018) add the stronger claim that BTC's fundamental value is zero and prices carry a large speculative bubble component, which reframes the "correlation with equities" discussion: if BTC is dominated by speculative flows, its correlation with equities is really a correlation with speculative risk appetite, not a fundamental asset-pricing fact.

Commodities round out T1. Gorton and Rouwenhorst (2006, P019) famously documented that 1959–2004 collateralised commodity futures delivered equity-like returns and were NEGATIVELY correlated with both equities and bonds. Erb and Harvey (2006, P020) showed that the gain came from the rebalancing of a diversified commodity basket, not from individual commodity premia. Driesprong et al. (2008, P021) found oil prices predict equity returns — so oil-equity correlations are non-trivially forecastable.

**Bottom line for T1:** cross-asset correlations are time-varying, regime-dependent, and rise in joint tails. Standard correlation metrics are heteroskedasticity-biased; DCC and regime-switching are the standard tools. Gold is a well-documented short-horizon safe haven only for certain equity markets; BTC is not. Commodities USED to diversify equities. T2 and T3 explain why the latter two facts have changed.

## Theme 2: Macro regime drivers of cross-asset correlations

The T2 literature asks: conditional on the macroeconomic regime, what correlations *should* we expect?

The deepest mechanism paper is Campbell, Pflueger and Viceira (2020, P026), which builds a New-Keynesian model where the sign of the inflation–output-gap correlation determines whether nominal bonds are a safe asset (negative inflation-output correlation → bonds rally in recessions → negative bond-stock correlation) or a risky asset (positive inflation-output correlation → bonds fall when output falls → positive bond-stock correlation). They document that this sign empirically flipped around 2001, explaining why bond-stock correlations moved from the positive levels of the 1980s to the negative levels of the 2000s–2010s. The 2022–2023 inflation shock flipped the sign again, and bonds and stocks fell together. This is the single most important mechanism paper for our project, because the same reasoning directly applies to gold (a real asset whose price should rise with unexpected inflation if real rates fall, but fall if real rates rise) and to bitcoin (whose 2020-2022 correlation pattern looks like a real-asset story that subsequently broke). The earlier Campbell, Sunderam and Viceira (2017, P027) paper documents the empirical time-variation of bond-stock covariance that the 2020 model explains.

The monetary-policy shock identification literature is central. Rigobon and Sack (2004, P024) use heteroskedasticity-based identification around FOMC days to show a monetary-policy tightening reduces stocks and raises the yield curve — an effect we can use as a regime dummy when conditioning correlations. Forbes and Warnock (2012, P025) show that global capital-flow waves (surges, stops, flight, retrenchment) are driven primarily by global risk factors, not domestic fundamentals — the global financial cycle is a plausible common factor driving synchronised cross-asset correlation shifts. Adrian and Shin (2010, P032) show leverage is procyclical at marked-to-market intermediaries, and forced deleveraging connects liquidity crises to simultaneous cross-asset drawdowns — the mechanism behind the empirical fact that "in a panic everything correlates."

The financialisation-of-commodities literature is critical for T2 because it documents a STRUCTURAL break in commodity-equity correlations. Tang and Xiong (2012, P022) document that after index investment surged post-2004, non-energy commodity futures became more correlated with oil AND with equities, especially for commodities that are members of major indices. Cheng and Xiong (2014, P023) review the literature and conclude that financialisation substantially changed commodity-equity correlations: pre-2008 the correlation was in a [−0.2, +0.1] band, and post-Lehman it jumped to +0.5 and stayed elevated. This directly undermines Gorton–Rouwenhorst's 1959-2004 result: the negative commodity-equity correlation they documented is a pre-financialisation phenomenon. Any 2024–2026 observation of positive commodity-equity correlation is therefore not unprecedented — it has been the norm for 15 years.

The inflation-hedging literature for gold is nuanced. Iqbal (2017, P030) finds gold hedges US inflation only in average/bearish gold regimes, not bullish ones. Beckmann and Czudaj (2013, P031) formalise this with time-varying coefficients: gold's inflation-hedging coefficient varies substantially across the US, UK, Japan, and Euro Area, and within each is regime-dependent. The practical takeaway: "gold is an inflation hedge" is not a constant-correlation fact — it holds in some macroeconomic states but not others.

The uncertainty-index literature supplies conditioning variables. Baker, Bloom and Davis (2016, P028) construct the Economic Policy Uncertainty (EPU) index; Caldara and Iacoviello (2022, P029) construct the Geopolitical Risk (GPR) index with newspaper text analysis going back to 1985, with a historical series to 1900. Both are candidates for direct regressors (Fed regime, inflation regime, uncertainty regime, geopolitical regime) in our conditional-correlation model.

The cross-asset textbook reference is Ilmanen's *Expected Returns* (2011, P033), which emphasises that volatilities, correlations, returns, and risk premia all vary with the underlying macro factors (growth, inflation, liquidity, tail risk). Ilmanen's framework is the natural organising structure for our regime taxonomy: we condition correlations on four macro axes (Fed rate slope, CPI YoY, DXY trend, GPR level) precisely because those are the underlying factors Ilmanen identifies.

**Bottom line for T2:** once you condition on the macroeconomic regime, many correlation "puzzles" disappear. Positive commodity-equity correlation is the post-financialisation norm. Positive bond-stock correlation in 2022-2023 is the inflation-output-gap flip. Gold is an inflation hedge in some regimes but not others. A credible answer to whether 2024–2026 is "unusual" requires conditioning on the regime, not just on the calendar year.

## Theme 3: Modern safe-haven failure narratives — debasement trade, fiscal dominance, BTC-as-risk-asset

The T3 literature is the most recent and most informal — much of it is still in working-paper form, industry research, or press analysis, because we are asking about 2024–2026 phenomena.

The "debasement trade" narrative (CME 2025, P045; Gold-Focus 2024, P039; industry analyses) is that fiscal deficits, rising US debt, and a weakening dollar have driven investors to seek real-asset stores of value simultaneously, causing gold AND bitcoin AND equities (as claims on real cash flows) to rally together against a depreciating fiat benchmark. Under this narrative, the "positive correlation" observed in 2024–2026 is not an anomaly but the coherent response of real assets to fiat debasement. Its intellectual foundation is Cochrane's fiscal theory of the price level (Cochrane 2023, P034): inflation is ultimately pinned down by the present value of future surpluses relative to outstanding debt, so when markets believe surpluses will not rise, they expect inflation and bid up nominal-cash-flow assets. Sims (2016, P035) complements this with the fiscal-dominance view that at the zero lower bound and under high debt, monetary independence is effectively constrained and inflation is a fiscal phenomenon.

The BIS work (Rees 2023, P041; BIS 2023, P042) documents empirically that the historical inverse correlation between commodity prices and the US dollar broke down in 2021–2023: both rose together, both fell together. Obstfeld and Zhou (2023, P043) formalise the global dollar cycle as a dominant driver of EM asset returns — but their framework assumes the historical sign pattern, which is what has recently broken. Caldara et al. (2024, P040) show geopolitical risk shocks raise inflation and depress output in importing countries — a stagflationary shock, which would push gold and equity-as-real-claim upward simultaneously but for different reasons. These papers supply the macro context for why 2024–2026 correlations look unusual by historical standards: the underlying macro regime IS unusual.

For bitcoin specifically, the post-2020 empirical story is decisive. The January 2024 SEC approval of spot bitcoin ETFs (Statista 2025, P044; CME 2025, P045) is widely identified as an inflection: BTC's correlation with the S&P 500 rose sharply to near-permanent positive territory and its correlation with the Nasdaq is persistently strong. The institutional-adoption channel — BTC held by traditional asset managers as a risk-on allocation alongside tech equities — is the operative mechanism. This is consistent with Smales (2024, P037) and Rodriguez and Colombo (2025, P038), who argue bitcoin is NOT an effective inflation hedge despite the "digital gold" narrative; returns are dominated by risk appetite flows. Bouri et al. (2020, P036) using wavelets show BTC's safe-haven role is frequency-dependent and largely absent at high frequency — consistent with short-horizon BTC-equity correlation being driven by shared risk-on/off flows.

The BTC-gold dichotomy that CME Group (2025, P045) documents — gold strengthening the debasement trade while BTC rejoins the equity block — is the single most important stylised fact for our 2024–2026 analysis. If correct, the casual "all these assets are positively correlated" observation is actually two different stories: gold up with equities because both are real claims against fiat debasement; bitcoin up with equities because the ETF has made it structurally a risk-on tech-adjacent asset.

The critical cautions from T1 apply throughout T3: most of the "debasement trade" and "BTC is digital gold / BTC is tech-beta" evidence is at the 1–2 year horizon, is embedded in a particular (high-deficit, high-uncertainty) macro regime, and has not survived a full business-cycle turnover. The claim that 2024–2026 is a NEW regime is plausible but not yet supported by a decade of data.

**Bottom line for T3:** three narratives are actively alive in 2024–2026: (a) the debasement trade, which makes the correlation pattern coherent once framed against fiat; (b) BTC's ETF-era transformation into a risk-on tech-correlated asset; (c) the BIS-documented breakdown of the historical commodity-dollar relationship. All three are under-identified: they are consistent with the data but alternative explanations (e.g., a regime of shared liquidity drivers where everything correlates) cannot yet be ruled out. A careful conditional-correlation analysis on the 20-year panel — conditioning on Fed regime, inflation regime, dollar regime, and geopolitical regime — is exactly what the literature needs to disentangle them.

## Synthesis

The literature supplies three clear lessons for our project design:

1. Naive rolling Pearson correlations are biased in high-volatility periods (P001); any 2024–2026 correlation spike needs a heteroskedasticity-robust check. DCC-GARCH (P003) is the parametric standard.

2. The relevant question is not "what is the correlation now?" but "what is the conditional correlation given the macro regime?" T2 supplies the conditioning framework: Fed stance (P024), inflation-output-gap sign (P026-P027), policy/geopolitical uncertainty (P028-P029), global dollar cycle (P043), and commodity financialisation structural break (P022-P023).

3. The 2024–2026 narrative is itself contested. Three substantively different stories — debasement trade, ETF-era BTC, commodity-dollar regime shift — all predict positive gold/BTC/commodity-equity correlation, but they identify different mechanisms. A successful exploratory analysis at minimum distinguishes between them by looking at which sub-assets decoupled and when.
