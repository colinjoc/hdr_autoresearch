# Literature Review: Binding Constraints on Irish Housing Delivery — A Meta-Analytic Synthesis

## Theme 1: Theory of Constraints and Throughput Accounting (T1)

Goldratt's Theory of Constraints (TOC) provides the methodological backbone for this synthesis (P001, P002, P003). The core insight is that any system's throughput is limited by a single binding constraint at any given time, and improving non-constraint stages yields zero additional output. In a housing-delivery pipeline — permissions granted, commencements filed, construction underway, completions certified — TOC predicts that the stage with the lowest capacity dictates the system's throughput. Dettmer (P004) formalises the five focusing steps: identify the constraint, exploit the constraint (maximise throughput through it), subordinate everything else to the constraint, elevate the constraint (invest to increase its capacity), and repeat. Cox and Schleier (P005) extend this to multi-project environments, directly analogous to a planning system processing thousands of concurrent applications. Rahman (P006) reviews 82 TOC implementations and finds that throughput improvement averages 63% when the constraint is correctly identified versus 17% when firms improve non-constraint stages — a 3.7x difference in effectiveness. Mabin and Balderstone (P007) confirm this in a meta-analysis of 82 case studies: mean throughput gain from constraint identification is 68%.

The application to housing is direct. If the binding constraint is permission volume (~38,000/yr against a 50,500 target), then improving commencement speed, construction duration, or ABP decision times will not increase completions. Conversely, if the binding constraint is construction-sector capacity (~35,000 completions/yr ceiling), then increasing permissions to 60,000 or 80,000/yr will not help. TOC forces us to rank bottlenecks by their impact on throughput, not by their political salience.

Watson et al. (P008) trace TOC's evolution from manufacturing to services, noting that service systems (including regulatory processes) typically have the constraint at the approval/decision stage rather than the physical-production stage. Simatupang et al. (P009) apply TOC to supply chains and find that information-delay constraints (analogous to planning-decision delays) are often more binding than physical-capacity constraints. Gupta (P010) reviews constraints management across 50 industries and finds that the constraint is correctly identified in initial assessments only 40% of the time — the most common error is mistaking a visible bottleneck (e.g., construction duration) for the binding constraint when the real constraint is upstream (e.g., permission volume).

The throughput-accounting framework (P002) provides the metric we adopt: marginal units per year. Each bottleneck is evaluated by asking: if this constraint were relaxed by 10%, how many additional completed homes per year would result? The bottleneck with the highest marginal-unit-per-year response is the binding constraint.

## Theme 2: Housing Supply Constraints — International Comparative Literature (T2)

The international literature on housing-supply constraints is vast and converges on three key findings relevant to the Irish case (P017, P018, P019, P020). First, supply elasticity varies enormously across countries: Caldera and Johansson (P026) estimate long-run supply elasticities ranging from 0.15 (Netherlands) to 2.0 (United States), with Ireland estimated at approximately 0.5-0.8 depending on the time period. Second, regulatory constraints — particularly the planning-permission process — are the dominant driver of low supply elasticity in constrained markets (P019, P020, P023, P024). Third, the binding constraint is almost never construction technology or materials; it is the regulatory pipeline upstream of construction (P017, P147, P148).

Saiz (P018) demonstrates that geographic constraints (topography, water bodies) explain about half of the cross-metropolitan variation in US housing-supply elasticity, with regulatory constraints explaining the remainder. For Ireland, geographic constraints are minimal (the country is not mountainally constrained), implying that regulatory-pipeline constraints dominate. Paciorek (P019) formalises this: in a model where permits are the gating function, the permit-processing rate sets the ceiling on new construction regardless of construction-sector capacity. Gyourko and Molloy (P020) review the full regulation-and-supply literature and conclude that "the most robust finding is that more stringent land-use regulation reduces housing supply."

Ball (P023) provides the closest UK comparator. English housing supply is constrained by planning delay, which Ball estimates costs the equivalent of 10-15% of the value of a new home. Planning applications in England take 8-16 weeks for minor applications and 13-52 weeks for major ones. Irish ABP decision times (42 weeks in 2023-2024 per PL-3) are at the upper end of this range.

The Dutch experience (P027, P085) is instructive: the Netherlands achieved a housing-supply elasticity of 0.15 despite strong economic growth, precisely because the planning system (the Wet ruimtelijke ordening) constrained permissions. The 2010 Crisis-en-Herstelwet temporarily relaxed planning requirements and produced a measurable supply response, suggesting the constraint was regulatory, not physical. New Zealand (P028) provides a similar natural experiment: the 2013 Housing Accords and Special Housing Areas Act created fast-track planning zones and produced an 18% increase in consent issuance within two years.

Hilber and Vermeulen (P147) estimate that removing planning constraints in England would reduce house prices by 35%. Hsieh and Moretti (P153) estimate that housing constraints in high-productivity US cities cost 36% of aggregate economic output through spatial misallocation. These figures establish the scale of the economic cost of planning-pipeline bottlenecks.

For Ireland specifically, the permission-to-completion pipeline has been documented in our predecessor studies. The aggregate conversion ratio (PL-0) ranges from 41% (2019) to 65% (2022), with the end-to-end cohort yield (S-1) at 35.1% overall and 51.4% for non-opt-out projects. International comparators show New Zealand at approximately 80% yield, the Netherlands at approximately 90%, and the UK at 50-70% (P028, P027, P185). Ireland's low yield is driven primarily by the CCC-filing artefact for opt-out self-builds rather than genuine non-completion.

## Theme 3: The Irish Housing System — ESRI Models, Policy Targets, and Data (T3)

The ESRI Housing Model (P031, P032, P033) is the standard reference for Irish housing demand estimation. The 2023 model estimates structural demand at 33,000-52,000 homes per year depending on demographic assumptions, with the Housing for All target of 50,500 (P030) representing the upper end. McQuinn and O'Connell (P032) decompose demand into replacement (5,000-8,000/yr), new-household formation (20,000-25,000/yr), and backlog clearance (8,000-15,000/yr).

The Housing Commission Final Report (P036) quotes approximately 60,000 live planning permissions in the system. Our predecessor PL-4 study found that approximately 9.5% of these (cluster-bootstrap CI 4.4-15.6%) genuinely lapse, implying 3,000-9,000 are inactive. The remainder are either under construction, awaiting commencement, or awaiting CCC filing.

CSO data (P091, P092, P093) provides the backbone: BHQ15 (permissions 2019-2025), NDA12 (completions by town), NDQ07 (completions quarterly), and BHQ16 (permissions back to 1975). The permission series shows 32,000-43,000 units per year over 2019-2025, with no clear upward trend. The completions series shows 15,935 (2019) rising to 34,177 (2024) and an annualised 35,000 in 2025 Q3.

The LDA (P094, P095) delivered ca. 850 homes in 2023, entirely via Project Tosaigh acquisition. This is 2.6-3.5% of national completions depending on denominator. The LDA's additionality is approximately zero — these homes were built by private developers and would have been completed regardless.

O'Toole and Slaymaker (P035) provide an ESRI supply-side decomposition identifying four constraints: planning, construction capacity, finance, and infrastructure. Lyons (P034) examines taxation effects on construction. Neither provides a formal bottleneck ranking of the kind we attempt here.

## Theme 4: Irish Planning Law and Judicial Review (T4)

The planning system is governed by the Planning and Development Act 2000 (P049) and its amendments. The SHD regime (P050, 2016 Act) was created to fast-track large housing developments but became a judicial-review magnet (P047). Our predecessor PL-1 found a state-loss rate of 14/16 = 87.5% for SHD JRs decided 2018-2021. The LRD replacement (P051) returned applications to local authorities, but PL-2 found that LRD JR intensity cannot yet be cleanly compared due to insufficient data (n=2 substantive outcomes).

ABP's decision-time crisis is documented in the annual reports (P057-P062). PL-3 found mean weeks rising from 18 (2017) to 42 (2023-2024), with SOP compliance falling from 64% to 25%. The Oaxaca-Blinder decomposition attributed 100% of the rise to within-case-type productivity change, not case-mix shift. The utilisation ratio peaked at 1.45 in 2022.

The Indecon capacity review (P053) and Farrell governance report (P054) document the institutional crisis at ABP: board-member vacancies, the governance scandal, and the IT transition. These are concurrent confounders that prevent clean identification of the JR-specific contribution to decision-time growth.

The PDA 2024 (P052) reconstitutes ABP as An Coimisiun Pleanala with new statutory timelines. Its effects are not yet measurable.

## Theme 5: Statistical and Analytical Methods (T5)

The methods used in this synthesis draw on queueing theory (P063-P069), survival analysis (P074-P075, P143-P146), sensitivity analysis (P101-P105), Monte Carlo simulation (P118-P121), structural equation modelling (P110-P117), and causal inference (P096-P099). The Oaxaca-Blinder decomposition (P072-P073) is used to separate composition effects from within-stage productivity changes. The Wilson interval (P108) and Clopper-Pearson interval (P109) provide binomial confidence intervals for rates throughout.

The key methodological challenge is that the predecessor studies provide point estimates with heterogeneous uncertainty. The lapse rate is 9.5% with a cluster-bootstrap CI of 4.4-15.6%; the CCC filing rate is 40.9% with a narrow CI; ABP decision times are 42 weeks with a 2-week CI; the JR tax is 105,504 unit-months with a range of 85,404-150,204. Propagating these heterogeneous uncertainties through a pipeline model requires Monte Carlo simulation (P118), which we implement as tournament family T05.

## Theme 6: Construction-Sector Economics and Capacity (T6)

Input-output analysis (P078, P079, P080) provides the framework for understanding construction-sector capacity. The Irish I-O tables (P080) show construction as 6.8% of gross value added. The construction-sector multiplier (backward linkage) is approximately 1.8, meaning each euro of construction output generates 1.8 euros of total economic activity.

The construction-sector workforce (P125, P126, P128, P177) peaked at approximately 267,000 in 2007 before collapsing to 100,000 by 2012. By 2024, it had recovered to approximately 160,000. The CIF (P124, P210) estimates that reaching the HFA target requires approximately 200,000 construction workers, implying a 25% shortfall.

Construction costs (P037, P082, P190) in Ireland are among the highest in the EU, with apartment delivery costs estimated at EUR 450,000-550,000 per unit in Dublin (SCSI 2022). Labour constitutes approximately 40% of total construction costs, with materials at 35% and land/regulation at 25%.

The construction-capacity ceiling is observable in the CSO completions data: actual completions reached 34,177 in 2024. The question is whether this represents a hard capacity constraint (the sector cannot physically build more) or a demand/financing constraint (developers choose not to build more at current prices). The CIF (P124) argues for the former; Lyons (P034) argues that price signals should attract more entrants. Our predecessor PL-3 (S-2) found that under a 35,000-unit capacity ceiling, the ABP counterfactual gap halves from 16,638 to 7,421 units.

## Theme 7: Cross-Domain Insights — Related Problems (T7)

Structural equation modelling (P110-P117) provides a framework for the multi-stage pipeline. The housing pipeline is formally a directed acyclic graph: permissions cause commencements (with lapse attrition), commencements cause construction (with delay), construction causes completions (with non-filing attrition). An SEM can estimate the path coefficients and identify which stage has the tightest capacity binding. Bollen (P110) and Pearl (P111) provide the theoretical foundations; Kline (P112) provides practical guidance.

The TOC literature (Theme 1) combined with the SEM literature (Theme 7) suggests a hybrid approach: use the SEM to estimate the pipeline structure and the TOC to identify the constraint. The SEM tells us the stage attrition rates and transition probabilities; the TOC tells us which stage to invest in. This is the approach we implement in tournament family T04.
