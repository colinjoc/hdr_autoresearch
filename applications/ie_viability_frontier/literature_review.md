# Literature Review: Development Viability and the Irish Residential Land Market

## Theme 1: Domain Fundamentals — The Residual Method and Development Economics

The residual method of valuation is the standard tool for assessing development viability. The method computes the residual land value (RLV) by subtracting all development costs — construction, professional fees, finance, developer profit — from the gross development value (GDV) of the completed scheme (Wyatt 2013; Isaac and O'Leary 2012). When the RLV exceeds the asking price of the land, the development is viable; when it falls below, no rational developer proceeds.

The method has deep theoretical roots. Ricardo (1817) established that land rent is a residual — the surplus after all costs of production are met. Von Thunen (1826) extended this to spatial markets, showing that land values decline with distance from the centre as transport costs consume the residual. Alonso (1964) and Muth (1969) formalised this in the monocentric urban model, where the bid-rent gradient emerges from the competition of households and firms for accessible locations. Mills (1972) provided the empirical foundation, and Wheaton (1974) derived the comparative statics.

In professional practice, the Royal Institution of Chartered Surveyors (RICS 2019a, 2019b) codifies the residual method in its valuation standards (VPS 3 and VPGA 9). Crosby and Wyatt (2016) found that the basic residual method is used in virtually all UK planning viability assessments, despite well-documented criticisms. The method is highly sensitive to assumptions: Scarrett and Osborn (2014) show that a 5% change in construction costs or sale prices can flip a site from viable to unviable. Henneberry and Crosby (2019) demonstrate that this sensitivity makes development appraisals particularly vulnerable to uncertainty, and French and Gabrielli (2004) advocate Monte Carlo simulation as a response.

The RICS residual approach calculates: RLV = GDV - Construction Costs - Professional Fees - Marketing Costs - Finance Costs - Developer's Profit - Contingency. In the Irish context, additional line items include Section 48/49 development contributions (analogous to UK Section 106 obligations) and Part V affordable housing obligations (Housing Agency 2021). The viability equation in this study simplifies to: Sale Price > Land Cost/unit + Construction Cost/sqm × avg_sqm + Finance Cost + Development Contributions + Profit Margin.

Baum et al. (2021) position development appraisal within the broader property investment framework, emphasising that the residual is not merely a calculation but an investment decision under uncertainty. The developer is buying an option on future value (Titman 1985; Dixit and Pindyck 1994), and the decision to develop depends not just on current viability but on expected future price and cost trajectories.

The core tension in development viability is between the landowner's reservation price and the developer's maximum bid. Crosby, Devaney and Wyatt (2018) call this the "threshold land value" problem: if the landowner expects a price reflecting hope value (the potential for planning permission), but the residual calculation yields a lower figure, no transaction occurs. Evans (2004) shows that this creates a systematic drag on land supply, because landowners with unrealistic expectations withdraw land from the market rather than sell at the residual.

In Ireland, the gap between landowner expectation and developer viability is particularly acute. CSO data (RZLPA02, 2024) shows median zoned land prices ranging from €156,000/ha in Tipperary to €1,588,000/ha in Dublin. At a density of 40 units per hectare, this translates to a land cost per unit of €3,900 to €39,700. When combined with construction costs of €2,500-3,000/sqm (Buildcost.ie 2025) on a 110sqm unit, the total cost frequently exceeds the achievable sale price, particularly outside Dublin and the Greater Dublin Area (GDA).

## Theme 2: Construction Economics and Cost Inflation in Ireland

Construction costs are the single largest component of total development cost in Ireland. The Society of Chartered Surveyors Ireland (SCSI 2023) reports that hard costs (materials and construction labour) account for 53% of total delivery costs, with soft costs (land, margin, levies, finance, VAT) comprising the remaining 47%. The national average cost of delivering a 3-bed semi-detached house was €397,000 in 2023, ranging from €354,000 in the Northwest to €461,000 in the GDA.

Construction cost inflation has been a persistent challenge. The Central Bank of Ireland (2023) documented the impact of rising construction costs on the residential market, noting that building and construction materials inflation — although moderating from its 2022 peak — continued to affect project pricing. The SCSI Tender Price Index recorded 3.0% annual tender price inflation in 2024, following 3.9% in 2023, indicating a continued slowdown from the double-digit increases of 2021-2022. For H1 2025, Buildcost.ie reports regional tender price increases of 1.0% in Dublin, 1.0% in Rest of Leinster, 1.5% in Munster, and 2.0% in Connacht/Ulster.

The Buildcost.ie Construction Cost Guide (H1 2025) provides EUR/sqm figures by house type and region. For a reference 4-bed semi-detached house (115 sqm): Dublin €3,020/sqm, Cork €2,720/sqm, Galway €2,610/sqm, Waterford €2,635/sqm, Limerick €2,591/sqm, North West €2,428/sqm, North East €2,690/sqm. National averages range from €2,520/sqm for a detached bungalow to €2,865/sqm for a 2-bed terraced townhouse.

The CSO Building and Construction Production Index (BEA04) provides the volume and value indices from 2000 to 2024 (base 2015=100). The residential building sub-index captures the trajectory from the Celtic Tiger peak through the crash, the prolonged depression (2009-2014), and the gradual recovery. O'Brien (2018) decomposed Irish housing costs and found that the cost structure had shifted significantly: regulatory compliance costs (including energy efficiency standards, fire safety, and disability access) added €30,000-50,000 per unit compared to pre-crisis norms. Mahon (2024) identifies labour shortages — particularly in carpentry, electrical trades, and plastering — as the binding constraint on supply expansion, with skilled labour wage inflation of 4.7% per annum forecast for 2024-2026.

International comparison (Turner and Townsend 2024) places Ireland in the upper quartile of construction costs among developed economies. The National Competitiveness Council (2024) flags construction costs as a key competitiveness challenge. The Arcadis (2025) Ireland Market View projects tender price inflation of 4-5% for 2025, driven by strong public sector demand, labour shortages, and materials inflation.

The cost trajectory matters for viability because it is not symmetric with the price trajectory. Between 2015 and 2025, the CSO RPPI for Dublin rose by approximately 60%, but construction costs rose by approximately 70% (SCSI 2023). This widening gap between costs and achievable prices is the structural driver of the viability crisis — costs are rising faster than the prices developers can charge, compressing margins even in premium locations.

## Theme 3: The Irish Housing Market and Price Determination

Irish house prices are determined by the interaction of demand (driven by income, population, credit availability, and interest rates) and supply (constrained by planning, land, labour, and viability). The literature on Irish house prices is extensive, reflecting the severity of the boom-bust-recovery cycle.

Roche (2001) modelled Dublin house prices and found that income and credit were the primary demand-side drivers. Murphy and McQuinn (2004) estimated a structural model for Irish house prices 1996-2002 and identified credit availability as the key marginal driver during the Celtic Tiger period. Kelly (2009) documented the Irish credit bubble, showing that mortgage lending expanded far beyond what fundamentals could support. Honohan (2010) provided the definitive regulatory post-mortem, identifying the failure of prudential supervision to constrain lending.

The crash was devastating: national prices fell 51% from peak (2007) to trough (2012-2013). Dublin prices fell 56%. Malzubris (2008) identified the bubble before its peak. Connor et al. (2012) compared the Irish and US credit crises, finding distinctive Irish features including a more concentrated banking sector and greater reliance on foreign wholesale funding. Lane (2011) situated the housing crisis within the broader macroeconomic collapse.

The recovery since 2013 has been sustained but uneven. The CSO RPPI (HPM09) shows national prices rising from a 2013 trough back to approximately 170 (base 2015=100) by end 2025. Regional divergence is substantial: Dublin prices recovered fastest but have since been outpaced by other regions on a percentage basis, partly because Dublin started from a higher base and faces more binding supply constraints.

McQuinn and O'Connell (2024) identify the key current challenge: the housing system's ability to produce viable projects at scale. The Central Bank's macroprudential mortgage measures (introduced 2015, reviewed 2022, adjusted 2025) set loan-to-income limits of 4x gross income for first-time buyers and 3.5x for subsequent buyers, with a 10% minimum deposit. These rules cap the effective demand ceiling: at a median household income of approximately €55,000 (CSO 2025), the maximum FTB mortgage is €220,000, plus a 10% deposit, yielding maximum purchase capacity of approximately €244,000. This is well below the national median new house price, indicating that most new housing is purchased by higher-income households or those with significant equity.

Lyons (2015, 2019) has documented the long-run supply dynamics, showing that Ireland's housing stock grew rapidly during the boom (15,000 completions/year in 2000 rising to 93,000 in 2006-2007) before collapsing to under 5,000 in 2013. The recovery has been slow: completions reached 30,330 in 2024, still far short of the Housing for All target of 50,500/year (Government of Ireland 2021). The Department of Finance (2025) projects that the housing crisis will persist for another 15 years at current trajectories. Davy (2025) estimates a need for 93,000 new homes per year to 2031.

## Theme 4: Land Price Determination and the Residentially Zoned Land Tax

Land prices are endogenous to the viability equation: in theory, land takes the residual after all other costs are met (Ricardo 1817). In practice, land transactions reflect expectations about future planning permission, future sale prices, and the option value of holding undeveloped land (Titman 1985; Cunningham 2006).

The CSO's Residentially Zoned Land Prices (RZLPA02) dataset is the first systematic attempt to measure zoned land transaction prices in Ireland. Published in 2024 (experimental series), it covers 355 transactions nationally, recording median and mean prices per hectare by county. The data reveals enormous spatial variation: Co. Dublin at €1,588,000/ha, Co. Kildare at €1,163,000/ha, versus Co. Tipperary at €156,000/ha and Co. Leitrim at €173,000/ha.

The Residential Zoned Land Tax (RZLT), introduced by the Finance Act 2021 and effective from 2025, imposes a 3% annual levy on the market value of zoned, serviced, undeveloped residential land (Revenue Commissioners 2025). The policy rationale is to discourage land hoarding and incentivise development. Goodbody (2024) identified 7,911 hectares of residentially zoned and serviced land nationally — enough for approximately 417,000 homes at prevailing densities. The predecessor study (U-1) found that the RZLT announcement was followed by a 7.1% decline in planning applications, though this was not statistically significant.

Cheshire and Sheppard (2005) and Hilber and Vermeulen (2016) show that planning constraints are capitalised into land values. In the Irish context, this means that land prices already reflect the expected viability of development — or more precisely, the expected future viability. If current viability is poor but prices remain high, it indicates that landowners are holding for future appreciation (real options behaviour) or that the transaction sample is biased toward the most viable sites.

Dixit and Pindyck (1994) provide the theoretical framework for understanding land as a real option. The option to develop is valuable because it is irreversible: once land is converted to housing, the decision cannot be reversed. This creates an "option premium" above the immediate residual value, which explains why landowners resist selling at the current residual even when it is positive. Williams (1991) and Quigg (1993) provide empirical evidence that the option premium can be 6-30% of land value, depending on price volatility and interest rates.

Capozza and Li (1994) show that the optimal timing of development depends on the growth rate of rents, the interest rate, and the irreversibility of conversion. In areas with rapid price appreciation (like Dublin 2014-2022), the option value of waiting can exceed the value of immediate development, leading to strategic delay. This is consistent with the low conversion rates documented in U-1.

## Theme 5: Development Contributions and Planning Obligations

Development contributions are charges levied on developers to fund infrastructure (roads, water, parks, community facilities) necessitated by new development. In Ireland, Section 48 of the Planning and Development Act 2000 empowers local authorities to adopt development contribution schemes, with Section 49 allowing for supplementary contributions for specific infrastructure projects.

The level of contributions varies substantially across local authorities. Dublin development contributions are approximately €25,000-30,000 per residential unit, reflecting higher infrastructure costs and land values. Rural counties typically charge €5,000-15,000 per unit. These charges are non-negotiable in Ireland — unlike the UK's Section 106 system, which allows viability-based negotiation (GOV.UK 2024; S106 Management 2024).

In the UK, the interaction between Section 106 obligations and development viability has been extensively studied. Crook et al. (2016) document the growth of planning gain, showing that developer contributions increased substantially from 1990 to 2015. Carozzi et al. (2020) evaluate the effectiveness of planning obligations, finding that they reduce the supply of market housing but increase the provision of affordable units. Morrison and Burgess (2014) examine the tension between inclusionary housing requirements and development viability.

Ireland's Part V obligation requires developers to transfer 20% of units in schemes of 10+ units to the local authority for social and affordable housing (Housing Agency 2021). The obligation was reduced from 20% to 10% in 2015 and restored to 20% in 2021 under the Affordable Housing Act. The impact on viability is significant: Part V at 20% effectively removes one-fifth of the developer's revenue stream while the units must be transferred at a price below market value. The Housing Agency estimates the Part V cost to developers at approximately €30,000-50,000 per affordable unit delivered, depending on the gap between transfer price and market value.

The combined impact of development contributions and Part V on viability is substantial. For a scheme in Dublin with a sale price of €480,000 per unit: €28,000 in development contributions plus an effective Part V cost of approximately €40,000 per market unit (representing the cross-subsidy for the affordable units) reduces the effective sale revenue by approximately 14%. In marginal viability conditions, these policy costs can be the difference between development proceeding and land sitting idle.

Colenutt (2020) argues that the viability framework has been captured by developer interests in the UK, with viability assessments used to negotiate down affordable housing obligations. Christophers (2014) extends this critique, arguing that viability models performatively construct the market rather than passively measuring it. McAllister (2017) provides a more measured assessment, noting that while viability models are imperfect, they provide a necessary framework for balancing public and private interests in planning.

## Theme 6: International Comparisons — Viability Testing in UK and EU Planning Systems

The UK provides the closest comparator for Ireland's viability framework. Crosby and Wyatt (2016) document the evolution of viability testing in English planning, from informal negotiation to formalised assessment under the National Planning Policy Framework (NPPF). The 2019 NPPF revision moved viability assessment to the plan-making stage, meaning that individual site viability assessments should be unnecessary if the local plan's requirements are set at a viable level (RICS 2019a).

Barker (2004) identified planning constraints as the primary driver of housing unaffordability in England, a finding subsequently confirmed by Hilber and Vermeulen (2016) who estimated that planning constraints add approximately 35% to English house prices. However, Cheshire (2018) argues that supply-side reforms alone are insufficient — the British planning system is "broken" in ways that go beyond simple regulatory relaxation.

In other EU member states, the viability framework differs substantially. Oxley (2004) provides an international comparison, showing that continental European countries generally rely more on direct state provision of social housing and less on planning obligations to cross-subsidise affordable units from private development. Stephens et al. (2010) document the diversity of EU housing policy, from the Netherlands' extensive social housing sector to Spain's emphasis on homeownership. Kemeny (1995) theorises the distinction between "dualist" rental markets (like Ireland and the UK, with a residualised social sector) and "unitary" markets (like Sweden, with a large, competitive rental sector).

The Letwin review (2018) examined build-out rates in England and found that large developers deliberately slow build-out to maintain local prices — a "drip-feed" strategy that constrains supply even when viability is strong. This has implications for Ireland, where a small number of large developers dominate the market and may have similar incentives.

Internationally, construction cost comparisons (Turner and Townsend 2024) show Ireland in the upper quartile. Part of this reflects higher specification requirements (energy efficiency standards), part reflects labour market tightness, and part reflects the diseconomies of a small construction sector that lost capacity during the 2008-2013 crash and has not fully recovered.

## Theme 7: Related Problems — Real Options, Housing Finance, and Macro-Financial Linkages

The viability question connects to several broader economic literatures. The real options framework (Dixit and Pindyck 1994; Titman 1985) explains why land remains undeveloped even when the immediate residual value is positive: the option to wait has value because development is irreversible and prices are uncertain. Cunningham (2006) provides empirical evidence that housing starts are delayed when price uncertainty increases, and Capozza and Li (1994) show that the optimal trigger price for development can be 10-30% above the breakeven level, depending on volatility.

Housing finance plays a dual role in viability. On the demand side, mortgage availability determines the achievable sale price (Mian and Sufi 2009; Mian et al. 2013). The Central Bank of Ireland's macroprudential measures (LTI limit of 4x income, LTV limit of 90% for FTBs) cap effective demand and therefore the GDV in the residual calculation (Central Bank of Ireland 2022, 2025). BIS (2021) documents Ireland's macroprudential framework in comparative context. On the supply side, development finance rates and availability determine the finance cost in the viability equation. Current development finance rates in Ireland are approximately 7-12% per annum, with 60-70% loan-to-cost ratios typical (Cushman & Wakefield 2025).

The macro-financial stability literature provides cautionary context. Kelly (2009), Honohan (2010), and Connor et al. (2012) document how the pre-2008 housing boom was fuelled by excessive credit expansion, which inflated both house prices and land values. When the bubble burst, the collapse in land values destroyed bank balance sheets and triggered a sovereign debt crisis. This history conditions the current policy environment: regulators are unwilling to relax lending standards to improve viability, even though tighter credit constraints cap achievable prices.

Piketty (2014) and Shiller (2015) provide broader theoretical context. Piketty's analysis of wealth accumulation shows that housing has been the primary vehicle for wealth concentration in developed economies, creating political incentives to maintain high prices. Shiller documents the role of irrational exuberance in property markets and the difficulty of identifying bubbles in real time — relevant to any assessment of whether current Irish prices represent a new equilibrium or another unsustainable peak.

The Tobin's Q framework (Tobin 1969; Hayashi 1982) offers a useful analogy: development viability is essentially Q for housing — the ratio of the market value of a completed house to the replacement cost. When Q > 1, development is profitable; when Q < 1, no rational agent builds. The national average viability margin calculated in this study is effectively the inverse of the Q ratio, measuring the gap between price and cost.

Glaeser et al. (2008) examine the relationship between housing supply elasticity and bubble formation, finding that inelastic supply (as in Ireland, where planning and viability constraints bind) amplifies price volatility. Saiz (2010) shows that geographic constraints create persistent supply inelasticity. In Ireland, both geographic constraints (Dublin is bounded by the sea and mountains) and regulatory constraints (planning system, development contributions, Part V) create the conditions for the viability squeeze documented in this study.
