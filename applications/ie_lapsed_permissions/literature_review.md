# Literature review — Lapsed Irish planning permissions (PL-4)

## Theme 1. Real-options theory of development timing

The intellectual backbone for thinking about why a developer might hold an unbuilt permission is the real-options theory of irreversible investment (Titman 1985; McDonald & Siegel 1986; Dixit & Pindyck 1994). Once a developer is granted planning permission, they own a real option: the right, but not the obligation, to build at a given density on a given site within some window (in Ireland, 5 years by default under s40 of the Planning and Development Act 2000, extendable to a further ~5 under s42, and since the 2025 Amendment Act s28, by a further up to 3 years for uncommenced residential permissions). The more uncertain the payoff at the time of decision, the more valuable the option to wait. Quigg (1993), Capozza & Helsley (1990) and Cunningham (2006) provide empirical corroboration on US commercial and residential land: prices of vacant land exceed the discounted present value of the best current use by the size of the option premium, and price volatility is the main driver. Bulan, Mayer & Somerville (2009) show that competition erodes — but does not eliminate — the option to wait, and crucially that developers time starts with respect to idiosyncratic demand for their specific product mix, not just the aggregate market.

For Ireland, the implication is stark. A permission granted in 2014 for 50 units on an edge-of-town site in Cork is in 2020 economically a very different asset than it was in 2014: construction costs (SCSI 2024; CIF 2021) have doubled in some components, financing costs have risen sharply from 2022 onward (CBI FSR 2023), the S23 tax incentive has long gone, and the post-COVID remote-work shock (Mondragon & Wieland 2023; Gupta et al 2022) reshaped location preferences. A rational developer who expected sale prices to rise faster than construction costs would build immediately; a rational developer who expected costs to escalate faster, or who had balance-sheet constraints (post-NAMA survivors, see Byrne 2015), would wait and extend or let lapse. Hence lapse is not automatic evidence of bad faith or hoarding: it is the exercise-versus-wait decision in its starkest form.

A key refinement from the literature: lapse is a costly signal. If the developer truly intended the land to be built, they would have pursued extension, which Section 42 makes administratively easy. Letting a permission lapse abandons accumulated sunk cost in architect fees, planning contributions, and the years of land-banking carry cost. Therefore an observed lapse rate gives us a lower bound on the share of permissions the developer has concluded are not worth building (at current product mix). Murray (2014, 2016, 2020) argues that "hoarding" in its strong form — deliberately holding permissions to manipulate market supply — is unsupported by UK and Australian data; instead, lapse rates track cyclical conditions tightly, with lapses concentrated in periods following demand collapses. Lichfields (2016, 2023) and Molior London (2014) reach similar conclusions for the UK: the "1m unimplemented consents" figure often cited is largely composed of consents where schemes are staged, phased, or face external constraints (infrastructure, S106 negotiations, judicial review) rather than genuine strategic delay.

The Irish literature is thin on this point — Sirr & Phillips (2020) is an unpublished UCD working paper, and the Housing Commission Final Report (2024) quotes ~60k "live" permissions but does not distinguish lapse, extension, or delivery status. Our contribution is to measure directly. The real-options framing tells us the expected heterogeneity: lapse rates should be highest where price uncertainty was highest at time of grant (2014-2016 grants, peak-uncertainty post-crash recovery), and lowest where an immediate-build incentive existed (build-to-rent demand 2018-2020, Dublin apartment cells).

Real-options theory also predicts a size asymmetry: large schemes have higher fixed exercise costs (mobilising contractor, equity stack, infrastructure prerequisites), which pushes optimal exercise later and increases both the lapse-through-delay risk and the extension uptake. Small single-dwelling permissions — one-off houses — have near-zero exercise cost and should exhibit much lower lapse rates conditional on the owner's intent to live there. This predicts the empirical wedge we'll examine in E03. Finally, the option-exercise game literature (Grenadier 2002; Lambrecht & Perraudin 2003) predicts that in thin local markets with a small number of developers, lapse decisions become strategic: a developer may let a permission lapse to signal to competitors that the market is soft, influencing their entry decisions.

## Theme 2. Irish planning history: Section 42, SHD, LRD and the 2025 amendment

Ireland's Planning and Development Act 2000 established the modern regime: a planning permission is valid for 5 years by default (s40), and Section 42 allows an extension of duration ("EoD") where (a) substantial works have been carried out in the 5-year period, or (b) exceptional circumstances justify extension. The 2010 Amendment Act tightened s42 to prevent indefinite extensions, and the 2015 Amendment Act introduced the Vacant Site Levy (VSL) to penalise holding zoned residential land undeveloped. Neither change appears to have materially changed behaviour at the aggregate level — CSO BHQ/PLA series show a permissions-to-completions gap that widens from 2016 to 2023. The Strategic Housing Development (SHD) regime (2016-2021) allowed direct-to-ABP applications for schemes of 100+ units, cutting LA involvement and compressing decision timelines to ~16 weeks from ~36 weeks under the normal route (ABP Annual Reports 2018-2022). SHD was terminated following the wave of judicial reviews (Byrne & Deaf 2022; Harrington 2022): the 2021 LRD (Large-scale Residential Development) regime returned consenting to local authorities while preserving a fast-track timetable. The Planning and Development Act 2024 recasts the whole permissions regime, and its Section 28 commencement — the 2025 Planning and Development (Amendment) Act — inserts provisions allowing un-commenced residential permissions to be extended by a further 3 years, up to a total of 8 years from grant.

This regulatory trajectory matters for our analysis in three ways. First, the SHD regime changed who obtained permissions: institutional PRS developers and Approved Housing Bodies became major players (Waldron 2020; Duffy & Byrne 2021), and their exercise decisions differ from legacy builders. Second, the 2025 S28 amendment legalises what was previously a grey area: extensions for permissions with zero substantial works. If the observed extension rate is already high — which we cannot measure directly from BCMS, since extension events are not recorded there — the amendment may simply codify status quo; if it is low, the amendment changes incentives materially. Third, the political framing of "60k live permissions" as available supply assumes exercise. This paper's central empirical contribution is to quantify how much of that stock is (a) already commenced, (b) extended but uncommenced, (c) genuinely lapsed.

The Irish legal literature on S42 (Peake 2015; Galligan 2016; Simons 2020) documents that extensions are granted by the LA, not ABP, and that court challenges to refusal of S42 are uncommon and usually fail. There is no national aggregate dataset of S42 decisions; the Housing Commission (2024) recommended that DHLGH publish such a series, but as of ETL date 2026-04-14 no such series exists. This is the central data-availability caveat for our work: we can observe grant (from the planning register) and commencement (from BCMS), but not extension events. Therefore "no BCMS match within X years of grant" conflates (i) lapsed permissions never commenced, (ii) extended permissions not yet commenced, and (iii) data-join failures (name variants, typos, withdrawn). Our E01/E02 experiments quantify (iii); the remaining lapse rate is an upper bound on (i).

International comparators deepen the framing. In the UK, the Letwin Review (2017-2018) considered permission-to-delivery gaps on large sites and concluded that strategic land banking is not the primary driver; build-out rates are set by absorption — how quickly a developer can sell at their target margin. In New Zealand under the RMA, resource consent lapse regimes are similar (5 years default, extendable); Auckland Council data (2016) show lapse rates of ~8-15% in normal market conditions but rising to 20%+ in post-crash years. The Productivity Commission NZ (2017) used lapse rate variation across regions to argue that lapse correlates more with local market conditions than with applicant type. This informs our E07 (Dublin vs non-Dublin) and E10 (top/bottom LAs) experiments.

## Theme 3. Development economics and permission hoarding

The empirical development-economics literature divides into two camps. The "hoarding" camp (Crosby & Henneberry 2016; Christophers 2021; Ryan-Collins 2018; Nightingale Hall 2022) argues that developers strategically delay exercise to manipulate local supply, extract planning gain, or await upzoning. The "rational delay" camp (Murray 2016; Bulan Mayer Somerville 2009; Mayer & Somerville 2007; HBF 2013) argues delay is primarily driven by cyclical conditions, infrastructure readiness, S106/development-contribution negotiations, and site-specific constraints. Empirical tests favor the latter: Lichfields (2016) shows UK lapse rates of ~11-14% across all permissions and 6-8% for residential, consistent with rational delay. Murray (2014) decomposes Australian permission stocks and finds that roughly 80% of "held" land is either uneconomic at current prices, pending infrastructure, or being phased; only 20% is compatible with strategic-hoarding behaviour. Molior London (2014) tracked 82k London consents and concluded the "land-banking" narrative was overstated.

The Irish context supports the rational-delay view with one twist: the credit channel. Post-2008 NAMA-era survivors were capital-constrained for a decade (Byrne 2015; Waldron 2020); permissions granted in 2014-2016 often sat because the developer could not raise equity or debt, not because they were waiting for price appreciation. Duffy & Byrne (2021) document that institutional PRS capital arrived around 2016-2018 and was concentrated in Dublin apartment schemes; this implies (hypothesis) lapse rates should be lowest in Dublin apartment stratum and highest in outer-ring commuter-belt counties where institutional capital did not follow.

A second Irish-specific consideration is Section 42's asymmetric visibility: the Planning Authority records the grant of EoD in its own register but these are not federated into the national register. Consequently, an observed "no BCMS match" at Grant+5 years may be an extended permission still live, a genuinely-lapsed permission, or a commencement that happened but failed to join (e.g., because the applicant used a different Planning Permission Number format). The standard methodological response is to bound the estimate: a pessimistic upper-bound treats all unmatched as lapsed; an optimistic lower-bound attempts fuzzy matching (E02) and removes structural non-matches (E05 RETENTION, E06 REFUSED, E18 withdrawals).

Finally, the development-economics literature cautions against inferring policy from aggregate lapse rates without size-stratifying: one lapsed 500-unit permission equals 250 lapsed two-unit permissions in housing-supply terms, but represents a very different decision structurally. Our E09 experiment stratifies by scheme size precisely for this reason.

## Theme 4. Measurement and methods: survival analysis for time-to-commence

Methodologically, this is a survival analysis problem with right-censoring. The event of interest is commencement (observed via BCMS); censoring occurs when the observation window ends before commencement AND the permission is still live (not yet expired including any s42 extension). Failure to commence before expiry is a competing risk — the permission "dies" — but for simplicity we code lapse as a terminal event where no commencement is ever observed by the data ETL date. The Cox proportional hazards model (Cox 1972; Kalbfleisch & Prentice 1972) with Breslow ties provides the workhorse regression; lifelines (Davidson-Pilon 2016) is the Python implementation. We will use Cox on time-from-grant to time-of-commence, censored at ETL date, with covariates LA, scheme size, application type, one-off flag, grant year, and Dublin indicator. An accelerated-failure-time (AFT) Weibull model (Therneau & Grambsch 2002; Brilleman et al 2015) provides a parametric sensitivity check (E17), with the shape parameter indicating whether the hazard is increasing (plausible: as the 5-year clock winds down, commencement becomes more urgent for those who will exercise), constant, or decreasing.

For the classification framing (lapsed = 1, commenced = 0, given grant cohort with matured expiry), logistic regression (Hosmer & Lemeshow 2013), gradient boosting (Friedman 2001; Chen & Guestrin 2016; Ke et al 2017), and random forests (Breiman 2001) are all appropriate; the linear model acts as the Phase 1 sanity check. Predictive performance is measured by AUC-ROC for classification and C-index for Cox. SHAP values (Lundberg & Lee 2017) allow feature-importance attribution. Because classes may be imbalanced and many covariates are high-cardinality categorical (31 LAs), target-encoded features (Pargent et al 2019) or CatBoost (Prokhorenkova et al 2020) handle the cardinality without full one-hot.

Binomial confidence intervals on lapse shares should use Wilson (1927) or Clopper-Pearson (1934) — never the normal-approximation — because the proportion ranges across large datasets where the approximation is nearly fine but on stratified cells (e.g., a specific LA-size bucket) small-sample intervals matter. We report Wilson 95% CIs on all lapse rates.

Because the outcome is reporting-channel observed (BCMS matching hinges on a CN_Planning_Permission_Number field that developers choose to fill), program.md mandates a lookalike-placebo check: is the "non-match" population structurally similar to the match population on observables, or are there cells where BCMS coverage is known-poor? We address this with E01 (join-match rate), E02 (fuzzy matching), and E19 (number-format normalisation).

## Theme 5. Feature engineering for lapse prediction

Feature candidates derive from the observable register fields plus domain-engineered derivatives. Primary candidates:

1. **grant_year** — cohort effect (cyclical + policy-regime).
2. **LA** (31 levels) — regime-enforcer heterogeneity, local market.
3. **app_type** (PERMISSION / OUTLINE / RETENTION) — outline permissions are not build-ready; retention is backward-looking.
4. **decision** (CONDITIONAL / GRANT / REFUSED) — we filter to granted only but conditions vary.
5. **scheme_size** — number of residential units; strong non-linearity.
6. **one_off_flag** — individual-dwelling economics distinct from multi-unit.
7. **floor_area** — size proxy.
8. **area_of_site** — hectares; proxies large-scheme infrastructure need.
9. **appeal_status** — permissions appealed to ABP often have conditions changing timing.
10. **applicant_individual_flag** — presence of Applicant Forename as proxy for individual vs corporate.
11. **dublin_flag** — derived from LA.
12. **SHD_era_flag** — granted 2017-2021 under SHD regime.
13. **COVID_granted** — granted 2020+ (post-March 2020).
14. **rubicon_crossed** — grant-year in cycles (pre-SHD 2014-2016, SHD 2017-2021, post-SHD 2022+).
15. **decision_lag** — time from ReceivedDate to DecisionDate; long lags may signal contested sites.
16. **appeal_lag** — time from DecisionDate to AppealDecisionDate when appealed.
17. **expiry_horizon** — ExpiryDate minus ETL date (if still live, quantifies remaining exercise window).
18. **SI** — land use code flagged commercial-in-residential.
19. **retention_nearby** — proxy for site having been partly built.
20. **FI_requests** — FIRequestDate populated; indicates multiple further-information rounds.

Feature-engineering literature (Varian 2014; Mullainathan & Spiess 2017) emphasises domain interpretability: we want features a planning officer or economist could name, not arbitrary hashes. The design variables for Phase B are LA x scheme_size x app_type x grant_year — these are the levers a prospective developer can reason about at site-selection time.

## Theme 6. International comparables and policy implications

Lichfields (2016, 2023) estimates UK residential permissions lapse rates at 6-8% for schemes <10 units and 11-14% for all permissions, but with wide regional variation. The Letwin Review (2018) found no evidence of strategic hoarding at scale; instead large-site build-out is absorption-constrained. In New Zealand, Auckland Council (2016) reported lapse rates ~10% in stable markets but 20%+ in post-GFC years. The UK LGA's headline figure of "1 million unimplemented consents" (LGA 2020) has been consistently challenged as conflating phased consents with genuinely-lapsed ones (HBF 2013; Lichfields 2023).

The international pattern implies the Irish lapse rate should fall in a 10-25% range in steady-state, with concentration in rural one-off housing (where individual intent can shift with personal circumstances) and in 2014-2016 post-crash grants where developer solvency was binding. We expect 2019 grants to show lower lapse rates given the stronger 2019-2024 market, mitigated by COVID shock in early implementation periods.

Policy implications of our estimates: if the headline lapse rate exceeds ~25% in the 2014-2019 cohort, the "live permissions = imminent supply" narrative that underpins Housing for All demand-side policy (extending Help-to-Buy, shared equity, etc.) is fragile. If the lapse rate is concentrated in specific LAs or regimes, targeted levers (VSL enforcement, LA-level CPO powers, phased planning gains) are indicated. The 2025 S28 amendment is a revealed-preference signal: government sees enough extendable permissions in the pipeline that locking them in for 3 more years is worth legalising. If most permissions already take s42 extensions, S28 changes little; if extensions are rare, S28 represents a substantial incentive shift toward holding.

## Theme 7. Cross-domain insights: from energy leases to software licenses

The oil-and-gas lease literature (Paddock Siegel & Smith 1988) offers the cleanest real-options analog: oil-lease owners have a right but not an obligation to drill, with a use-it-or-lose-it clock. Observed drilling rates in lease data have been used to back out the option premium, and drill-timing matches the McDonald-Siegel model well. The methodological transfer to planning permissions is that lapse rates can be used to estimate the option premium embedded in a permission: a high lapse rate implies high option premium (high uncertainty), and vice versa.

Software-license and patent-renewal literatures provide another analog: patents have renewal fees and expiration clocks, and renewal-fee data are used to infer the private value of the patent. The Irish s42 extension regime is a non-fee version of this: extension is granted if substantial works have been done OR if exceptional circumstances exist. Applying the patent-renewal logic, the stratum-specific lapse rate (conditional on observable development characteristics) is a valuation signal about which permission types have the lowest private value — these are the permissions that neither commence nor extend.

Finally, the forest-economics literature on optimal harvest timing (Faustmann 1849; Clarke & Reed 1989) offers a normative benchmark: when a resource owner has an option to harvest (build) at any time, the optimal timing is when the marginal benefit of waiting (appreciation) equals the carrying cost (planning extensions, VSL, architect re-validation). If observed lapse/extension behaviour systematically deviates from the normative benchmark, the system has frictions worth characterising — this is a Phase B discovery direction.
