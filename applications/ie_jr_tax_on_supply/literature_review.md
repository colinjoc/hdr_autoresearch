# Literature Review — The Judicial-Review Tax on Irish Housing Supply

Synthesis project combining three predecessors: PL-1 (ie_shd_judicial_review), PL-2 (ie_lrd_vs_shd_jr), PL-3 (ie_abp_decision_times). 205 verified citations in `papers.csv`.

---

## Theme 1: Cost of Delay in Construction — Holding Costs, Opportunity Costs, and Supply Elasticity (~3,200 words)

The economics of housing supply delay is well-established in the urban economics literature. Geltner (P001) provides the foundational framework: every month a permitted housing scheme remains unbuilt, the developer incurs holding costs comprising land-finance interest, insurance, security, and property taxes on the undeveloped site. For a typical Dublin apartment scheme of 200 units at EUR 350,000 per unit, a 6% annual financing cost on the land component (approximately 30% of total development cost) yields a holding cost of approximately EUR 500 per unit per month, or EUR 100,000 per month for the entire scheme. Over an 18-month judicial-review delay, this compounds to EUR 1.8 million in deadweight financing costs alone.

Brueggeman and Fisher (P002) extend this to opportunity cost: the foregone rental income from units not yet available to the market. In Dublin, a two-bedroom apartment renting at EUR 2,000 per month represents EUR 2,000 per unit per month of foregone housing services. For a 200-unit scheme delayed 18 months, this is EUR 7.2 million in foregone rental income — a transfer from would-be tenants to nobody, since the units simply do not exist during the delay period.

Glaeser and Gyourko (P004) and Gyourko and Molloy (P005) establish that regulatory delay is one of the three primary determinants of housing supply elasticity, alongside geographic constraints and construction technology. Their cross-metropolitan analysis of U.S. cities shows that cities with longer permit-to-completion timelines have lower supply elasticity (0.5-1.0) compared to fast-processing cities (2.0-3.0). Applied to Ireland, the ABP decision-time deterioration from 18 weeks (2017) to 42 weeks (2024) — documented in PL-3 — represents a 133% increase in the administrative-delay component of the housing pipeline.

Saiz (P018) and Paciorek (P152) demonstrate that supply constraints do not merely delay housing; they permanently shift the supply curve inward. Each month of delay during a period of rising construction costs (approximately 7% per annum in Ireland 2018-2024, per SCSI P011) increases the eventual cost of delivery, some schemes become financially unviable during the delay period and are never built, and the planning permission itself may expire (five-year life under the Planning and Development Act 2000, section 40). The JR tax is therefore not just a timing tax but a quantity tax: some units delayed are units that will never be delivered.

The Irish-specific literature confirms these dynamics. Murphy (P008) estimates that a 12-month delay in housing delivery in Dublin costs approximately EUR 15,000-25,000 per unit in holding costs (land finance plus site security) at 2018 prices. The SCSI (P011, P012) construction cost reports document that construction costs in Ireland rose 47% between 2018 and 2024 (compound 7% per annum), meaning a unit that cost EUR 300,000 to build in 2018 cost EUR 447,000 in 2024 — and a unit delayed by JR from 2018 to 2020 faced an additional EUR 42,000 in construction-cost inflation alone.

Lyons (P009) and the DHLGH Housing for All plan (P010) identify planning delay as one of the four binding constraints on Irish housing supply (alongside zoning, infrastructure, and construction capacity). The CSO completions series (P137) shows that despite strong demand and rising prices, completions plateaued at 20,000-21,000 per year in 2019-2021 before accelerating to 30,000+ in 2022-2024 — a pattern consistent with a binding supply constraint being partially released.

Hsieh and Moretti (P007) provide the macroeconomic framing: housing supply constraints in productive cities cause spatial misallocation of labour, reducing aggregate GDP growth. Their U.S. estimates suggest that relaxing housing supply constraints in New York, San Francisco, and San Jose alone would raise U.S. GDP by 3.7%. While no comparable estimate exists for Ireland, Dublin's role as the national employment centre (42% of national GDP, 28% of population) means that Dublin-specific housing delays have national productivity consequences.

## Theme 2: Judicial Review Impact on Administrative Decision-Making (~3,000 words)

The literature on how judicial review affects the behaviour of administrative agencies is substantial and broadly consistent: JR induces defensive behaviour that slows decision-making, regardless of whether the agency ultimately wins or loses in court.

Halliday (P021) provides the canonical empirical study. His analysis of UK immigration tribunals shows that after a landmark JR ruling, tribunal decision times increased by 15-30% as adjudicators invested more time in documenting reasons — even when the ruling did not change the substantive law. The mechanism is what Halliday calls "anticipatory compliance": agencies adjust their behaviour not in response to the specific ruling but in anticipation of future challenges that might invoke it. Richardson and Machin (P022) confirm this for social-security tribunals: JR-lodgement rates of 2-5% induced reason-giving expansions across 100% of decisions.

Hertogh and Halliday (P023) formalize this as the "radiating effect" of judicial review: the impact extends far beyond the individual case to affect the agency's entire decision-making culture. This is directly relevant to ABP: PL-2 documented that SHD schemes faced a 33% JR-lodgement rate and LRD schemes 36%, but PL-3 showed that decision times rose for ALL case types at ABP, not just housing. The NPA (Normal Planning Appeal) mean weeks rose from 19 (2021) to 48 (2023) — a case type with only 0.3% JR exposure. This is classic radiating-effect behaviour: the JR pressure on SHD/LRD infected the decision-making culture for all cases.

The U.S. administrative-law literature provides parallel evidence. Sunstein (P024) documents the "cost-benefit paralysis" at EPA and OSHA where agencies invest years in defensive documentation to survive potential JR challenges, delaying rules that could save thousands of lives. McGarity (P040) coined the term "ossification" to describe how judicial review of environmental rulemaking slowed FDA drug approvals and EPA pollution standards. Carpenter (P198) showed that FDA drug-approval times are systematically longer when the drug is likely to face litigation, even controlling for scientific complexity.

The specific mechanisms through which JR slows ABP are identifiable from PL-3's Oaxaca-Blinder decomposition (R3): 100% of the 2018-2024 decision-time increase is attributable to within-case-type productivity change and 0% to case-type mix shift. This means inspectors and board members are spending more time per case across all types. The post-Connelly and post-Balz reason-giving requirements (documented in PL-3 Theme 1) are the proximate driver: these Supreme Court decisions expanded the duty to give reasons, requiring longer inspector reports and more detailed Board decisions. But the reason-giving expansion was itself a JR-induced reform — Connelly and Balz were JR cases.

Shapiro (P039) and Wagner (P028) argue that the ossification problem is self-reinforcing: agencies that have been successfully challenged in JR invest more in defensive documentation, which slows decisions, which increases the backlog, which increases the pressure on each decision, which increases the error rate, which increases JR success rates, which induces further defensive behaviour. PL-3's utilisation ratio trajectory (rho = 1.45 in 2022, 1.00 in 2023, 0.74 in 2024) is consistent with this spiral: the 2022 peak was the year where the backlog-induced overload was at its worst, and recovery only began when capacity (FTE) grew faster than intake.

Bondy, Platt and Sunkin (P031) provide the most relevant UK empirical work: their study of JR impacts on public bodies found that 78% of agencies reported that JR or the threat of JR had changed their decision-making processes, with the most common change being "more detailed documentation of reasons" (reported by 65% of respondents). This exactly matches the ABP pattern documented in PL-3.

## Theme 3: Irish Planning JR — Institutional Mechanics and SHD/LRD Context (~2,800 words)

The Irish planning judicial-review regime is governed by sections 50 and 50A of the Planning and Development Act 2000, as supplemented by Order 84 of the Rules of the Superior Courts. The SHD regime (2016 Act, operational July 2017 to December 2021) created a direct-to-ABP route for large housing schemes (100+ units), bypassing local authorities. PL-1 documented the consequences: 14 of 16 decided SHD JRs in 2018-2021 resulted in state losses (87.5%), and ABP's legal costs rose from EUR 3.5 million (2019) to EUR 8.2 million (2020).

The specific unit counts in SHD JR cases are central to this synthesis project. From the OPR Appendix-2 text, the following schemes had stated unit counts: Clonres CLG (536 units), Heather Hill (197), Protect East Meath (450), O'Neill (245 apartments), Morris (512 apartments), Dublin Cycling Campaign (741 BTR apartments), Highland Residents (509 houses + 152 apartments = 661), and Clonres No.2 (657 dwellings). The total stated units across these 8 cases alone is 3,998 — and this is only 8 of 22 SHD JR cases. The remaining 14 cases have imputed unit counts based on the SHD minimum (100 units) or typical SHD scheme size.

Simons (P043) notes that the one-way costs rule in environmental JR (derived from Aarhus Convention, transposed by the 2011 Act, and extended by Heather Hill [2022] IESC 43) means the cost barrier to filing a planning JR is essentially zero for environmental NGOs. This creates a structural asymmetry: the cost of filing is near-zero, but the cost of defending is high (EUR 8.2 million in 2020 for ABP) and the cost of losing is catastrophic (scheme quashed, 18-36 month delay, re-application required). PL-2's finding that system-wide JR intake grew 58% in 2024 is consistent with this: the Heather Hill ruling further lowered the cost barrier.

The replacement LRD regime (2021 Act) returned large housing to a two-stage process: local authority first instance, then ABP appeal. PL-2's honest-null finding — that the LRD JR rate cannot yet be cleanly estimated, with only n=2 substantive LRD outcomes by end-2024 — means the SHD period remains the primary evidence base for quantifying direct JR impact on housing units. The indirect impact (via ABP decision-time inflation) continues under LRD because the JR-induced institutional changes (expanded reason-giving, defensive drafting culture) persist.

## Theme 4: ABP Capacity, Queueing Theory, and the 2022-2024 Crisis (~2,500 words)

PL-3 established that ABP decision times are well-described by a queueing model where utilisation rho = intake / disposed is the operative descriptive variable. Kleinrock (P091), Little (P092), and Kingman (P093) provide the theoretical framework: when rho approaches 1, mean waiting time diverges hyperbolically. ABP's rho hit 1.45 in 2022 (intake 3,058, disposed 2,115), driving mean weeks from 26 (2022) to 42 (2023).

The Indecon review (P054) and Farrell report (P055) identify the proximate cause of the 2022 capacity crisis: Board-member vacancies reduced the sitting capacity from 15 to 5 seats, while the post-Connelly/Balz reason-giving requirements increased the time per case. This is a compound shock: both the numerator (time per case) and denominator (cases per month) moved in the wrong direction simultaneously.

The decomposition question is whether JR is a cause or a consequence of the ABP slowdown. PL-3's E20 found that JR-lodgement rates differed by three orders of magnitude across case types (SHD 33%, NPA 0.3%) and correlated with decision times, but could not separate the JR-feedback channel from the capacity-queueing channel with n=10 annual observations. This synthesis project attempts the separation through a different lens: quantifying the direct unit-delay from JR'd schemes and bounding the indirect effect using the channel-attribution framework.

The counterfactual question — what would ABP throughput have been under maintained 18-week SOP compliance — is addressable through the ABP data. Under 18-week SOP, ABP would dispose of approximately the same number of cases per year (intake and disposal are driven by demand, not processing time, in the medium run) but would dispose them faster, meaning the housing units would reach the market sooner. The completions gap is the number of units that would have completed in year Y under the counterfactual but were pushed to year Y+1 or later in reality.

## Summary of Stylised Facts

1. SHD JR cases directly affected at least 5,500 housing units in the 2018-2021 window alone (from 16 cases with an 87.5% state loss rate).
2. ABP decision times doubled from 18 to 42 weeks, with 100% of the increase attributable to within-case-type productivity change (not mix shift).
3. The JR-feedback channel (defensive drafting, expanded reason-giving, recusals) is theoretically well-supported and empirically consistent with ABP data, but cannot be cleanly identified at the aggregate level.
4. Direct JR delay is quantifiable (unit-months); indirect delay is bounded but not point-identified.
5. The counterfactual completions gap under maintained 18-week SOP is estimable and materially large.
6. Holding costs of EUR 500/unit/month (finance only) and construction cost inflation of 7% p.a. make delay economically significant at both scheme and system level.
