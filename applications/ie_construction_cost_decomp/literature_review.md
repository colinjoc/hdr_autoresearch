# Literature Review: Component-Level Cost Drivers of Irish Residential Construction

## Theme 1: Construction Economics Fundamentals

The construction sector occupies a unique position in national economies due to its large share of gross fixed capital formation and its extensive backward and forward linkages with other industries. Bon (1992) established the foundational "Bon curve" describing how the construction sector's share of GDP follows an inverted-U pattern across stages of economic development, a finding refined by Ruddock and Lopes (2006) using panel data from OECD countries. Hillebrandt (2000) and Myers (2016) provide the standard textbook treatments of construction economics, emphasising that construction differs from manufacturing in its project-based production, site-specificity, and the dominance of subcontracting arrangements.

Ireland's construction sector has been particularly volatile. During the Celtic Tiger period (1997-2007), construction grew to approximately 25% of GDP before collapsing to under 5% during the financial crisis (Kitchin et al. 2012, Norris and Coates 2014). The recovery since 2013 has been constrained by capacity limits, particularly in skilled labour and management capacity (Central Bank of Ireland 2023). The DKM Economic Consultants (2018) review of delivered housing costs established that hard construction costs represent approximately 53% of total delivery cost nationally and 49% in Dublin, with the remainder comprising land, professional fees, finance costs, development levies, VAT, Part V obligations, developer margins, and Building Control (Amendment) Regulations (BCAR) certification costs.

McKinsey (2020) documented that construction is among the least digitised and productivity-improved sectors globally, with labour productivity in many countries having stagnated or declined over 20 years. This productivity gap is particularly relevant to Ireland where the combination of regulatory complexity (NZEB, BCAR, fire certification) and labour shortages creates compound cost pressures. The World Economic Forum (2016) and Barbosa et al. (2017) identify modular construction, digital project management, and industrialised building systems as potential solutions, though Irish adoption remains limited.

The OECD (2023) housing policy review for Ireland highlighted that construction costs are a binding constraint on housing supply, more so than land costs — a finding confirmed by the predecessor viability frontier analysis showing construction cost is 10 times more price-sensitive than land cost in determining project viability. Egan (1998) and Latham (1994) established the UK reform framework that Irish construction policy has broadly followed, while Wolstenholme (2009) documented the limited success of these reforms.

The distinction between "hard costs" (direct construction: materials, labour, site works) and "soft costs" (land, fees, finance, VAT, margins) is central to any cost decomposition. The SCSI's annual surveys consistently find hard costs at approximately 53% nationally, though this varies by location (49% in Dublin where land costs are higher) and by building type (apartments have higher hard-cost shares due to structural complexity). Understanding which components within the hard-cost envelope drive inflation requires disaggregated data at the material and trade level, which is the focus of this study.

## Theme 2: Construction Materials Price Dynamics

Construction material prices exhibit distinct cyclical patterns driven by global commodity markets, local supply conditions, and regulatory-induced demand shifts. The CSO's Wholesale Price Index for Building and Construction Materials (WPM28) tracks 40 material sub-categories monthly since 2015 with a base of 2015=100, providing the most granular publicly available dataset for Irish material costs.

Shahandashti and Ashuri (2013) and Ashuri and Lu (2010) developed time-series models for the ENR Construction Cost Index, establishing that construction material prices follow non-stationary processes with structural breaks at macroeconomic shock points. Xu and Moon (2013) extended this with cointegrated VAR models, demonstrating that materials prices are linked through shared underlying factors — a finding our PCA analysis (Theme 4) confirms for Irish data.

The COVID-19 pandemic caused unprecedented material price disruption. Globally, timber prices spiked by over 300% between March 2020 and May 2021 before reverting, while steel prices approximately doubled (CBRE 2022). In Ireland, the WPM28 data shows timber (rough softwood) prices rising 70% peak-to-trough during COVID, structural steel fabricated metal rising 30%, and the All-Materials index rising 16%. The Ukraine conflict in February 2022 triggered a second price shock, disproportionately affecting energy-intensive materials: cement (+32%), steel (+37%), and plumbing materials (+31%), while timber — already in oversupply after the COVID spike — rose only 4%.

Cao et al. (2024) developed network models of inflation transmission between construction materials, showing that energy-intensive materials (cement, steel, glass) form a cluster that transmits inflation to downstream materials (concrete products, precast elements). This clustering is consistent with our PCA finding that just 3 principal components explain 90% of the variance in 39 material price series — suggesting a small number of underlying cost drivers (energy, global commodities, domestic demand) generate the observable price movements.

Moon and Chi (2024) demonstrated that macroeconomic indicators of trading partners improve forecasting of domestic material prices, particularly for globally-traded materials like steel and timber. This is consistent with Ireland's open economy where steel is largely imported (mainly from the UK, Netherlands, and Turkey) and timber from Scandinavia. Cement, by contrast, is largely a domestic product due to high transport-cost-to-value ratios, and its price trajectory reflects Irish domestic demand and energy costs rather than global markets.

The World Steel Association (2024), FAO (2024), and CEMBUREAU (2024) provide global context for the three most important construction materials. Steel production is dominated by China (>50% of global output), making it vulnerable to Chinese trade policy; timber markets are influenced by Canadian and Scandinavian production; cement is the most locally-produced major construction material but is highly energy-intensive, linking its cost to natural gas and electricity prices.

## Theme 3: Irish Construction Labour Market

Labour costs represent approximately 25% of total residential construction delivery costs in Ireland (SCSI calibration), making the labour market a critical determinant of construction cost trajectories. The CSO's Earnings, Hours and Employment Costs Survey (EHQ03) provides quarterly data on hourly labour costs, employment, and hours worked in the construction sector (NACE F).

SOLAS (2024) identifies construction as one of the sectors with the most persistent skills shortages in Ireland. The National Skills Bulletin 2024 found that 28% of all reported hiring difficulties are concentrated in construction, with particular shortages in plumbers, carpenters, and bricklayers. The Expert Group on Future Skills Needs (2022) estimated that Ireland would need to recruit 120,000 additional skilled construction workers and reskill 164,000 existing workers by 2030 to meet both housing and climate retrofit targets.

The labour cost trajectory from EHQ03 shows a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of 4.03% for total hourly labour costs in construction between 2015 and 2025 — marginally above the materials CAGR of 3.99%. This near-parity is itself a notable finding: despite intense media focus on materials inflation during COVID and Ukraine, over the full decade the two cost components grew at essentially identical rates. The difference is in their volatility: materials prices spiked and partially reverted, while labour costs rose steadily without reverting.

The Farmer Review (2016) of the UK construction labour model — widely cited in Irish policy circles — warned of a structural decline in construction workforce capacity, driven by an ageing workforce, poor industry image, and competition from other sectors for skilled workers. These pressures are evident in Ireland where the CIF (2024) reports that construction competes with data centres, pharmaceutical plants, and renewable energy projects for the same pool of electricians and mechanical fitters.

Dainty et al. (2004) and Loosemore et al. (2003) provide the human resource management framework for understanding construction workforce dynamics, including the shift from direct employment to subcontracting that characterises Irish construction. This structural shift means that official "employment" statistics may undercount the actual construction workforce, and that hourly labour cost data may not capture the full cost of labour to developers (which includes subcontractor margins, coordination costs, and rework).

Employment in construction doubled between 2015 and 2025 (EHQ03 data: +103%), while residential building volume output grew only 9% (BEA04). This enormous gap suggests either a severe productivity decline, a compositional shift toward more labour-intensive building types (apartments vs. houses), or measurement issues in the volume index. The SCSI annual survey consistently identifies labour as the primary cost pressure, ahead of materials.

## Theme 4: Analytical Methods for Cost Decomposition

The decomposition of construction costs into component contributions requires several complementary analytical approaches. This study employs five: CAGR ranking, variance decomposition, principal component analysis (PCA), regression analysis, and structural break detection.

CAGR ranking (our T01) is the simplest approach: compute the annualised growth rate for each material and rank them. This identifies the fastest-growing and fastest-falling components but does not account for the weight of each material in total cost. The spread of CAGRs across materials — ranging from 0.5% (glass) to 11.4% (structural steel fabricated metal) in our data — reveals the heterogeneity masked by aggregate indices.

Variance decomposition (T02) asks a more targeted question: which individual material price series contribute most to the variance of the All-Materials aggregate index? By computing the covariance of each material's monthly returns with the All-Materials return and normalising by the aggregate variance, we identify the materials whose price movements drive the index. Our analysis finds that treated timber, fabricated steel, and structural steel are the dominant variance contributors — not because they are the largest cost items, but because they are the most volatile.

PCA (T03) reveals the latent factor structure underlying all 39 material price series. Jolliffe (2002) provides the standard reference, and Stock and Watson (2002) established the use of factor models in economic forecasting. Our finding that PC1 explains 82.4% of variance and just 3 components capture 90% indicates that a small number of common factors — interpretable as energy costs, global commodity cycles, and domestic demand — drive the majority of material price co-movement. This has practical implications: forecasting the 3 factors is far more tractable than forecasting 39 individual series.

Linear regression (T04) tests whether a small subset of material indices can reconstruct the aggregate. Our finding that 5 materials achieve R² = 0.993 confirms that the aggregate index is effectively determined by a handful of components, consistent with the PCA factor structure.

Structural break detection (T05) uses the Chow (1960) test to identify regime changes. Bai and Perron (1998, 2003) extended this to multiple unknown breakpoints. Our tests confirm statistically significant (p < 0.001) structural breaks at both COVID (March 2020) and Ukraine (February 2022), validating the intuition that these events fundamentally altered cost trajectories rather than being temporary perturbations within a stable trend.

## Theme 5: Building Regulation and Cost Impact

Ireland's construction regulatory framework has undergone substantial tightening since 2014, with cost implications at every stage. The three most significant regulatory changes affecting construction costs are:

**NZEB (Nearly Zero Energy Buildings) — Part L 2019:** Effective from 1 November 2019, the revised Part L of the Building Regulations requires all new dwellings to be nearly zero-energy buildings, defined as achieving a 70% reduction in carbon emissions relative to 2005 standards (typically a Building Energy Rating of A2). SEAI (2019) estimated the average cost uplift at 1.9% of construction costs, ranging from 0.7% to 4.2% depending on house type.

Our E20 experiment provides a more granular view: by comparing pre-NZEB (2015-2019) and post-NZEB (2019-2024) inflation trajectories for regulatory-affected materials, we find excess inflation of +5.1 percentage points per year for electrical fittings, +4.2pp for insulation, +3.6pp for glass, and +2.3pp for HVAC equipment. These excess rates substantially exceed the SEAI's 1.9% one-off uplift estimate, suggesting either that the SEAI estimate was conservative, that supply-side constraints (limited heat pump installers, insulation manufacturers) amplified the regulatory demand shock, or that COVID/Ukraine effects confound the pre-post comparison.

**BCAR (Building Control Amendment Regulations) — SI 9 of 2014:** BCAR introduced mandatory certification by an Assigned Certifier and design certifier for all new buildings, adding professional fees and compliance documentation costs estimated at €2,000-€5,000 per dwelling. While BCAR is a "soft cost" rather than a direct material/labour cost, it has indirect effects on hard costs by requiring more detailed design documentation (reducing on-site improvisation) and by delaying starts (extending project timelines and finance costs).

**European Energy Performance of Buildings Directive (EPBD) 2024 recast:** The updated EPBD (European Parliament 2024) will require further improvements in building energy performance, potentially increasing requirements for heat pump installation, solar panel readiness, and enhanced insulation. These anticipated regulatory changes are already influencing material demand forecasts.

The interaction between regulation and cost is not simply additive. Boardman (2007), Lowe (2007), and Oreszczyn and Lowe (2010) document that progressive tightening of energy standards creates compound effects: each new regulation requires more complex building services, which in turn require more skilled labour (which is already scarce), which drives up both the labour cost per hour and the hours required per dwelling.

## Theme 6: International Construction Cost Comparisons

Ireland's construction costs must be understood in an international context to distinguish Ireland-specific factors from global trends. Turner and Townsend (2025) publishes the most comprehensive annual comparison, ranking cities by construction cost per square metre. RICS (2024) and Gardiner and Theobald (2024) provide additional benchmarking.

Glaeser and Gyourko (2003) and Gyourko and Saiz (2006) established the theoretical framework for understanding how regulatory constraints translate into higher construction costs. Their finding that US cities with more restrictive zoning have higher construction costs — even after controlling for input prices — suggests that regulatory complexity itself is a cost driver, through compliance requirements, delay, and reduced competition.

Somerville (1999) decomposed US residential construction costs into land, materials, labour, and regulatory components, finding that regulatory costs represented 20-30% of total cost in heavily-regulated cities. Malpezzi and Maclennan (2001) extended this internationally, finding that the UK had lower supply elasticity than the US, consistent with higher regulatory costs.

For Ireland specifically, Murphy (2014) compared Irish housing costs to international benchmarks, finding Ireland to be expensive by European standards but not exceptionally so. The Central Bank (2023) analysis showed that Irish construction cost inflation has broadly tracked the euro area average since 2019, suggesting that COVID and Ukraine effects were primarily global rather than Ireland-specific. However, Ireland's pre-existing cost level was already elevated due to regulatory complexity and labour scarcity.

## Theme 7: Construction Cycles and Macroeconomic Context

Construction costs are embedded in broader macroeconomic cycles that determine demand, financing conditions, and capacity utilisation. Barras (2009) provides the definitive treatment of building cycles, documenting long waves (15-20 years) driven by demographic and economic fundamentals, overlaid with shorter cycles (3-5 years) driven by credit conditions and policy changes.

Leamer (2007) argued provocatively that "housing IS the business cycle" for the US economy, a claim that resonates strongly for Ireland where the construction boom and bust was the proximate cause of both the Celtic Tiger and the subsequent banking crisis (Norris and Coates 2014, Honohan and Walsh 2002). The current recovery, which has seen construction employment more than double since 2015, is occurring in a different institutional environment: tighter bank regulation, mandatory building standards, and a shift from developer-led to state-supported housing delivery.

Davis and Heathcote (2005), Iacoviello (2005), and Kiyotaki and Moore (1997) provide the theoretical framework for understanding how construction costs interact with credit conditions and house prices through the financial accelerator mechanism. When house prices rise, collateral values increase, credit expands, construction demand grows, and costs are bid up — creating a positive feedback loop that the Irish experience from 2003-2007 illustrates vividly.

The current period (2024-2025) shows a moderation in materials inflation (All-Materials index +0.9% year-on-year) alongside persistent labour cost inflation (+4.0% year-on-year). This divergence is consistent with the post-crisis reversion pattern: globally-traded commodities (steel, timber) are mean-reverting as supply chains normalise, while local labour costs — driven by structural shortages — continue to ratchet upward. The SCSI Tender Price Index (+3.0% for 2024) reflects the composite of both, weighted toward labour given that materials inflation has subsided.
