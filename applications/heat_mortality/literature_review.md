# Literature Review: Predicting Excess-Mortality Heat Waves from Day-Night Humid Heat Exposure

References below cite paper IDs from `papers.csv` in this directory.

## 1. The puzzle: not every heat wave kills

Public-health monitoring repeatedly shows that heat waves are unequally lethal. Two events with similar maximum daytime temperatures can produce very different excess-mortality signatures, and the difference is usually not fully predicted by the meteorological magnitude alone [9, 10, 98, 103]. The 1995 Chicago heat wave killed at least 700 people over roughly five days [71, 72]; the 2003 European heat wave killed more than 70,000 over three weeks [14]; the 2010 western Russian event killed an estimated 55,000 [110, 112]; the 2022 European summer produced ~61,672 excess deaths across 35 countries [13]; the 2023 global heat season is associated with ~178,486 excess deaths, roughly half attributable to anthropogenic climate change [39]; and the 2024 Hajj pilgrimage in Mecca produced more than 1,300 deaths during a week in which wet-bulb conditions approached uncompensable values for sustained outdoor exertion [48, 201, 202]. In between these events lie many heat waves that local health systems describe as uncomfortable but without detectable excess mortality [103].

The operational question that motivates the heat-health early warning community is therefore not "how hot is it" but "how hot, how humid, how long, how cool overnight, and for which population" [95, 96, 101, 161]. The Multi-Country Multi-City (MCC) Collaborative Research Network — currently coordinated by LSHTM and Antonio Gasparrini — has shown across more than 1,150 locations in 53 countries that the temperature–mortality relationship is U-shaped, with a minimum-mortality temperature (MMT) that varies by climate, city, age structure, and access to cooling [6, 7, 124, 193]. In the Gasparrini et al. 2015 Lancet analysis of 384 locations, cold was responsible for ~7.29% of all-cause mortality and heat only ~0.42% [6], but most cold-related burden came from moderately cold days, whereas the heat burden was concentrated in the tail of the temperature distribution. That tail is what HHWS operators care about, because it is where interventions such as cooling centres, public alerts, outdoor-work suspensions, and hospital surge planning can change outcomes [96, 98, 101].

## 2. The 35 C wet-bulb threshold and its empirical revision

Sherwood and Huber's 2010 PNAS paper [18] proposed a theoretical upper limit for human tolerance to heat stress based on thermoregulatory physics: a sustained ambient wet-bulb temperature (T_w) above 35 C should make skin-to-air heat transfer fail, because skin temperature is tightly regulated near 35 C and metabolic heat can no longer be dissipated. The paper argued that this represents a hard thermodynamic cap on human survivability, and that although this value has not been routinely exceeded historically, future climates would push tropical coastal regions past the limit. This framing was adopted widely in the climate-change literature [22, 23, 24, 168, 217] and used to drive regional projections for the Persian Gulf [24], South Asia [23], the Indus and Ganges plains [23, 29], and eventually the rest of the globe [22, 25, 87]. Raymond, Matthews and Horton [22] showed that T_w ≥ 35 C has in fact already occurred briefly at stations in the Persian Gulf and South Asia, decades earlier than earlier models projected.

However, the 35 C number was derived from a physical argument, not measured in humans. Between 2021 and 2025 the Penn State University Human Environmental Age Thresholds (PSU HEAT) project conducted controlled chamber experiments with young healthy adults walking at the metabolic rate of activities of daily living [19]. The key result of Vecellio et al. 2022 [19] is that the critical wet-bulb temperature (T_w,crit) at which heat storage became uncompensable averaged 30.55 ± 0.98 C in warm-humid environments (36–40 C ambient air), not 35 C. In drier environments the critical value was even further below 35 C once expressed as a wet-bulb equivalent. The Wolf et al. 2023 follow-up [20] extended these experiments to older adults and found that critical limits drop further for people 65+ and drop again for sedentary versus active adults [209]. Vanos et al. 2023 [21] then reworked the global exposure arithmetic under the revised thresholds and showed that the population-weighted exposure to "dangerous humid heat" is several-fold larger than under the 35 C assumption, because 31 C T_w at moderate humidity is commonly reached in tropical and sub-tropical climates today. The Matthews et al. 2023 companion [25] confirmed the pattern across stations. Wolf et al. 2025 [27] validated the PSU HEAT critical limits in additional subgroups; Cottle et al. 2022 [207] and Vecellio et al. 2023 [208] provide detailed physiological data.

The practical consequence is that the dominant lethality metric in compiled heat-mortality databases may not be dry-bulb T_max at all, but T_w and its time-integrated exposure [21, 25, 26, 53, 170]. This is one of the two active hypotheses the HDR project will test.

## 3. Compound day–night heat exposure

The second active hypothesis is about night-time minimum temperature. Mechanistically, humans rely on overnight cooling — by radiative loss, convective loss, and adaptive sleep behaviours — to reset core body temperature after a hot day. When the nocturnal minimum remains elevated, hyperthermia accumulates across days rather than resolving. Murage, Hajat and Kovats [28] showed in London mortality data (1993–2015) that hot days followed by hot nights produced significantly greater excess mortality than hot days followed by cool nights, with the strongest effect for stroke (relative risk 1.65 at 99th vs 80th percentile), chronic ischaemic disease, and younger age groups. Royé et al. [35] replicated this pattern in 148 southern European locations. He et al. [34] extended the analysis to 178 locations and showed an independent night-time mortality effect controlling for daytime maximum, an effect consistent across climates. The He et al. 2022 Lancet Planetary Health paper [121] projected this night-time component into future climate scenarios and showed a disproportionate future burden from warming minimum temperatures rather than maximum temperatures, because minima are projected to rise faster under greenhouse-gas forcing.

Mukherjee and Mishra [29] and subsequent work [30, 36] quantified "concurrent hot days and hot nights" events in India, projecting a sixfold rise under 2 C warming. Tang et al. [31] analysed daytime–nighttime compound temperature extremes in China and found that mortality risks from compound day–night events were larger than for daytime-only or nighttime-only events and that patients with ischaemic heart disease and respiratory disease were particularly vulnerable. The most recent Nature Communications contributions — Lee et al. [32] and Yin et al. [33] — extended this to European projection and showed that compound heatwaves display a super-linear exposure–response with mortality, while daytime-only and nighttime-only heatwaves have nonlinear but less steep responses. Lee et al. [32] projected an increase of 103.7–135.1 heat-related deaths per million people per degree of global warming by 2100 in Europe under compound-exposure assumptions, with Western and Eastern Europe hit hardest.

Operationally, many current heat-health warning systems still trigger on daytime thresholds alone or on a two-day "hot day" criterion (e.g. the 95th-percentile definition used by Anderson & Bell [10]). Only a subset explicitly use compound day–night criteria, and among those that do, the thresholds are typically based on local climatology rather than on the physiology of compound exposure [101, 105, 106]. This gap is one of the potential predictive wins the HDR loop will aim at.

## 4. Heat-mortality epidemiology and the DLNM framework

The methodological workhorse of modern heat-mortality epidemiology is the distributed lag non-linear model (DLNM), developed by Gasparrini, Armstrong and Kenward [1, 2, 3, 4, 5]. DLNM defines a bi-dimensional cross-basis — one dimension for the non-linear exposure–response of mortality to temperature, the other for the non-linear lag structure over which past temperature affects current mortality — and fits them jointly, typically embedded in a Poisson regression with smooth controls for season and long-term trend. The dlnm R package [2] has become the de facto implementation in the field. The approach solves the problem that heat effects are not instantaneous: most heat-related excess mortality occurs on the same day or the following day, but cardiovascular mortality can be displaced forward by several days (harvesting), and cold-related mortality has a much longer lag structure [3, 6].

The Gasparrini 2015 Lancet paper [6] used a two-stage meta-analytic strategy: city-specific DLNMs in the first stage, then meta-regression to pool city-specific exposure–response curves across the MCC Network [32, 127, 193]. This approach is the backbone of most recent multi-country heat-mortality studies [12, 66, 115, 116, 117, 127]. Sera et al. [193] used the meta-regression stage to analyse moderators: socioeconomic variables, climate, air pollution, and urbanicity all modify the heat-mortality slope. The Gasparrini 2015 EHP paper [7] used time-varying DLNMs to show a declining heat-mortality slope in several high-income countries — evidence of adaptation over time.

An alternative is the case–crossover / case-time-series design, developed by Gasparrini et al. 2022 [120] for small-area analyses. This approach trades some statistical efficiency for higher spatial resolution and robustness against confounding by slowly varying neighbourhood factors. Bhaskaran et al. [68] is the standard tutorial on the time-series Poisson framework generally. The underlying mathematical machinery draws on penalised spline regression as described in Wood's textbook [65] and Ruppert, Wand & Carroll [69]; the earlier Hastie-Tibshirani [64] treatment of generalized additive models supplies the conceptual foundation.

## 5. Heat-health early warning systems in practice

The first operational heat-health warning system (HHWS) was Kalkstein's Philadelphia system, deployed in 1995 [183]. It relied on synoptic air-mass classification, not simple temperature thresholds, and was credited with a measurable mortality reduction [182, 184, 185]. France's Plan Canicule was designed in direct response to the ~15,000-death toll of August 2003 [99, 100]: the 2006 heat wave was used as a natural experiment and showed that observed excess mortality was only ~2,065 compared with a pre-system prediction of ~6,452, a reduction attributed to the new alert system, outreach to isolated elderly, cooling-centre deployment, and hospital preparedness [98, 102]. EuroHEAT [95] extended this framework across nine European cities, defining a standard heat-wave criterion and a probabilistic forecast tool based on ECMWF output up to ten days ahead; D'Ippoliti et al. [95] quantified 7.6–33.6% mortality increases during heat waves across the nine EuroHEAT cities.

The UK Heat-Health Alert System has evolved since the 2003 event; Thompson et al. [103] show mortality patterns under the 2020 alert structure. Germany's HHWS across 15 cities was evaluated by Karwat et al. 2025 [104] using a difference-in-differences design, demonstrating a mortality reduction concentrated in the vulnerable elderly. Spain's MoMo system [151, 152] provides rapid weekly excess-mortality monitoring fused with the national heat-health action plan. WHO's European Heat-Health Action Plan guidance [109] provides the template, and Martinez et al. 2011 [176] inventoried European HHAPs. The EuroMOMO network [154, 155] aggregates weekly all-cause mortality across 24 European countries for early-warning purposes.

Outside Europe, the Ahmedabad Heat Action Plan in India [178, 179] is the first well-documented HHWS in a low/middle-income country setting; Hess et al. [179] show a measurable mortality reduction after implementation. Chersich et al. [97] describe the first experimental heat-health warning in Africa. The US National Weather Service HeatRisk tool [106, 107], expanded nationally in 2024, combines a 5-level colour code (0–4) with local daily climatology and CDC heat-health guidance, and is the closest operational system to what the HDR loop will try to improve. The global review by Ebi et al. in The Lancet [161] calls for "smart" HHWSs that combine personalisation, local climatology, epidemiology, and built-environment factors. An analysis in PLoS Climate [160] advocates a global heat early-warning system. Systematic reviews [28 in paper 96 is 96, reviews 2023] consistently report that HHWSs do reduce mortality but that current systems miss events with atypical compound or nocturnal exposure and underweight vulnerable subgroups [101, 105, 161, 198].

## 6. Temperature exposure metrics: heat index, WBGT, UTCI, PET

Raw air temperature is an incomplete predictor of heat-stress mortality because humans also respond to humidity, wind, radiation, clothing, and activity level. A series of composite metrics have been developed:

**Heat Index / Apparent Temperature** — Steadman 1979 Part I [46] introduced a temperature–humidity index based on clothing and human physiology, with extensions for wind, radiation and pressure in Part II [47] and the universal scale [48]. The US NWS heat index tables are a polynomial fit to Steadman's results. The heat index is inputs-light (dry-bulb T and relative humidity or dewpoint) and is therefore operationally attractive, but it was designed for sedentary shaded adults and tends to under-weight the wet-bulb signal under extreme humidity [48, 53].

**Wet-Bulb Globe Temperature (WBGT)** — developed for US military heat-stress prevention and now the ISO 7243 standard for occupational exposure. Outdoor WBGT combines natural wet-bulb, globe (radiant), and dry-bulb temperatures with weights 0.7/0.2/0.1; indoor WBGT drops the globe term. Liljegren et al. 2008 [49] derived a mass/energy-balance model that produces WBGT from standard meteorological inputs (T, RH, wind, solar radiation, pressure), with RMSE < 1 C against direct measurements; this method was adopted by the US NWS. Kong and Huber 2022 [50] compared full Liljegren calculations against common approximations and showed that approximations materially underestimate extreme-heat exposure in labour-productivity assessments. Brimicombe et al. 2022 [136] produced a global WBGT gridded product from ERA5.

**Universal Thermal Climate Index (UTCI)** — developed by COST Action 730 and described by Jendritzky, de Dear and Havenith [51]. UTCI is defined as the isothermal reference-condition air temperature that would produce the same dynamic physiological strain, computed through a multi-node Fiala thermoregulation model. Brode et al. 2012 [133] describe the derivation of the UTCI scale. Pappenberger et al. 2015 [134] evaluated UTCI forecast skill. The ERA5-HEAT product [55] provides UTCI globally.

**Physiological Equivalent Temperature (PET)** — developed by Matzarakis, Mayer and Iziomon [52] on the basis of the Munich Energy-balance Model for Individuals (MEMI). PET expresses thermal strain in degrees Celsius — the air temperature that would produce the same core/skin temperature in a reference indoor environment — which makes it intelligible to urban planners. It is widely used in urban climate studies [77, 78, 81].

**Wet-bulb temperature (T_w)** itself is also used directly as an exposure metric [22, 25, 26, 58, 59, 170]. Davies-Jones 2008 [43] is the canonical high-accuracy computation by inverting Bolton's equivalent-potential-temperature formula; Stull 2011 [44] provides a gene-expression-programming empirical approximation valid at sea level for 5–99% RH and –20 to 50 C, widely used where speed matters. Buzan, Oleson and Huber 2015 [45] collected 13 heat-stress metrics in the Community Land Model HumanIndexMod; Buzan and Huber 2020 [216] is a review of T_w calculation methods. The Lo et al. 2023 comparison [53] is critical for the HDR loop: across 604 locations in 39 countries, no single heat-stress metric was optimal everywhere; temperature-only metrics performed best in about 40% of countries and apparent temperature in about 40%, with humidity-weighted metrics winning in particular climates.

## 7. Vulnerability moderation: urban heat island, housing, AC, age, isolation

Heat mortality is mediated by a long list of vulnerability factors whose relative importance depends on location [77, 78, 80, 82, 195]. The canonical list, confirmed across multiple meta-analyses [94, 228]:

1. **Age (65+)** — the dominant risk factor globally [9, 11, 37, 90, 100, 163, 209]
2. **Pre-existing cardiovascular, respiratory, or renal disease** [9, 92, 94]
3. **Social isolation** (living alone, not leaving home daily) [71, 196, 229]
4. **Housing quality** — top-floor, no cross-ventilation, poor insulation [71, 211]
5. **No air conditioning or working AC** [71, 73, 74, 75, 76, 210, 212, 213]
6. **Urban heat island intensity** [77, 83, 84, 85, 86, 172, 175, 235, 236]
7. **Low socioeconomic status, low education** [74, 78, 122]
8. **Psychiatric conditions and psychotropic / anticholinergic / diuretic medication** [94, 227]
9. **Bed confinement, inability to self-care** [71, 100]
10. **Outdoor occupation, unacclimatised labour** [130, 131, 132]
11. **Homelessness and rough sleeping** [35 vs Hondula — from Arizona heat reports, 129]
12. **Pregnancy (preterm birth, stillbirth)** [128, 191, 192]
13. **Race and ethnic minority status** (as proxy for structural AC and housing disparities) [73, 210]
14. **Dehydration and limited water access** [230]
15. **Obesity / low aerobic fitness** [164, 204, 205]
16. **Recent arrival in hot climate (lack of acclimatization)** [7, 124, 125, 127]
17. **Mental health conditions** [188]
18. **Co-exposure to PM2.5 and ozone** [110, 113, 237]

The urban heat island is particularly important in population-level forecasting because the UHI effect is largest at night — precisely the compound day–night exposure period the HDR hypothesis targets. Iungman et al. [85, 86] estimated that 4.33% of all summer deaths in European cities in 2015 were attributable to the UHI. Macintyre et al. [84] attributed 38% of London's 2022 heat-wave deaths to the UHI specifically, and the 2025 Lancet Planetary Health follow-up [83] quantified the economic burden. The 1995 Semenza et al. NEJM study in Chicago [71] is the classical vulnerability paper: the strongest protective factor was a working air conditioner, estimated to have prevented > 50% of heat-wave deaths, and the top risk factors were bed confinement, not leaving home, top-floor apartment, and living alone.

AC access is itself strongly patterned by SES and race. Barreca et al. [76] showed that residential AC diffusion explains roughly an 80% reduction in hot-day mortality across US counties since 1960. Sera et al. [75] extended this to multi-country longitudinal analysis. Gross et al. 2025 [212] and ORNL [213] provide recent fine-grained US AC-prevalence datasets. The open question is whether AC access should be treated as a direct predictor of the heat-mortality slope or as a confounder absorbed by location fixed effects [11, 75, 76].

## 8. Climate model attribution of heat waves

The World Weather Attribution (WWA) consortium has systematised rapid attribution studies that compare observed heat waves against counterfactual pre-industrial climate simulations. Key results include: the 2021 Pacific Northwest heat dome was at least 150 times more likely with human-caused climate change [16, 42]; the UK's 40 C July 2022 event was at least 10 times more likely and would have been "extremely unlikely" without climate change [41]; and the 2023 Mediterranean heat wave was "virtually impossible" without climate change [40, 215]. Vicedo-Cabrera et al. 2021 [12] used empirical data from 732 locations in 43 countries and concluded that 37% (range 20.5–76.3%) of warm-season heat-related deaths are attributable to anthropogenic warming, with the highest shares in Central and South America and South-East Asia. Méndez Turrubiates et al. 2024 [119] applied this to the 2022 European event; Zhao et al. 2025 [39] to the 2023 global event (54.29% attributable). Matthews et al. 2025 [88] argued that rare atmospheric circulation analogs already encode the potential for mass-mortality heat events, and that recurrence of historical weather patterns in the current climate would produce outcomes far worse than at the time of the original analog. The Lancet Countdown 2024 [37] reported that heat-related mortality among people 65+ has risen 167% compared with the 1990s, 102 percentage points higher than would be expected from temperature rise alone, a discrepancy it attributes to population ageing, urbanisation, and the timing of exposure. The 2025 Lancet Countdown [38] and the 2025 China report [231] continue this tracking.

## 9. Machine learning and deep learning approaches

The ML/DL heat-mortality literature is younger than the DLNM literature and is still mostly benchmarking. Boudreault, Campagna and Chebana [140, 141, 142] compared six machine and deep learning models (Decision Tree, Random Forest, GBM, SLP/MLP, LSTM) against classical statistical models (GLM, GAM, DLNM) on Montreal heat-mortality data. The ensemble tree methods (GBM and RF) had the best average performance across ordinary summer days, but when evaluated on the extreme days during actual heat waves, the non-linear statistical models (GAM and DLNM) and a simple decision tree better reproduced the observed mortality spikes. Their comprehensive 2025 literature review [142] summarises the field. Hirano et al. [139] built an ML model for in-hospital heatstroke mortality using Japanese clinical data. Ishizawa et al. [147] applied ML to weather-sensitive cardiovascular mortality in Tokyo. Li et al. 2023 [146] used a graph neural network with weather-station data for regional heatwave prediction. Khan et al. [144], Ahmadi et al. [145], Patel et al. [149], and Materia et al. [150] explored deep learning and explainable AI approaches at the event-forecasting rather than the mortality-regression level. Choi et al. [238] used geospatial explainable AI to extract key heat-illness factors at the Texas county level.

A consistent finding in this subfield: ML models do better on average, but worse on the tail events that matter most for public-health decision-making. The practical implication is that any ML heat-mortality model should be evaluated not on summer-long RMSE but on its ability to rank heat waves by excess-mortality risk and to detect high-mortality events versus the "merely uncomfortable" ones that the HDR question targets. Jones and Fleck [138] built exactly such a classifier, distinguishing high-mortality from moderate US heatwaves.

## 10. Open questions

The literature points to several unresolved issues directly relevant to the HDR question.

First, the **wet-bulb threshold** debate is live. Sherwood-Huber's 35 C [18] was never an empirical number; PSU HEAT [19, 20, 27, 207, 208, 209] measured ~30.55 C in humid lab conditions, and the revision in Vanos et al. 2023 [21] and Matthews et al. 2023 [25] implies that current heat exposure globally is already substantially closer to physiological limits than the 35 C literature suggested. However, the lab experiments are on *healthy exercising* adults; the dominant population-level heat-mortality signal is in elderly, often with comorbidity and on medications, at rest or at minimal activity. The extrapolation from physiological critical limits to population-level excess mortality is not settled [26, 209].

Second, the **compound day–night exposure hypothesis** is supported by single-city and multi-city evidence [28, 34, 35, 121] and by recent projection work [32, 33], but it has not been implemented in most operational HHWSs [101, 106]. The research question of whether adding a minimum-night-time T_w criterion to HeatRisk would have flagged the specific events that produced excess mortality (and not the ones that did not) remains open.

Third, the **choice of exposure metric** matters and is location-specific. Lo et al. 2023 [53] shows that no single metric — heat index, apparent temperature, WBGT, UTCI, T_w, dry-bulb T — is optimal everywhere. This motivates the HDR loop to test multiple metrics city-by-city rather than assume a single one.

Fourth, **adaptation is real but incomplete**. Bobb et al. [11] show a 63% decline in US heat-mortality risk from 1987 to 2005, Gasparrini et al. [7] show city-level declines, Folkerts et al. [125] show MMT shifts in the Netherlands, and Yin et al. [126] show global MMT changes. The Lancet Countdown [37] notes that observed heat-mortality in 65+ rose 167% vs the 1990s despite these adaptive gains, because population ageing and urbanisation outrun them. Adaptation also introduces a methodological difficulty: heat-mortality slopes fit on historical data will overstate future risk if adaptation continues, and understate it if adaptation stalls or if events exceed the envelope under which adaptation was acquired (Matthews et al. 2025 [88]).

Fifth, **air conditioning is both a solution and a confounder**. AC reduces individual heat-mortality risk dramatically [71, 75, 76] but its diffusion is highly unequal by race, income, and region [73, 210, 211, 212]. Models that use AC as a covariate risk conflating cause and consequence; models that ignore it miss a large part of the explainable variance.

Sixth, **existing ML models underperform on the tail events** that matter most for HHWS decision-making [140, 142]. A model that is right on average but wrong on the few days per year that drive 80% of excess mortality is not operationally useful. This is the single biggest opening for methodological improvement.

Seventh, **there is no operational global HHWS** [160, 161]. The WHO-WMO guidance [108, 109] exists, individual national systems exist, and rapid monitoring networks (EuroMOMO [154, 155], MoMo Spain [152]) exist, but there is no integrated real-time system that converts ERA5-scale meteorological forecasts into city-level excess-mortality probability forecasts.

## 11. Implications for the HDR loop

The HDR question is whether night-time wet-bulb temperature (or a compound day–night humid-heat metric) is the missing predictor in current heat-health early-warning systems for distinguishing mortality-producing heat waves from "merely uncomfortable" ones. The literature supports this as a well-posed hypothesis for three reasons:

1. Compound day–night exposure has been shown to produce super-linear mortality effects relative to daytime-only exposure [31, 32, 33, 34]. This effect is consistently reported across London [28], southern Europe [35], China [31], and multi-country aggregations [34, 121].
2. The empirical wet-bulb thresholds from PSU HEAT [19, 20, 209] are below 35 C and below many historical regional T_w maxima, which implies that T_w-based exposure metrics should have mortality-predictive power even in climates where dry-bulb maxima do not reach spectacular values.
3. Current HHWSs mostly alert on daytime temperature and/or heat index [96, 101, 106], so adding a night-time wet-bulb (or WBGT or UTCI) criterion is an identifiable and deployable intervention.

The comparison benchmark is the current state of the art: DLNM city-level models fit on dry-bulb T or apparent temperature, used by the MCC Network [6, 7, 12, 32, 127, 193] and by operational systems [102, 106, 118]. The HDR loop should:

(a) build a city-week dataset combining weekly all-cause mortality (Eurostat weekly deaths [155], CDC Wonder multiple cause of death, MoMo Spain [152]) with weekly ERA5-based exposure metrics (dry-bulb Tmax, Tmin, T_w, heat index, WBGT, UTCI) computed by the Liljegren and Davies-Jones methods [43, 49];
(b) train and compare DLNM, GBM, and compound-criterion threshold models on the task of predicting excess mortality (P-score [60, 61, 158]) at the city-week level;
(c) evaluate on out-of-sample heat events, scored not on average RMSE but on true-positive / false-positive classification of high-mortality events;
(d) ablate the night-time T_w predictor specifically to quantify its marginal information content beyond dry-bulb and humidity.

The literature surveyed above supplies both the methods and the baselines; the open methodological contribution is whether operational HHWS skill can be improved at the tail.
