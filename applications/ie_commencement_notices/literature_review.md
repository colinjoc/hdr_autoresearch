# Literature Review — PL-1 Irish Commencement-Notice Cohort Study

This review sets the methodological and substantive backdrop for a cohort study of Irish residential building projects. We trace four themes (compact form, per program.md descriptive-project allowance): (1) survival/duration analysis as the statistical framework, (2) the economics of housing supply and construction delays, (3) Irish institutional plumbing — planning, Building Control, SHD/LRD, NAMA, the Celtic Tiger collapse and the 2014-onwards BCMS era, and (4) machine-learning methods for time-to-event data applied to construction and housebuilding.

---

## Theme 1 — Survival and duration analysis as the statistical framework

The statistical theory of time-to-event modelling has a long lineage. Kaplan & Meier (1958) established the nonparametric product-limit estimator for the survival function in the presence of right-censored observations, and Cox (1972) introduced the semiparametric proportional hazards regression that became the workhorse of clinical, industrial and social-science survival modelling. The partial-likelihood argument of Cox (1975) made the PH model tractable; Breslow (1974) and Efron (1977) supplied the main tie-handling corrections. Cox & Oakes (1984), Kalbfleisch & Prentice (2002), Klein & Moeschberger (2003), Collett (2015), Hosmer-Lemeshow-May (2008) and Lee & Wang (2003) remain the canonical textbooks. Therneau & Grambsch (2000) is the standard reference for extending the Cox model — stratification, time-varying covariates, counting-process notation. Andersen, Borgan, Gill & Keiding (1993) generalised the entire framework to counting processes, which is the natural mathematical object for the filing data we hold.

For this project the most relevant subliterature is *accelerated failure time* (AFT) modelling. Where Cox's PH model leaves the baseline hazard nonparametric, the AFT model multiplies the event time itself by a covariate-dependent acceleration factor, yielding coefficients that are direct log-time-ratios. Wei (1992) is the reference paper; Bagdonavicius & Nikulin (2002) the comprehensive textbook. Because a construction "event" (commencement, completion) is naturally modelled as a positive duration in calendar time, and because the hazard is empirically non-monotone with long right tails, log-normal and Weibull AFT models are often better-fitting than the Cox PH assumption. Royston & Parmar (2002) offer flexible parametric alternatives with spline-based baseline hazards; Royston & Lambert (2011) give a Stata-centric textbook treatment of this class. The lifelines Python library (Davidson-Pilon 2019) implements Weibull, log-normal, log-logistic, and generalised-gamma AFT fitters — we use these directly.

Competing risks theory becomes central when a project can "fail" via multiple routes. A granted permission can lapse unbuilt (dark permission), be superseded by a new application, be commenced but abandoned, or be commenced and completed. Fine & Gray (1999) introduced the subdistribution hazard; Gray (1988) developed the associated K-sample test. Pintilie (2006), Putter-Fiocco-Geskus (2007) and Austin & Fine (2017) give pedagogical and practical treatments. Latouche (2013) reminds us that a competing-risks analysis must report all cause-specific hazards, not only one.

Frailty models (random-effects survival models) accommodate unobserved heterogeneity. Hougaard (2000) and Wienke (2010) are textbook treatments; Vaupel-Manton-Stallard (1979) established the conceptual apparatus. In our setting, projects within the same Local Authority, same developer, or same planning permission share unobservables — funding, ground conditions, regulatory culture — and a shared-frailty Cox model is the natural way to encode this.

Long-term survivors / cure-fraction models are relevant because a material share of permissions never commence — "dark permissions" in the Irish planning debate. Maller & Zhou (1996) is the classical treatment; Farewell (1982), Kuk & Chen (1992) and Tsodikov (1998) formalised mixture-cure models. Lambert (2007) is the practitioner reference. The share of permissions that are cured (never commence) is itself a policy-relevant estimand.

Discrete-time survival — modelling monthly or yearly hazards via pooled logistic regression on person-period data — is well-established (Allison 1982, Prentice-Gloeckler 1978, Jenkins 1995, Sueyoshi 1995). It is convenient for large administrative datasets because it sidesteps tie-handling and admits gradient boosting or neural networks as the link function (Gensheimer-Narasimhan 2019).

Model comparison in survival relies on the concordance index (Harrell 1982, 2015), which extends the ROC-AUC to censored outcomes. Uno (2011) provides an unbiased inverse-probability-of-censoring-weighted variant. The integrated Brier score (Graf 1999) is the mean-squared-error analogue. AIC and BIC (Akaike 1974, Schwarz 1978) arbitrate parametric comparisons. These four metrics will populate `tournament_results.csv`.

Random survival forests (Ishwaran 2008) and gradient-boosted survival (Hothorn 2006) extend the machine-learning toolkit to censored outcomes. Wang (2019) surveys the field. Grinsztajn (2022) and Shwartz-Ziv (2022) document that tree ensembles remain the first choice for tabular survival-like tasks — an expectation we carry into Phase 1.

Missing-data and censoring theory matters because our Certificate of Completion and Compliance (CCC) indicator is present for 39.2 percent of rows, and the remaining 60.8 percent are a mixture of genuinely uncompleted projects and completed-but-unrecorded projects. Little & Rubin (2019) and Van Buuren (2018) are the textbooks; Helsel (2012) covers censored observations; Leung-Elashoff-Afifi (1997) reviews censoring issues specifically for survival analysis. We will encode missing CCC as right-censoring at dataset end, with sensitivity analyses that vary the administrative-censoring date.

## Theme 2 — Housing supply, construction delay, and the economics of build-out

DiPasquale & Wheaton (1992, 1994) established the canonical 4-quadrant asset-space-market framework that every subsequent housing-supply paper has iterated on. Topel & Rosen (1988) provided the first dynamic investment model of the housebuilding industry; Mayer & Somerville (2000) estimated urban-growth-consistent supply elasticities. Saiz (2010) linked supply elasticity to geographic constraints. Glaeser-Gyourko-Saks (2005), Gyourko & Molloy (2015) and Glaeser & Gyourko (2018) surveyed the voluminous regulation-restricts-supply literature. Hsieh & Moretti (2019) estimated the spatial misallocation cost of these restrictions. In the Irish context, Duffy-Byrne-McCarthy (2014), Kennedy & Stuart (2015), Duffy-Foley-McInerney-McQuinn (2016), McQuinn-Duffy-Foley (2020), and the ESRI housing model (2023) are the econometric foundation; Lyons (2018, 2015), Kennedy (2018), and the Housing Agency (2015, 2023) supply institutional detail.

A specific empirical thread — the permit-to-completion lag — has been chronically under-studied because the administrative data to measure it rigorously has only recently emerged. Harter & Morris (2021) used US Census Bureau data to measure permit-to-completion lags in the US and found a bimodal distribution: ~18 months for single-family, ~28 months for multifamily, with fat right tails. Eurostat's building-permits series permits aggregate cross-national lag measurement but not cohort tracking. The Irish predecessor project `ie_housing_pipeline` used aggregate BHQ15 (permissions) and NDQ (completions) CSO series to estimate a notional lag via cross-correlation of 4-quarter-smoothed series — a proxy, not a cohort measurement. This project is the cohort upgrade.

Real-options theory is critical because building a granted-but-not-commenced permission is an option to be exercised or left to lapse. Titman (1985) was the first to model urban land as an option; Capozza & Helsley (1990) formalised the "stochastic city"; Cunningham (2006), Bulan-Mayer-Somerville (2009) and Cunningham (2007) provide empirical evidence that irreversibility and uncertainty jointly determine development timing. The "dark-permission" phenomenon — permissions granted that never commence — is directly predicted by real-options models: when land-price volatility is high, option value of waiting dominates and build rates fall even with extant permissions. The Irish literature on this is thin: Sirr (2023), Lyons (2013) and Duffy et al. discuss supply unresponsiveness, but a rigorous cohort-level empirical treatment of dark permissions in Ireland has not been published.

The construction-management literature on project-duration prediction and delay attribution spans decades. Bromilow (1969) proposed the classical `T = K C^B` duration-on-cost regression that still anchors duration forecasting. Chan (1999), Ng (2001), Love et al. (2005) and subsequent work extended it to alternative jurisdictions. The meta-literature on construction-delay causes (Assaf & Al-Hejji 2006, Odeh & Battaineh 2002, Sambasivan & Soon 2007, Aibinu & Odeyinka 2006, Kazaz 2012, Doloi 2012, Mahamid 2012, Frimpong 2003, Chan & Kumaraswamy 1997, Shebob 2011, Ahmed 2003, and many others) consistently identifies the top factors: contractor cash-flow, design changes, material delays, labour shortages, permit administration, weather, ground conditions. Flyvbjerg (2003, 2014) on megaprojects showed that optimism bias and strategic misrepresentation add a systematic cost/duration slippage that grows with project size — an effect we expect to see clearly in the BCMS apartment-block cohort.

The lean-construction literature (Ballard 2000, Koskela 2000) attributes much of the Bromilow-style variance to production-system inefficiencies rather than external shocks. The relevant implication for our cohort study is that construction-duration is not a simple function of covariates; it has a structural non-stationarity that a static hazard model will smooth over. Stratification by commencement year is one way to handle this; time-varying covariates are another.

Irish-specific construction economics: SCSI, CIF, DKM Economic Consultants, and ESRI have published regular construction-cost and activity bulletins (Lutyens 2024, DKM 2024, SCSI 2023, Deloitte 2023, PwC 2023, RICS Ireland 2023). Apartment viability specifically is examined by Duff (2020), Coyne (2022), and the SCSI cost studies — the recurring theme is that apartment construction costs have ballooned above sale-price/rent-cap affordability, producing a structural bottleneck. This predicts that apartment cohort durations, if they commence at all, should be longer and more dispersed than one-off-dwelling durations.

The Kenny Report (1974) on the price of building land, and its 45-years-on retrospective (UCD 2019), are reminders that Irish housing supply dysfunction is not new. Norris (2016), Norris & Byrne (2017), Byrne & Norris (2019), Hearne (2017, 2020), Waldron & Redmond (2014), Kitchin (2012) and Fahey (2020) supply the political-economy backdrop that this study takes as context without interrogating directly.

## Theme 3 — Irish planning and Building-Control institutional architecture, 2014 to present

The Irish planning system runs on the Planning and Development Act 2000 as amended; a grant of permission is typically valid for 5 years (the "default life"). Extensions under Section 42 of the Act extend this. The Planning and Development (Amendment) (Large-scale Residential Developments) Act 2021 ended the Strategic Housing Development (SHD) fast-track regime, which from 2017-2021 allowed developments of 100+ units (or 200+ bedspaces for student housing) to apply directly to An Bord Pleanála bypassing Local Authority first-instance. SHD was controversial (SHD reviews 2018-2021, Arkins 2016, Oneil 2022) because it produced a wave of judicial reviews and delayed actual delivery. The LRD regime restored the LA/ABP sequence but with compressed statutory timelines. For cohort analysis this is a regime shift we must stratify on — permissions granted under SHD have a different delay distribution than those granted under LRD or the pre-SHD regime.

Building Control is the separate statutory regime under which our data is generated. The Building Control Regulations 1997 first created the Fire Safety Certificate (FSC) and Disability Access Certificate (DAC) regimes. The Building Control (Amendment) Regulations 2014 (BC(A)R 2014, in force from 1 March 2014) introduced the Assigned Certifier regime, mandatory Commencement Notices with full documentation, and the Certificate of Completion and Compliance (CCC) on statutory completion — these are the columns we observe in BCMS. The 2015 amendment created the Opt-Out Commencement Notice for single dwellings on a single site, a route most one-off self-builds use. Before 1 March 2014, commencement notices were a lighter administrative filing and no electronic national register existed. BCMS (Building Control Management System) itself was launched in 2014 and has expanded coverage continuously since; the first full year of usable data is arguably 2015 or 2016 depending on LA. This affects Phase 2 experiment E01 (exclude pre-2015).

The National Building Control Office (NBCO) is the umbrella body for Building Control Authorities (BCAs, usually the same LA). NBCO annual reports (2022, 2023) document coverage, filing volume, and regulatory priorities. The BCMS open-data publication at data.nbco.gov.ie dates from late 2022 and is refreshed weekly. To our knowledge, the cohort-level latency analysis proposed here has not been published in the peer-reviewed Irish housing literature; the closest prior work is aggregate lag estimates in ESRI QEC boxes.

Housing for All (2021 onwards), the current government housing plan, and its progress reports (DHLGH 2024) commit to specific annual completion targets. The Land Development Agency (LDA 2022, 2023), Approved Housing Bodies, the Housing Delivery Coordination Office, and the County and City Management Association coordinate social and affordable delivery. The CCC_Units_Completed column lets us segment private vs AHB vs LA-direct, although the AHB flag is incomplete.

COVID-19 was a distinct shock. Construction sites were closed from 28 March 2020 until 18 May 2020 (non-essential) and again from 8 January 2021 to 13 April 2021 in the winter wave (CBI 2020, PwC 2020, Feeney 2022). Projects commenced before these dates would have construction-phase gaps absent from the BCMS record; their commencement-to-completion duration is a biased upper bound unless we adjust. E08 (before/after COVID) captures this.

Brexit (1 January 2021 in trade terms) and the Ukraine war (24 February 2022 onwards) disrupted construction supply chains, pushing material costs up 25-40 percent in 2021-2022 (ESRI Brexit 2024, DKM 2024, SCSI Tender Price Index 2023). Projects commencing in this window faced cost inflation that some developers absorbed by stretching schedules; others by abandonment. This is another structural break we can stratify on.

## Theme 4 — ML and gradient-boosting methods for time-to-event data

Deep-survival models (Katzman 2018 DeepSurv, Lee 2018 DeepHit, Gensheimer 2019, Kvamme 2019, Ranganath 2016) extend the ML toolkit to censored outcomes. The consensus finding across benchmarks is that for medium-sized tabular datasets (N = 10^4 to 10^6), properly regularised gradient-boosted trees match or beat deep nets (Grinsztajn 2022, Shwartz-Ziv 2022). Random survival forests (Ishwaran 2008, Zhu 2012, Wright 2017, Steingrimsson 2019) provide a strong nonparametric baseline. Gradient-boosted survival (in scikit-survival, Pölsterl 2020, or the XGBoost-Cox, LightGBM-Cox variants) is our tournament inclusion. Lundberg-Lee (2017) SHAP values are the standard model-interpretation tool and will support Phase 3's feature-importance plot.

For the "dark-permission" classification sub-problem — will this granted permission ever commence? — we have a clean binary outcome (with right-censoring handled by excluding permissions granted too recently to be judged). This is a standard classification problem amenable to logistic regression, random forests, gradient-boosted trees, or neural nets. The tournament will include a logistic baseline (sanity) and a LightGBM classifier as a strong ML entry. Early-loop calibration (Harrell 2015) matters because policy use cases care about predicted probabilities, not just rankings.

Discrete-time survival as pooled logistic regression (Allison 1982, Jenkins 1995) is the natural bridge between classification and survival — it allows XGBoost or LightGBM to be used directly with the interval-level dataset. We will use a 3-month period granularity (matches the BHQ publication cadence) and a monotone time-effect spline.

Joint longitudinal-event models (Henderson 2002, Rizopoulos 2012, Tsiatis & Davidian 2004) are out of scope for this project — we do not have longitudinal covariates — but are a future direction.

Calibration (Harrell 2015, Graf 1999) matters more than discrimination for a policy user. A Cox model with a concordance of 0.62 that is well-calibrated will be preferred over an XGBoost with a concordance of 0.67 that systematically under-predicts long durations. The Brier score integrates both aspects and is part of our tournament metrics.

## Cross-cutting methodology notes

Cohort definition: we define the "full cohort" as residential BCMS rows (CN_Proposed_use_of_building contains `1_residential_dwellings`, `2_residential_institutional`, or `3_residential_other`) with non-missing CN_Date_Granted AND non-missing CN_Commencement_Date. This is ~188,000 rows. The "complete-timeline subcohort" further requires CCC_Date_Validated populated — ~73,000 rows. For permission-to-commencement the full cohort applies; for commencement-to-completion the complete-timeline subcohort applies; for the total permission-to-completion lag we combine both.

Censoring: administrative censoring date is the BCMS export date (2026-04-15 in the local file). Permissions granted after commencement-date-cap (e.g. 2024-01-01 with a 24-month expected-to-commence window) are right-censored. CCC-not-present rows in otherwise-commenced projects are right-censored at the export date.

Covariates: LA, year of permission grant, month of permission grant, apartment-vs-dwelling flag, total_units_declared, total_floor_area, project_type (seven-day-notice vs commencement-notice vs opt-out), approved-housing-body flag, foul-water type, geocoordinates (LAT/LNG).

Treatment: we are not claiming causal identification of any policy intervention. This is a descriptive cohort study with regime-shift stratification (pre-2015 vs 2015+, SHD vs non-SHD, pre- vs post-COVID).

Reproducibility: seed-stable random choices (train/test splits for classifiers, bootstrap CIs) use `np.random.default_rng(42)`.

## Gaps this project fills

1. **First cohort-level measurement of Irish permission→commencement→completion latency.** Prior Irish work has used aggregate series; the ESRI QEC lag-box uses cross-correlation of smoothed permissions and completions series. No prior publication tracks the same permission number across BCMS filings and aggregates the durations.
2. **First published estimate of the "dark-permission" cure fraction in Ireland**, with LA-level and development-type breakdowns.
3. **Heterogeneity documentation** across apartment vs one-off dwelling, Dublin vs rest, SHD vs non-SHD, pre- vs post-COVID.
4. **Benchmark of model families** on Irish cohort data — the descriptive-project tournament is a small contribution but records what floor each family achieves.

We do not claim methodological novelty — the statistical tools are textbook — and we are explicit that the framing is replication of established survival-analysis methodology on a previously under-studied dataset.
