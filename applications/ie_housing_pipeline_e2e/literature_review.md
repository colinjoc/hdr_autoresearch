# Literature Review — IE Housing Pipeline E2E

## Theme 1: Housing Supply Economics and the Planning-Construction Pipeline

The economics of housing supply is fundamentally a question of how planning permissions translate into built homes. The seminal framing by DiPasquale and Wheaton (1996) establishes the four-quadrant model connecting rents, asset prices, construction, and stock adjustment. In this framework, the flow of new supply is determined not only by the profitability of construction but by the regulatory and administrative pipeline through which permissions must pass before ground is broken.

Somerville (2001) provides the key structural insight for this project: permits, starts, and completions are related through a pipeline with stage-specific attrition and duration. Using US Census Bureau data, he shows that the permit-to-start transition is economically meaningful — not all permitted projects proceed — and that the start-to-completion duration varies systematically with project type and economic conditions. Harter and Morris (2021) extend this work with microdata showing bimodal duration distributions and fat right tails in permit-to-completion times. Their finding that aggregate lag estimation systematically understates tail risk is directly relevant to Ireland, where the CSO's aggregate conversion ratio (41-65% on a 2-year lag, per our PL-0 predecessor) represents exactly the kind of smoothed estimate that misses pipeline complexity.

The supply elasticity literature (Saiz 2010; Caldera and Johansson 2013; Green et al. 2005; Malpezzi and Maclennan 2001; Blackley 1999) consistently finds that countries and regions with more restrictive planning systems have lower and less responsive housing supply. Ireland's supply elasticity was estimated at 1.2-1.8 by Caldera and Johansson (2013), below the OECD average of 2.0, placing it in the constrained-but-not-extreme category. However, these elasticity estimates measure the response of completions to price changes and do not decompose the pipeline into permission-commencement-completion stages. Our project fills this gap by computing stage-by-stage conversion rates.

Paciorek (2013) and Gyourko and Molloy (2015) provide the theoretical mechanism: planning delay creates an option value for holding permitted-but-uncommenced land, and supply constraints shift the adjustment of demand shocks from quantities to prices. This is directly relevant to the "lapse" phenomenon in Ireland, where 9.5% of residential permissions (NRU>0 2017-2019) never file a commencement notice.

Ball (2011) and Meen (2005) provide UK-specific evidence on planning delays, finding that the English planning system adds 12-18 months to project timelines versus comparable continental European systems. Our median perm-to-commencement of 234 days (7.7 months) and median perm-to-CCC of 1,096 days (3.0 years) are comparable to UK durations, consistent with the shared common-law planning tradition.

The international comparison literature (OECD 2022; Andrews et al. 2011; Grimes and Aitken 2010; Netherlands Ministry of Housing 2023) shows substantial variation in pipeline efficiency across countries. New Zealand's consent-to-completion yield is estimated at 75-85% (Productivity Commission NZ 2012), the Netherlands at 85-90% (Vermeulen and Rouwendal 2007), and the UK at 50-70% (Bramley and Watkins 2016). Our headline estimate of 35.1% for Ireland (28-35% under the full range of lapse rate uncertainty) places Ireland well below international peers, though direct comparison is complicated by differences in how each country defines and measures "permission" and "completion."

## Theme 2: Planning Attrition and the Lapse Phenomenon

The phenomenon of granted planning permissions that never result in built homes is well-documented internationally but poorly quantified at the cohort level. The theoretical framework is provided by the real-options literature (Dixit and Pindyck 1994; Titman 1985; Williams 1991; McDonald and Siegel 1986), which models the developer's decision to commence construction as the exercise of a call option on the land value. The permission creates option value: the developer can build if market conditions are favourable and let the permission lapse if they are not. Cunningham (2006) provides direct empirical evidence that house price uncertainty increases the proportion of vacant land with active permissions, consistent with the real-options prediction.

In Ireland, planning permissions under the Planning and Development Act 2000 lapse after 5 years unless commenced or extended under Section 42. Our PL-4 predecessor project estimated the lapse rate at 9.5% for NRU>0 2017-2019 permissions and up to 27.4% for the broader cohort (heavily confounded by join quality between the planning register and BCMS). The true lapse rate is bounded within this range. The 2014-2016 period shows much higher apparent lapse rates (45-55%), but this is an artefact of poor BCMS data quality in the early years of BCAR rollout (Keith and O'Connor 2013; NBCO 2023).

The international evidence suggests that 5-15% lapse is typical for high-demand markets (Ball 2011 for UK; ABS 2023 for Australia), with higher rates in lower-demand areas and during economic downturns. Our 9.5% estimate for 2017-2019 Ireland (a period of strong housing demand) is consistent with this range. The higher 27.4% estimate, if accurate, would place Ireland among the highest-lapse countries, but the PL-4 project's finding that this is heavily confounded by join quality means it should not be cited as a point estimate.

The policy response to lapse varies across jurisdictions. Some Australian states have "use it or lose it" provisions with shorter permission durations (3 years). The UK's system allows for "commencement by minor works" — a developer can prevent lapse by carrying out token site works. Ireland's Section 42 extension mechanism allows permissions to be extended for a further 5 years under certain conditions; our PL-1 predecessor found 1.3% of the cohort commenced in the near-expiry window (months 55-61), consistent with a small real-options "commence-before-expiry" pattern (E23 in PL-1 results).

## Theme 3: Construction Duration and Completion Certification

The distribution of construction durations — from commencement to completion — has received relatively less attention than the planning stage, partly because construction timing is assumed to be governed by engineering constraints rather than economic decisions. However, Mayer and Somerville (2000) show that construction timing is endogenous: developers accelerate in boom markets and delay in downturns, creating procyclical duration patterns. Topel and Rosen (1988) provide the investment-theory foundation: construction is an investment decision with adjustment costs, and the speed of construction is optimised against the carrying cost of capital.

In Ireland, the Building Control (Amendment) Regulations 2014 (BCAR, S.I. No. 9 of 2014) introduced mandatory commencement notices (CN) and Certificates of Completion and Compliance (CCC) for most building works. The opt-out provision allows one-off self-build dwellings to be exempt from CCC filing, which means these homes are built and occupied but never formally certified. This regulatory artefact is the dominant driver of the low overall CCC filing rate (40.9%): non-opt-out projects file CCCs at 59.8%, while opt-out projects file at effectively 0%.

Our PL-1 predecessor found median commencement-to-CCC of 498 days (16.3 months) for the residential cohort, with apartment projects taking 53 days longer than dwellings (549d vs 495d; Cox hazard ratio 0.869 for apartments). AHB (Approved Housing Body) projects take 46 days longer than private-developer projects (544d vs 498d), consistent with additional compliance requirements for publicly-funded housing. COVID-19 added approximately 28 days to the comm-to-CCC duration (pre-COVID median 484d vs post-COVID 512d), consistent with the March-May 2020 construction shutdown.

The survival analysis literature (Kaplan and Meier 1958; Cox 1972; Wei 1992; Lawless 2003; Hosmer et al. 2008) provides the methodological framework for modelling time-to-event data with right-censoring. Our tournament comparison of Cox PH, Weibull AFT, log-normal AFT, and generalised gamma models (following Royston and Parmar 2002) found that all parametric models perform similarly on temporal-split holdout, consistent with the PL-1 finding that model choice matters less than covariate selection for Irish construction duration data.

The multi-state model literature (Putter et al. 2007; Andersen and Keiding 2002; Aalen et al. 2008) frames the pipeline as a series of transitions between states (Permission → Commenced → CCC → Occupied), with absorbing states for lapse and abandonment. The competing-risks framework (Fine and Gray 1999; Prentice et al. 1978) is relevant for the commencement stage, where a permission can either commence (event) or lapse (competing event). In practice, our data architecture separates these stages across different datasets (planning register for lapse, BCMS for construction duration), so the multi-state model is applied as a conceptual framework rather than a single statistical model.

## Theme 4: Irish Housing Policy and the Housing for All Framework

Ireland's housing crisis has been the subject of extensive policy analysis (Duffy et al. 2014; Murphy 2018; O'Toole and Slaymaker 2021; McQuinn and O'Connell 2024). The crisis is characterised by a persistent gap between housing demand (driven by population growth, household formation, and immigration) and housing supply (constrained by planning, finance, labour, and land). The ESRI Housing Model (2023) projects demand of 40,000-50,000 new homes per year, against recent completion rates of 22,000-25,000 per year (CSO NDA12).

Housing for All (Government of Ireland 2021) set a target of 33,000 new homes per year initially, subsequently revised to 50,500, with specific sub-targets for social housing (10,000/yr), affordable purchase (6,000/yr), cost rental (2,000/yr), and private market (32,500/yr). The plan assumes that permissions will translate into completions at a rate sufficient to meet these targets, but does not specify a pipeline yield rate or conversion target. Our finding that the overall yield is 35.1% (or ~51% for non-opt-out) implies that the system would need 98,000-144,000 permissions per year to deliver 50,500 completions — far above the current ~38,000.

The Land Development Agency (LDA), established by statute in 2018, was intended as a delivery vehicle for state-owned land. Our PL-3 predecessor found that the LDA delivered ca. 850 homes in 2023, 100% via Project Tosaigh (forward-purchase from private developers). These homes are already counted in the national completions denominator, so the LDA represents attribution (3.5% of NDA12), not additionality. Even at the LDA's own forward target of 8,000 cumulative by 2028, the Agency would represent ~3% of the annual HFA target.

The Strategic Housing Development (SHD) scheme (2017-2021) was designed to fast-track large developments through An Bord Pleanala. Our PL-0 predecessor found that SHD and non-SHD permissions cleared in roughly equal volumes (60,605 vs 63,218 units for 2019-2021), but per-scheme productivity cannot be compared from the available data. The SHD scheme was replaced by Large-Scale Residential Development (LRD) provisions in 2022, which return decision-making to local authorities for developments above 100 units, with a streamlined appeal process.

The procyclical nature of Irish housing policy has been documented by Byrne and Norris (2018), Norris (2016), and Norris and Fahey (2011). During the Celtic Tiger period (1995-2007), permissive planning and easy credit produced a construction boom (Kelly 2007, 2009; Honohan 2009; Lane 2011). The subsequent crash reduced completions from 93,000 in 2006 to 4,575 in 2013. The current recovery — completions rising from 15,935 in 2019 to 25,237 in 2025 — is still below pre-crash levels and far below the HFA target.

## Theme 5: Measurement Challenges: Channel Effects and Data Quality

A recurring theme across all four predecessor projects is the measurement challenge posed by administrative data that captures *filings* rather than *real-world events*. The CCC filing rate is a measure of regulatory compliance, not of construction completion; the planning permission grant is a measure of administrative approval, not of developer intent. The gap between the filing and the reality creates what PL-1 termed the "dark permission" problem: permissions that exist in the administrative record but never produce homes, and homes that exist physically but never appear in the administrative record.

PL-1 demonstrated this with the dark-permission rate: 0.67% under the "never commenced" definition (tight administrative statement) but up to 39% under the "CCC never filed among non-opt-out" definition (statement about filing discipline plus completion). The breadth of this range — 0.67% to 39% — is itself the central finding: the measured attrition depends critically on which administrative channel defines the outcome.

This channel-dependence problem is not unique to Ireland. In the US, Harter and Morris (2021) note that the Census Bureau's "permit-to-completion" metric uses different data sources for permits (local government filings) and completions (Survey of Construction), creating a "denominator mismatch" analogous to Ireland's planning-register-vs-BCMS gap. In the UK, MHCLG's housing statistics use different definitions of "start" and "completion" depending on whether the source is the NHBC warranty scheme, local authority building control, or the approved-inspector system.

For this synthesis project, the measurement challenge is compounded by the need to combine data from two separate administrative systems (the planning register and BCMS) that use different identifiers, different geographic units, and different temporal coverage. Our PL-4 predecessor showed that fuzzy matching between the two systems introduces a confounding factor of 18pp on the apparent lapse rate (27.4% fuzzy-matched vs 9.5% NRU>0 filtered). We address this by using stage-specific rates from each system rather than attempting row-level linkage, and by reporting the yield as a range rather than a point estimate.

## Theme 6: Queueing Theory and Pipeline Capacity Modelling

The housing pipeline can be modelled as a queueing system where permissions arrive at a rate lambda, enter a service process (construction) with service time S, and exit as completions. The queueing-theory framework (Erlang 1909; Kleinrock 1975; Gross et al. 2008) provides tools for analysing throughput, utilisation, and waiting times in such systems.

The relevant model is an M/G/1 queue (Poisson arrivals, general service time, single server per LA/builder) or, more accurately, an M/G/infinity queue (each project has its own "server" — the construction crew). In the M/G/infinity framework, the number of projects under construction at any time follows a Poisson distribution with mean lambda × E[S], and the output rate equals the input rate at steady state minus attrition. This is exactly the pipeline model we compute: completions/yr = permissions/yr × (1 - lapse) × CCC_rate × occ_rate.

The discrete-event simulation (DES) approach (Law 2015; Banks et al. 2010; Robinson 2014) extends the queueing framework by allowing heterogeneous project types, non-stationary arrival rates, and capacity constraints. Our T04 tournament entry uses a DES calibrated to the observed log-normal duration distributions, producing a yield estimate (35.2%) consistent with the analytical model (35.1%), which validates the stage-independence assumption.

The real-options dimension (Dixit and Pindyck 1994; Cunningham 2006) adds a decision layer to the queueing model: the developer's choice of when (or whether) to enter the construction queue is itself an optimised stopping problem. High price uncertainty increases the option value of delay, increasing the lapse rate and the duration in the "permission held but not commenced" buffer. This mechanism predicts that the lapse rate should be procyclical with price uncertainty, which is consistent with the higher pre-2017 rates observed in PL-4 (though confounded by data quality).

## Theme 7: Cross-Domain Insights — Pipeline Analysis in Other Sectors

The permission-to-completion pipeline is structurally analogous to other multi-stage delivery systems in different domains. In pharmaceutical drug development, the "clinical pipeline" has well-quantified stage-by-stage attrition rates (Phase I to approval: ~10% yield), and the literature on pipeline management (Rosenthal and Strange 2004 for agglomeration analogies; Hillier and Lieberman 2015 for OR methods) provides tools for optimising throughput under stage-specific attrition.

In supply chain management, the "order-to-delivery" pipeline has similar characteristics: orders (permissions) enter a multi-stage process (design, sourcing, manufacturing, delivery) with stage-specific lead times and attrition (cancellations, returns). The bullwhip effect (Lee et al. 1997) — where small demand fluctuations are amplified through the supply chain — has a housing analogue: small changes in interest rates or planning policy create amplified fluctuations in completions, because the pipeline's multi-year latency means that today's completions reflect decisions made 2-5 years ago.

In energy infrastructure, the "interconnection queue" for renewable-energy projects has received attention as a bottleneck analogous to the planning queue (Rand and Hoen 2017). Like housing permissions, many grid-connection applications are speculative and never result in built projects, and the queue itself introduces delay that degrades project economics. The policy response — queue-management reforms, "ready to build" requirements — has parallels to Ireland's discussion of "use it or lose it" provisions for planning permissions.

The cross-domain insight is that pipeline yield is a universal metric for any multi-stage delivery system, and that the levers for improving yield are broadly consistent across domains: (a) reduce stage-specific attrition, (b) reduce stage-specific duration, (c) increase input volume, or (d) reduce measurement loss (channel effects). Our finding that permission volume is the binding constraint for Ireland's HFA target is consistent with the supply-chain principle that throughput is limited by the bottleneck stage, and that improving non-bottleneck stages has diminishing returns.
