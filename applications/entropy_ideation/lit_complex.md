# Literature Review: Statistical-Physics / Complex-Systems Foundations for Edge-of-Chaos and Self-Organized Criticality in Trained Neural Networks

This review assembles the physics-theory scaffolding on which an ML-side programme investigating criticality signatures in trained transformers (nanoGPT, GPT-2) can rest. Citations resolve to `papers_complex.csv`. Uncertain venue or year details are flagged `VERIFY`. The target audience is an ML researcher who knows dynamical systems at textbook level but will be expected to defend physics claims against a statistical-physics reviewer.

## 1. Self-organized criticality (SOC): theory and canonical models

Self-organized criticality was proposed by Bak, Tang and Wiesenfeld in their 1987 *Physical Review Letters* paper introducing the sandpile cellular automaton as a mechanism by which extended driven dissipative systems might, without any apparent parameter tuning, end up at a stationary state whose fluctuations are scale-free [Bak 1987]. The phenomenology is simple in the extreme: sand is added grain by grain to a two-dimensional lattice; when the local height exceeds a threshold the pile topples, redistributing grains to nearest neighbours and potentially triggering further topplings. The steady state exhibits a power-law distribution of avalanche sizes P(s) ~ s^(-tau) with a cutoff set only by system size, and a 1/f-like power spectrum of dissipated activity. The original claim was programmatic: the same mechanism ought to operate in earthquakes, biological extinction, economic fluctuations, and anywhere else nature exhibits broadly-distributed dynamics. Bak's subsequent popular exposition [Bak 1996] broadcast the programme as a universal theory of "how nature works," with an evangelistic tone that has aged uncomfortably but remains historically important as the field-defining statement.

The strong thesis — that most scale-free behaviour in nature is SOC — has not aged well, and the careful statistical-physics distinction between true SOC and tuned critical points is laid out in Jensen's textbook [Jensen 1998] and in the critical retrospective by Watkins, Pruessner, Chapman, Crosby and Jensen [Watkins 2016]. The latter is invaluable reading for any modern researcher because it explicitly catalogues which claims from the 1987-2000 period have held up, which have been retracted or heavily qualified, and which remain open. What survived is a taxonomy of universality classes and a set of model systems in which SOC-like phenomenology is robustly reproducible. The original BTW sandpile, with deterministic toppling rules and an Abelian group structure, turns out to exhibit a multifractal avalanche spectrum rather than a single clean exponent; this subtlety was missed for years and is one of the standard cautionary tales about declaring universality from a single-exponent fit. The Manna sandpile [Manna 1991] replaces deterministic toppling with stochastic redistribution and lies in a cleaner universality class now identified with the Conserved Directed Percolation (C-DP) class. The Oslo rice-pile experiment [Frette 1996], one of the few bona-fide experimental realisations of SOC in a laboratory setting, produced power-law avalanche statistics consistent with the Oslo model's universality class — though with the usual caveats about finite-size cutoffs and the limited number of decades of scaling available in the data. The Bak-Sneppen model [Bak 1993] furnishes a wholly different mechanism: extremal dynamics on a co-evolving fitness landscape, with avalanches defined by extinction cascades of the least-fit species. This extremal-dynamics route to criticality is worth keeping in mind for ML because training dynamics — particularly those that interact with the most-wrong or most-surprising examples — share a structural resemblance to extremal dynamics.

The most important conceptual lesson from the SOC literature for a trained-neural-network programme is the tension between *conservative* SOC and *absorbing-state phase transitions*. Dickman, Vespignani and Zapperi showed in 1998 that sandpile SOC is equivalent to a driven absorbing-state phase transition tuned by a conservation law acting as a control parameter [Dickman 1998]. The follow-up review by Dickman, Muñoz, Vespignani and Zapperi [Dickman 2000] generalised this insight to a broader class of systems: what looks like parameter-free self-organisation is really separation of timescales between slow driving and fast relaxation, which effectively pins the system to the critical point of an underlying absorbing-state transition. The conservation law plays the role of the tuning parameter; the separation of timescales plays the role of the critical-manifold-projection mechanism. This is methodologically crucial for ML. If trained networks appear critical, one should ask *what absorbing-state transition are they poised against* and *what is the implicit conservation law or timescale separation* playing the role of tuning parameter, rather than invoking magical self-organisation. In a transformer there are several candidates for such a separation: the learning-rate/inference-rate ratio during training, the ratio of prompt length to positional-encoding scale, or the ratio of residual-stream norm growth to layer-normalisation feedback. Each corresponds to a different physical interpretation of any apparent criticality.

The universality-class question matters because different mechanisms produce different exponents. BTW-like conservative sandpiles in 2D give tau ~ 1.27, the Manna/C-DP class gives tau ~ 1.27-1.30 with different duration exponents, mean-field SOC gives the classical tau = 3/2 that matches critical branching processes, and directed percolation gives tau ~ 1.11 in 1+1 dimensions. An ML paper that measures tau ~ 3/2 in trained-network activations and declares "criticality" without distinguishing among these mechanisms is not making a physics claim — it is making a statistical claim about the heavy tail of a distribution. The Markovic-Gros review [Markovic 2014] is pointed on this: many empirical power laws have nothing to do with SOC, and SOC is one hypothesis among several that must be competed against before being accepted. Its catalogue of alternative power-law mechanisms — preferential attachment, multiplicative noise with reflecting boundaries, mixtures of exponentials, finite-size crossovers — is the right mental checklist for any criticality claim. The mature stance taken by Watkins et al. [Watkins 2016] is that SOC is a narrow phenomenon — genuinely manifest in a handful of lattice models and perhaps in rice piles, earthquake statistics and neuronal avalanches — and its empirical invocation elsewhere should be held to the exponent-and-scaling-law standard developed by the crackling-noise community. Any claim about trained-transformer criticality must include, at minimum: a proposed universality class, measured exponents with uncertainties, scaling-relation cross-checks, and a null hypothesis that is not merely "power law vs lognormal" — it must also rule out the Griffiths-phase and neutral-model nulls discussed in §10.

A final methodological observation from the SOC literature worth porting: the distinction between *stationary* and *driven* measurements. Sandpile models reach a stationary avalanche distribution only after a transient in which the pile fills to its steady-state profile; measurements taken during the transient do not lie on the critical manifold even when the asymptotic state does. In ML the analogue is the transient of training: networks at epoch 1 and networks at convergence are dynamically different systems, and criticality claims made on snapshots taken during training transients cannot be compared to claims made at convergence without explicit treatment of the non-stationarity. Equally, fine-tuned models and from-scratch-trained models are dynamically distinct; a criticality claim should specify which regime is being analysed and what the implicit stationarity assumption is.

## 2. Absorbing-state phase transitions and directed percolation

Directed percolation (DP) is the canonical non-equilibrium universality class for transitions into an absorbing state in the presence of a scalar order parameter, short-range interactions, and no additional symmetries or conservation laws. The *DP conjecture*, due to Janssen [Janssen 1981] via Reggeon field theory and independently to Grassberger [Grassberger 1982] via numerics on the Schlögl model, states that any continuous phase transition with these properties falls in the DP class. This is one of the strongest universality statements in non-equilibrium statistical mechanics: unlike equilibrium universality where a handful of symmetry classes dominate, DP sweeps up an enormous variety of microscopic models into a single set of critical exponents. Hinrichsen's authoritative 2000 *Advances in Physics* review [Hinrichsen 2000] tabulates the DP exponents (beta ~ 0.2765, nu_perp ~ 1.0969, nu_parallel ~ 1.7338 in 1+1 D; mean-field values at and above four dimensions), describes the contact process as the paradigmatic DP model, and catalogues the rare classes that *do not* reduce to DP: compact DP (Z2 symmetry), parity-conserving class (mod-2 conservation), Manna/C-DP (conservation law), and the voter-model class. The Henkel-Hinrichsen-Lübeck textbook [Henkel 2008] extends this into a book-length treatment with exponent tables, scaling forms, and experimental realisations including turbulent transitions in Couette flow and electroconvection in liquid crystals.

The connection to neural dynamics is direct and has been exploited in the computational-neuroscience literature for two decades. A contact-process-like model on a network — each node either active or quiescent, active nodes infect neighbours with rate lambda and spontaneously decay with rate 1 — undergoes an absorbing-state transition at a critical lambda_c, above which activity is sustained indefinitely and below which it dies out almost surely. Neural avalanches, whether in cortex slices [Beggs 2003] or in recurrent network models, naturally map onto such dynamics: a pulse of activity propagates until it hits the quiescent-everywhere absorbing state, at which point the avalanche is over and a new one (driven by external input) may begin. Marro and Dickman [Marro 1999] provide a lattice-model textbook treatment of these dynamics with all the combinatorial machinery needed to compute exponents from first principles. The DP exponents are a baseline null hypothesis: if a trained network produces avalanches with tau consistent with mean-field DP (tau = 3/2) and duration exponent tau_t = 2, this is the mean-field answer expected from any branching-process-like spreading process — it is not evidence of specialised learned structure. A paper claiming criticality must go beyond this baseline by either (a) measuring non-mean-field exponents specific to a lower-dimensional DP class, or (b) demonstrating the scaling-relation cross-check (§4), or (c) performing avalanche-shape collapse.

The deeper point for the ML programme is that absorbing-state transitions require *something* playing the role of a tuning parameter. In the cortical case, homeostatic synaptic plasticity has been proposed as the mechanism — feedback adjusting synaptic strengths until the effective branching ratio hits one. In trained transformers, candidates include: the gain of the non-linearity (analogous to the Sompolinsky g); the spectral radius of the weight matrix (analogous to Jaeger's ESN threshold); the effective branching ratio of post-activation propagation; the ratio of residual-stream scale to layer-norm scale. Each is a physically distinct mechanism and cannot be conflated. It is entirely possible that training induces several of these to sit simultaneously near criticality because the loss landscape rewards computational flexibility, and it is also possible that only one is near criticality and the others are merely correlated with it through a common training signal. An empirical paper must disentangle these by perturbing each individually and observing whether the apparent criticality persists.

The ML-physics correspondence is fragile in one specific way that has not been widely appreciated. Directed percolation requires a genuine absorbing state — activity, once extinguished, cannot spontaneously restart from within the system. Trained transformers are *not* in that regime in inference: every forward pass starts from input-driven activations, so the system is perpetually driven out of any would-be absorbing state. The relevant comparison is therefore driven DP or the contact process with spontaneous creation (rate h > 0 for quiescent-to-active transitions), which is not critical for any value of the control parameter — the transition is smeared out into a crossover [Hinrichsen 2000]. A careful paper either (a) identifies a genuinely quiescent regime within trained-network dynamics (for example, activations far from input tokens in a long context, where input drive has effectively decayed) where DP applies, (b) considers only unforced/prompt-free generation regimes where the driving term can be treated as stochastic noise, or (c) uses a Griffiths-phase argument (see §10) to justify extended scaling without strict criticality. The compact-DP variant with Z2 symmetry is relevant to residual-stream dynamics with sign symmetry, and the parity-conserving class is relevant to dynamics where token counts are conserved modulo two — these are exotic but physically meaningful options to keep in mind.

The experimental-realisation catalogue in [Hinrichsen 2000, Henkel 2008] is also useful as a calibration exercise. DP has been observed in very few laboratory experiments — turbulent Couette flow and nematic electroconvection are the cleanest examples — despite being a universality class that theory says should be ubiquitous. The lesson is that *observation* of DP requires careful control of boundary conditions, driving, and measurement protocol; theoretical universality does not imply empirical ubiquity. The same lesson applies to trained networks: even if the theoretical model is in a DP-class, measuring it empirically may require careful choices about what counts as an avalanche, how avalanches are bounded, and how the system is driven.

The field-theoretic derivation of the DP exponents proceeds via the Reggeon field theory formulated by Janssen [Janssen 1981], whose Lagrangian contains a single scalar order-parameter field with particle-creation and annihilation vertices and a "rapidity reversal" symmetry that is the signature of the class. The epsilon-expansion (around the upper critical dimension d_c = 4) gives one-loop and higher-order corrections to the mean-field exponents, and high-precision Monte Carlo in 1+1 D and 2+1 D has validated the field-theory predictions to several decimal places. For the ML practitioner, the takeaway is that DP exponents are known *extremely* precisely from theory; any claim of DP-class behaviour in a trained network can be compared against exponents with uncertainty orders of magnitude smaller than typical ML-data uncertainties, which is an unusually stringent test. A measured tau that differs from the DP prediction by 0.05 or more is a strong signal that the universality class is not DP, whereas in many ML settings exponent differences of 0.1 are within experimental uncertainty. This asymmetry — theory is precise, experiment is noisy — means DP claims in ML will typically *fail* cleanly rather than succeed cleanly, which is a useful epistemic property.

A complementary perspective on absorbing-state transitions comes from the contact process and related interacting particle systems, treated rigorously by Liggett and others in the probability-theory literature. The contact process on Z^d has a unique critical infection rate above which activity persists; rigorous results establish continuity of the order parameter, universality of exponents at and above d = 4, and stretched-exponential lifetime tails in the subcritical regime. These results translate into ML as follows: if a trained network behaves as a contact process with infection rate determined by the effective branching ratio, then subcritical networks should exhibit stretched-exponential perturbation-decay tails, critical networks should exhibit power-law perturbation-decay tails, and supercritical networks should exhibit sustained activity with a non-trivial density order parameter. Direct measurement of perturbation-decay-tail shape (not just its exponent) discriminates among these regimes more reliably than any single exponent estimate.

## 3. Branching processes

Branching processes are the mean-field workhorse for avalanche dynamics and the single most useful theoretical tool for interpreting apparent criticality in spreading systems. The Galton-Watson process — each individual produces a random number of offspring drawn from a fixed distribution with mean sigma — exhibits a phase transition at sigma = 1. For sigma < 1 the lineage dies out almost surely; for sigma > 1 there is positive probability of infinite growth; at sigma = 1 the tree is critical, with avalanche (tree) sizes distributed as P(s) ~ s^(-3/2) and durations as P(t) ~ t^(-2), precisely the mean-field SOC exponents. The foundational texts are Harris [Harris 1963] and Athreya and Ney [Athreya 1972]; the latter remains the standard mathematical reference for critical, subcritical and supercritical regimes, multi-type processes, continuous-time branching, Galton-Watson trees with offspring distributions having various moment conditions, and limit theorems (Kesten-Stigum, Seneta-Heyde). For the ML-applied reader, the key facts from this literature are: (i) the exponent tau = 3/2 at criticality is universal across offspring distributions with finite variance; (ii) distributions with infinite variance give different, Lévy-flight-like exponents; (iii) the finite-size scaling form P(s) ~ s^(-3/2) exp(-s/s_c) with s_c ~ (sigma - 1)^(-2) near criticality gives a two-parameter fit target that distinguishes a true critical branching process from a tuned power-law-with-cutoff.

For neural-network application the relevant construction is the *multi-type* branching process, in which offspring of each type are drawn from type-specific distributions encoded in a mean matrix M_ij. Criticality is controlled by the largest eigenvalue of M; criticality holds when the leading eigenvalue equals one, which is the Perron-Frobenius generalisation of the scalar sigma = 1 condition. In a trained transformer the analogue of M is the Jacobian of post-activation propagation across layers and positions: the expected norm-squared of the perturbation propagated to each (layer, position) in the next step given a perturbation at a given (layer, position) in the current step. The criticality condition is therefore spectral-radius-equals-one of the Jacobian — the same condition as Jaeger's echo-state property boundary [Jaeger 2001] and as Sompolinsky's g = 1 in the random-network case [Sompolinsky 1988]. This is not a coincidence: all three are mean-field statements of the same branching-process criticality condition, translated into the language of their respective model classes. Recognising this equivalence allows results from the branching-process literature to be imported directly into the ML context.

Three theoretical consequences matter for the ML programme. First, finite-size effects in branching processes take the universal scaling form P(s, L) = s^(-3/2) f(s/s_c(L)) near criticality, where s_c(L) ~ L^D for system size L with D = 4 in mean field; the scaling function f has been calculated exactly for many cases — it is the Borel distribution for the Poisson-offspring Galton-Watson tree — and provides a functional-form fit target rather than a mere exponent fit. This is important because a good fit to a functional form across multiple system sizes is orders of magnitude stronger evidence than a good fit to a power law at a single size. Second, multi-type branching processes can look critical in the largest eigenvalue of M even when individual offspring distributions are far from criticality, so heterogeneous systems (like transformers with residual stream vs attention vs MLP components, each with its own effective branching structure) may show criticality along one dominant mode while the bulk is subcritical. This connects to the Griffiths-phase discussion in §10: a trained transformer may be subcritical on average but have a well-connected dominant mode that is critical, producing heavy-tailed avalanches along that mode. Third, critical branching does not imply SOC — the branching process has a *tunable* parameter sigma that must be set to unity by hand. SOC arises only when an external mechanism (separation of timescales, conservation law, homeostatic feedback) pins sigma to 1 as an emergent outcome of the system's dynamics. In the ML context this pinning mechanism would need to come from training dynamics and would itself be a research question worth pursuing, not an assumption to be made in passing.

A further technical point from Athreya and Ney [Athreya 1972] worth emphasising: the extinction probability q at sigma > 1 satisfies q = g(q) where g is the generating function of the offspring distribution, and the Malthusian parameter controls the exponential growth rate in the supercritical regime. Translating these to the ML context: a supercritical trained network would exhibit exponentially-diverging perturbation norms along the dominant mode, which is not what one observes in stable trained models. A subcritical trained network would exhibit exponentially-decaying perturbations and would have a well-defined linear-response regime. Networks that appear critical are the ones exhibiting polynomial perturbation-norm decay, and this is a directly measurable quantity. Measuring the perturbation-norm decay exponent and comparing it to the branching-process prediction at criticality is a falsifiable test of the branching-process interpretation.

The Harris [Harris 1963] text also develops the theory of age-dependent branching processes (where offspring-production times are non-deterministic) and continuous-state branching processes (where the "population" is a continuous variable rather than discrete individuals). Both generalisations are relevant to ML: post-activation magnitudes are continuous, not discrete counts, so a continuous-state formulation is more natural than Galton-Watson, and the relevant timescale for perturbation propagation is determined by the forward-pass depth rather than a fixed step-size, motivating an age-dependent formulation. The continuous-state branching process with Poisson offspring converges in the large-population limit to a stochastic differential equation (the Feller diffusion) whose stationary distribution and extinction dynamics are analytically tractable; for the ML application this provides a concrete mean-field equation for perturbation propagation through a critical network. Exact results from this literature — moments, hitting times, quasi-stationary distributions — can be compared with measurements on trained networks and would give a stronger test of the branching interpretation than exponent-fitting alone.

One final nuance: the assumption of independent offspring, which underlies the Galton-Watson analysis, is violated in any real recurrent dynamics where neighbouring units' perturbations are correlated through shared input. Correlated branching processes have been studied less thoroughly but are known to shift the mean-field exponents in systematic ways (typically increasing tau above the uncorrelated 3/2). For the ML application, the relevant question is how strongly correlated the post-activation perturbations are across units within a layer, and whether that correlation is strong enough to push the effective exponent away from the mean-field value. Empirically measuring the across-unit correlation of perturbation responses is a prerequisite for any claim that the observed exponents match the mean-field branching prediction.

## 4. Crackling noise and avalanche statistics

Crackling noise — a response consisting of discrete, broadly-distributed events in time — is the statistical-physics umbrella under which neural avalanches, Barkhausen jumps in ferromagnets, earthquakes, and plastic micro-deformation in sheared solids all fall. The Sethna-Dahmen-Myers 2001 *Nature* review [Sethna 2001] is the field-defining statement and is mandatory reading for anyone porting avalanche methodology to a new domain. Its programmatic recommendation is that a criticality claim must rest not on a single power-law exponent but on the *joint* structure of size, duration and shape distributions, with scaling relations among them providing non-trivial consistency checks. Fit size and duration distributions, extract exponents tau and alpha (or tau_t), *and then verify the scaling relation* tau_t - 1 = nu_T z (tau - 1), equivalently tau = 1 + (tau_t - 1)/(nu_T z), that relates them via the dynamical exponent z. Any power-law fit that passes the individual exponent tests but fails the scaling relation is not universal — it is either a non-critical mechanism producing accidental heavy tails or a crossover between two regimes that happens to look power-law over a limited range.

Beyond exponents, Papanikolaou, Bohn, Sommer, Durin, Zapperi and Sethna showed that *avalanche shape* — the temporal profile of activity within an avalanche, collapsed onto a universal curve after rescaling by duration T and a T-dependent amplitude — is a much more stringent test of universality than any scalar exponent [Papanikolaou 2011]. Two mechanisms that share an exponent need not share an avalanche shape, and the shape reveals information about the underlying dynamics (presence or absence of negative effective mass in the flux response, shape asymmetry indicating retardation, plateau structure indicating pinning, and so on) that the exponents conceal. For ML, this means reporting not only P(s) ~ s^(-tau) but also the mean temporal profile of an avalanche at fixed duration T — the ensemble-averaged activity as a function of scaled time tau/T — rescaled by a T-dependent amplitude and overlaid across T, to check collapse. A collapse failure is decisive evidence that the power law is not universal. A collapse success is meaningful evidence that it may be. The shape-collapse test is also unusually robust to the methodological artefacts that plague exponent estimation (finite-size cutoffs, threshold sensitivity, event-definition choices): shapes are invariant under many reasonable changes in protocol, so a shape collapse that survives protocol variation is compelling.

Sethna's later pedagogical writing, including the book-length treatment of crackling noise and its lecture notes available online (VERIFY citation for Sethna's later crackling-noise notes — several unpublished manuscripts circulate), emphasises a further set of diagnostics that ML practitioners should adopt. First, the *power spectrum* of crackling noise has a universal form S(f) ~ f^(-(1 + (tau_t - 2)/(tau - 2))) derivable from exponents alone; measuring the spectrum provides a cross-check against the exponent-plus-shape diagnostic. Second, the *avalanche hierarchy* — the distribution of small avalanches nested inside larger ones — has universal scaling structure that can be measured directly and compared across systems. Third, *crossover scaling* near first-order transitions or near critical-end-points produces distinct phenomenology from genuine criticality, and distinguishing them requires examining behaviour across multiple control-parameter values.

The methodological pipeline recommended by the crackling-noise literature, applied to ML, is therefore: (i) define an event (e.g., a residual-stream unit exceeding threshold, or a token-level spike in gradient norm, or a head-activation-norm event); (ii) define an avalanche as a contiguous run of events bounded by inactivity gaps exceeding a separation time; (iii) fit s-distribution, t-distribution and mean-shape using Clauset-style MLE with bootstrap (§8); (iv) extract tau, tau_t and z by independent fits; (v) check the scaling relation among them with propagated uncertainties; (vi) check shape collapse across multiple T; (vii) measure the power spectrum as an independent cross-check; (viii) only then claim a universality class. Each of these steps has a standard error-bar protocol (usually bootstrap). Skipping any of them invalidates the physics interpretation. The number of published "edge-of-chaos in neural networks" papers that complete even steps (i)-(iv) is small; the number that complete all of (i)-(viii) is effectively zero, which represents both a gap in the literature and an opportunity for methodologically rigorous work.

A final critical observation: threshold sensitivity is the largest empirical-methodology failure mode. The choice of threshold for defining an "event" determines the avalanche statistics, and different thresholds can shift the apparent exponent by 0.1-0.3 units — enough to move between universality classes. The correct response, per Sethna-Dahmen-Myers, is to compute exponents across a range of thresholds and verify plateau-like stability; if the exponent drifts continuously with threshold, there is no universal exponent and no universality class to claim. This is a simple sanity check that most ML-criticality papers omit.

Crackling noise also has a deep connection to disordered depinning transitions — the motion of interfaces (domain walls in magnets, contact lines in wetting, fracture fronts in brittle materials) pinned by quenched disorder and driven slowly through the pinned-to-moving transition. The universality classes here differ from DP (because the order parameter is an interface height rather than a density) but share the crackling-noise phenomenology, with size, duration and shape distributions displaying universal exponents and collapse. The depinning universality class that has been studied most thoroughly is that of the quenched-Edwards-Wilkinson equation, relevant to interface depinning with short-range elasticity. For ML, the depinning analogy may be relevant in a subtle way: gradient-descent dynamics moving through a disordered loss landscape is literally an interface-depinning problem in a high-dimensional space, and the avalanches of loss decrease during training could in principle exhibit crackling-noise statistics in the depinning class. Studies of this correspondence in neural-network training are sparse and would be a natural companion to any edge-of-chaos analysis of the trained endpoint.

The Sethna et al. methodology also emphasises the importance of *continuous* control-parameter sweeps rather than single-point measurements. True criticality is a codimension-one (or higher) submanifold in parameter space; on the critical surface exponents are universal, off-surface they drift continuously. A measurement at a single point in parameter space cannot distinguish between a critical point and a Griffiths-phase plateau. The Sethna programme demands measurements across a range of control parameter values, with exponents and scaling functions reported as functions of parameter, to enable identification of critical manifolds by their characteristic scaling-function-invariance signature. Applying this to ML would mean measuring avalanche statistics across a range of e.g. spectral-radius values, gain values, or training checkpoints, and identifying whether exponents stabilise near specific values (indicating criticality) or drift continuously (indicating Griffiths phase or non-criticality).

## 5. Edge of chaos in dynamical systems

The phrase "edge of chaos" entered dynamical-systems parlance through Langton's 1990 *Physica D* paper on cellular automata [Langton 1990], in which a lambda parameter (the fraction of non-quiescent transitions in the rule table of a k-state radius-r CA) was shown to label a continuum from ordered (low lambda) to chaotic (high lambda) CA behaviour, with a narrow lambda_c regime of "complex" rules exhibiting long transients and rich computation. Langton connected this empirical observation to Wolfram's earlier classification of CA into fixed-point, periodic, chaotic and complex classes, and argued that the complex class sits at the order-chaos boundary and is precisely the class capable of universal computation. The claim was bold and speculative but empirically based on observed statistics of rule spaces at various lambda. Packard's earlier work argued that a genetic algorithm evolving CA rules for computation adapts toward this regime [Packard 1988], providing an adaptive-dynamics mechanism for systems to self-organise toward the computational sweet spot. The primary Packard citation is a conference-proceedings chapter whose bibliographic details are worth verifying for any formal literature review (VERIFY) — it is widely cited but not always correctly.

Kauffman's NK Boolean-network programme [Kauffman 1993] articulated a similar trichotomy — ordered, critical, chaotic — in random Boolean networks where each node has K inputs and implements a random Boolean function. The ordered regime (K=1) has short transients and short attractors; the chaotic regime (large K) has exponentially long transients and attractors scaling with N; the critical regime (K=2 in random Boolean networks with bias-1/2 functions) sits between and was proposed as the generic regime for cell-fate networks in biology. Kauffman's book is a rich and occasionally speculative tour of consequences and conjectures; the solid results are the phase-diagram analysis and the critical-K identification, which have been verified and extended by subsequent work. For the ML programme, NK networks are a useful toy model because they share with feedforward deep nets the feature of composed random Boolean-like operations, and the K=2 critical point gives a concrete example of a tuned discrete-dynamics criticality that is not absorbing-state and not DP — it is a separate dynamical phase transition in the Boolean-network class.

Mitchell, Hraber and Crutchfield's 1993 replication study [Mitchell 1993] is a cautionary tale that is absolutely essential for any modern "edge of chaos in deep networks" claim. They re-ran Packard's experiment with tighter controls and did *not* find evolved CA rules clustering at lambda_c. Their conclusion — that the edge-of-chaos hypothesis as originally stated was at best over-generalised, and that the lambda parameter is a poor proxy for computational capacity — has shaped the field's subsequent sobriety. The remaining defensible version of the edge-of-chaos thesis is narrower: *certain* quantities (Lyapunov exponents vanishing, memory capacity peaking, information-processing metrics maximising) coincide with certain dynamical transitions in *specific model classes*. This narrower claim is empirically defensible but must be evaluated per-model, not asserted as a universal principle. An ML paper citing Langton 1990 or Packard 1988 must also cite Mitchell-Hraber-Crutchfield 1993 and address the critique it raises; omitting the replication study is intellectually dishonest in 2025.

Two methodological traps recur in the edge-of-chaos literature and must be avoided in any ML transcription. The first is parameter-tuning: it is trivial to construct a one-parameter family of systems that passes through a dynamical transition (variance of initial weights, spectral radius, learning rate scale, temperature), and equally trivial to find metrics (Lyapunov exponent, spectral gap, autocorrelation time) that change sign or diverge there. Showing a metric peaks at lambda_c is not showing that the system *chooses* to be at lambda_c. The non-trivial claim is that the system arrives at lambda_c by itself — which is the SOC claim — or alternatively that some training dynamics pushes it to lambda_c as an emergent consequence of loss minimisation, which is a meaningful claim if supported by showing that pre-training networks are away from lambda_c and post-training networks are at lambda_c along a well-defined trajectory. The second trap is the computational-capacity measurement itself: many published metrics (memory length, mutual-information bandwidth, separation-property measures, input-distinguishability indices) are not intrinsic properties of the dynamical system but depend on the task, the readout, and the input distribution. An ML paper that measures an information-theoretic quantity peaking "at the edge of chaos" in a trained transformer must be specific about which quantity, which input distribution, and which readout — and must establish that the peak is not an artefact of how the tuning parameter is swept (the sweep schedule, resolution, and endpoint choices).

A third, subtler methodological trap is the choice of *ordering* for multi-parameter systems. In a CA there is a single parameter lambda; in a random recurrent network there is a single gain g; in a trained transformer there are millions of parameters and the "edge of chaos" is at best a codimension-one submanifold in weight space. Speaking of "the" edge of chaos in a trained transformer requires specifying the direction in which one approaches the submanifold, and different directions give different dynamical behaviour near the transition. The cleanest published claims in this space (e.g., the deep-learning theory literature on signal-propagation depth at initialisation) identify a specific direction — the variance of the initialisation — and make their claims relative to that direction, not the bulk parameter space. Any edge-of-chaos claim in a trained transformer should do the same.

A historical caveat worth noting: the "edge of chaos" idea predates Langton 1990 in the physics literature, appearing implicitly in work on order-chaos transitions in iterated maps (Pomeau-Manneville intermittency, the Feigenbaum cascade, crisis transitions in dissipative systems) from the 1970s and early 1980s. What Langton and Packard added was the computational-capacity interpretation — the claim that systems near the order-chaos boundary are optimal for certain information-processing tasks. This claim is what Mitchell et al. 1993 contested, and it is the specific claim that any ML paper invoking "edge of chaos" must defend. Generic dynamical-systems transitions to chaos are physics, not ML-specific; the ML-specific content of edge-of-chaos is the computational-capacity peak, and that is the testable claim that requires careful empirical work.

Finally, the connection between edge-of-chaos and generalisation in ML is not established theoretically and is at best suggestive empirically. The argument "edge of chaos = maximum computational capacity = best generalisation" has three links, each of which is weak: the first link (edge of chaos = maximum computational capacity) is supported in reservoir computing for standard tasks but is not known to hold for trained networks; the second link (maximum computational capacity = best generalisation) confuses expressive capacity with generalisation capacity, which are known to decouple in deep learning (high-capacity networks can generalise well because of implicit regularisation, not despite it); the third link (edge of chaos = best generalisation via both intermediate links) is a conjecture, not a theorem. An ML paper claiming the first link is making a specific empirical claim; one claiming the third is speculating. The distinction should be made clearly.

## 6. Random recurrent networks: the Sompolinsky lineage

Sompolinsky, Crisanti and Sommers [Sompolinsky 1988] solved the dynamical mean-field theory (DMFT) of a continuous-time random recurrent network with tanh nonlinearity and i.i.d. Gaussian weights of variance g^2/N. The equation of motion is dx_i/dt = -x_i + sum_j J_ij phi(x_j) with phi(x) = tanh(x), and the DMFT trick is to replace the sum over j with a Gaussian-distributed effective field whose variance is determined self-consistently by the autocorrelation C(t, t') = <x_i(t) x_i(t')>. Below g = 1 the only stationary state is the origin x_i = 0, stable against perturbations; above g = 1 the origin becomes unstable and the network enters a chaotic regime with a positive maximal Lyapunov exponent and stationary autocorrelation function determined by the self-consistency equation. The g = 1 transition is the canonical "edge of chaos" in continuous-time random neural networks, and it is formally an *instability* of a trivial fixed point, not an SOC or absorbing-state transition — this distinction is crucial and widely muddled in the literature. The Sompolinsky transition is a Hopf-like bifurcation to chaos via the Ruelle-Takens-Newhouse scenario; it is a transition in the *dynamical phase* of the system, not a phase transition in a thermodynamic order parameter.

Molgedey, Schuchhardt and Schuster [Molgedey 1992] showed that additive noise shifts the chaotic transition and can suppress chaos: adding Gaussian noise of variance D to each neuron's dynamics pushes the transition to g > 1, with the critical g_c(D) computable from the noisy DMFT. This result is directly relevant to ML because stochastic training (mini-batch noise, dropout, weight decay fluctuations) is always present and naturally acts as additive noise on the dynamics. The SGD-trained network is therefore always in an effective "Molgedey regime" rather than a pure "Sompolinsky regime," and the critical g for a trained network may differ from one for an i.i.d.-weight network with the same spectral radius.

Cessac, Doyon, Quoy and Samuelides derived the discrete-time analogue with similar phase diagram (VERIFY exact citation — commonly cited as Doyon-Cessac-Samuelides 1993 in some sources and Cessac et al. 1994 in others) [Cessac 1994]. The discrete-time case is the more relevant one for recurrent networks trained by backpropagation through time, and the phase diagram is similar but with exponents shifted from the continuous-time values. Crucially, for discrete time the transition involves a period-doubling cascade rather than a pure Hopf bifurcation, and the approach to chaos is via a quasi-periodic route that has implications for empirical signatures (e.g., the power spectrum near criticality has characteristic peak structure).

Schuecker, Goedeke and Helias [Schuecker 2018] extended DMFT to driven networks, showing that input drive shifts the edge of chaos and that memory/information-processing capacity is maximised at the shifted transition. This is directly relevant to ML: trained transformers are always input-driven, and the naive g = 1 condition on undriven weights is not the relevant criticality condition. The right object is the DMFT of the input-driven, trained network, with the control parameter being whatever quantity actually traces out the transition (spectral radius of input-averaged Jacobian, or the Lyapunov exponent of the input-trajectory flow). This is theoretically non-trivial but tractable, and an ML paper claiming edge-of-chaos in trained GPT-2 should derive or estimate the relevant DMFT-analogous quantity rather than defaulting to spectral-radius-of-W. The Schuecker et al. result also shows that the edge of chaos is not a thin submanifold but an extended region when driving is present, which connects to the Griffiths-phase discussion in §10.

An important conceptual clarification from the Sompolinsky lineage: the "edge of chaos" in this literature is a transition between order (decaying perturbations) and chaos (exponentially-growing perturbations), measured by the maximal Lyapunov exponent crossing zero. This is *not* the same as the avalanche-criticality of SOC, which is a transition between subcritical (avalanches die) and supercritical (avalanches grow) propagation, measured by the branching ratio crossing one. In mean field these two transitions coincide — both are sigma = 1 / g = 1 conditions on the Jacobian's spectral radius — but in finite dimensions or in systems with absorbing states they are distinct. Conflating them is one of the most common theoretical errors in the ML-criticality literature. A defensible paper must either (a) state that it is using the Sompolinsky/Lyapunov notion of edge-of-chaos, or (b) state that it is using the branching/avalanche notion, or (c) argue specifically that in the system under study the two coincide.

The Sompolinsky DMFT has seen substantial technical development since 1988 in directions directly relevant to ML. The inclusion of multiple populations (excitatory/inhibitory) with non-Gaussian weight statistics produces phase diagrams with regions of oscillatory chaos, multi-attractor dynamics, and coexistence phases. The inclusion of structured (e.g., block-diagonal or low-rank) weight matrices produces transitions between attractor dynamics and chaotic fluctuations on top of slow attractor-like dynamics, with the low-rank structure carrying computationally useful content while the full-rank random component provides chaotic enrichment. Trained networks, with their mix of structured (task-specific) and unstructured (initialisation-residual) components in their weights, are naturally in this regime, and the modern DMFT literature (work by Helias, Mastrogiuseppe-Ostojic, Landau-Sompolinsky among others) provides the tools to analyse them. Importing this machinery to trained transformers is a significant but tractable programme.

Another conceptual point from the Sompolinsky literature deserves emphasis: the chaotic phase above g = 1 is a *dynamical* phase, not a thermodynamic one, meaning that it is defined by properties of the trajectory flow rather than a thermodynamic order parameter. Consequently, the usual statistical-mechanical tools (susceptibility divergences, Binder cumulants, finite-size collapse of static observables) do not straightforwardly apply. The relevant tools are dynamical: Lyapunov spectrum, attractor dimension, autocorrelation-time divergence, memory-capacity scaling. Any ML paper drawing on the Sompolinsky lineage must use the dynamical toolkit rather than the thermodynamic one, and must be careful not to carry over concepts that don't translate (e.g., "fluctuation-dissipation" has a specific meaning out of equilibrium that requires a non-equilibrium formulation like the one in the Martin-Siggia-Rose formalism).

A practical remark on measuring Lyapunov exponents in trained transformers is warranted. For continuous-time random networks, the DMFT gives the Lyapunov spectrum analytically in the thermodynamic limit. For discrete-time trained networks, the practitioner must resort to numerical methods — Benettin's algorithm with Gram-Schmidt reorthogonalisation, or more sophisticated covariant-Lyapunov-vector methods. The relevant observable for edge-of-chaos analysis is the maximal Lyapunov exponent lambda_1 along the input-driven trajectory; it crosses zero at the transition and its sign is the order parameter. Measuring lambda_1 in a transformer requires perturbing the forward pass at an intermediate layer, tracking divergence through subsequent layers, and extracting the exponential growth rate. This is computationally feasible for GPT-2-scale models but requires careful numerical treatment because the standard Benettin algorithm assumes autonomous dynamics, whereas transformer inference is input-dependent and non-autonomous in the natural time variable (position index). The appropriate generalisation involves averaging the Lyapunov exponent over an ensemble of input trajectories, which reintroduces the input-distribution dependence of edge-of-chaos metrics discussed above. These technical details are genuinely non-trivial and represent a methodology gap that ML-criticality papers must address.

## 7. Reservoir computing and echo-state networks

Reservoir computing uses a fixed random recurrent network (the "reservoir") as a high-dimensional non-linear filter whose state is read out linearly, with the reservoir weights frozen and only the readout trained. Jaeger's echo-state approach [Jaeger 2001] defines the *echo state property* (ESP): asymptotic dependence of the reservoir state on inputs but not initial conditions, formalised as the condition that two reservoir trajectories driven by the same input from different initial conditions converge in time. A sufficient condition for ESP is that the spectral radius of the recurrent weight matrix is less than one; a necessary condition is weaker and involves the input-dependent Lipschitz constant of the dynamics. For tanh reservoirs with spectral radius slightly above one, ESP can still hold for non-trivial input distributions because inputs effectively clip the Jacobian. Maass, Natschläger and Markram [Maass 2002] articulated the parallel "liquid state machine" framework with the *separation property* (different inputs produce separable reservoir states) and the *approximation property* (simple readouts can approximate arbitrary functions of reservoir history) as the two properties defining a useful reservoir. These two frameworks are largely equivalent for analytical purposes but differ in emphasis and in the specific metrics used to quantify reservoir quality.

The edge-of-chaos thesis in reservoir computing [Bertschinger 2004, Boedecker 2012] is that various capacity measures peak at the ESP boundary — spectral radius approximately one — consistent with the general edge-of-chaos story. Bertschinger and Natschläger [Bertschinger 2004] provided a clean quantitative demonstration: in discrete-time random recurrent networks with threshold non-linearities, the computational capacity for binary classification tasks is maximal at a specific value of the weight-variance parameter coinciding with the order-chaos transition. Their proof-of-concept uses the NARMA task family and demonstrates a clear peak in the input-separation/memory tradeoff at the transition. Boedecker, Obst, Lizier, Mayer and Asada [Boedecker 2012] extended this with information-theoretic measures — transfer entropy, active information storage, and the Crutchfield-Young statistical complexity — and confirmed peaks near spectral-radius-one across a range of tasks and reservoir sizes. Tanaka et al.'s review of physical reservoir computing [Tanaka 2019] provides the modern synthesis, covering photonic, spintronic, and mechanical reservoirs with the common theme that the edge-of-chaos regime is where physical reservoirs perform best as computational substrates.

For the ML-on-transformers programme, the relevant translation is that a trained transformer, with input embeddings, layer structure and residual stream, is not a classical reservoir but shares one critical feature: the linear (LayerNorm-then-unembed) readout at the logits depends on non-linear high-dimensional internal state produced by a stack of attention and MLP layers. The "effective spectral radius" analogous to Jaeger's is the spectral radius of the input-averaged end-to-end Jacobian of the residual stream. This Jacobian is not a single matrix but a distribution — it depends on the input trajectory — and the relevant scalar summary is its expected log-spectral-radius, which for a trained network can be estimated by Jacobian-probing methods. Several warnings attach to this translation. First, reservoir computing's edge-of-chaos claim is well-tested for *random* reservoirs but is not known to extend to *trained* reservoirs, and the training objective may push the system away from rather than toward the edge depending on the task structure. Second, the "memory capacity" metric used throughout this literature is task-specific; the analogous quantity in a language model would need to be something like in-context learning capacity or retrieval accuracy as a function of context length, and this is an active research frontier whose link to dynamical criticality is not established. Third, reservoir computing typically uses sparse tanh networks with fixed topology; transformers have dense attention with content-dependent routing, which may change the effective critical regime in ways not captured by the random-reservoir analysis.

A deeper conceptual point: in reservoir computing the *argument* for edge-of-chaos is that at the transition the reservoir has maximum dimensionality of its response to input perturbations (chaos side) while still retaining memory of recent inputs (order side). This tradeoff is inherent to the reservoir-plus-linear-readout architecture. In a transformer with trainable intermediate representations, the tradeoff is different: the network can store memory explicitly in KV-cache and in residual-stream position structure, and it can enlarge its functional-dimensionality without relying on chaotic dynamics. It is therefore not obvious a priori that a trained transformer should exhibit an edge-of-chaos signature in the reservoir-computing sense. An empirical finding that it does would be a genuine scientific result; an empirical finding that it does not would also be a result and would require reconciliation with the reservoir-theory baseline.

The reservoir-computing literature also introduces several metrics that can be directly translated to trained-transformer analysis with appropriate care. The *memory capacity* defined by Jaeger as the sum of squared correlations between linear readouts and delayed inputs is a scalar that is maximal at the ESP boundary in random reservoirs; for a trained transformer an analogous quantity could be the in-context-learning memory capacity measured by task-completion accuracy as a function of the separation between cue and target. The *separation property* of Maass-Natschläger-Markram, quantified by the distance in reservoir state space produced by slightly different inputs, translates to the transformer as the mean-squared divergence between hidden-state trajectories produced by inputs that differ in a single token. Both metrics have natural transformer analogues and both should peak at criticality in the reservoir-theory baseline; empirically verifying or falsifying this is a well-defined experiment.

A related body of work concerns the connection between reservoir computing and kernel methods: a linear readout on a reservoir is equivalent to a kernel regression with a reservoir-induced kernel, and the kernel-based view gives explicit formulas for generalisation bounds in terms of reservoir spectral properties. Recent work (post-2015) has extended this connection to deep networks at initialisation via the Neural Tangent Kernel and related constructs. For trained transformers, the relevant question is whether the reservoir-kernel view survives training, and if so what the trained-kernel's spectral structure looks like. Preliminary evidence suggests the answer is "partially" — trained networks share some but not all properties with the NTK-predicted reservoir — and this is an active research area that connects naturally to edge-of-chaos analyses.

One final empirical caveat: reservoir computing's edge-of-chaos results are robust for *structured* tasks where memory and nonlinearity both matter (NARMA, narma-10 and variants, sinusoidal prediction). For tasks where either memory or nonlinearity dominates — pure memory tasks (parity with long delay), pure nonlinearity tasks (XOR on instantaneous inputs) — the optimal spectral radius is not near one, and edge-of-chaos is not the right regime. Transformers are trained on language modelling, which is a mixed-memory-and-nonlinearity task, so the reservoir-theory baseline prediction is that trained transformers *should* exhibit edge-of-chaos-like structure for language-modelling-relevant metrics. But this prediction is tentative, and the task-dependence of the optimum means that different evaluation tasks might disagree about whether the network is at the edge of chaos, with each answer being correct for its own task.

## 8. Power-law fitting methodology

The single most important methodological reference for any criticality claim is Clauset, Shalizi and Newman's 2009 *SIAM Review* paper [Clauset 2009]. Their procedure, which has become the de facto standard in network science, complex systems and statistical physics, comprises the following steps. (i) Fit a power-law tail by maximum likelihood assuming P(x) ~ x^(-alpha) for x > x_min, selecting the lower cutoff x_min by minimising the Kolmogorov-Smirnov distance between the empirical CDF above x_min and the fitted CDF. (ii) Bootstrap the fit to obtain uncertainties on alpha and x_min, typically by resampling the data 1000 times and refitting. (iii) Use a Vuong-like likelihood-ratio test to compare against alternatives: lognormal, exponential, stretched exponential, power-law with exponential cutoff, and power-law-with-cutoff are the standard alternatives. (iv) Report a goodness-of-fit p-value by bootstrap resampling: generate synthetic datasets from the fitted power law, refit, compute KS distance, and report the fraction of synthetic fits with KS distance exceeding the empirical value. (v) Refuse to claim a power law unless alternatives are rejected at conventional significance and the p-value does not reject the power-law hypothesis. Their paper works through many classical datasets (earthquake magnitudes, word frequencies, blackout sizes, cities, etc.) and shows that most previously-claimed power laws do not pass the full test.

The `powerlaw` Python package [Alstott 2014] implements this procedure and is the standard software in the field; its main virtue is that it makes the Clauset procedure operationally convenient, though users must still understand the methodology rather than treating it as a black box. For binned data — common in ML contexts where avalanche sizes are counted into bins for computational convenience — Virkar and Clauset [Virkar 2014] provide the correct MLE adapted to binned observations; naive log-binned-histogram OLS regression is biased (because log-binning distorts the likelihood) and should not be used despite its prevalence in older literature. Deluca and Corral [Deluca 2013] handle truncated distributions (finite-size cutoffs, which are endemic in neural-network data where avalanche size cannot exceed the total number of neurons) with improved p-value estimation via a "truncated power law" fit that properly accounts for the upper cutoff. Newman's earlier review [Newman 2005] is a good conceptual entry point and discusses the range of mechanisms that produce power laws (rich-get-richer, combinations of exponentials, random-walk boundaries, inverse quantities of broadly-distributed variables, etc.) without requiring criticality.

The common pitfalls, any one of which kills a criticality claim, are worth enumerating explicitly because they appear in the literature with discouraging frequency. (a) Naive OLS on log-binned histograms — biased exponent estimate, no proper uncertainty, and highly sensitive to bin choice; avoid entirely and use MLE. (b) Too few decades of scaling — power-law distinguishability requires at least 2-3 decades of scaling range because over fewer decades a power law and a lognormal are essentially indistinguishable in finite data. (c) Not testing alternatives — in many neural datasets lognormal or power-law-with-exponential-cutoff fits as well or better than a pure power law, and the Vuong likelihood-ratio test is the correct way to adjudicate. (d) Not reporting x_min — quoting alpha without x_min is uninterpretable because alpha depends strongly on the tail-fraction of data used. (e) Not bootstrapping — point estimates without uncertainty cannot be compared across conditions, and many claimed "shifts in criticality" reflect sampling variability rather than genuine differences. (f) Ignoring the scaling-relation cross-check (§4) — a good fit to each marginal distribution does not imply universality because unrelated mechanisms can produce consistent marginal fits. (g) Using a single threshold for event definition — threshold sensitivity analysis is essential. Any ML paper claiming edge-of-chaos via power-law statistics must satisfy all of (a)-(g). A separate class of issues arises with *correlated* data, where the i.i.d. assumption underlying Clauset's MLE is violated; bootstrap procedures must then be block-bootstrap or otherwise account for correlation, and standard p-values are unreliable.

One subtle point deserves emphasis: the Clauset-Shalizi-Newman test can *fail to reject* a power-law hypothesis without providing evidence *for* a power law — it is a null-hypothesis test in the frequentist sense. Many ML papers misread "cannot reject power-law at p > 0.1" as "power-law is supported." The correct reading is that the data are consistent with a power law among the alternatives tested, not that the data rule in a power-law origin. Bayesian model comparison, where available, gives a more direct measure of relative support and should be preferred when the computational budget allows.

Another technically important point concerns *sample size* requirements. The Clauset-Shalizi-Newman procedure gives reliable exponent estimates with uncertainties of order 0.05 when the number of samples in the tail (x > x_min) exceeds roughly 50-100; below this, uncertainty is large and likelihood-ratio tests against alternatives have low power. For ML avalanche data, this means that networks small enough to be studied cheaply may not produce enough avalanches in the tail to support a confident criticality claim without very long simulation or inference runs. The tail-sample requirement is often overlooked in favour of the total-sample count, which is misleading because x_min selection typically discards 90+% of the data. Any criticality claim should report the tail-sample count alongside the total sample size.

The "powerlaw" package's default behaviour has some subtle pitfalls worth naming. The package uses the Kolmogorov-Smirnov distance for x_min selection, which is known to under-select x_min (choose too small a cutoff) for heavy-tailed distributions with slowly-decaying corrections to scaling. This produces alpha estimates biased low (shallower tails) when the underlying distribution has a corrected-to-scaling form. Alternatives include the Anderson-Darling distance (more sensitive to tail deviations, recommended by Deluca and Corral), or explicit visual inspection of the distance-vs-x_min curve for a stable plateau. Papers that report Clauset-style fits should at minimum perform a sensitivity analysis varying the distance metric used for x_min selection.

Finally, the comparison-against-alternatives step has its own subtleties. The Vuong test assumes both models are mis-specified (or both true) and gives a test of which is *closer* to the true distribution. If both power-law and lognormal are badly wrong (which is common when the true mechanism is a Griffiths phase or a neutral process), the Vuong test can declare either one the "winner" based on nothing more than accidental curvature. The correct extended procedure is to also perform absolute goodness-of-fit tests (not just relative comparisons) for all candidate distributions, and to accept a criticality claim only when the power-law has absolute p > 0.1 and also beats alternatives in the relative test. Virkar and Clauset 2014 and Deluca and Corral 2013 give the extended procedure.

## 9. Non-equilibrium statistical mechanics and brain criticality

Muñoz's 2018 *Reviews of Modern Physics* colloquium [Munoz 2018] is the modern synthesis of criticality in biological systems and the essential reading for anyone porting statphys-criticality to a new domain. It catalogues mechanisms (absorbing-phase transitions, synchronisation transitions, Griffiths phases, quasi-criticality, neutral dynamics), reviews empirical evidence in cortex, collective animal behaviour, gene-regulatory dynamics, and flocking, and lays out a programme for distinguishing genuine critical phenomena from coincidental power laws or Griffiths-phase phenomenology. Its central methodological recommendation — that exponent measurement must be supplemented with scaling-relation checks, data collapse across system sizes, and rejection of neutral-theory null models — is directly transferable to ML. Muñoz also discusses the "criticality hypothesis" in biology, which is the proposal that evolutionary pressure generically drives biological systems toward critical dynamics because criticality optimises information-processing and adaptive capacity; this hypothesis is attractive but not settled, and the colloquium gives a balanced treatment of the evidence for and against.

Markovic and Gros [Markovic 2014] offer a parallel *Physics Reports* review with more emphasis on distinguishing SOC from other sources of scale-free behaviour and with a more sceptical tone than Muñoz's. Their taxonomy of "reasons a power law might appear" — SOC proper, preferential attachment [Barabasi 1999], multiplicative processes (yielding lognormals that masquerade as power laws over limited ranges), mixtures of exponentials, finite-size crossovers, and measurement artefacts — is the right mental checklist for any criticality claim. They particularly emphasise that the Bak 1996 programme of universal SOC has not been empirically vindicated and that most claimed empirical SOC reduces on inspection to either (a) tuned absorbing-phase transitions or (b) non-SOC mechanisms producing power laws. The review is especially pointed about the role of *conservation laws* in distinguishing genuine SOC from superficially similar phenomena: the former requires a local conservation law that pins the system to a critical manifold; the latter does not.

Beggs and Plenz [Beggs 2003] discovered neuronal avalanches in cortical slices with tau ~ 3/2 consistent with mean-field branching, launching the brain-criticality programme. Their data — multi-electrode array recordings in acute slices, with avalanches defined by contiguous spike events across electrodes — have been replicated in many laboratories and extended to in-vivo recordings, EEG, and MEG. The tau ~ 3/2 finding is robust across these extensions, though the methodological details (bin size, threshold, electrode number) affect the measured exponents. Beggs and Timme [Beggs 2012] provide a balanced review of the neuronal-avalanche field with explicit attention to methodological caveats: the sensitivity to bin size (choosing the bin to equal the average inter-electrode spike delay is a convention that can bias exponent estimation), the role of subsampling (fewer electrodes can shift apparent exponents), the issue of external driving (in vivo versus slice), and the ambiguity between critical and Griffiths-phase explanations. Their review is a model of how to write about criticality with appropriate epistemic humility.

Poil, van Ooyen and Linkenkaer-Hansen [Poil 2012] demonstrated SOC-like dynamics in non-conservative network models with plasticity, specifically in networks with activity-dependent synaptic modification (VERIFY exact title and journal). This is directly relevant to the ML programme because non-conservative mechanisms — plasticity-induced self-tuning of the effective gain — are what training dynamics in ML would have to rely on to produce any SOC-like behaviour. If trained networks are to be interpreted as SOC systems, the mechanism must be that the training loss plays the role of the slow driving and the gradient-descent dynamics plays the role of the fast relaxation, with some implicit "conservation" (perhaps the total perturbation budget of a given parameter update) providing the tuning. Working out this correspondence in detail is an open research question and would be a substantial theoretical contribution.

Additional physics background worth mentioning for completeness: the Nishimori line in random-bond Ising models, a special line in the parameter space where disorder-averaged observables can be computed exactly and where the ferromagnet-spin-glass transition meets the paramagnet. The Nishimori line is directly relevant to the "ground-truth recoverable" condition in inference problems in statistical physics and appears in modern ML theory as the boundary of the identifiability region for Bayes-optimal inference. While not a direct tool for edge-of-chaos work, it is worth having in the theoretical toolkit because it provides a concrete example of an exactly-solvable non-equilibrium-relevant critical surface.

A conceptual point from Muñoz 2018 that deserves extra emphasis: the distinction between *static* criticality (thermodynamic phase transitions with order-parameter fluctuations at a critical point) and *dynamical* criticality (non-equilibrium transitions with time-dependent order parameters). Static criticality is characterised by spatial correlation-length divergence and diverging susceptibility; dynamical criticality is characterised by temporal correlation-time divergence and critical slowing down. The two are related via dynamical scaling — the dynamical exponent z relates spatial and temporal correlations — but they are conceptually distinct, and biological and ML systems are typically discussed in the dynamical rather than the static category. The Muñoz colloquium is careful about this distinction, and any ML paper should be too: measuring "susceptibility" in a trained network requires specifying whether the static or dynamical quantity is meant, and they are measured differently.

Muñoz's colloquium also draws attention to the *quasi-criticality* concept: systems that hover near criticality without ever being exactly critical, exhibiting extended but non-universal scaling regimes. Quasi-critical dynamics arise from several mechanisms, Griffiths phases being the most common, but also including systems with slow adaptation of the control parameter around a critical value, systems with spatiotemporal heterogeneity, and systems with multiple nearly-critical subsystems. For ML applications, quasi-criticality may be the single most plausible hypothesis for apparent critical statistics in trained networks, because training dynamics plausibly produce slow-varying effective gains and extensive heterogeneity simultaneously. A paper that rules out true criticality and argues for quasi-criticality would be on solid ground; a paper that claims true criticality without ruling out quasi-criticality is overclaiming.

Another branch of non-equilibrium statistical mechanics worth flagging is the theory of fluctuation theorems (Jarzynski equality, Crooks fluctuation theorem, Gallavotti-Cohen theorem). These exact identities constrain work and entropy production distributions in driven non-equilibrium systems and have been applied to trajectory-level analysis of biological and artificial dynamical systems. For trained neural networks viewed as non-equilibrium dissipative systems (weights evolve under training, activations evolve under inference), fluctuation theorems in principle provide constraints that criticality-related observables must satisfy. This is speculative but suggests a line of theoretical inquiry: do the loss-fluctuation statistics during training or the activation-fluctuation statistics during inference obey fluctuation-theorem relations, and if so what do they imply about effective thermodynamic quantities of the network? This goes beyond the current ML-criticality literature and is a potential direction for novel theoretical work.

## 10. Griffiths phases and quasi-criticality

The Griffiths-phase concept, originally developed for disordered magnets (Griffiths 1969, not included as a separate entry to maintain focus on neural/SOC-relevant literature), is the most important physics mechanism to appreciate when interpreting apparent criticality in heterogeneous trained networks. In a Griffiths phase, rare regions with locally supercritical dynamics persist in an otherwise subcritical medium, producing activity with heavy-tailed lifetime distributions that mimic criticality *over a range of control-parameter values*, not just at a single critical point. The underlying mechanism is that rare well-connected subclusters in a disordered system can individually satisfy the local criticality condition even when the global average is subcritical, and activity trapped in these subclusters decays slowly (as a stretched exponential or power law) rather than exponentially. The result is a "smeared" critical behaviour over an extended region of parameter space, mathematically distinct from true criticality but phenomenologically similar.

Moretti and Muñoz [Moretti 2013] showed that hierarchical modular networks generically host Griffiths phases: the heterogeneity of connectivity produces rare well-connected clusters that act as activity reservoirs. Their *Nature Communications* paper is the key reference for brain-network applications, because real cortical networks are hierarchical and modular, and the Moretti-Muñoz argument explains how cortex can exhibit apparent scale-free statistics across animals and preparations without requiring each brain to be fine-tuned to a knife-edge critical point. The Griffiths-phase explanation has several empirical signatures distinct from true criticality: exponents drift continuously with the control parameter rather than being universal, finite-size scaling is anomalous and often non-universal, and the heavy tails are *slower* than critical power laws at short times and *cut off* at longer times than a critical-point power law would be. Measuring these signatures is feasible with sufficient data and is a principled way to distinguish Griffiths-phase from critical-point phenomenology.

di Santo, Villegas, Burioni and Muñoz [di Santo 2018] developed a Landau-Ginzburg field theory for cortical dynamics that connects avalanche criticality to a synchronisation transition, providing a physical mechanism by which edge-of-chaos (a synchronisation-threshold phenomenon) and avalanche-criticality (an absorbing-state phenomenon) can coincide in the same system. Their *PNAS* paper shows that near the edge of synchronisation, the Landau-Ginzburg effective theory generates scale-free avalanches with mean-field exponents tau = 3/2 and tau_t = 2. This is an important theoretical bridge because it provides the first concrete mechanism by which the two kinds of criticality that the ML community tends to conflate (synchronisation/edge-of-chaos and avalanche/SOC) can be unified in a single dynamical system. For the ML programme, the di Santo et al. construction suggests that a natural way to search for criticality in trained transformers is to look for a synchronisation-like transition in the residual-stream dynamics (perhaps measured by phase-locking metrics across layers or across positions) that coincides with avalanche-like scaling in activation-spike statistics.

Martinello, Hidalgo, Maritan, di Santo, Plenz and Muñoz [Martinello 2017] provide an essential null-model check published in *Physical Review X*: neutral (non-critical) models — essentially random walks in frequency space with absorbing boundaries — reproduce the observed neural avalanche power laws with apparent mean-field exponents. This is a devastating result for naive critical-interpretation of avalanche exponents, because it shows that mean-field tau = 3/2 can arise from a model that has no critical point at all. The neutral null model must therefore be explicitly rejected before any critical interpretation can be defended. Their paper provides the methodology for the rejection: compare not only marginal exponents but also the joint distribution structure (size-duration correlations, shape, power spectrum) between empirical data and neutral-model simulations, and check whether the empirical data show features beyond what neutrality can produce. This is a template that the ML community should adopt directly.

For ML, the Griffiths-phase perspective is a double-edged sword with strong methodological implications. On the one hand, it explains how a trained network could exhibit apparent criticality without ever sitting exactly at a critical point — training-induced heterogeneity in effective connectivity (attention heads with very different roles, some layers near saturation others linear, some circuits narrowly specialised and others distributed) generates rare supercritical substructures whose activity dominates the tail. On the other hand, it means that *measuring power-law avalanche statistics in a trained network is not, by itself, evidence of criticality*, because Griffiths phases reproduce the same phenomenology over a broad parameter range and neutral models reproduce mean-field exponents without any critical structure. A defensible ML paper must therefore either (i) demonstrate fine-tuning to a critical manifold via scaling-relation checks, data collapse across model sizes, and rejection of both Griffiths-phase and neutral-model null models, or (ii) explicitly claim a Griffiths phase and provide the heterogeneity statistics, exponent-drift evidence, and rare-region analysis that supports it, or (iii) explicitly claim neutrality and show that the apparent criticality is a neutral-model artefact. A vague "edge-of-chaos" label satisfies none of these options.

A further subtlety from the Griffiths-phase literature: the rare regions responsible for Griffiths-phase scaling are typically well-connected *subgraphs* of the overall network. In heterogeneous networks their identity can be read off from topology (high-degree clusters, densely-connected modules). In a trained transformer the analogous subgraphs would be specific attention heads, specific MLP pathways, or specific circuit structures identified by interpretability methods. This suggests a bridge between mechanistic interpretability (which identifies circuits) and criticality analysis (which measures scaling statistics): if a Griffiths-phase interpretation is correct, criticality should be dominated by identifiable circuit subgraphs, and measuring scaling statistics within identified circuits should give sharper power laws than measuring them network-wide. This is a testable prediction that has not been explored in the literature and could form the core of a novel research project.

The di Santo-Villegas-Burioni-Muñoz construction [di Santo 2018] deserves further unpacking because it is the theoretical backbone for connecting synchronisation-type edge-of-chaos to avalanche-type criticality. Their Landau-Ginzburg theory starts from a coupled-oscillator description of cortical units and derives an effective field theory for the order-parameter fluctuations near the synchronisation transition. The mean-field exponents predicted are tau = 3/2 and tau_t = 2, matching both the branching-process baseline and the neural-avalanche measurements, but crucially the scaling function is determined by the Landau-Ginzburg effective theory and can be compared against measured shape collapses. This provides a non-trivial test: if real neural systems (or trained transformers) have scale-free avalanches for the reason the Landau-Ginzburg theory predicts, they should have the specific scaling functions the theory gives, not generic mean-field branching shapes. Testing this level of detail against trained-transformer data would be a strong result either way.

Neutral theories of neural dynamics, developed by Martinello et al. [Martinello 2017] and extended in subsequent work, provide the null model that every criticality claim must rule out. The neutral model assumes that activity is produced by a non-critical mechanism — essentially a random walk in frequency space — and yet reproduces the mean-field avalanche exponents that are the signature of criticality. The reason is combinatorial: any random-walk-plus-absorbing-boundary process has first-passage-time distributions with universal tau = 3/2 and tau_t = 2, independent of any underlying dynamical criticality. This is devastating for the "mean-field tau = 3/2 therefore critical" inference that pervades the neural-avalanche literature. The correct response is to identify features of the dynamics that *distinguish* critical from neutral — typically higher-order statistics (cumulants beyond the marginal), cross-correlations between distant parts of the system, and response functions to external perturbation — and to test these against both models. This extended-feature testing is standard in statistical-physics work on criticality and is the methodology that ML-criticality work should adopt.

## Stylised facts

**Robustly known.**
- Absorbing-state phase transitions fall in a small number of universality classes (DP, C-DP/Manna, parity-conserving, compact DP) with tabulated exponents [Hinrichsen 2000, Henkel 2008].
- Critical branching processes have mean-field exponents tau = 3/2 (size) and tau_t = 2 (duration); this is a *baseline* not a discovery [Harris 1963, Athreya 1972].
- Random continuous-time recurrent networks with Gaussian weights have a chaotic transition at g = 1 computable from DMFT [Sompolinsky 1988].
- Random reservoirs and ESNs have computational/memory-capacity metrics that peak near the spectral-radius-one boundary for standard benchmark tasks [Jaeger 2001, Bertschinger 2004, Boedecker 2012].
- The Clauset-Shalizi-Newman procedure is the de facto standard for power-law inference, and naive log-binned OLS is a disqualifying methodological error [Clauset 2009, Virkar 2014].
- Scaling-relation cross-checks (tau, tau_t and z consistency) and avalanche-shape collapse are stronger universality tests than exponent matching alone [Sethna 2001, Papanikolaou 2011].
- SOC is equivalent to a driven absorbing-state phase transition with a conservation law acting as tuning parameter [Dickman 1998, 2000].
- Neuronal avalanches in cortex have mean-field tau ~ 3/2 across many preparations, though the interpretation is contested [Beggs 2003, Beggs 2012].

**Contested or conditional.**
- The strong edge-of-chaos thesis (that systems *self-organise* to a critical boundary) is not supported by the CA replication literature [Mitchell 1993]; the weaker thesis (that certain capacity metrics peak at dynamical transitions in specific model classes) is supported [Bertschinger 2004].
- Cortical criticality is empirically suggestive [Beggs 2003] but methodologically contested [Beggs 2012]; neutral null models reproduce much of the phenomenology [Martinello 2017].
- Genuine SOC (scale-free behaviour without a tuning parameter) is narrower than the Bak 1996 programme claimed; most apparent SOC is an absorbing-phase transition driven by separation of timescales [Dickman 1998, 2000; Watkins 2016].
- The status of power-law statistics as evidence of criticality is weak because Griffiths phases and neutral models generate similar phenomenology over broad parameter ranges [Moretti 2013, Martinello 2017].
- The relation between Sompolinsky-Lyapunov edge-of-chaos and avalanche-criticality is only guaranteed in mean field; in finite systems with absorbing states they can be distinct [di Santo 2018].

**Methodologically fragile.**
- Exponent estimates from fewer than 2-3 decades of scaling are not reliable and cannot distinguish power-law from lognormal [Clauset 2009].
- Any claim based solely on marginal P(s) or P(t) fits, without the scaling-relation cross-check, is an incomplete universality statement [Sethna 2001].
- "Computational capacity peaks at edge of chaos" metrics are often task-, readout- and input-distribution-dependent and do not directly translate between model classes.
- Spectral-radius-based edge-of-chaos diagnostics in *trained* networks do not have a rigorous theoretical backing comparable to DMFT for random networks — direct extension from Sompolinsky 1988 to trained transformers is an assumption, not a theorem.
- Synthetic data or heavily pre-processed activation statistics can bias heavy-tail estimation; the pipeline choice (event definition, avalanche boundary, threshold sensitivity) often determines the answer, and sensitivity analyses are rarely reported.
- Threshold sensitivity of avalanche statistics is a major empirical failure mode; exponents should plateau across reasonable threshold ranges or the universality claim fails.
- Bootstrap assumes i.i.d. data; for the autocorrelated activations of a recurrent or autoregressive model, block-bootstrap or similar correlation-aware resampling is required.
- Neutral-model and Griffiths-phase null hypotheses are rarely tested in ML-criticality papers, despite being the most plausible alternatives to genuine criticality.
