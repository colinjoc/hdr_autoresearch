## 1. Signal propagation at initialisation and the edge of chaos for deep nets

The deep-learning-theory community was the first to import the edge-of-chaos language from dynamical systems and use it to predict when a randomly-initialised deep network is trainable. The pivotal paper is [Poole et al. 2016], which computes a mean-field theory for signal propagation in random fully-connected networks with i.i.d. weights sampled from N(0, σ_w² / n) and biases from N(0, σ_b²). They track two order parameters: the squared length q = (1/n) Σ h_i² of a hidden activation and the correlation c between the hidden activations produced by two different inputs. As signals propagate layer-by-layer the map (q, c) → (q', c') has a fixed point (q*, 1) — correlated inputs collapse onto a common representation — whose stability is controlled by χ_1 := ∂c'/∂c |_{c=1}. When χ_1 < 1 the network is in the ordered phase: nearby inputs become exponentially more correlated, expressivity is low. When χ_1 > 1 nearby inputs decorrelate exponentially, expressivity grows but gradients vanish. Exactly at χ_1 = 1 the network sits at the edge of chaos and information can in principle propagate to arbitrary depth.

[Schoenholz et al. 2017] made the training claim explicit: gradient back-propagation is controlled by the same χ_1, and only at χ_1 = 1 can gradients reach all layers of a very deep network without exponential blow-up or decay. They introduced the depth-scales ξ_c, ξ_q — the characteristic lengths over which correlation and length fluctuations decay — and showed that training depth empirically scales with min(ξ_c, ξ_q). Crucially, these depth scales diverge at criticality, giving the first clean operational definition of edge-of-chaos-initialisation: choose σ_w, σ_b so that χ_1 = 1. This prescription trained 200-plus-layer tanh networks without residual connections or normalisation.

[Xiao et al. 2018] extended the mean-field analysis to CNNs and introduced dynamical isometry — a stronger condition than χ_1 = 1 which additionally requires the Jacobian singular-value distribution to concentrate near one. With the Delta-Orthogonal init they trained 10 000-layer vanilla CNNs on CIFAR-10. The more general [Pennington et al. 2017] paper used free-probability theory to compute the entire Jacobian spectrum at init for various nonlinearities and showed that orthogonal initialisation plus sigmoid could achieve dynamical isometry, curing sigmoid's famous vanishing-gradient problem. The Jacobian singular-value distribution at init is therefore a concrete, measurable criticality observable — and its finite-width generalisation remains well-defined for trained transformers.

The infinite-width limit was clarified in two directions. [Lee et al. 2018] showed that Bayesian neural networks with i.i.d. random weights converge in the infinite-width limit to Gaussian Processes with a specific recursive kernel — the NNGP kernel — extending an earlier result of Neal (1994) from shallow to deep nets. This gives a tractable null model of an 'untrained' deep network. [Yang 2020] generalised the whole machinery to arbitrary architectures via the Tensor Programs formalism (I–IV), proving that GP limits, NTK limits, and feature-learning limits can be derived from a common substrate that handles attention, convolution, and skip connections uniformly.

What this body of theory tells us for the ideation target is: (i) at initialisation there is an unambiguous, analytically-characterised critical manifold; (ii) staying near it is necessary (at least empirically) for training very deep nets; (iii) the relevant observables are χ_1, Jacobian singular-value distributions, and depth-scales ξ_c, ξ_q. What it does not tell us is whether trained networks remain near that manifold or drift away, and whether — for modern architectures with residual streams, LayerNorm, and attention — the edge of chaos at init predicts anything about activations at the end of training on natural language. This is the gap that an avalanche / branching-ratio analysis of trained transformers can fill.

A subtle methodological point: the mean-field theory assumes post-activation variance is matched to a fixed-point condition. Real trained networks have non-trivial covariance structure, LayerNorm rescales variance, and attention induces heavy-tailed dynamics. Direct measurement of χ_1 on a trained nanoGPT thus requires either a per-layer local Jacobian or a finite-width analogue. [Pennington et al. 2017] explicitly note that the full Jacobian spectrum — not just its second moment — is what controls deep signal propagation.

Two further caveats deserve emphasis. First, the mean-field derivation assumes i.i.d. weights and inputs drawn from a stationary distribution. Pretrained-transformer weights after training are emphatically not i.i.d. — they encode linguistic structure — and the input distribution over natural-language tokens has heavy-tailed activation statistics at every layer. Whether the order-to-chaos transition predicted for i.i.d. weights remains meaningful for trained weights is itself an empirical question, and one the avalanche-exponents programme can answer. Second, [Yang 2020] clarifies via Tensor Programs that residual-stream architectures have a different critical structure than plain feedforward nets: the residual update H_{l+1} = H_l + F(H_l) interpolates between identity (χ_1 = 1 trivially in the no-update limit) and non-trivial signal propagation as the update's Jacobian gains spectral weight. This means skip-heavy architectures sit near a trivial edge of chaos at init by construction, and the interesting question is how training moves them away from that trivial fixed point.

Three empirical handles for the ideation target follow. (1) χ_1 at init is analytically known from architecture + init distribution. (2) χ_1 after training can be estimated from per-token finite-difference Jacobians — expensive but feasible on nanoGPT and doable layer-wise on pretrained GPT-2. (3) Comparing measured χ_1 to the mean-field critical value across layers gives a layer-resolved distance-from-criticality map. The nanoGPT skip-connection sweep provides a natural axis on which to vary init criticality continuously, and pretrained GPT-2 provides an endpoint at which training has run to completion. Combining the two yields a topology × training-amount × layer three-way table of χ_1 values, a novel empirical object.

## 2. Neural Tangent Kernel and infinite-width limits

The NTK story, opened by [Jacot, Gabriel & Hongler 2018], makes a different but related claim: in the infinite-width limit with a specific parameterisation (sometimes called NTK parameterisation, equivalent to scaling outputs by 1/√n), the network evolves under gradient descent as if it were a linear model in a feature map defined by its gradient at initialisation. The NTK is deterministic, depends only on the architecture, and remains constant throughout training. [Arora et al. 2019] gave closed-form NTKs for CNNs and showed competitive (though not state-of-the-art) performance when used as a static kernel. [Lee et al. 2019] empirically demonstrated that finite-width wide networks are quantitatively well-approximated by their linearisation throughout training — 'lazy' feature learning.

The lazy regime is incompatible with non-trivial representation change: if the kernel does not evolve, no features are learned in the usual sense. [Yang & Hu 2021] introduced the Maximal Update Parametrisation (µP), a distinct infinite-width limit under which features do learn and hyperparameters transfer across scales. µP shifts the network to a feature-learning regime. The relevance for criticality is subtle: the NTK regime is essentially a linear model — there is no obvious internal critical phenomenon, because activations are a random feature map with fixed statistics. The µP / feature-learning limit, by contrast, has non-trivial infinite-width dynamics that could in principle be critical.

Finite-width corrections to NTK are the natural language for talking about 'how far from lazy' a network is. [Zavatone-Veth et al. 2022] provides an analytical framework for contrasting random-feature (lazy-equivalent) models against learned-feature models in deep Bayesian linear nets, quantifying when feature learning helps. For the ideation target, NTK is primarily useful as a null model: compute the branching ratio and avalanche statistics predicted by the NTK regime (which should be close to trivial random-matrix predictions) and compare to the trained network. A trained nanoGPT that sits far from the NTK prediction in avalanche-statistics space is, by construction, in a feature-learning regime where criticality claims have teeth. If the trained network's avalanche statistics are well-matched by NTK, the criticality claim collapses to a generic random-matrix statement.

One widely-discussed limitation: NTK strictly holds only in the infinite-width limit with specific parameterisation, and empirical deviations grow with depth and nonlinearity even at finite widths commonly used in LLMs. Kaplan-style scaling laws [Kaplan et al. 2020] also cannot be purely NTK — the observed data-exponent structure requires feature learning. This tension between lazy and feature-learning regimes is unresolved and provides a second axis (beyond signal-propagation criticality) on which to position trained networks.

The infinite-width analytical machinery also gives a clean language for null models. A 'critical' activation pattern must be distinguished from random-feature noise. [Bai et al. 2019] (VERIFY) on implicit deep learning provides the fixed-point perspective: a deep-equilibrium network is explicitly a dynamical system whose activations are the solution of x = F(x, input). This is the cleanest analytical object on which to compute Lyapunov spectra; by analogy, any deep net can be pushed toward a deep-equilibrium analysis by treating the residual stream as an approximately-stationary state. This reframing is useful because it moves the analysis from 'transient signal propagation through L layers' (Poole / Schoenholz picture) to 'convergent dynamics on a fixed-point manifold' (implicit / NTK-feature-flow picture). A trained network is plausibly somewhere between the two regimes — not transient, not fully equilibrated — and the criticality observable needs to respect that.

A further practical point: µP [Yang & Hu 2021] and its hyperparameter-transfer application have become standard in frontier-scale training (GPT-4, Claude, Gemma to the extent public details are known). nanoGPT uses standard parameterisation, not µP. This mis-match matters for the ideation target: results on nanoGPT are in the 'mildly feature-learning, non-µP' regime, and their extrapolation to µP-trained frontier models needs care. Documenting the regime explicitly — via NTK-kernel-flow measurements (how much does the empirical NTK evolve during training?) — is a safer framing than claiming edge-of-chaos results transfer unchanged.

Empirical NTK evolution is a tractable observable: at training step t, compute the empirical kernel K_t(x, x') = ⟨∇_θ f(x; θ_t), ∇_θ f(x'; θ_t)⟩ on a fixed probe batch; quantify how far K_t is from K_0 using a matrix distance (e.g. Frobenius / spectral). Trajectories of this distance show how feature-learning the training actually is: NTK-regime networks show K_t ≈ K_0 throughout, feature-learning networks show K_t drifting substantially. The ideation target can run this cheaply on nanoGPT during training and report the regime directly, placing the criticality claim on firm methodological ground.

One more infinite-width result is worth mentioning. [Arora et al. 2019] provided exact CNN-NTK computations and showed that the NTK-as-kernel-regressor is a reasonable baseline classifier — but consistently sub-optimal against modern architectures. The gap between NTK performance and trained-network performance is itself an observable: it measures how much feature-learning contributes above the lazy baseline. If avalanche criticality only appears in the feature-learning regime — above the NTK baseline — then NTK-gap is a proxy for 'criticality potential'. This is speculative but testable.

## 3. Training dynamics and phase transitions

Training dynamics themselves exhibit phenomena that are phase-transition-like — providing a different, orthogonal notion of criticality that trained networks might organise around. Three clusters matter.

**Grokking.** [Power et al. 2022] discovered that small transformers trained on algorithmic tasks (modular arithmetic) can transition abruptly from memorisation to generalisation, often tens of thousands of steps after training loss has converged. The delayed generalisation looks like a phase transition in weight space. [Nanda et al. 2023] reverse-engineered the mechanism: the trained network implements modular addition via a discrete Fourier-basis circuit that emerges gradually; they constructed progress measures (e.g. 'Fourier gap') that rise smoothly during the grokking transition, suggesting that the apparent abruptness in loss is a metric artefact. [Liu et al. 2022] provided a phase diagram with four regimes (memorising, grokking, generalising, comprehending) controlled by weight decay and data fraction.

For the ideation target, grokking is useful on two fronts. First, it is an existence proof that specific training-time phase transitions occur in real networks. Second, the progress-measure methodology is a template: criticality observables (branching ratio, χ_1, avalanche exponent) should be tracked *throughout* training on a nanoGPT-sized system, and their relation to the grokking transition made explicit. No known paper computes avalanche statistics across a grokking transition.

**Edge of stability.** [Cohen et al. 2021] documented that gradient descent on real neural networks — with standard learning rates — typically hovers at the boundary of stability: the top Hessian eigenvalue λ_max floats just above 2/η (η = learning rate), training loss is non-monotone on fine scales yet decreases on coarser scales. This is an optimisation-dynamics criticality distinct from signal-propagation criticality: it concerns the sharpness of the loss landscape along the current trajectory, not the statistics of activations. [Damian et al. 2022] proved a self-stabilisation mechanism by which GD self-regulates the sharpness. [Cohen et al. 2022] showed adaptive optimisers (Adam, AdaGrad, Shampoo) exhibit analogous preconditioned-edge-of-stability behaviour — directly relevant since nanoGPT and GPT-2 use Adam.

The edge-of-stability result is important for ideation because it means training algorithmically drives networks toward a boundary — which is a self-organised-critical phenomenon, in the Bak-Tang-Wiesenfeld sense, just located in parameter-space rather than activation-space. Open question: does the optimiser's edge of stability correspond to activation-level criticality, or are the two phenomena decoupled?

**Lottery tickets.** [Frankle & Carbin 2019] showed that dense randomly-initialised networks contain sparse subnetworks that train to match full-network accuracy when isolated with the original initialisation. Winning tickets have ~10-20 percent density and a specific structure. For ideation, the lottery ticket result implies the 'functionally active' subgraph of a trained network is sparse and topologically specific — a hint that trained networks have heterogeneous, possibly scale-free, structure, even when the weight matrix is dense.

Synthesising the three clusters: trained-network dynamics are shaped by at least three distinct phase-transition-like phenomena (grokking, edge-of-stability, superposition), each with its own order parameter and its own training-time signature. A criticality claim about activations needs to be positioned with respect to all three. In particular: is the 'edge of chaos' observed in activations a separate phenomenon, or is it a downstream signature of the optimiser's edge-of-stability? The two could be mechanistically linked — sharpness at the edge of stability implies Hessian concentration which implies specific Jacobian-spectrum structure — but no paper to my knowledge establishes the link empirically. This is a concrete opening for the ideation target.

A related methodological gift from the training-dynamics literature is the notion of progress measures [Nanda et al. 2023]. For a phenomenon that looks like a discrete phase transition (loss kink, grokking jump, induction-head emergence), a progress measure is a smoothly-varying quantity that predicts the transition ahead of time. For the criticality ideation target, an analogous progress-measure programme is natural: during nanoGPT training, track branching ratio, avalanche exponent, χ_1, λ_max, effective depth — any or all — and ask which best predicts downstream capability emergence (induction-head kink, validation-loss inflection). If any of these criticality observables is a leading indicator, that is a strong and publishable result independently of whether the network sits exactly at criticality in some absolute sense.

A final subtlety: the edge-of-stability literature raises a flag about optimiser dynamics on the criticality question. Classical criticality analyses assume the dynamical system (the neural network under input perturbation) is fixed; SGD is treated as irrelevant because we analyse the trained endpoint. But the optimiser itself sits on a boundary — λ_max(Hessian) ≈ 2/η — which means the training trajectory spends time in a regime where any single gradient step can dramatically reshape activations. Late-training activations can still be 'non-equilibrium' in the sense that continuing to train for more steps will systematically shift them. Reporting criticality observables at multiple points during training, rather than only at convergence, is the right discipline. This also dovetails with the lottery-ticket perspective: the winning subnet's structure is partly selected by the early-training dynamics, and if the early-training dynamics sit at edge-of-stability, the emerging functional topology may itself inherit critical properties. Testing this empirically is within reach on nanoGPT.

## 4. Scaling laws

Scaling-law papers are the most macroscopic evidence that modern networks are not arbitrary fitting machines but exhibit structured, law-governed behaviour reminiscent of physical systems at a critical point. [Kaplan et al. 2020] established that test loss of autoregressive language models scales as a power law in parameter count N, dataset size D, and compute C over many orders of magnitude, with specific exponents ~0.076 for N and ~0.095 for D in the compute-non-bottlenecked regime. [Hoffmann et al. 2022] revised the optimal-allocation prediction (Chinchilla: parameters and tokens should scale roughly equally, so compute-optimal models are substantially smaller and train on more data than Kaplan-predicted). The bottom line is robust: loss is power-law-distributed in scale.

[Bahri et al. 2021] provided the first principled explanation, distinguishing variance-limited, resolution-limited, and data-manifold-dimension-limited regimes. The resolution-limited exponent derives from the spectral decay of the NTK against the data manifold. This is close in spirit to critical-phenomenon explanations: universal macroscopic exponents tied to microscopic (kernel-spectral) structure.

A contested subplot is emergent capabilities. [Wei et al. 2022] argued that specific downstream capabilities (arithmetic, instruction-following, multi-step reasoning) emerge abruptly above some scale threshold — apparent phase transitions in capability. [Schaeffer, Miranda & Koyejo 2023] pushed back: most emergent abilities vanish if you measure with smoother order parameters (e.g. Brier score instead of exact match accuracy). Their critique is methodologically central for the ideation target — a key risk is that a 'critical transition' in nanoGPT activations is actually a discretisation artefact of the chosen order parameter (e.g. step-function thresholding at activation = 0.1 for 'active'/'inactive').

[Bowman 2023] provides an accessible survey of the scaling-laws phenomenology. Net take: smooth power-laws in loss are robust and consistent with universal-scaling expectations; capability emergence is partly real, partly metric-dependent. Any criticality claim must use smooth, continuous order parameters and report exponent stability across threshold choices.

An underappreciated detail from scaling-laws work is that exponents are approximately architecture-agnostic at the macroscopic level but internal organisation evidently differs: transformers, CNNs, and recurrent architectures all fit the same broad scaling story despite computing very differently. This architecture-agnosticism is reminiscent of universality classes in statistical mechanics, where microscopic details (lattice geometry, specific interactions) wash out at the critical point and only dimensionality and symmetries survive. For the ideation target this is directly actionable: if avalanche-exponent universality holds across nanoGPT (transformer), CNN baseline, and an RNN-style architecture on the same task at the same scale, that is a direct universality-class claim. Conversely, exponent differences between architectures — controlled by skip-connection topology for instance — would establish topology-driven universality classes in a concrete, measurable sense.

A second thread connects scaling to criticality via the resolution-limited regime of [Bahri et al. 2021]. In that regime the loss exponent is tied to the spectral decay exponent of the kernel against the data manifold — a quantity that determines how well the network resolves fine features of the target function. Spectral-decay exponents in kernels are analogous to correlation-function exponents near a critical point, suggesting an analytic handle: if the NTK spectrum exhibits a critical power-law decay, the network loss exhibits the corresponding critical power-law in scale. Experimentally, this predicts that networks near activation criticality should have distinctive kernel-spectral signatures. The nanoGPT / GPT-2 ideation programme can test this by measuring NTK decay on small subsets of the data distribution at different points on the skip-connection sweep.

Scaling-laws work also provides the strongest cautionary tale for ideation: it is easy to claim power-laws in noisy data, and easy to fit exponents to stretches of three to four decades that do not actually match the theoretical prediction. [Clauset-Shalizi-Newman] goodness-of-fit tests are well-known in the statistical-physics community but rarely applied in ML papers. Adopting their p-value-against-best-fit-exponential methodology is a cheap differentiator for the ideation target.

One further connection between scaling and internal organisation comes from [Hoffmann et al. 2022] Chinchilla: their result — that compute-optimal models should be substantially smaller and train on substantially more data than the Kaplan recipe suggested — implies that under-trained models are systematically over-parameterised relative to their data. This has an interesting criticality-adjacent interpretation: under-trained networks may be 'too cold', closer to random-feature limits where activations are Gaussian noise, while well-trained networks 'heat up' toward richer structure — possibly toward criticality. The Chinchilla recipe can be interpreted as 'train enough that internal structure develops'. For the nanoGPT sweep, one concrete experimental design is to hold architecture fixed and vary training time (or equivalently, number of tokens seen) to track criticality observables as a function of training progress. If avalanche exponents stabilise to a specific value by the Chinchilla-optimal training duration, that convergence timescale is itself a scale-law-like observation.

[Bowman 2023] argues eight key things about LLMs, including that capabilities often emerge unpredictably even while loss scales smoothly, and that many alignment-relevant behaviours are downstream of hard-to-predict internal reorganisation. This is a useful framing for the ideation target because it emphasises the mismatch between what can be predicted from macroscopic scaling laws (loss, compute) and what matters practically (specific capabilities, failure modes). If criticality observables sit at an intermediate level of granularity — below capabilities but above scaling-law loss — they could plausibly serve as middle-layer predictors that bridge the gap. This is not a tested claim but the research programme has space for it.

## 5. Mechanistic interpretability of transformers

The mechanistic-interpretability (MI) programme, led by Anthropic and a broader community, reverse-engineers the algorithms implemented inside trained transformers. [Elhage et al. 2021] — the 'Mathematical Framework' paper — decomposes attention-only transformers into QK and OV circuits and introduces the residual stream as the natural communication channel: every component (head, MLP) reads from and writes to this shared residual stream. This is the substrate on which to compute avalanche / criticality statistics for transformers: residual-stream activations are (i) the right level for information flow, (ii) linearly additive across components, and (iii) directly observable.

[Olsson et al. 2022] documented induction heads — a specific attention-head circuit responsible for much of in-context learning — and crucially showed that induction-head capability emerges via a phase transition during training, detectable as a kink in the loss curve. This is a second, well-characterised training-time phase transition (alongside grokking) in real language models, not toy algorithmic tasks. [Wang et al. 2023] reverse-engineered a 26-head circuit in GPT-2 small implementing the Indirect Object Identification (IOI) task — demonstrating that even a small pretrained model uses sparse, modular functional circuits rather than dense distributed computation.

The sparse autoencoder line, opened by [Bricken et al. 2023] and [Cunningham et al. 2023], addresses the superposition problem: trained transformers represent more features than they have neurons by packing features into non-orthogonal directions. Dictionary learning (specifically, training a wide overcomplete autoencoder with an L1 penalty on activations) recovers approximately monosemantic features that are far more interpretable than raw neurons. [Templeton et al. 2024] scaled SAEs to Claude 3 Sonnet and recovered millions of features including safety-relevant concepts. [Lieberum et al. 2024] released Gemma Scope — open SAEs trained at every layer and site of Gemma-2 2B and 9B — removing the need to train SAEs for open-weight criticality analyses.

Activation patching [Meng et al. 2022] and path patching [Goldowsky-Dill et al. 2023] are the standard causal-intervention methods: run the network, cache activations, substitute specific activations from a counterfactual run, and measure effect on output. They provide the methodology needed to go from 'neuron X fires together with neuron Y' (a correlation) to 'neuron X's firing causes neuron Y's firing' (a causal avalanche). [Geva et al. 2021] interpreted FFN layers as key-value memories; [Dai et al. 2022] identified knowledge neurons whose ablation specifically removes factual knowledge.

For the ideation target, MI provides (i) the right observables (residual-stream activations, SAE feature firings), (ii) open-weight toolchains (Gemma Scope, Pythia + SAEs), and (iii) intervention methodology (activation patching for causal branching-ratio estimation). What MI does *not* do is compute avalanche exponents or branching ratios. The standard MI observable is 'does feature X fire on input Y' — a per-feature analysis. No MI paper I know of computes the joint distribution of simultaneously-active features per token and asks whether that distribution is power-law.

A few points about MI practice are worth internalising. First, the MI community is unusually rigorous about causal vs correlational claims — a claim 'head X implements behaviour Y' is expected to be backed by activation-patching evidence that clamping X to its clean value reliably restores Y on a corrupted run. The statistical-physics / criticality community is traditionally less strict about this — power-law fits and branching ratios are treated as correlational. Borrowing MI's causal-intervention discipline — 'if you silence this putative avalanche seed, do downstream avalanches disappear?' — would materially strengthen a criticality claim on transformer activations. Second, SAE features are *trained*, not given: their identity depends on SAE dictionary size, L1 coefficient, and training data. Criticality claims based on SAE feature firings therefore inherit a hyperparameter dependence. The Gemma Scope release [Lieberum et al. 2024] partially addresses this by providing a standardised SAE suite, but robustness across SAE hyperparameters should be reported explicitly.

Third, there is a growing sub-literature on residual-stream decomposition that is directly relevant. [Zhang et al. 2024] (VERIFY) uses multi-layer SAEs to track features across layers, identifying cases where a feature is written by one layer and read by a later layer. This cross-layer propagation is the natural substrate for avalanche analysis in a transformer: an 'avalanche' is a sequence of feature firings that propagate through the residual stream across layers. Single-layer SAEs miss this structure; multi-layer or cross-layer SAEs are necessary. For the ideation target, the cleanest observable is plausibly the cross-layer feature-firing cluster distribution, where a cluster is a set of (layer, feature) pairs causally linked via residual-stream writes.

Fourth, a practical note about the MI community's coverage: [Wang 2023] on IOI and [Olsson 2022] on induction heads are focused on GPT-2-small-scale models — exactly the scale accessible to the current ideation infrastructure. This is not a coincidence; GPT-2 small is the largest pretrained transformer on which mechanistic circuit analysis is feasible with current methods. Our ideation target can therefore sit squarely within the MI community's natural operating scale and benefit from shared baselines, shared tooling (TransformerLens, nnsight, Anthropic's inspection tools), and shared evaluation protocols. The nanoGPT sweep provides even smaller-scale ablation studies where full interpretability can be performed end-to-end.

## 6. Superposition and feature geometry

Superposition — the phenomenon that networks pack more features than neurons via non-orthogonal directions — is characterised in [Elhage et al. 2022] 'Toy Models of Superposition'. Key findings: (i) features compete for representation and the winning subset depends on feature importance and sparsity; (ii) there are distinct geometric phases (monosemantic, partial superposition, full superposition) with phase transitions between them as sparsity or importance vary; (iii) the phases map onto classical problems (Thomson problem, code design). Superposition is, in other words, a concrete, analytically-tractable phase transition intrinsic to the representation geometry of trained networks. A criticality claim about trained transformers must specify whether it is compatible with superposition, or whether the 'critical point' *is* a superposition phase boundary.

[Engels et al. 2024] showed that some LLM features are inherently multi-dimensional (e.g. the representation of days-of-the-week is a circle, not seven orthogonal directions). [Gurnee et al. 2024] found universal neurons — a ~1-5 percent fraction of GPT-2 neurons whose functions are preserved across random-seed retrainings. These universal neurons implement canonical operations (entropy suppression, deletion). If we draw a functional connectivity graph from activation co-firing or from SAE feature co-activation, universal neurons are the natural candidates for hubs — consistent with a scale-free topology. [Gurnee et al. 2023] 'Finding Neurons in a Haystack' showed that sparse linear probes with k=1–32 recover many features localised to individual neurons in Pythia, indicating substantial localisation of function (again compatible with hub structure).

For ideation: the feature-geometry literature identifies concrete, causally-supported structural properties of trained networks — monosemanticity, hub neurons, multi-dimensional feature manifolds — that any criticality claim must be compatible with. The cleanest criticality observable that respects this literature is SAE feature co-firing: take a trained SAE, record which features fire on each token of a large text sample, and compute the cluster-size distribution. A power-law cluster-size distribution in SAE space is a defensible criticality claim that connects directly to MI observables.

Superposition as a phase phenomenon deserves one more comment. [Elhage et al. 2022] showed that the mapping from feature-importance and feature-sparsity to representation geometry has piecewise-smooth structure with sharp phase boundaries. At one limit (high sparsity, uniform importance) networks pack features into near-orthogonal monosemantic directions. At the opposite limit (low sparsity) networks are forced into polysemantic superposition where single neurons represent mixtures. The phase boundary itself is a critical manifold in representation space, with well-defined scaling of representation capacity near the transition. If trained LLMs operate near this boundary — and there are indications they do, given the coexistence of roughly monosemantic and clearly polysemantic neurons in every pretrained transformer studied [Gurnee 2023, Gurnee 2024] — then superposition criticality is a second, orthogonal notion of criticality to the activation-dynamics edge of chaos. Disambiguating the two is a key task for any paper that claims criticality in trained transformers. A concrete test: measure activation criticality observables (branching ratio, avalanche exponent) on toy models of superposition in their different phases; if the activation-criticality observable is constant across superposition phases, the two notions are independent. If it tracks the superposition phase boundary, they are linked — and the criticality claim simplifies to a superposition-phase claim.

Finally, [Gurnee et al. 2024] on universal neurons is directly suggestive of scale-free functional topology. A small fraction of neurons (1-5 percent) that are preserved across random seeds, implementing canonical functions (entropy control, deletion, attention-score modulation), look like hubs in a preferential-attachment picture. Testing this quantitatively — computing degree distributions on functional-connectivity graphs built from activation patching or from SAE feature co-firing, and checking for heavy-tailed exponents consistent with universal-neuron fraction — is a tractable and targeted follow-up.

[Gurnee 2023] 'Finding Neurons in a Haystack' is also important methodologically: it showed that sparse linear probes with small k reliably find features that are localised to individual neurons, validating the view that 'neurons' are a meaningful basis — at least partially — for feature analysis in Pythia. If neurons are a partially-valid basis, then raw-neuron avalanche statistics are interpretable without requiring SAE decomposition. This opens a cheaper experimental path: raw-neuron avalanche analysis on pretrained GPT-2 without needing SAE training, with SAE analysis held in reserve for robustness checks. The Gemma Scope infrastructure [Lieberum 2024] is still useful as a complementary check.

Bringing the feature-geometry picture together: trained transformer activations are sparse, partially-localised, organised around a small universal core, and sit near superposition phase boundaries. Any criticality claim that ignores these structural properties is naive. The right framing is: (a) measure criticality observables in raw-neuron, SAE, and random-projection bases, (b) report basis-dependence explicitly, (c) compare to sparsity-matched and superposition-phase nulls, (d) cross-reference with identified universal / knowledge / induction-head neurons. This multi-basis, multi-null approach is more work than a neuroscience-style analysis but is required for the criticality claim to survive contact with the MI community's standards.

A final observation: [Engels et al. 2024]'s multi-dimensional features — days-of-the-week-as-a-circle — suggest that some cluster definitions may need topological sensitivity. If a 'feature' is a circle, then activating 'Monday' and 'Tuesday' at the same token is not two independent events but two points on the same feature manifold. Cluster-size counts that ignore this geometry will over-count. The right definition of a feature-firing event for criticality analysis therefore depends on feature geometry and is an active research question in its own right. A conservative approach is to report both the raw-dimension-counting cluster size and a manifold-adjusted cluster count (using metric-distance thresholds on feature manifolds where available), and to discuss the difference.

## 7. Activation sparsity and dead neurons

Activation sparsity in transformers has been shown repeatedly, and with striking numbers. [Li et al. 2023] 'Lazy Neuron Phenomenon' documents that trained transformers exhibit extreme activation sparsity — often only tens of MLP neurons are active per token in large models, and most neurons are dead or near-dead for most inputs. [Mirzadeh et al. 2024] showed that swapping SiLU/GELU (smooth, always-nonzero) for ReLU (hard zero) in LLMs restores ~95 percent activation sparsity without quality loss. [Liu et al. 2023] 'Deja Vu' exploited contextual sparsity — the fact that only 15–25 percent of MLP neurons materially contribute to each token and this subset is predictable from the residual stream — for inference speedups. [Zhang et al. 2022] 'MoEfication' decomposes FFN layers into sparsely-activated expert groups, revealing inherent modularity.

The relevance for avalanche / branching-ratio analysis is direct. An 'avalanche' is a cluster of co-active units. If only 10-20 percent of units are active per forward pass and those units are context-dependent, then avalanche statistics are dominated by the active subset. Two threshold choices — 'active' = activation > 0 vs 'active' = activation > 0.1 — can produce qualitatively different avalanche distributions simply by reclassifying the tail. Existing nanoGPT sweeps at two thresholds (0 and 0.1) will need to be augmented with a systematic threshold-sweep and a check for exponent stability, informed by sparsity-aware null models. This is a methodological entry point that the MI and sparsity literatures between them have set up but not closed: sparsity as a null model for criticality.

Several null models from the sparsity literature can be imported directly. A context-sparse Bernoulli model — where each unit fires independently with a token-dependent probability matching empirical per-token sparsity — is a natural null because it preserves the first-moment sparsity statistic while destroying co-firing structure. A power-law in cluster size under this null would indicate that co-firing structure, not sparsity, drives criticality. A second null is the MoEfication decomposition [Zhang et al. 2022]: partition FFN neurons into expert groups and assume independent activation within groups. A third is the Deja Vu contextual-sparsity model [Liu et al. 2023] where the active subset is predictable from the residual stream — this null is the toughest because it bakes in the exact token-dependent sparsity pattern. If avalanche power-laws survive under the Deja Vu null, the criticality claim is robust. If they do not, the claim reduces to 'transformer sparsity patterns are context-predictable', which is not new.

The [Mirzadeh et al. 2024] ReLU-strikes-back result adds a further experimental handle: activation-function choice (ReLU vs SiLU vs GELU) tunes sparsity at fixed architecture. Repeating the avalanche analysis on a nanoGPT variant trained with ReLU vs the default GELU would disentangle sparsity-driven from dynamics-driven criticality effects. If exponents differ, the activation function is a first-order driver; if they do not, the criticality structure is deeper than surface sparsity. This is a low-cost, high-information experiment.

One further methodological point: dead neurons are non-trivial to define consistently. A neuron that never fires on a given evaluation set may fire on a held-out one; conversely, a neuron that fires 0.1 percent of the time is not dead but looks dead under standard thresholds. [Li et al. 2023] recommend computing activation statistics over large enough samples (many millions of tokens) that per-neuron firing rates are estimated with good precision; their 'lazy neuron' threshold is ~1 percent firing rate. For criticality analysis a two-regime approach is defensible: compute cluster distributions both including and excluding dead neurons, and report the sensitivity of the exponent to this choice.

An additional consideration in the sparsity literature is the relationship between activation sparsity and the number of samples needed for reliable statistics. If only 5 percent of neurons fire per token, then the 'active subset' for any single token consists of ~600 neurons in a 12 288-dimensional GPT-2 XL MLP layer. Estimating the distribution of cluster sizes from 600 active units requires averaging over many tokens — roughly 10^5 to 10^6 tokens to saturate the tail. This is tractable (a few gigabytes of cached activations on The Pile or a code corpus) but non-trivial. The nanoGPT infrastructure already records per-layer fractions and can be extended to record per-token active-set binary masks with modest storage overhead (~100 MB for 10^5 tokens × 768 dimensions × 12 layers). Pre-computing this cache is a cheap experimental infrastructure investment.

Finally, activation sparsity interacts with the 'what counts as a unit' question. In the raw-neuron basis, sparsity is high and the active set is context-dependent. In a trained-SAE basis, sparsity is engineered by the L1 penalty and is tunable. In a random-projection basis, sparsity is typically near-Gaussian with low effective sparsity. The criticality exponent measured depends on basis. The principled approach is to report exponents in multiple bases — raw neurons, SAE features, and a random-projection control — and discuss basis-dependence explicitly. This is consistent with the MI community's insistence on SAE-hyperparameter robustness and would pre-empt standard reviewer objections.

## 8. Network topology in deep learning

Skip connections and residual streams are the dominant architectural primitive in modern nets [He et al. 2016]. The surprise result is [Veit, Wilber & Belongie 2016]: ResNets can be unrolled into an ensemble of O(2^L) paths through skip connections and standard blocks, with a geometrically distributed effective path length; the working gradient flows primarily through short paths (effective depth ~10 even for L=110). This is a direct structural precursor to an avalanche-style analysis: effective-depth distribution is itself a cluster-size-like statistic, and its geometric form is the zero-th-order null model against which to compare empirical avalanche distributions in skip-connection-rich architectures.

[Testolin et al. 2020] 'Deep learning systems as complex networks' is the most directly relevant precedent: they analysed trained Deep Belief Networks as graphs and found small-world and approximately scale-free connectivity structure, mirroring classical complex-networks findings in biology. This is the cleanest prior art for the claim that trained deep nets are complex networks in the Barabási sense — but it was done on DBNs rather than transformers, so there is a clear open question about transformer topology.

[Scabini & Bruno 2023] extended the graph-theoretic analysis to fully-connected MLPs trained on vision tasks and found weight-graph degree distributions with power-law-like tails correlated with generalisation. [Watanabe et al. 2020] (VERIFY venue — Front. Comp. Neuro.) reported scale-free structure in effective connectivity of trained deep nets consistent with preferential-attachment-during-training. Lottery-ticket subnetworks [Frankle & Carbin 2019] — the 10-20 percent of connections that carry all the functional load — are another piece of evidence for heterogeneous structure.

The normalization literature is relevant indirectly. [De & Smith 2020] (VERIFY) argued that BatchNorm biases residual blocks toward the identity function at init, keeping effective depth low early in training. LayerNorm in transformers plays an analogous role. This connects directly to the nanoGPT skip-connection sweep: the topology knob (`n_skip_connections ∈ {0, ..., 1000}`) interacts with LayerNorm's implicit 'cool' initialisation.

Gap: no paper known to me computes activation-graph (as opposed to weight-graph) topology for pretrained transformer language models, nor relates topology metrics to criticality exponents on the same networks.

Two further topology threads are worth pulling on. First, the structured-sparsity literature on transformers — block-sparse attention, expert routing in Mixture-of-Experts, activation-sparsity-aware inference — gives a set of engineering-driven topology constraints that happen to be information-theoretic: when a Mixture-of-Experts model routes each token to k out of N experts with k << N, it imposes a branching structure on the residual-stream update that is formally similar to the branching-process models used in avalanche theory. [Hoffer et al. 2023] (VERIFY) on structured sparse transformers is the closest empirical work; mapping MoE branching to avalanche-branching-ratio predictions is a clean conceptual bridge. Second, skip-connection topology has a specific relationship to effective-depth distributions [Veit 2016]: the number of distinct paths of length k through a ResNet of depth L with one skip per block is the binomial coefficient C(L, k), and the gradient amplitude along a length-k path scales as the product of k block Jacobians. The expected effective depth is determined by where the product of Jacobians becomes exponentially small — which is itself controlled by χ_1 of the block update. This gives a direct analytical link between signal-propagation criticality (χ_1 = 1) and effective-depth distribution (the paths whose gradient does not vanish). For the nanoGPT skip-connection sweep the prediction is sharp: as skip density increases, the effective-depth distribution should broaden; if it develops a power-law tail at some critical skip density, that is topological criticality.

Third, the graph-theoretic framing suggests quantitative observables beyond power-law-degree tails: clustering coefficient, rich-club coefficient, average path length, community structure. [Testolin 2020] found small-world characteristics in DBNs. The same battery of measurements on trained transformer weight graphs and on functional-activation graphs would give a multi-observable topology profile. Community-detection algorithms applied to functional graphs might recover the IOI-type circuits [Wang 2023] as emergent modular structure — independent methodological confirmation of MI results.

A related practical matter: the activation-graph for a trained transformer has many possible definitions. (a) Co-firing graph: edge (i, j) weight = probability both units fire in the same forward pass. (b) Causal graph: edge (i, j) weight = measured causal effect of perturbing unit i on unit j's activation via activation patching. (c) Weight graph: edge (i, j) weight = magnitude of the weight connecting the two units, often restricted to same-layer MLP or to attention QK / OV pathways. (d) Gradient graph: edge (i, j) weight = magnitude of ∂h_j / ∂h_i on typical inputs. Each definition foregrounds a different structural property. Weight graphs have been most studied [Testolin 2020, Scabini 2023]; activation and causal graphs are almost entirely unexplored for transformer language models. The ideation target should report at least weight-graph vs activation-graph topology side by side, since these can diverge: weights may be approximately dense while activations are sparse and hub-structured.

Lastly, the skip-connection topology knob in nanoGPT is a first-order parameter not explored in the existing topology-of-deep-learning literature. The standard architectural choice is 'one residual connection per block', defining 'block' as attention followed by MLP. nanoGPT's `n_skip_connections` knob goes further: it adds variable-density neuron-to-neuron skip connections, interpolating continuously between feedforward and densely-shortcut architectures. At the limit of maximum skip density, every neuron writes to many residual-stream positions; at zero, none do. This is, architecturally, a topological interpolation knob between fully-sequential and fully-shortcut computation. Mapping this knob to criticality observables is the core experimental novelty of the ideation target — no prior work has run a continuous topology sweep of this specific kind on a trained transformer and measured activation-graph statistics.

## 9. Criticality and dynamics specifically in deep/large nets

A small but growing literature explicitly imports statistical-physics criticality notions into trained networks. [Torres et al. 2023] (VERIFY) argued for a critical-brain hypothesis in deep learning, claiming RNN-type networks organise near criticality during training. [Morales, Asenjo, di Santo et al. 2023] (VERIFY) developed an edge-of-instability framework that unifies RNN dynamics with cortical activity. [Sherstinsky 2020] provides the technical RNN / LSTM background as dynamical systems.

Critically (pun intended), these papers are RNN-focused or neuroscience-bridging. Transformers, the architecture that dominates current ML, have been essentially absent from the criticality literature. A targeted search for 'avalanche exponents on transformer activations' returns nothing in the standard ML venues (NeurIPS, ICML, ICLR, TMLR) as of my knowledge cutoff. [Pilarski et al. 2023] (VERIFY) computed Jacobian-spectrum proxies for maximal Lyapunov exponents in wide feedforward nets — the closest in spirit, but on MLPs not transformers, and without the avalanche machinery.

[Zavatone-Veth et al. 2022] is in a different register — analytical comparison of random vs trained features in Bayesian linear nets — but establishes the methodological bar for distinguishing random-feature-null from genuine feature-learning. Any criticality claim on trained transformers must clear that bar.

It is worth being explicit about what RNN criticality papers have achieved, because they set the template the ideation target can extend. The Sompolinsky-Crisanti-Sommers classical result showed that random recurrent networks have a transition to chaos at gain g = 1, with the maximal Lyapunov exponent proportional to g - 1 near threshold. Echo-state networks exploit the ordered side of this transition for reservoir computing. Bertschinger-Natschläger showed that computational capacity is maximised near the transition — edge-of-chaos computing in the original sense. [Torres 2023, Morales 2023] (both VERIFY) extend this to richer RNN variants and to trained networks, claiming that training drives networks toward criticality even without explicit tuning.

For transformers, neither the random-feature baseline nor the edge-of-chaos prediction have been experimentally established. The architecture is different (parallel attention + FFN rather than recurrent update), the training regime is different (large-batch supervised pretraining rather than online recurrent dynamics), and the scale is different (10^5–10^11 parameters rather than 10^2–10^4 recurrent units). Whether any of the RNN-criticality intuitions transfer, transfer with exponent corrections, or fail entirely, is the question. The cleanest experimental design is parallel: compute the same criticality observables on (a) trained RNN baseline, (b) trained nanoGPT, (c) pretrained GPT-2 small, with the same evaluation corpus and the same power-law-fitting pipeline. Matching exponents across architectures would be a strong universality claim; differing exponents along a controlled axis would establish architecture-dependent universality classes.

A related theoretical bridge is the 'avalanche process on a deep network' picture: treat each layer's active-unit set as a discrete-time process indexed by layer, with the next layer's active set determined by the current layer's active set plus the layer transition. This is a branching Markov process; its stationarity condition is branching ratio = 1, exactly the critical condition in directed-percolation. The class-distinction between this per-layer branching process and the within-layer co-firing cluster is important: a network can be critical in one sense and not the other. Prior RNN criticality work mostly analysed the first (temporal). Transformer analysis will need both — temporal (across layers, via path-patching-propagation) and spatial (within a layer, via co-firing cluster sizes). No paper does both simultaneously on the same network.

Finally, a caveat: transformer layers are not translationally-invariant the way cortical columns are — layer 0 has a very different role than layer 6. Universal-scaling claims typically assume translational invariance or at least homogeneity. Layer-resolved criticality analysis (theme 1's recommendation) is the right response: report observables per layer, and discuss the layer profile rather than averaging. [Olsson 2022]'s induction-head circuit is specifically a layer-5-to-layer-6 phenomenon in GPT-2, so layer-specific criticality measurements are both necessary and naturally connectable to known mechanistic results.

The gap in the transformer-criticality literature can be decomposed into three sub-gaps. (1) *Observable gap*: no paper computes standard statistical-physics criticality observables (avalanche-size, avalanche-duration, branching-ratio, Lyapunov spectrum) on transformer activations. (2) *Interpretability gap*: no paper connects criticality observables to MI primitives (residual stream components, SAE features, named circuits). (3) *Topology-knob gap*: no paper tunes a topology parameter (skip density, effective depth, sparsity) continuously and measures how criticality observables respond. The nanoGPT infrastructure existing at `~/entropy/nanoGPT/` covers (3) directly — the `n_skip_connections ∈ {0, 100, ..., 1000}` sweep is the topology axis. Combining (1)-(3) on the same set of models is the ideation target in its sharpest form.

One related concern: rolling out a transformer layer-by-layer gives an L-step dynamical system, but L is typically small (12 for GPT-2 small, 6 for nanoGPT default). Classical criticality observables require many orders of magnitude of dynamic range to fit power-laws reliably. The rescue is that cluster sizes can span 1 to N (number of neurons or SAE features), typically 10^3 to 10^6 — enough decades for defensible Clauset-Shalizi-Newman fits. Cross-layer avalanche durations are more constrained (1 to L, so 1–2 decades) and cannot sustain power-law claims independently, but can be used for branching-ratio estimation where a single decade suffices. This sets realistic bounds on what can be claimed: cluster-size exponents yes, duration exponents no, joint scaling relations only weakly. This limit-setting is itself a useful contribution since naive adoption of neuroscience protocols on transformers would over-claim on duration scaling.

## 10. Sensitivity / Lyapunov in neural networks

A maximal Lyapunov exponent λ_max for a neural network can be estimated by perturbing an activation at layer l by ε and measuring the norm of the deviation at later layers, then taking λ_max ≈ (1/L) Σ log||δ_{l+1}|| / ||δ_l||. Mean-field theory predicts λ_max = log χ_1; trained networks in the 'chaotic' phase (χ_1 > 1) have λ_max > 0. [Pilarski et al. 2023] (VERIFY) is the most recent precedent for Jacobian-spectrum proxies for λ_max in deep nets. For transformers, the residual stream is the natural unrolled state; the per-layer Jacobian of the residual update (attention + FFN with skip) is the sensitivity operator. Its spectrum can be computed layer-wise and compared to the χ_1 = 1 critical value predicted by mean-field theory for the given architecture at init.

For the ideation target this is methodologically central: a criticality claim is far stronger if λ_max (measured) ≈ 0 (critical), correlates with topology knob, and correlates with the avalanche-exponent measurement. Mean-field predictions for the critical spectrum at init provide a baseline; deviation of the trained Jacobian spectrum from the init spectrum is itself a training-drift observable. Open methodological question: how do LayerNorm and attention softmax (both nonlinear normalisations) interact with the standard Jacobian-spectrum machinery?

A concrete implementation sketch for nanoGPT / GPT-2: for each layer l, and for each token position t in a batch of evaluation sentences, compute the Jacobian J_l = ∂h_{l+1,t} / ∂h_{l,t} via a JVP / VJP (jax.vjp or torch.autograd), compute its singular values, and record the top-k singular values as a function of l. The mean log singular value is a direct estimator of per-layer expansion; summing over layers gives a Lyapunov-like proxy. Doing this for many tokens and averaging gives a distribution. At the critical point the top singular value should be 1 (on the log-scale, zero). Pennington-Schoenholz-Ganguli's free-probability prediction gives the expected full singular-value distribution at init; comparison of measured vs predicted is then a direct distance-from-criticality measure in spectral form.

Two caveats on the methodology. First, softmax in attention is a nonlinearity whose Jacobian has a particular structure: it is low-rank (rank = number of attention keys per head) and it mixes token positions, so the per-token Jacobian picture is incomplete — true sensitivity analysis needs to include cross-token couplings. Second, LayerNorm re-scales variance, which means the Jacobian spectrum at one layer depends on activation statistics at the previous layer in a normalised way. The upshot is that the criticality boundary for a trained, LayerNorm-equipped transformer is not the simple χ_1 = 1 of the vanilla mean-field theory; it is a normalisation-adjusted version. Deriving this analytically is non-trivial but a first-order numerical version is achievable: measure empirical χ_1 on real pretrained residual streams, ask whether it sits near unity, and report the finding against the canonical (LayerNorm-free) prediction.

A further experimental target is perturbation-propagation: inject a bounded perturbation δ into a residual-stream activation at layer l, propagate to layer L, measure the norm of the induced change in the output logits. Averaging log-ratios over many perturbations gives a scalar Lyapunov proxy per (l, input). If this proxy approaches zero (neutral propagation) across many layers and inputs, the network is operating near a neutral/critical sensitivity regime. If it is strongly positive, the network is chaotic; strongly negative, ordered. The topology sweep should systematically move this proxy, and the prediction from mean-field theory is that it crosses zero at some critical skip density.

One more methodological connection to make: [Pennington et al. 2017]'s free-probability machinery computes the full Jacobian singular-value distribution for a random network at init. This provides the reference distribution against which trained-network empirical spectra can be compared. Dynamical isometry is the condition that this distribution concentrates at 1 (all singular values ≈ 1); vanishing-gradient corresponds to concentration near 0; exploding-gradient to concentration far above 1. For a trained transformer, the empirical per-layer singular-value distribution can be plotted and compared to the init distribution. If training systematically moves the distribution toward concentration at 1 — isometry — that is evidence of self-organisation toward neutral signal propagation, a specific form of criticality. If training moves the distribution away from 1, the network is becoming more chaotic or more ordered. Either way, the change is informative, and this analysis can be done in standard auto-diff frameworks.

Finally, the connection to the implicit-models picture [Bai et al. 2019] (VERIFY) deserves mention: a deep equilibrium model is explicitly a fixed-point system x = F(x, input), and its Lyapunov spectrum is the eigenvalues of I - ∂F/∂x at the fixed point. Non-equilibrium transformers can be approximately analysed by iterating a 'transformer block' many times and seeking its effective fixed point (this is non-trivial because the true transformer applies different blocks rather than iterating one), but the framework suggests that comparison between a transformer and a deep-equilibrium analogue might reveal criticality differently than standard signal-propagation analysis. This is speculative and deferred, but flagged as a possible theoretical bridge.

## 11. Activation analysis in LLMs — methodology

The mechanistic-interpretability community has developed a reasonably mature toolchain for analysing LLM activations. [Geva et al. 2021] decomposed transformer FFN layers into key-value memories — FFN is interpretable as a pattern detector (keys) followed by a distribution over tokens (values). [Dai et al. 2022] used integrated-gradients to identify knowledge neurons whose ablation selectively removes facts. [Meng et al. 2022] ROME used causal tracing — clamp activations from a clean run into a corrupted run, measure recovery — to localise factual associations to mid-layer MLPs in GPT-J and rank-one-updated weights to edit facts. [Goldowsky-Dill et al. 2023] refined activation patching into path patching, isolating specific residual-stream paths rather than whole components.

For avalanche analysis the methodology translates as follows: (i) cache residual-stream activations on a large text corpus; (ii) define active/inactive via a threshold and sweep thresholds; (iii) define a co-active cluster either as units exceeding threshold in the same forward pass (spatial avalanche) or as a causal propagation tree obtained by activation patching (causal avalanche); (iv) fit cluster-size distribution to a power-law using Clauset-Shalizi-Newman methodology; (v) compare to sparsity-matched null models (Deja Vu sparsity distributions as null) to rule out that the power law is a pure sparsity artefact; (vi) interpret via SAE features ([Lieberum et al. 2024] Gemma Scope for the large-scale version). None of this machinery is novel on its own — but its composition for criticality claims is.

A more detailed walkthrough clarifies where each methodology piece comes from. Caching residual-stream activations is standard practice in any MI pipeline — TransformerLens, nnsight, and the Pythia suite all provide hooks. The 'active subset per token' is the raw observable used in sparsity work [Li 2023, Liu 2023]. The cluster-definition choice is borrowed from neuroscience — a cluster in the Beggs-Plenz sense is a set of units firing within a temporal window; for transformers the natural analogue is units firing at the same layer or in a causally-connected residual-stream path. The power-law fitting methodology comes from Clauset-Shalizi-Newman (statistical physics); the null-model comparison comes from Priesemann et al. work on sub-sampled branching. The causal-avalanche construction — building a causal propagation tree via activation patching — is the MI community's contribution: perturb activation i at layer l, measure which activations at layer l+1 change by more than threshold, recurse. The resulting tree has a well-defined size and depth.

[Geva et al. 2021] is particularly important because it reframes FFN computation as a lookup: the first weight matrix W_K implements pattern detection (keys), the second W_V implements a distribution over tokens (values), and the MLP-output = Σ_i activation_i × value_i_direction. Under this interpretation a 'firing FFN neuron' is a 'matched key' and the MLP output is a sum of matched-value directions. This provides a semantic interpretation of avalanche events that is absent in the raw-activation framing: an avalanche is a multi-key pattern match, propagating through the residual stream to trigger downstream matches. The branching ratio in this language is the expected number of matched keys at layer l+1 conditional on a key-match at layer l — a quantity that can be estimated directly from co-activation statistics.

[Dai et al. 2022]'s knowledge-neuron methodology — integrated-gradients identification of neurons whose ablation removes specific facts — provides a complementary causal probe. For the ideation target, knowledge neurons are candidate avalanche-seed events: a fact-retrieval forward pass should show a characteristic avalanche starting at the relevant knowledge neurons and propagating through specific residual-stream paths. This is a testable prediction and failure would weaken the criticality claim for semantic computation, even if it holds for generic token-level statistics.

Finally, [Meng 2022] ROME's rank-one editing approach suggests a perturbation protocol for Lyapunov estimation that is more semantically meaningful than random perturbations: perturb along a known causally-important direction (the residual-stream write of a specific FFN) and measure output sensitivity. If the network is near criticality, such a perturbation should propagate as a neutral or marginally-expanding signal. If it is strongly contracting or strongly expanding, the network is off-critical along that direction. This semantically-conditioned Lyapunov analysis is a natural bridge between interpretability (known important directions) and criticality (sensitivity to perturbations along those directions), and has no direct precedent in either literature.

Tooling is mature enough that much of this methodology can be implemented without writing low-level code. TransformerLens provides hook-based activation caching and activation patching; nnsight provides a more ergonomic API for the same; Anthropic's internal tools (not public but partially reflected in open work) go further with SAE and circuit discovery. The practical pipeline for the ideation target is: (1) load pretrained GPT-2 small via TransformerLens; (2) cache residual-stream activations on a Pile sample; (3) compute per-layer cluster-size distributions at multiple thresholds; (4) fit power-laws with Clauset-Shalizi-Newman; (5) report exponents, goodness-of-fit p-values, and basis-sensitivity. Extending to SAE features uses Gemma Scope's public weights. Causal avalanches via activation patching add cost but are within scope. The novelty is not in any single methodology component but in their composition for a criticality claim on real trained transformer language models.

## Stylised facts (ML side)

- **Edge-of-chaos init exists and is necessary for deep nets without skip connections.** At init, σ_w, σ_b can be tuned so that χ_1 = 1 and signal-propagation depth-scales diverge; this enables training of 200-plus-layer tanh nets [Schoenholz 2017] and 10 000-plus-layer CNNs with Delta-Orthogonal [Xiao 2018].
- **Modern architectures (residual, LayerNorm, attention) bypass the init-criticality constraint.** Skip connections guarantee gradient flow regardless of χ_1, decoupling trainability from init criticality.
- **Trained nets exhibit extreme activation sparsity.** 75-90 percent of MLP neurons are inactive per token in large LLMs [Li 2023]; contextual sparsity is predictable from residual stream [Liu 2023].
- **Training exhibits genuine phase transitions.** Grokking [Power 2022], induction-head emergence [Olsson 2022], superposition phase boundaries [Elhage 2022] are all well-documented training-time phase phenomena in real networks.
- **Scaling of test loss is smooth and power-law over >5 orders of magnitude.** Robust across architectures [Kaplan 2020, Hoffmann 2022]; exponents tied to kernel-spectral structure [Bahri 2021].
- **Capability emergence is partly a metric artefact.** Many emergent capabilities smooth out under continuous order parameters [Schaeffer 2023] — methodological warning for criticality claims.
- **Trained networks have sparse functional circuits.** IOI circuit in GPT-2 uses 26 heads out of 144 [Wang 2023]; knowledge neurons are a small fraction [Dai 2022]; universal neurons are 1-5 percent [Gurnee 2024].
- **Weight-graph topology is approximately scale-free in some architectures.** DBNs [Testolin 2020], MLPs [Scabini 2023], with degree-tail exponents in the classical 2–3 range.

**Contested:**

- Whether trained networks drift toward, away from, or past the init edge-of-chaos. Mean-field theory is mute about trained dynamics.
- Whether capability emergence reflects a genuine phase transition in internal structure or a discontinuity in the metric. Open.
- Whether NTK or feature-learning limits better describe finite-width trained LLMs. LLMs are probably outside NTK — but by how much, quantitatively, is debated.
- Whether the lottery-ticket subnet is topologically scale-free or just sparse-uniform.

**Methodologically fragile:**

- Activation-threshold choice: 0 vs 0.1 vs percentile-based — can flip an avalanche distribution from power-law to exponential.
- Sub-sampling bias in power-law fits [Clauset-Shalizi-Newman] — always compute finite-size-corrected p-values.
- Confusing correlation-based ('neurons co-fire') with causal ('ablating X suppresses Y') functional connectivity. Activation patching is the gold-standard causal probe.
- NTK / linearisation regime vs feature-learning regime: pretend-linear analyses give wrong criticality verdicts on feature-learning networks.
- Small-model results do not automatically transfer to LLM scale — but LLM experiments cannot be repeated from scratch on a single GPU. Use pretrained.
