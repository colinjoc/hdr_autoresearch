# Literature Review — Dublin/Cork NO2 Source Attribution

## 1. Domain Fundamentals: Atmospheric NO2 Chemistry and Health Effects

Nitrogen dioxide (NO2) is a secondary pollutant formed primarily through the oxidation of nitric oxide (NO) emitted by combustion sources. The NO-NO2-O3 photostationary state governs the equilibrium between these species in urban environments [Seinfeld & Pandis, 2016]. In Dublin's latitude and climate, photochemical activity is lower than in Mediterranean or continental cities, meaning that NO2 levels are more directly linked to primary emissions than in sunnier locations.

The health effects of NO2 exposure are well-documented. The WHO's 2021 guideline revision from 40 to 10 µg/m³ was based on meta-analyses showing adverse respiratory and cardiovascular effects at concentrations below the previous guideline [WHO, 2021]. Short-term exposure studies show increased emergency department visits for asthma at NO2 levels as low as 20 µg/m³ [Atkinson et al., 2018]. Long-term exposure is associated with reduced lung function growth in children [Gehring et al., 2013].

## 2. NO2 Sources in Urban Environments

Road transport is consistently identified as the dominant NO2 source in European cities, typically contributing 50-80% of urban NO2 at roadside locations [Carslaw et al., 2011; EEA, 2023]. Within road transport, diesel vehicles produce disproportionately high NOx emissions due to the high-temperature, lean-burn combustion process. Real-world driving emissions often exceed laboratory test values by factors of 2-10, particularly for Euro 5 and earlier diesel cars [Franco et al., 2014].

Residential heating is the second major source in Irish cities, particularly in areas with high solid fuel usage. Ireland has the highest rate of solid fuel heating in Western Europe, with peat, coal, and wood commonly burned in open fires and stoves. The EPA's smoky coal ban, progressively expanded from Dublin (1990) to all towns >10,000 population, has reduced but not eliminated this source [EPA Ireland, 2024].

Shipping and port activities contribute to NO2 in coastal cities. Dublin Port handles 4.3 million tonnes of unitised goods annually, with ferry and freight vessel emissions contributing to local air quality [Viana et al., 2014; Dublin Port Company, 2023].

## 3. Source Attribution Methods

Receptor-based methods decompose measured pollutant concentrations into source contributions using statistical or physical models applied at the measurement location. Key approaches include:

- **Positive Matrix Factorisation (PMF)**: Requires concurrent multi-pollutant measurements. Widely used in the US and Europe [Hopke, 2016].
- **Chemical Mass Balance (CMB)**: Requires known source profiles. Less commonly used for gaseous pollutants.
- **Station-type differencing**: Uses the EEA station classification (traffic/background/rural) to estimate the traffic increment as the difference between traffic and co-located background stations. Simple, transparent, and well-validated [Cyrys et al., 2012].
- **COVID-19 as natural experiment**: The 2020 lockdowns provided unprecedented validation data. Multiple studies used the "accidental experiment" to validate emission inventories and source attribution models [Venter et al., 2020; Shi & Brasseur, 2020; Mahato et al., 2020].

## 4. COVID-19 and Air Quality

The COVID-19 pandemic lockdowns of 2020 produced well-documented air quality improvements worldwide. In Europe, NO2 reductions of 20-60% were observed at traffic stations during strict lockdown periods [Venter et al., 2020]. The response was largest at traffic-dominated sites and smallest at rural/background sites, confirming that the observed reductions were primarily due to traffic changes rather than meteorological variation.

In Ireland specifically, the EPA's Air Quality in Ireland 2020 report [EPA, 2021] documented significant NO2 reductions during the March-June 2020 lockdown period, with the largest reductions at traffic-type monitoring stations in Dublin.

## 5. Irish Air Quality Context

Ireland's air quality monitoring has expanded significantly since 2017, from approximately 30 stations to 115 by 2024. The Dublin Region Air Quality Plan 2021 [Dublin City Council, 2021] identified road transport as the primary NO2 source and proposed measures including low-emission zones, fleet electrification, and active travel infrastructure.

The EPA's annual Air Quality in Ireland reports consistently show that while Ireland meets EU limit values for NO2, many urban areas exceed the stricter WHO guidelines. The gap between EU legal compliance and WHO health-based recommendations is a recurring theme in Irish air quality policy.

## 6. Meteorological Controls on NO2

Boundary layer meteorology strongly influences surface NO2 concentrations. Low wind speeds, temperature inversions, and stable atmospheric conditions trap pollutants near the surface, leading to elevated concentrations. The relationship between wind speed and NO2 is well-established, with NO2 concentrations approximately inversely proportional to wind speed in urban areas [Vardoulakis et al., 2003].

In Ireland, the predominant south-westerly airflow brings clean Atlantic air, generally favouring good dispersion. However, periods of anticyclonic conditions (especially in winter) can produce stagnant conditions with poor dispersion and elevated pollution episodes [Met Eireann climatological reports].

## 7. Related Problems: Source Attribution in Other Domains

The methodology of using natural experiments for source validation has parallels in other fields. In epidemiology, "difference-in-differences" designs exploit policy changes or events that affect one group but not another. In transport, traffic count data before and after road closures provides analogous validation. The COVID lockdown is unusual in its scale and completeness as a natural experiment for air quality.
