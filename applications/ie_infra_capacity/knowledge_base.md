# Knowledge Base: U-3 Infrastructure Capacity Blocks

## Established Facts

1. **National capacity status**: Of 1,063 WWTPs in the Uisce Éireann register, 790 (74.3%) are GREEN, 109 (10.3%) are AMBER, and 164 (15.4%) are RED. Combined RED+AMBER = 25.7%.
2. **County variation is extreme**: Kerry has 41.7% constrained WWTPs, while Dublin City/DLR/South Dublin have 0% RED or AMBER.
3. **Ringsend WWTP is GREEN**: The plant serving most of Dublin City has been upgraded (EUR 550M) and classified GREEN with project planned for further growth.
4. **Greater Dublin Drainage Project delayed to 2032**: Originally expected 2025. Critical for north/west Dublin growth.
5. **Investment committed**: EUR 10.3bn (2025-2029) plus EUR 2bn NDP ring-fenced for housing capacity.
6. **UWWTD non-compliance**: Ireland was found in breach in 28 agglomerations (CJEU 2019); 16 still non-compliant as of 2024.
7. **Recast UWWTD 2024/3019**: Must be transposed by July 2027; introduces micropollutant treatment and energy neutrality.
8. **7,911 hectares zoned nationally** (Goodbody 2021).
9. **83% economically stranded** (U-2 viability frontier finding).
10. **4.8 applications/ha/yr** on zoned land ex-Fingal (U-1 finding).
11. **Only 25.6% of RED WWTPs have a project planned**; 74.4% of RED plants have no upgrade scheduled.
12. **Large plants more constrained**: D-registration (>2000 PE) plants show 30.8% RED/AMBER vs 20.3% for smaller A-registration plants.
13. **Estimated 1,524 hectares blocked**: County-weighted estimate of zoned land in RED/AMBER catchments (19.3% of total).
14. **RED-only scenario**: 954 hectares blocked (12.1%); AMBER classification adds 570 hectares.
15. **Dublin commuter belt**: Meath 24.4%, Kildare 14.6%, Wicklow 12.8% constrained.

## Known Pitfalls

1. **County-level aggregation assumes uniform distribution** of zoned land across settlements within a county. In reality, zoned land is concentrated in larger settlements.
2. **Settlement-to-WWTP mapping is imperfect**: Some settlements share WWTPs; some WWTPs serve multiple settlements.
3. **Capacity register is a snapshot**: Status can change as upgrades complete or demand increases.
4. **AMBER is ambiguous**: Some AMBER plants can accommodate small developments; others are effectively at capacity.
5. **One-off rural houses use septic tanks**, not connected to WWTP network — they are not affected by WWTP capacity.
6. **Zoned land estimates are approximate**: Regional figures disaggregated to county level using population weights.
7. **No historical capacity data available**: Cannot track capacity changes over time; can only observe current status.
8. **Planning applications ≠ built housing**: Many permissions lapse (37% per U-1) or are never activated.

## Mechanisms

1. **Hard constraint mechanism**: WWTP at capacity → Uisce Éireann cannot confirm connection → planning authority must refuse or defer.
2. **Investment lag mechanism**: WWTP upgrade takes 7-15 years from identification to operation → constraints persist for a generation.
3. **Double-stranding mechanism**: Land that is both infrastructure-blocked and economically unviable requires two independent solutions simultaneously.
4. **Rural displacement**: Infrastructure blocks in growing areas push development to one-off rural houses with septic tanks, creating dispersed settlement patterns.
5. **Prioritisation tension**: Investment follows political salience (Dublin) more than constraint severity (rural counties).

## What Works / What Doesn't

- **Consolidation into national utility**: Creation of Uisce Éireann improved strategic planning but created a single bottleneck for investment decisions.
- **EU regulatory pressure**: UWWTD compliance drives investment but competes with capacity expansion for resources.
- **Private developer contributions**: Too small to fund WWTP upgrades; infrastructure levy system does not capture full costs.
- **Modular/temporary treatment**: Some settlements use temporary solutions pending permanent upgrades; these are classified AMBER.

## Updates from Experiments

- E04: Counterintuitively, high-constraint counties show LOWER refusal rates (8.1%) than low-constraint counties (18.5%). This likely reflects composition: high-constraint counties are rural with simpler applications.
- E07: Large plants (D-reg) are more constrained (30.8%) than small (20.3%), suggesting the largest settlements face the most binding capacity issues.
- E13: Dublin commuter belt averages 17.6% constrained, with Meath (24.4%) being the most constrained commuter county.
- E16: Weak positive correlation (r=0.189) between residential demand and constraints — high-demand areas are somewhat more constrained.
- E19: AMBER sensitivity is material: 1,524 ha blocked with AMBER included vs 954 ha with RED only — a 60% difference.
