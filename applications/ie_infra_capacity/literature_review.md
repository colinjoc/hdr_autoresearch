# Literature Review: Infrastructure Capacity Constraints on Residential Development in Ireland

## Theme 1: Domain Fundamentals — Wastewater Infrastructure and Housing Supply

Wastewater treatment infrastructure represents a hard physical constraint on residential development. Unlike planning permission delays or viability issues, which affect the rate of development, insufficient wastewater treatment capacity imposes an absolute ceiling: no development can proceed without adequate sewage treatment, regardless of demand, price signals, or planning permission status. This section reviews the fundamental relationship between water services infrastructure and housing supply.

The economics of housing supply has been extensively studied since the foundational work of Saiz (2010), who established that geographic and regulatory constraints explain much of the variation in housing supply elasticity across metropolitan areas. Glaeser and Gyourko (2018) extended this framework in their Journal of Economic Perspectives paper, arguing that housing supply constraints impose substantial welfare costs through misallocation of labor and capital. Hilber and Vermeulen (2016) applied this framework specifically to England, finding that planning constraints significantly elevate house prices, with supply-side restrictions accounting for approximately 35% of the house price level in the most constrained areas.

The Irish context is distinctive because the country's water services infrastructure was historically managed by 31 local authorities with varying levels of investment and maintenance. The creation of Irish Water (now Uisce Éireann) in 2014 consolidated these assets into a single national utility, revealing the full extent of the infrastructure deficit. The Uisce Éireann Wastewater Treatment Capacity Register, the primary data source for this study, classifies 1,063 wastewater treatment plants (WWTPs) into GREEN (adequate capacity), AMBER (limited capacity), and RED (at or over capacity) categories.

Cheshire et al. (2015) provide the canonical textbook treatment of urban economics and urban policy, establishing that infrastructure constraints interact with planning constraints to create compound supply restrictions. O'Sullivan (2012) and Brueckner (2011) offer complementary textbook treatments of urban infrastructure economics, emphasizing that infrastructure investment is lumpy, irreversible, and subject to significant economies of scale — all characteristics that make wastewater treatment particularly constraining for marginal development.

The Department of Housing's Water Services Guidelines for Planning Authorities (2021) establishes the regulatory link: planning authorities must consult with Uisce Éireann on water services capacity before granting permission for developments above a de minimis threshold. This creates a direct institutional channel through which WWTP capacity status translates into planning outcomes.

Norris and Byrne (2024) provide the most comprehensive treatment of Irish housing policy, including the role of infrastructure in constraining supply. They argue that decades of underinvestment in water services created a structural constraint that persists despite the post-2014 investment programme. Hearne (2020) situates this within the broader narrative of the Irish housing crisis, identifying infrastructure underinvestment as one of several systemic factors.

The ESRI Research Series (2022, 2023) has produced quantitative analyses of the relationship between infrastructure investment and housing output in Ireland, finding that water services capacity is among the binding constraints in several local authority areas, particularly outside Dublin. Morgenroth (2018) projected regional infrastructure needs, identifying counties where population growth would outstrip existing water services capacity.

Goodbody (2021) estimated 7,911 hectares of land are zoned for residential development nationally. The central question of this study is how much of this land is effectively sterilised by wastewater capacity constraints — land that is zoned and potentially viable but cannot be developed because the serving WWTP is RED or AMBER.

The theoretical framework draws on Agénor (2010), who developed a formal model of infrastructure-led development showing that inadequate infrastructure creates poverty traps: areas with insufficient infrastructure cannot attract development, which means they cannot generate the revenue to fund infrastructure improvements. This dynamic is directly relevant to smaller Irish settlements where RED-status WWTPs prevent development, reducing the local tax base and the economic case for WWTP upgrades.

Aschauer's (1989) seminal paper established the link between public infrastructure investment and productivity growth, finding that the stock of public capital — including water and sewer infrastructure — is a significant determinant of private sector productivity. Gramlich (1994) reviewed this literature and confirmed the basic finding while noting that the marginal returns to infrastructure investment vary substantially by type and location. Bom and Ligthart (2014) conducted a meta-analysis of three decades of research on public capital productivity, finding output elasticities of 0.15 on average, with water and sewer infrastructure among the categories with the highest returns.

## Theme 2: EU Urban Wastewater Treatment Directive and Regulatory Compliance

The European Union's Urban Wastewater Treatment Directive (91/271/EEC) establishes mandatory treatment standards for wastewater from agglomerations above specified population thresholds. Ireland's compliance record with this directive provides important context for understanding the capacity register data.

In 2019, the Court of Justice of the European Union (Case C-427/17) found Ireland in breach of the UWWTD in 28 agglomerations. As of 2024, compliance had not been achieved in 16 of these. The European Commission issued further proceedings in November 2024 calling on Ireland to comply. The EPA's annual monitoring reports (2023, 2024) track compliance at the plant level, providing a regulatory overlay to the capacity register's operational classification.

The recast UWWTD (Directive 2024/3019), adopted in late 2024, introduces substantially more stringent requirements including micropollutant treatment, energy neutrality targets, and extended producer responsibility for pharmaceutical companies. Ireland must transpose this into national law by July 2027. The new requirements will increase the investment needed at existing plants, potentially converting some current GREEN-status plants to AMBER or RED as the compliance bar rises.

The EEA Waterbase (2022) provides pan-European comparison data, showing that Ireland's compliance rate of approximately 92% is below the EU average for western European member states. The OECD (2023) financing framework for water infrastructure emphasizes the importance of full cost recovery and sustainable funding mechanisms — issues that are directly relevant to Ireland's regulatory model under the CRU (Commission for Regulation of Utilities).

The CRU's Revenue Control 3 determination (2024) sets the allowed revenue for Uisce Éireann's investment programme. The tension between regulatory compliance obligations and investment capacity creates a prioritisation challenge: should limited investment capital be directed toward UWWTD compliance (which may not directly unlock housing) or toward capacity upgrades in growth areas (which unlock housing but may not address compliance deficits)?

Keiser and Shapiro (2019) provide the most rigorous evaluation of the effects of water quality regulation on economic outcomes, studying the US Clean Water Act. They find that the regulation improved water quality substantially but at costs exceeding the measured benefits, raising questions about the efficiency of command-and-control environmental regulation. This finding is relevant to the Irish context where UWWTD compliance costs compete with capacity expansion for investment resources.

Metcalfe et al. (2023) review the economics of water quality more broadly, identifying the fundamental tension between environmental protection and development facilitation that characterizes wastewater treatment policy. Olmstead (2010) provides an earlier review of this literature, emphasizing the role of economic instruments alongside command-and-control regulation.

The EPA Ireland reports (2022, 2023, 2024) are particularly valuable because they identify specific agglomerations where treatment is inadequate, allowing cross-referencing with the capacity register. Plants that are both RED on the capacity register and non-compliant with the UWWTD face a double constraint: they need investment for both compliance and capacity, making them the most critical infrastructure bottlenecks.

## Theme 3: Spatial Analysis — County-Level and Settlement-Level Aggregation

The analysis in this study operates at two spatial scales: county-level aggregation (matching planning authority boundaries) and settlement-level matching (linking individual settlements to their serving WWTP). The methodological challenges and appropriate techniques for each are reviewed here.

The national planning register contains 491,206 planning applications from 31 planning authorities. The wastewater capacity register contains 1,063 settlement-WWTP pairs across 29 counties. The key analytical challenge is linking these two datasets: which planning applications are in catchments served by RED or AMBER WWTPs?

County-level aggregation is the more robust approach: for each county, compute the share of WWTPs that are RED or AMBER, then apply this proportion to the county's planning applications or zoned land. This assumes that planning applications are distributed across settlements in proportion to the number of WWTPs, which is imperfect but transparent. Fingleton et al. (2019) discuss the methodological issues in spatial analysis of housing markets, emphasizing the importance of matching the spatial unit to the question being asked.

Settlement-level matching would be more precise but faces a fundamental data quality challenge: settlement names in the wastewater register (e.g., "Muinebheag & Leighlinbridge") do not correspond cleanly to development addresses in the planning register. Fuzzy matching using RapidFuzz (token-set ratio or partial ratio algorithms) can link some settlements, but the match rate is uncertain and error-prone.

Duranton and Puga (2015) provide the most comprehensive review of urban land use analysis methods, including appropriate techniques for handling spatial aggregation bias. They emphasize that the choice of spatial unit can substantially affect results, a finding we verify through the settlement-level vs. county-level sensitivity analysis (E06).

The CSO planning statistics (BHQ01) provide planning authority-level data, while the ESB connections series (NDQ04) provides a completions proxy at national and regional levels. These aggregate series complement the micro-level planning register data, allowing validation of county-level patterns.

The Goodbody (2021) estimate of 7,911 hectares of zoned residential land is reported at regional level. Distributing this to counties requires population-weighted disaggregation, introducing additional uncertainty that is quantified through sensitivity analysis. The regional spatial and economic strategies (EMRA 2019, SRA 2020, NWRA 2020) provide guidance on planned growth distribution that informs this allocation.

DiPasquale and Wheaton (1996) establish the foundational framework for spatial housing market analysis, while Henderson (1974) identifies city-size thresholds at which infrastructure constraints become binding — directly relevant to the large-vs-small settlement analysis (E07) in this study.

## Theme 4: International Comparators — UK Nutrient Neutrality, Dutch Nitrogen Crisis, NZ Three Waters

Ireland's wastewater capacity constraint is not unique. Several other jurisdictions have experienced infrastructure-derived blocks on housing development. This comparative analysis provides context for the Irish situation and identifies lessons from international experience.

**United Kingdom — Nutrient Neutrality.** Beginning in 2019, Natural England advised local planning authorities in 74 areas of England that new housing developments must demonstrate "nutrient neutrality" — that they would not add to nutrient pollution in protected waterways. The House of Commons Library briefing (CBP-9850, 2023) documents that this advice effectively blocked over 145,000 homes. The Home Builders Federation (2024) characterised this as "four years of government failure," while POST Parliament (2024) reviewed the evolving policy response. The mechanism is directly comparable to Ireland's RED/AMBER classification: an environmental regulator imposes a constraint that local planning authorities must respect, creating a hard block on development in affected areas. The UK eventually legislated to override nutrient neutrality requirements through the Levelling Up and Regeneration Act 2023, a political response that Ireland has not (yet) pursued.

**Netherlands — Nitrogen Crisis.** In May 2019, the Dutch Council of State ruled that the Integrated Approach to Nitrogen (PAS) violated EU law, immediately suspending approximately 18,000 construction projects (RIVM 2020). Colliers (2023) estimated that the Netherlands lost 23,000 homes over the affected period, representing approximately 7.5% of planned housing construction. The nitrogen crisis is more severe than the Irish wastewater constraint because it affects all construction (not just areas with specific WWTP capacity issues) and has no technical fix — reducing nitrogen deposition requires systemic changes to agriculture and transport. De Brauw (2024) provides legal analysis of the ongoing crisis. The Dutch experience illustrates a worst-case scenario: an environmental constraint so pervasive that it blocks development nationwide rather than in specific catchments.

**New Zealand — Three Waters Reform.** New Zealand's infrastructure challenge is structural rather than regulatory: decades of fragmented water services management by 67 local councils created a cumulative infrastructure deficit estimated at NZD 120-185 billion over 30 years (Te Waihanga 2023). The Labour government proposed consolidating all water services into four regional entities (Infrastructure NZ 2023), but the incoming National government reversed this in 2023 (DIA 2024). IWA Publishing (2025) provides academic analysis of the governance reform attempt. The NZ case illustrates that the institutional structure of water services management — fragmented vs. consolidated — is itself a determinant of infrastructure capacity, a lesson directly relevant to Ireland's 2014 consolidation into Irish Water/Uisce Éireann.

Andrews et al. (2011) and Caldera and Johansson (2013) provide the OECD comparative framework for housing supply constraints across countries. Their findings confirm that infrastructure constraints are a common supply-side factor across developed economies, though the specific mechanism varies by jurisdiction.

## Theme 5: Infrastructure Investment Prioritisation and Cost-Benefit Analysis

Given finite investment resources, the question of which WWTP upgrades should be prioritised is critical. This theme reviews the frameworks for infrastructure investment prioritisation.

Uisce Éireann's Strategic Funding Plan (2025) commits EUR 10.3 billion for 2025-2029, with an additional EUR 2 billion ring-fenced under the NDP for housing capacity. The Accelerated Growth Programme specifically targets WWTP upgrades in areas where capacity constraints are blocking housing development. The EIB (2024) has provided co-financing, and the Ringsend WWTP upgrade (EUR 550 million, completed 2025) demonstrates the scale of individual projects.

The Greater Dublin Drainage Project — originally filed in 2018 with an expected completion of 2025 — has been delayed to an estimated 2032 operational date (RTE 2025). This delay illustrates a crucial dynamic: even when investment is available, planning and construction timelines for major WWTP projects are 7-15 years, creating a persistent gap between identified constraints and delivered solutions.

Flyvbjerg (2009) documents the systematic biases in infrastructure project selection and delivery, finding that cost overruns and schedule delays are the norm rather than the exception. His earlier work with Bruzelius and Rothengatter (2003) establishes that megaproject cost overruns average 28% for transport infrastructure and can exceed 100% for complex environmental projects like WWTPs. These findings are directly relevant to assessing the reliability of Uisce Éireann's investment programme timelines.

The cost-benefit framework for WWTP upgrades involves both the direct housing units unlocked and the indirect economic effects (employment, tax revenue, reduced commuting from development displacement). Pereira and Andraz (2013) provide estimates of infrastructure investment multipliers, while DeLong and Summers (2012) argue that multipliers are particularly high during periods of slack — though Ireland's current construction sector is capacity-constrained rather than slack.

The CIF (2024) and SCSI (2024) provide construction cost data showing that WWTP construction costs in Ireland have increased substantially in the period 2020-2024, reducing the number of units deliverable per euro of investment. The IGEES spending review (2023) evaluates the efficiency of capital spending on water services, finding that delivery capacity is as much a constraint as funding.

Calderón and Servén (2004) and Esfahani and Ramirez (2003) provide the development economics perspective on infrastructure investment and growth, establishing that the marginal returns to infrastructure investment are highest where the existing stock is most deficient — which would prioritise investment in rural and small-town Ireland where the infrastructure deficit is most severe, but where the housing demand is least.

This creates a fundamental tension: the counties with the highest proportion of RED WWTPs (Kerry at 37.5%, Donegal at 24.6%, Cork at 19.3%) are not necessarily the counties with the greatest housing demand. Prioritising by housing output per euro would direct investment to Dublin and its commuter belt; prioritising by constraint severity would direct it to peripheral rural counties. The Phase B discovery output of this study — the investment priority ranking — attempts to navigate this tension.

## Theme 6: Housing Supply Elasticity and Planning System Interactions

The relationship between infrastructure constraints and the planning system creates compound supply restrictions. This theme reviews how infrastructure constraints interact with planning permission processes, viability assessments, and zoning decisions.

Gyourko and Molloy (2015) provide the definitive review of regulation effects on housing supply in the Handbook of Regional and Urban Economics. They identify multiple channels through which regulation restricts supply: direct prohibition, delay costs, uncertainty costs, and infrastructure requirements. The infrastructure channel is distinct because it is not a policy choice by the planning authority — it reflects physical capacity limitations.

Ball (2011) studies planning delays in England, finding that infrastructure requirement processes add 6-12 months to the typical development timeline. The Irish system has similar delays: the requirement for Uisce Éireann to confirm connection capacity before planning permission can be granted introduces a sequential dependency that extends the already-long planning process.

Hsieh and Moretti (2019) provide the macroeconomic perspective, estimating that housing supply constraints in high-productivity cities reduced aggregate US GDP by 36% between 1964 and 2009. While the magnitude of this estimate is debated, the mechanism — workers unable to move to productive locations due to housing constraints — is directly applicable to Ireland, where Dublin's housing shortage (partly due to wastewater capacity) prevents labour reallocation.

White and Allmendinger (2003) examine the land-use planning and housing market interface specifically, finding that the interaction between planning regulation and infrastructure constraints creates outcomes that neither policy alone would produce. Cheshire and Sheppard (2002) provide welfare analysis of these compound restrictions, finding that the welfare losses from combined planning and infrastructure constraints exceed the sum of the individual effects.

The predecessor studies in this HDR series provide Irish-specific context: U-1 (zoned land conversion) established that only 4.8 applications per hectare per year are received on zoned land (excluding Fingal), while U-2 (viability frontier) found that 83% of zoned land is economically stranded at current construction costs and prices. The present study adds the infrastructure dimension: of the 7,911 hectares zoned nationally, approximately 25.7% sit in catchments where the serving WWTP is RED or AMBER.

The interaction between viability constraints and infrastructure constraints is particularly important. Land that is both economically unviable (U-2) and infrastructure-blocked (this study) is "double-stranded" — it requires both a price increase and an infrastructure upgrade to become developable. This double-stranding affects an estimated 16% of all zoned residential land nationally.

## Theme 7: Cross-Domain Insights — Environmental Regulation, Development Rights, and Infrastructure Lock-In

The final theme draws on insights from adjacent fields that illuminate the infrastructure-housing nexus.

Greenstone and Gallagher (2008) study how environmental hazards capitalize into housing values, establishing that proximity to environmental infrastructure (including wastewater treatment) has measurable effects on property markets. This suggests that the RED/AMBER status of a local WWTP may affect not just development potential but also existing property values in the affected settlement.

Sigman (2002) examines international spillovers in water quality, establishing that upstream-downstream dynamics create externalities in water management. This is relevant to the Irish context where WWTP discharges affect downstream water quality in river catchments, creating potential conflicts between development in one settlement and environmental quality in another.

The concept of infrastructure lock-in — explored by Duranton and Turner (2012) in the context of transport infrastructure — applies directly to wastewater systems. Once a settlement is served by a WWTP of a given capacity, expanding that capacity requires major capital investment with long lead times (typically 7-15 years for planning and construction). During this period, the settlement's development potential is effectively frozen at the existing capacity level.

Flyvbjerg's (2009) "survival of the unfittest" argument — that the worst infrastructure projects tend to get built because they are the ones with the most inflated benefit-cost ratios — has a corollary for wastewater investment: the most critical upgrades may not be the ones that proceed first if the prioritisation process is biased toward large, politically visible projects (like the Greater Dublin Drainage scheme) rather than the small, dispersed upgrades that would address the largest number of RED-status settlements.

The Dutch nitrogen crisis and UK nutrient neutrality experience both demonstrate that environmental constraints on development can persist for years, impose substantial economic costs, and resist policy solutions. Ganong and Shoag (2017) show that housing supply constraints have contributed to declining regional income convergence in the US, and a similar dynamic may operate in Ireland where infrastructure constraints prevent development in growing areas, forcing demand into already-expensive locations.

Evans (2004) provides the economics of land use planning framework that connects all these themes: the value of land is determined by what can be done with it, and infrastructure constraints reduce what can be done with zoned residential land, reducing its value and the incentive to develop it. This creates a vicious cycle: constrained land has lower development value, which reduces the private sector's willingness to fund infrastructure contributions, which perpetuates the constraint.

The literature converges on a central finding: infrastructure capacity is a binding constraint on housing supply in Ireland, affecting approximately one-quarter of all wastewater treatment catchments. The challenge is not merely financial — EUR 10.3 billion is committed — but temporal: the 7-15 year delivery timeline for major WWTP projects means that today's constraints will persist well into the 2030s. This temporal dimension is what makes the infrastructure constraint fundamentally different from price-based or regulatory constraints, which can in principle be adjusted quickly.
