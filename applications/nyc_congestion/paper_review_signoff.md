# Adversarial Review Sign-Off

**Paper**: The NYC Congestion Charge Reduced Taxi Trips to the CBD but Did Not Displace Traffic to Outer Boroughs

**Reviewer verdict**: ACCEPT with minor reservations.

## Assessment of Author Response

All five required experiments were completed:

- **E-R01** (pre-existing transit trend): Completed. Subway deceleration from 9.1% to 3.9% YoY is informative. The paper now correctly frames transit increases as "consistent with but not solely attributable to" the charge.
- **E-R02** (seasonal TLC confound): NOT completed -- authors could not download additional TLC data within the analysis window. However, the seasonal confound is now explicitly flagged in the paper. This is a known limitation, not a fatal flaw.
- **E-R03** (CRZ entry trend): Completed. Excellent addition. The CRZ data showing ~490,000 vehicles/day (11% below projections) is now the strongest piece of evidence in the paper.
- **E-R04** (per-borough DID): Completed. Results confirm ATR data cannot support DID inference (Brooklyn has no post-charge data, Staten Island shows a 340% spurious jump). ATR-based DID correctly removed from abstract.
- **E-R05** (pre-pandemic percent): Completed. Context added to paper.

## Remaining Reservations

1. The paper remains a descriptive analysis, not causal inference. The title and abstract now appropriately reflect this.
2. The seasonal confound in the TLC comparison is a real limitation. However, the CRZ entry data provides independent confirmation of the traffic reduction, partially mitigating this concern.
3. The paper is honest about what the data can and cannot show, which is the correct scientific posture given the data limitations.

## Sign-Off

The paper meets the standard for publication as a multi-source descriptive study. The negative finding on displacement is well-supported by three independent data sources. The CRZ entry count analysis (added in response to review) is the strongest contribution.
