# Literature Review: Irish BER vs Real Home Energy Gap

## 1. Domain Fundamentals: Building Heat Transfer and Energy Balance

The energy performance of a dwelling is governed by the thermodynamics of heat transfer through the building envelope and the efficiency of its heating and ventilation systems. The three fundamental modes of heat transfer -- conduction through walls, roofs, and floors; convection through air movement (ventilation and infiltration); and radiation through windows and between surfaces -- collectively determine how much energy a building requires to maintain comfortable indoor temperatures [P032, P034, P035].

The thermal transmittance (U-value) of a building element, measured in W/m2K, quantifies the rate of conductive heat loss per unit area per degree temperature difference. Lower U-values indicate better insulation. For a typical Irish house, the wall U-value is the dominant contributor to fabric heat loss, followed by the roof, windows, and ground floor [P032]. Hens [P035] provides the theoretical framework: for a composite wall, the U-value is the reciprocal of the sum of resistances (R = d/lambda for each layer, where d is thickness and lambda is thermal conductivity). In practice, the calculated U-value depends on assumed material properties, which may differ from in-situ measurements [P042, P043, P111, P167].

The degree-day method [P051, P053] provides the bridge between building physics and energy prediction. Heating Degree Days (HDD) represent the cumulative temperature deficit below a base temperature (typically 15.5C in Ireland) over a heating season. Annual space heating demand is approximately proportional to HDD times the total heat loss coefficient divided by floor area. This quasi-steady-state approach, originally developed for industrial energy accounting [P051], forms the basis of the monthly energy balance method standardised in ISO 13790 [P165] and adopted by most European Energy Performance Certificate (EPC) calculation procedures, including Ireland's Dwelling Energy Assessment Procedure (DEAP) [P025, P026].

Clarke [P033] provides the authoritative treatment of dynamic building energy simulation, documenting how transient effects -- thermal mass, intermittent heating, solar gain timing -- create systematic errors in steady-state methods. The ISO 13790 monthly method attempts to account for thermal mass through utilisation factors, but these are approximations that work best for lightweight construction and fail for heavyweight masonry typical of older Irish buildings [P142, P195]. This is a known source of bias in the Irish BER system: DEAP undervalues the thermal mass benefits of solid stone and brick construction common in pre-1940s Irish dwellings.

Ventilation heat loss, often underappreciated in policy discussions, accounts for 30-50% of total heat loss in older dwellings [P032, P122]. The air permeability of a building -- measured by blower door test at 50 Pascal pressure difference -- determines the rate of uncontrolled air infiltration. Pan [P077] measured air permeabilities in Irish dwellings ranging from 3 m3/h/m2 (airtight modern homes) to over 20 m3/h/m2 (pre-1940s stock). Sinnott and Dyer [P078] confirmed this wide variation and showed that new Irish homes are significantly tighter than existing stock, a trend accelerated by the 2019 nZEB regulations [P027].

## 2. The Performance Gap: Predicted vs Actual Energy Consumption

The performance gap in buildings -- the systematic discrepancy between designed or calculated energy performance and actual metered consumption -- is among the most extensively documented phenomena in building science. De Wilde [P014] provides the definitive typology, distinguishing three levels: the design-stage gap (between design intent and as-built), the prediction gap (between calculated rating and actual use), and the operational gap (between commissioning performance and long-term use). The EPC performance gap falls primarily in the second category.

Sunikka-Blank and Galvin [P002, P003] introduced the concept of the "prebound effect" using data from 3,400 German dwellings with matched energy performance certificates and metered consumption. They found that dwellings with poor energy ratings (high calculated demand) systematically used less energy than predicted, while dwellings with good ratings used more than predicted. The prebound ratio (actual/predicted) was approximately 0.6 for the worst-rated dwellings and approximately 1.2 for the best-rated. The mechanism is straightforward: occupants of poorly-rated homes cannot afford to heat to the standard temperature assumption (typically 20-21C), so they under-heat, close off rooms, and wear extra clothing. This "energy frugality" means the certified energy demand overstates what these households actually consume [P003, P093].

Majcen et al. [P004, P005] replicated these findings with a much larger Dutch dataset (approximately 200,000 dwellings), confirming the convergence pattern: actual gas consumption varies far less across EPC labels than the certificates predict. A Dutch G-rated dwelling used approximately 2.5 times as much gas as an A-rated dwelling, not the 7-8 times that EPC calculations implied. The explanation combined prebound effects for poorly-rated homes with rebound effects (or "comfort taking") for well-rated homes [P114, P115].

The rebound effect in residential heating has been reviewed by Sorrell et al. [P011], who estimate the direct rebound (increased consumption due to reduced marginal cost of warmth after efficiency improvements) at 10-30% for space heating. Gillingham et al. [P012] argue the rebound is smaller than commonly assumed, but their analysis focuses on marginal efficiency improvements, not on the comparison between EPC-predicted and actual consumption that defines the prebound-rebound pattern.

Cali et al. [P129] provided some of the strongest evidence using a German dataset of 1,600 apartments with matched EPC and individual metered data, confirming that the gap increases with calculated demand and that the prebound effect dominates for poorly-rated dwellings. Van den Brom et al. [P091] conducted a meta-analysis across European studies and found the pattern is remarkably consistent across countries, climates, and EPC calculation methods: the prebound effect is universal, with actual consumption converging across rating bands.

In the UK, Johnston et al. [P016] used coheating tests to show that new homes used 50-120% more heat than designed, documenting the design-stage gap. Stafford et al. [P099] measured building fabric heat loss in occupied homes and found systematic discrepancies between measured and modelled thermal performance. Li et al. [P042] conducted in-situ U-value measurements of English walls and found measured values differed from calculated values by 30-100%, with solid walls performing better than assumed and cavity walls performing worse.

The EPC-specific literature is extensive. Brøgger et al. [P039] showed Danish EPCs have limited correlation with actual consumption. Hardy and Glew [P067] analysed 500,000 English homes with matched EPC and smart meter data, finding that median actual consumption was approximately half of EPC-predicted for the worst-rated homes. Cischinsky and Diefenbach [P197] documented German EPCs deviating from actual consumption by a factor of 2-3 for the worst-rated homes. Laurent et al. [P008] questioned EPC reliability across EU member states, and Crawley et al. [P066] documented significant differences in EPC calculation methods across countries, making cross-border comparison unreliable.

## 3. The Irish BER System: DEAP, Assessors, and Data Quality

Ireland's BER system was introduced in 2007, implementing the EU EPBD. The calculation engine is DEAP -- the Dwelling Energy Assessment Procedure [P025, P026], based on the ISO 13790 monthly energy balance method adapted to Irish conditions. DEAP uses standardised assumptions for occupancy, internal temperatures (20C living areas, 18C elsewhere), heating schedules (16 hours/day living areas), and climate (14 regional weather stations from Met Eireann [P158]).

The BER assessor scheme [P102] requires assessors to complete FETAC Level 6 training and register with SEAI. Assessors survey the dwelling, record construction details, and enter data into the DEAP software. Where specific data is unavailable (e.g., wall construction behind plaster), assessors use default values from DEAP tables, which are intentionally conservative (worst reasonable case) [P190].

Assessor variability is a documented problem. Donal [P104] and SEAI's own quality audits [P150, P157] have found that different assessors can rate the same dwelling differently -- in one documented case, the same house received D2 from one assessor and C1 from another. The sources of variability include: (a) different interpretations of construction type, (b) different decisions about which details to inspect vs default, (c) different survey thoroughness, and (d) data entry errors. Moran and Goggins [P161] assessed the quality of the national BER database and identified systematic data quality issues, including implausible combinations of building age and construction type, and suspiciously round values suggesting default usage.

The Irish BER rating distribution reflects the country's housing stock characteristics. Approximately 60% of Irish dwellings are rated D or worse [P123, P201]. The strongest predictor of BER rating is building vintage: pre-1940s homes cluster in E-G, 1940s-1970s in D-F, 1980s-1990s in C-D, 2000s in B-C, and post-2010 in A-B [P019, P105, P031]. This reflects the progressive strengthening of Irish building regulations: the first Part L (insulation) requirements in 1978, significant tightening in 1992 and 2006, and the nZEB standard (SI 426/2019) [P027, P109, P153].

Coyne and Denny [P021] produced the key Irish performance gap study, matching approximately 3,000 BER certificates to metered energy data from the CER Smart Meter Trials [P071]. Their findings confirmed the international pattern: Irish E-G rated homes used 35-55% less energy than BER predicted (prebound), while A-B rated homes used 10-30% more (rebound). The gap was largest for space heating and smallest for hot water. Building vintage was the strongest predictor of gap magnitude. This is the most direct evidence available on the Irish BER performance gap.

## 4. Irish Housing Stock and Retrofit Landscape

Ireland's residential building stock is distinctive in several respects. The country experienced rapid construction during the Celtic Tiger era (1997-2008), with approximately 25% of the current stock built in this period [P074]. The housing stock is predominantly low-rise: approximately 90% of dwellings are houses (detached, semi-detached, or terraced) rather than apartments [P072, P106]. Rural dwelling density is unusually low, with approximately 40% of dwellings outside urban areas [P074].

Famuyibo et al. [P072, P140, P192] developed a comprehensive characterisation of the Irish housing stock using BER data and national statistics, identifying representative archetypes by construction period, dwelling type, and region. McLoughlin et al. [P106] and Ahern et al. [P073] applied clustering methods to the BER database to identify typical dwelling types, finding that construction period and wall type were the primary discriminating features.

Heating fuel mix in Ireland differs from most EU countries. Oil heating remains dominant in rural areas (approximately 37% of stock nationally), while natural gas serves approximately 34% (primarily urban areas connected to the gas grid) [P110]. Solid fuel (coal, peat, wood) still accounts for approximately 12%, concentrated in rural midlands and west [P137, P138]. Heat pump penetration is growing rapidly from a low base, driven by SEAI grants and the nZEB regulation requirement [P046, P118]. This fuel diversity creates a distinct BER distribution: oil-heated rural homes cluster in the D-F range, gas-heated urban homes in C-D, and heat pump homes in A-B.

Ireland's National Retrofit Plan [P030] targets 500,000 home retrofits to BER B2 or better by 2030, requiring approximately €8 billion in investment. The ESRI March 2026 bulletin [P113] concluded that Ireland is "lagging" on retrofit pace, with current rates insufficient to meet 2030 targets. The SEAI Better Energy Homes scheme [P108] and the National Home Energy Upgrade scheme provide grants for insulation, heating upgrades, and deep retrofits, but uptake has been concentrated in higher-income urban areas [P143, P170].

Byrne et al. [P075, P191] conducted cost-benefit analyses of retrofit strategies for Irish housing archetypes, finding that cavity wall insulation is the most cost-effective single measure, followed by attic insulation and boiler upgrade. Deep retrofit (external wall insulation + heat pump + windows + ventilation) achieves the largest BER improvement but at the highest cost. Rouleau et al. [P022] documented that actual energy savings from Irish retrofits are typically 50-70% of BER-predicted savings, consistent with the prebound-rebound pattern.

## 5. Machine Learning for Building Energy Prediction

The application of machine learning to building energy prediction has been extensively reviewed by Amasyali and El-Gohary [P059], Wei et al. [P060], and Seyedzadeh et al. [P061]. The consensus is that gradient boosting methods (XGBoost [P094], LightGBM [P095]) and random forests [P098] dominate tabular building energy prediction tasks, with neural networks offering limited additional benefit for structured building characteristics data [P178].

Robinson et al. [P062] applied random forests to building energy prediction from characteristics, demonstrating that tree-based methods naturally handle the mixed categorical/continuous nature of building data. Fan et al. [P063] showed XGBoost achieving state-of-the-art results on commercial building energy estimation. Kontokosta and Tull [P185] analysed a large NYC building energy benchmarking dataset using gradient boosting, demonstrating the value of feature engineering over model architecture changes.

For EPC-based prediction specifically, Seyedzadeh et al. [P061] compared multiple ML methods on UK EPC data and found that ensemble tree methods outperformed linear and neural approaches, with building age, floor area, and wall type being the most important features -- consistent with the physical understanding of what drives EPC calculations. Gao et al. [P178] reviewed deep learning approaches and found limited advantage over gradient boosting for tabular EPC features.

The feature engineering literature [P125] emphasises domain-informed feature construction. For building energy, this means encoding physical relationships (U-values from wall type and insulation, degree-day-scaled heating demand, system efficiency ratios) rather than relying on raw categorical features alone. SHAP-based model interpretation [P173] and partial dependence analysis [P174] have become standard tools for understanding what ML building energy models learn.

Cross-validation strategy matters: Manfren et al. [P171] showed that random train/test splits systematically overestimate model performance for building energy tasks because temporal and spatial autocorrelation create information leakage. They recommend stratified or grouped splits that prevent the same building or location from appearing in both train and test sets.

## 6. Occupant Behaviour and the Human Factor

The DEAP calculation uses standardised occupancy assumptions, but actual occupant behaviour varies enormously and is the primary driver of the performance gap. Gram-Hanssen [P048] conducted ethnographic studies of Danish households showing that heating practices vary hugely even within identical dwellings: some households heat to 24C with windows open, while others accept 17C with extra clothing. Shove [P049] argues from social practice theory that energy consumption is embedded in social routines (cooking, bathing, entertaining) rather than being a rational economic choice.

Lutzenhiser [P050, P130] questioned the standard occupancy profiles used in building energy models, showing that actual occupancy patterns bear little resemblance to the standardised assumptions. Delzendeh et al. [P015] systematically reviewed occupant behaviour modelling in building energy simulation, concluding that the gap between assumed and actual behaviour accounts for most of the performance gap in residential buildings. Yan et al. [P131] proposed a framework for modelling occupant-building energy interactions.

In the Irish context, Orr et al. [P188] and Humphreys et al. [P189] conducted field surveys showing that Irish occupants typically maintain indoor temperatures of 18-19C, significantly below the 20-21C assumed by DEAP. This systematic under-heating is consistent with the prebound effect and explains why DEAP overpredicts energy consumption for most of the existing stock. The under-heating is more pronounced in rural areas, older homes, and lower-income households [P083, P145, P202].

Shipworth et al. [P204] documented enormous variation in actual heating schedules in UK homes, with some households heating only 4-6 hours per day compared to the 16 hours assumed in standard calculations. Part-house heating (only heating the living room rather than the whole dwelling) is common in Ireland but not modelled by DEAP, which assumes the whole dwelling is heated to the standard temperature schedule [P025].

## 7. Cross-Domain Insights: Radon, Geology, and Indoor Air Quality

The EPA UNVEIL project [P028] brought an unexpected dimension to the building energy discussion by documenting that energy-efficient retrofits -- particularly those improving airtightness -- can significantly increase indoor radon concentrations. Radon is a naturally occurring radioactive gas that enters buildings through the ground; Ireland has significant radon risk areas, particularly over granitic bedrock in parts of Wicklow, Galway, Donegal, and Kerry [P080].

Colgan et al. [P160] established the systematic relationship between building airtightness and indoor radon levels: tighter buildings accumulate more radon because the reduced air change rate allows soil gas to build up. Fennel et al. [P080] mapped the baseline radon risk across Ireland, and Synnott et al. [P081] evaluated remediation techniques. The UNVEIL finding [P028] creates a policy tension: the National Retrofit Plan [P030] aims to improve airtightness for energy efficiency, but this may increase radon exposure in high-risk areas unless mechanical ventilation with heat recovery (MVHR) is installed simultaneously.

The GSI Tellus programme [P159] provides high-resolution geological data that could, in principle, be linked to both radon risk and building material characteristics. Bedrock geology affects local building traditions (limestone vs granite vs sandstone construction) and hence the thermal properties of older buildings. This cross-domain linkage between energy efficiency, indoor air quality, and geology represents an unexplored research direction for the Irish building stock.

The interaction between building fabric improvements, ventilation strategy, and indoor environmental quality (IEQ) has been studied by Broderick et al. [P208] and Coggins et al. [P122] in the Irish context. Broderick et al. found that energy retrofits generally improve IEQ but can create overheating risk in well-insulated homes with large south-facing windows -- a concern highlighted by Kearney [P209] for the Irish climate, which is experiencing warming trends [P52, P193].
