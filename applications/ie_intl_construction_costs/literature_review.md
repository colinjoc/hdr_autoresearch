# Literature Review: Are Irish Construction Costs Structurally Higher Than Comparable European Countries?

## Theme 1: Construction Cost Measurement and International Comparison Frameworks

The measurement of construction costs across national boundaries presents fundamental methodological challenges that have been examined extensively in the academic and practitioner literature. The Eurostat Short-Term Statistics (STS) framework, codified in Regulation (EU) 2019/2152, requires EU member states to submit quarterly construction cost or producer price indices for new residential buildings (Eurostat, 2019). The STS_COPI_Q dataset distinguishes between two related but distinct concepts: the Construction Cost Index (COST), which tracks input costs (labour and materials) from the builder's perspective, and the Construction Producer Price (PRC_PRR), which tracks the output price charged to clients. Not all countries report both indicators --- Ireland, for instance, reports only PRC_PRR, while Germany reports both. This distinction is substantively important: cost indices measure what builders pay, while producer prices measure what builders charge, and the gap between them reflects margins and market power (Best and Meikle, 2015; Eurostat, 2008).

The base-year index methodology (2015=100 or 2021=100) shows relative change over time within each country but does not permit direct cross-country level comparisons. To compare absolute construction costs across countries, researchers must anchor the indices to external price-level data. The primary academic framework for this is the Purchasing Power Parity (PPP) approach developed for construction by Walsh and Sawhney (2004), which adapts the Eurostat-OECD PPP programme's Bills of Quantities method to construction-specific baskets. The 2023 edition of the Eurostat-OECD Methodological Manual (Eurostat, 2024) provides the current standard methodology, using approximately 15 construction project types as reference baskets. Walsh (2007) documented significant limitations of construction PPPs, including the difficulty of standardising specifications across countries with different building traditions, climates, and regulatory requirements.

The professional cost surveying literature offers an alternative comparison framework through annual international cost surveys. Turner and Townsend's International Construction Market Survey (ICMS) 2024 ranked Dublin as the fourth most expensive city in Europe for commercial construction at EUR 3,692 per square metre, behind Zurich (EUR 4,978), Geneva, and London. The ICMS methodology prices a standardised office building specification across cities, which provides a cleaner like-for-like comparison than aggregate indices but may not reflect residential construction costs. Other professional benchmarks include Arcadis International Construction Costs (2024), Rider Levett Bucknall's quarterly market survey, and the RICS International Construction Measurement Standards (2023), each with different scope, methodology, and coverage. Spon's European Construction Costs Handbook (Davis Langdon/AECOM, 2012) provided comprehensive country-by-country cost data but has not been updated recently.

The reliability of international construction cost data has been questioned. Briscoe (2006) found that even within a single country, construction statistics suffer from inconsistent definitions, survey non-response, and classification changes. Cross-country comparisons compound these problems. Meikle and Best (2004) argued that the combination of different national data collection methods, different definitions of "residential construction," and different VAT/tax treatments means that international construction cost rankings should be interpreted with considerable caution. Ball (2003, 2006) demonstrated that structural differences in national housebuilding industries --- from the prevalence of self-build in Ireland and Germany to the dominance of large developers in the UK and Netherlands --- make simple cost-per-square-metre comparisons misleading unless the product being compared is carefully controlled.

Despite these limitations, the convergence of multiple data sources --- Eurostat indices, Turner and Townsend benchmarks, Arcadis surveys, CBRE cost guides, and national statistical offices --- provides a reasonably robust picture of relative positions. For this study, we use the Eurostat PRC_PRR index (available for all comparator countries) to track relative cost trajectories, anchored to absolute EUR/sqm estimates from industry sources (Buildcost.ie for Ireland, BCIS for the UK, Destatis for Germany) to establish level comparisons.

## Theme 2: Irish Construction Costs and the Housing Crisis

Ireland's construction sector has been the subject of intense policy and media scrutiny since the post-2008 recovery exposed a persistent gap between housing demand and supply. The ESRI (2024) estimated structural housing demand at 44,000 units per year, while completions reached only 30,300 in 2024 --- a 6.7 per cent decrease from 2023 and far below the government's revised target of 50,000 units under the National Planning Framework (Irish Examiner, 2025). The Central Bank of Ireland (2023) documented that construction costs increased approximately 23 per cent from end-2019 to Q3 2023, faster than general consumer price inflation.

The drivers of Irish construction costs are multifaceted. Labour costs are a primary factor: the CSO (2025) reported Irish construction labour productivity at the seventh highest level among EU member states, but hourly labour costs of EUR 34.22 (CSO EHQ03, Q2 2025) are above the EU average. The construction workforce of approximately 170,000 in 2025 remains well below the Celtic Tiger peak of 237,000 (CCL Recruitment, 2025), and with the broader economy at near-full employment (unemployment 4.3 per cent, September 2024), the sector faces acute skills shortages in critical trades including carpenters, plasterers, plumbers, and bricklayers (Sonas Technical, 2025). A new pay deal approved at end-2024 for nearly 50,000 construction workers reflected these pressures, and wages rose a further 10 per cent in Q2 2025 with an additional 3.4 per cent from a Sectoral Employment Order (Irish Times, 2025). With labour representing 30-40 per cent of total build costs, these wage increases added an estimated 1-1.5 per cent to housing costs in H2 2025 alone.

Material costs have been a second major driver, with steel prices surging due to global demand, pandemic-era production cuts, and geopolitical tensions affecting major producing countries (CIS Ireland, 2024). Ireland's island geography creates an import premium on bulk materials that landlocked or mainland European countries do not face. Planning delays represent a third constraint: apartment planning approvals fell 39 per cent from 21,487 in 2023 to 13,194 in 2024 (My Little Home, 2025), creating pipeline uncertainty that increases financing costs and discourages investment.

The Ireland Construction Industry Databook (ResearchAndMarkets, 2025) projected sector expansion from EUR 11.1 billion in 2024 to EUR 15.38 billion by 2029, suggesting continued cost pressures as demand grows. The European Construction Sector Observatory (2024) Country Fact Sheet for Ireland documented the structural mismatch between ambitions and capacity, noting that Ireland's small domestic market limits economies of scale in construction and makes the sector more vulnerable to individual-project cost overruns.

The policy response has included Housing for All (Department of Housing, 2024), increased direct state construction through the Land Development Agency, and various grant schemes. However, the Oireachtas Library (2025) analysis of capacity constraints concluded that without addressing the fundamental labour and skills bottleneck, additional demand-side stimulus risks inflating costs further rather than increasing output --- a finding consistent with the ScienceDirect analysis (2023) of government spending impacts on the housing market.

## Theme 3: Labour Productivity and Skills in Construction

Construction labour productivity has been a persistent concern across developed economies. McKinsey (2017) documented that global construction productivity improved by only 1 per cent per year over 20 years, compared with 2.8 per cent for the total world economy and 3.6 per cent for manufacturing. An updated McKinsey analysis (2024) argued that improving construction productivity is "no longer optional" given housing affordability pressures. The ECB Economic Bulletin (2024) found that the construction sector showed the largest cumulative productivity decline in the euro area between Q4 2019 and Q1 2024, falling approximately 8 per cent as employment and hours worked increased faster than gross value added.

Ireland's position in this landscape is nuanced. The CSO (2025) placed Irish construction labour productivity at seventh highest among EU member states in 2023, behind Belgium (EUR 53.3 per hour), Luxembourg, the Netherlands, France, Austria, and Denmark. However, productivity in construction is notoriously difficult to measure because the output is heterogeneous (each building is different), quality adjustment is imperfect, and the mix of project types changes over time (Crawford and Vogl, 2006; Briscoe, 2006).

The academic literature on construction skills and labour markets is extensive. Chan and Dainty (2007) analysed the UK construction skills crisis, finding that structural changes in the industry --- including the shift to subcontracting, casualisation of labour, and underinvestment in training --- created a persistent skills deficit that could not be resolved by short-term wage increases alone. Similar dynamics are observed in Ireland, where the post-2008 collapse saw an exodus of skilled workers to other sectors and countries, and the subsequent recovery has struggled to rebuild the workforce (CCL Recruitment, 2025; Necto Selection, 2026).

International comparisons of construction labour costs reveal significant variation. Eurostat data show that hourly labour costs in construction range from under EUR 10 in Eastern Europe to over EUR 40 in Scandinavia, with Ireland positioned in the upper-middle tier. The premium that Irish construction workers command relative to general wage levels is lower than in many other European countries, suggesting that high absolute costs partly reflect Ireland's high general wage level rather than a construction-specific premium (Agapiou et al., 1995; Dainty et al., 2007; Loosemore et al., 2003).

Productivity improvement strategies in the academic literature include industrialised building methods (Lessing, 2006), lean construction (Koskela, 2000; Ballard and Howell, 1998; Green and May, 2005), Building Information Modelling (Eastman et al., 2011; Azhar, 2011; Succar, 2009), and Construction 4.0 digital transformation (El-Adaway, 2023; Sawhney et al., 2020). The Farmer Review (2016) of the UK construction labour model recommended a wholesale shift toward offsite manufacturing, but adoption has been slow outside Scandinavia.

## Theme 4: Regulatory Environment and Building Standards

The regulatory environment for construction varies substantially across European countries and contributes to cost differences through both direct compliance costs and indirect effects on productivity, innovation, and market structure. Gyourko and Molloy (2015) provided a comprehensive review of the academic evidence on regulation and housing supply, finding that stricter land-use regulation is consistently associated with higher housing prices, though the magnitude varies. Glaeser and Gyourko (2003) estimated that building restrictions in US metropolitan areas added 20-50 per cent to housing costs in heavily regulated markets.

European building regulations have been compared in several studies. The academia.edu comparison of building permit procedures across EU countries (2004) found wide variation in the number of procedures (from 6 to over 20), the time required (from weeks to months), and the fees charged. A more recent analysis (Tandfonline, 2024) investigating building permit processes across Europe found a dominant trend toward simplification but with persistent national differences. Ireland's planning system has been criticised for delays, with Judicial Reviews of An Bord Pleanala decisions adding months or years to project timelines.

Ireland's building regulations have become progressively more stringent, particularly following the Priory Hall and other building defect scandals. Part L (energy conservation) requirements, nearly Zero Energy Building (nZEB) standards, accessibility requirements, and fire safety standards all add to construction costs. The European Parliament Research Service (2025) analysis of construction and renovation in EU housing markets noted that energy efficiency requirements, while necessary for climate objectives, increase upfront construction costs by an estimated 5-15 per cent depending on the baseline standard.

The Barker Review (2004) of UK housing supply remains a foundational reference, documenting how planning constraints, land availability, and regulatory costs interact to restrict supply and inflate prices. Hilber and Vermeulen (2016) provided econometric evidence that supply constraints in England --- including planning restrictions and physical geography --- explained a substantial portion of house price growth. These dynamics are relevant to Ireland, where geographic constraints (flood zones, heritage areas) combine with regulatory requirements to limit the supply of developable land.

Cross-country variation in VAT treatment of construction also affects cost comparisons. Ireland applies a 13.5 per cent reduced VAT rate to new residential construction, compared with Germany's 19 per cent standard rate, the UK's zero-rating for new-build residential, and France's 20 per cent standard rate (with a reduced rate for social housing). These differences mean that pre-tax construction costs and post-tax costs to the consumer can rank countries differently.

## Theme 5: Offsite and Modular Construction

The adoption of offsite, modular, and prefabricated construction methods has been identified as a key lever for reducing costs and improving productivity. Sweden leads globally, with approximately 80 per cent of single-family housing using factory-prefabricated elements and an overall 45 per cent offsite construction rate (Mordor Intelligence, 2024; Lessing, 2006). This has been facilitated by Sweden's engineering timber industry, standardised building practices, and a construction culture that embraced industrialisation decades ago.

The Netherlands has rapidly expanded modular construction as part of its strategy to deliver 1 million new homes by 2031 (USP Research, 2024). Dutch developers have integrated Building Information Modelling with modular systems to accelerate design and approval processes. Germany is the second-largest European market for prefabricated housing, with 26 per cent of new single and two-family homes prefabricated in 2024, supported by federal subsidies for climate-efficient housing (Mordor Intelligence, 2024).

The European prefabricated housing market is growing at a compound annual growth rate of approximately 7 per cent, with modular homes commanding 43.8 per cent of the market in 2024 (Vantage Market Research, 2025). However, the relationship between modular construction adoption and cost outcomes is not straightforward. Pan et al. (2007) found that UK housebuilders perceived offsite methods as more expensive than traditional construction for small-scale projects, though cost advantages emerged at scale. Steinhardt and Manley (2016) analysed cross-country adoption of prefabricated housing and found that country-specific factors --- including industry structure, regulatory frameworks, building traditions, and consumer preferences --- explained more of the variation in adoption rates than pure cost-benefit calculations.

Ireland's adoption of modular and offsite construction remains low by European standards. Several factors explain this: the dominance of one-off rural housing in the Irish building tradition, the small scale of most Irish housebuilders, planning system uncertainty (which discourages investment in factory capacity), and a skills base oriented toward traditional methods. The Egan Report (1998) and Latham Review (1994) in the UK, and the Farmer Review (2016), all recommended industrialisation of construction, but progress has been slower than hoped even in the UK, which has more favourable conditions for scale.

A key finding from our data analysis is that high modular adoption does not necessarily translate to lower cost growth. Sweden, with 45 per cent offsite construction, saw cost growth of +47.4 per cent from 2015 to 2025 --- higher than Ireland's +41.3 per cent. The Netherlands, despite expanding modular construction, experienced the second-highest cost growth at +71.2 per cent. This suggests that modular construction may improve quality, speed, and predictability without necessarily reducing the rate of cost inflation, which is driven by broader macroeconomic factors (labour markets, material prices, energy costs) that affect modular and traditional construction alike.

## Theme 6: External Shocks --- COVID-19, Ukraine, and Energy Crisis

The period 2020-2023 subjected European construction to a sequence of unprecedented external shocks that disrupted supply chains, inflated material costs, and altered the trajectory of construction cost indices across the continent.

The COVID-19 pandemic (2020-2021) caused immediate disruption through site closures, social distancing requirements that reduced on-site productivity, and supply chain bottlenecks as global manufacturing shut down and restarted unevenly. The impact varied by country: Ireland implemented strict site closures in spring 2020 and again in early 2021, while Germany maintained construction activity as an essential service with fewer interruptions. Our Eurostat data show that during the COVID period (2020-Q1 to 2021-Q4), construction cost growth ranged from +6.6 per cent in France to +13.2 per cent in Austria, with Ireland at +9.6 per cent --- close to the EU-9 average of +9.9 per cent.

The Russian invasion of Ukraine in February 2022 triggered a more severe and sustained shock. Construction News (2022) documented immediate steel and timber price spikes, with steel hot-rolled coil reaching EUR 1,278 per tonne in March 2022 (up EUR 326 month-on-month). Russia had been the world's largest softwood lumber exporter in 2020-2021, and the invasion plus subsequent sanctions reduced global timber shipments from over 31 million cubic metres in 2020 to below 21 million in 2023 (Packaging Europe, 2023). Energy prices surged as European natural gas prices quadrupled, affecting energy-intensive construction materials including steel, aluminium, cement, and brick (Turner and Townsend, 2022).

Euroconstruct (2023) described the combination of material price inflation, energy costs, rising interest rates, and labour shortages as "a perfect storm for European construction." Our data show that the Ukraine/energy period (2022-Q1 to 2023-Q4) produced the most differentiated cost growth across countries: the Netherlands experienced +22.5 per cent growth, Austria +14.4 per cent, Germany +16.6 per cent, while Ireland at +9.9 per cent was below the EU-9 average of +12.5 per cent. Countries with higher energy import dependence and larger manufacturing sectors generally experienced greater material cost pass-through.

The recovery period (2024-Q1 to 2025-Q4) shows stabilisation but continued divergence. France is the only country with negative cost growth (-7.7 per cent), reflecting weakening demand. Most other countries show growth of 1-5 per cent, with Ireland at +3.9 per cent --- slightly above the EU-9 average of +2.0 per cent. This modest above-average recovery growth is consistent with Ireland's strong economic fundamentals and continued housing demand pressure.

## Theme 7: Econometric Methods for Construction Cost Analysis

The econometric analysis of construction cost indices draws on several well-established methodological traditions. Time series analysis, including ARIMA modelling (Box and Jenkins, 1970), has been applied to construction cost index forecasting since the 1990s (Rawlinson and Raftery, 1997). More recent applications have incorporated neural networks (ScienceDirect, 2019), interrupted time series (Springer, 2017), and combined frameworks (MDPI, 2022, 2024). A systematic literature review by Tandfonline (2023) identified VECM, ANN, and ARIMA as the three most frequently used forecasting models for construction cost indices.

Structural break detection is central to our analysis of whether Irish construction cost growth represents a structural shift or a cyclical phenomenon. The Chow test (1960) and Quandt (1960) provided the earliest formal frameworks for detecting breaks at known or unknown points. Bai and Perron (2003) extended this to multiple structural changes, while Killick et al. (2012) developed the PELT (Pruned Exact Linear Time) algorithm that permits efficient detection of multiple changepoints with a linear computational cost. Andrews (1993) and Zeileis et al. (2003) provided practical guidance on testing and dating structural changes. Hansen (2001) applied these methods specifically to US labour productivity, demonstrating their relevance to economic time series with regime changes.

Panel data methods are appropriate for our cross-country comparison framework. Wooldridge (2010), Baltagi (2013), and Hsiao (2014) provide comprehensive treatments of panel data econometrics. The country-fixed-effects model with country-time interactions that we employ follows the standard approach for testing convergence or divergence across units (Barro and Sala-i-Martin, 2004; Durlauf and Quah, 1999). Phillips and Sul (2007) developed transition modelling and convergence tests specifically designed for panel time series, while Bernard and Durlauf (1996) addressed the interpretation of convergence hypothesis tests.

Cluster analysis of country trajectories uses hierarchical methods, with Ward's (1963) minimum-variance criterion being the standard for grouping observations by similarity. Murtagh and Contreras (2012) provided an overview of hierarchical clustering algorithms and their properties. Our application clusters countries by the shape of their cost trajectories (normalised to start at zero), which groups countries by pattern rather than by level --- an important distinction when the question is whether Ireland's cost dynamics resemble those of fast-growing or moderate-growing economies.

Standard diagnostic procedures include unit root tests (Dickey and Fuller, 1979; Phillips and Perron, 1988; Kwiatkowski et al., 1992), cointegration analysis (Engle and Granger, 1987; Johansen, 1991), and heteroscedasticity-robust inference (White, 1980; Newey and West, 1987). For panel data, the Hausman (1978) test informs the choice between fixed and random effects, and Drukker (2003) provides tests for serial correlation. These are standard tools in applied econometrics (Greene, 2018; Stock and Watson, 2020; Hamilton, 1994) and statistical learning (Hastie et al., 2009; James et al., 2013).
