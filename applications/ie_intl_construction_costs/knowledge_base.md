# Knowledge Base: Irish International Construction Cost Comparison

## Established Facts

1. **Ireland's Eurostat PRC_PRR index grew +41.3% from 2015-Q1 to 2025-Q4** (base 2015=100). This is cumulative, not annualised.

2. **Ireland ranks #6 out of 10 EU comparators on cumulative cost growth** (2015-2025). Germany (#1, +74.7%), Netherlands (#2, +71.2%), and Austria (#3, +59.2%) grew much faster. Ireland is mid-pack, not an outlier.

3. **Ireland's absolute construction cost (~EUR 1,975/sqm base) is BELOW the EU-10 average (~EUR 2,017/sqm)**. On absolute level, Ireland ranks #8 out of 11 comparators. The UK (EUR 2,800), Germany (EUR 2,500), and Denmark (EUR 2,400) are all more expensive.

4. **Ireland clusters with Belgium and Sweden** by cost trajectory shape (Ward hierarchical clustering, 3 clusters). It does NOT cluster with the fast-growing DE/NL/AT group.

5. **Ireland's cost growth was BELOW the EU-9 average in every subperiod except Recovery (2024-2025)**:
   - Pre-COVID (2015-2019): IE +9.7% vs EU-9 avg +11.4%
   - COVID (2020-2021): IE +9.6% vs EU-9 avg +9.9%
   - Ukraine/Energy (2022-2023): IE +9.9% vs EU-9 avg +12.5%
   - Recovery (2024-2025): IE +3.9% vs EU-9 avg +2.0% (only period above average)

6. **Ireland's rank position worsened from #8 to #6** on the growth index (2015-2025). It was initially one of the slower-growing countries and moved to mid-table.

7. **The Eurostat construction cost index tracks RELATIVE growth, not absolute levels.** Cross-country level comparisons require anchoring to external absolute data.

8. **UK data stops at 2020-Q3** in the Eurostat PRC_PRR dataset. Post-Brexit, UK no longer reports to Eurostat.

9. **Ireland reports only PRC_PRR (producer prices), not COST (input costs)** in the Eurostat framework. Some countries (DE, DK, FI, AT, ES) report both.

10. **Panel regression R-squared = 0.9328** for the country-fixed-effects model with country-time interactions. Ireland's quarterly slope (1.121 index points/quarter) is significantly below Austria's (1.683), Germany's (1.946), and the Netherlands' (1.955).

## Known Pitfalls

1. **Base-year effect**: Countries that had high costs in 2015 (e.g., DK, UK) show lower percentage growth even if absolute cost increases were similar.

2. **PRC_PRR vs COST**: Producer prices include margins; cost indices do not. Ireland's PRC_PRR may reflect different margin dynamics than comparators' COST indices.

3. **Absolute EUR/sqm figures are approximate**: Drawn from industry sources with different scopes, definitions, and time periods. Use for ranking, not precision.

4. **PPP adjustment changes the picture**: Ireland's high general price level (127% of EU average) means PPP-adjusted construction costs are lower (~EUR 1,555/sqm).

5. **Compositional effects**: Changes in the mix of housing types (apartments vs houses, urban vs rural) affect the index without reflecting pure cost changes.

6. **VAT differences**: Ireland's 13.5% reduced rate for new residential vs UK zero-rating vs Germany's 19% affect consumer-facing but not builder-facing comparisons.

## Key Mechanisms

1. **Labour cost premium**: Ireland's construction hourly rate (EUR 34.22) is ~22% above estimated EU average (~EUR 28), but this reflects Ireland's high general wage level.

2. **Island logistics premium**: Ireland imports materials by sea, adding 5-8% to material costs vs mainland European countries.

3. **Small market scale**: Ireland's total construction output (~EUR 11B) is much smaller than Germany's (~EUR 150B), limiting economies of scale.

4. **Regulatory compliance costs**: nZEB standards, Part L energy requirements, fire safety post-Priory Hall add ~7% to base costs.

5. **Skills shortage**: 170,000 workers vs 237,000 at Celtic Tiger peak creates wage pressure and limits productivity improvement.

## Surprising Findings

1. **Ireland is NOT an outlier on costs** --- it is mid-pack or below average on both absolute level and growth rate.

2. **Modular construction leaders (Sweden, Netherlands) have HIGHER cost growth** than Ireland, contradicting the assumption that industrialised construction controls costs.

3. **The Netherlands had the second-highest cost growth (+71.2%) despite similar housing crisis** to Ireland, suggesting demand-side pressure is a stronger driver than structural factors.

4. **Ireland's cost-to-income ratio (4.0x for 100sqm) is better than Germany's (5.6x)**, reflecting Ireland's higher household incomes.

5. **France shows DECLINING costs** in the recovery period (-7.7%), the only comparator with negative growth --- reflecting weakening demand rather than structural improvement.
