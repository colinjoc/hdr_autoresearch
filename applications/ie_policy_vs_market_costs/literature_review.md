# Literature Review: Policy-Set vs Market-Driven Costs in Irish Residential Development

## Theme 1: Tax Incidence on Housing (VAT, SDLT, Transaction Taxes)

The theoretical framework for understanding how taxes affect housing costs draws from the canonical tax incidence literature. Crossley et al. (2012) establish that value-added tax (VAT) on new residential construction is borne primarily by the buyer in markets where supply is price-inelastic, which characterises most Irish housing markets outside Dublin. The Irish VAT rate on new residential construction stands at 13.5%, applied to the full construction cost including professional fees. This is higher than the UK rate of zero percent on new-build residential (though the UK charges 20% VAT on renovations), lower than the German rate of 19%, and comparable to the French rate of 10% on social housing.

Bettendorf and Cnossen (2015) analyse the incidence of housing taxation across European countries and find that the effective tax burden on new-build housing varies dramatically: from near-zero in the UK (zero-rated new builds) to over 25% in Scandinavian countries when VAT, stamp duty, and registration fees are combined. Ireland sits in the middle of this distribution. The key finding is that in markets with constrained supply, tax reductions on construction do not necessarily translate to lower consumer prices -- they may instead be captured by landowners through higher land prices (a form of tax capitalisation into land values).

The Mirrlees Review (2011) provides the foundational argument that housing taxation should be reformed to tax land values rather than construction activity. Taxing construction via VAT discourages the very activity that increases housing supply, while taxing land values is non-distortionary because the supply of land is perfectly inelastic. This distinction is central to the Irish policy debate: VAT on construction is a tax on supply, while the Residential Zoned Land Tax (RZLT, introduced 2024) is a tax on hoarding.

Best and Kleven (2018) study the UK Stamp Duty Land Tax using a bunching methodology and find large real responses to transaction taxes -- housing transactions decline sharply at notch points. While SDLT applies to secondhand transactions rather than new builds, the finding is relevant because transaction taxes affect the price that developers can charge for new builds. Dachis et al. (2012) find similar results for Canadian land transfer taxes.

Hilber and Lyytikäinen (2017) examine property transfer taxes and household mobility, finding that transfer taxes reduce mobility by 2-3 percentage points, which has implications for labour market efficiency. The Help to Buy scheme (Revenue Commissioners, 2023) and First Home scheme (HFA, 2023) partially offset the tax burden on first-time buyers of new builds, effectively recycling some of the VAT revenue back to buyers. Lyons (2019) argues that the net tax burden on new housing in Ireland, after accounting for these demand-side subsidies, is lower than the headline VAT rate suggests.

Glaeser and Gyourko (2018) provide the theoretical framework for understanding how tax policy interacts with housing supply elasticity. In markets where regulatory constraints bind (as in most Irish local authorities), tax reductions may be fully capitalised into land prices rather than reducing the price of new housing. This is the fundamental challenge facing any proposal to reduce VAT on construction: without addressing supply constraints simultaneously, the benefit may flow to landowners rather than to housing consumers.

The OECD (2021) comparative study of housing VAT across member countries finds no clear correlation between VAT rates and housing supply volumes when controlling for other policy variables. Countries with zero-rated new builds (UK) do not consistently outperform countries with standard-rated new builds (Germany, France) on housing completions per capita. This suggests that VAT policy alone is not a decisive determinant of housing supply, though it is one component of the overall cost burden.

The Department of Finance (2019, 2022) and Revenue Commissioners (2023, 2024) provide the Irish-specific data on construction taxation. The combined tax burden on a new dwelling includes VAT at 13.5%, employer PRSI on construction labour (at 11.05%), the Construction Industry Withholding Tax (relevant to subcontractors), and the recently introduced Construction Waste Levy (EPA, 2023). De la Feria (2013) analyses how these multiple layers of taxation interact and finds that the effective marginal tax rate on construction activity in Ireland is comparable to the EU average but higher than in the UK, which benefits from zero-rating.

The macroprudential rules of the Central Bank of Ireland (2022) also function as an implicit tax on development: by capping mortgage lending at 3.5 times income and requiring 10% deposits, they constrain the effective demand for new housing, which in turn limits the sale prices that developers can achieve. Lyons (2018) estimates that these rules reduced Dublin house prices by 10-15% relative to a counterfactual without them, directly reducing the revenue side of the viability equation.

## Theme 2: Affordable Housing Obligations (Part V, Section 106, Inclusionary Zoning)

The international literature on mandatory affordable housing obligations is extensive. Calavita and Mallach (2010) provide the comprehensive overview of inclusionary zoning across the United States, finding that the typical obligation ranges from 10% to 25% of units, with 15% being the most common requirement. Ireland's Part V obligation at 20% sits at the high end of international practice.

The English Section 106 system (Crook et al., 2016; Burgess et al., 2013) is the closest international comparator to Ireland's Part V. Under Section 106 of the Town and Country Planning Act 1990, developers negotiate affordable housing contributions as a condition of planning permission. The key difference from Ireland's Part V is that Section 106 contributions are negotiable and subject to viability assessment, whereas Part V is a flat 20% mandatory obligation with limited scope for viability-based exemption. Whitehead (2007) argues that this inflexibility makes Part V more distortionary than Section 106 because it cannot adapt to local market conditions.

Norris and Shiels (2007) provide the earliest comprehensive assessment of Part V's impact on the Irish housing market. They find that during the Celtic Tiger era, Part V was widely circumvented through buyout provisions that allowed developers to make cash payments in lieu of providing affordable units. The 2015 Urban Regeneration and Housing Act reformed Part V to require the transfer of completed units rather than financial contributions, significantly increasing the per-unit cost to developers.

Corrigan and Watson (2018) at the ESRI estimate that Part V adds approximately 5-8% to the cost of each market-rate unit in a development, depending on the gap between the cost price (at which Part V units are transferred) and the market price. In high-value locations like Dublin, this gap is larger, making Part V more costly per unit. Conversely, in areas where market prices are close to cost price, Part V has minimal impact because the cost-to-market gap is small.

Bento et al. (2009) study the impact of inclusionary requirements on development feasibility in the United States and find that mandatory affordable housing requirements reduce the total number of housing starts by 7-10% in affected jurisdictions, with the effect concentrated in lower-margin markets. This is directly relevant to Ireland, where many counties outside Dublin are already at or below the viability threshold.

Schuetz et al. (2011) reach a similar conclusion: mandatory inclusionary zoning reduces overall housing production and increases prices for market-rate units, partially offsetting the affordable housing gain. The net effect on total affordable housing availability (including naturally occurring affordable housing that is priced out) may be negative in some markets.

Thaden and Wang (2017) at the Lincoln Institute of Land Policy argue that the negative effects of inclusionary zoning are overstated in the literature and that well-designed programs can deliver substantial affordable housing without significantly constraining market-rate supply. Their key finding is that programs with density bonuses, fee waivers, or expedited permitting partially offset the cost of inclusionary requirements. Ireland's Part V does not include such offsetting provisions.

The Affordable Housing Act 2021 broadened the scope of Part V to include a cost-rental component, further increasing the potential per-unit cost to developers. The Housing Commission (2023) recommended reviewing the 20% Part V rate in the context of development viability, noting that the current rate was set during a period of much higher house prices relative to construction costs.

The international evidence suggests that the optimal affordable housing obligation rate balances three objectives: maximising affordable unit delivery, maintaining development viability, and minimising distortionary effects on market-rate supply. The Irish Part V rate of 20% is at the high end of international practice and, combined with other policy costs, may push many developments below the viability threshold.

## Theme 3: Development Contributions Theory and Practice

Development contributions (also called impact fees, developer charges, or infrastructure levies) are payments by developers to local authorities to fund the public infrastructure required by new development. The theoretical justification, as set out by Ihlanfeldt and Shaughnessy (2004), is that new development imposes costs on existing infrastructure (roads, water, sewerage, parks, community facilities) and developers should internalise these costs.

In Ireland, development contributions are set by each local authority under Section 48 of the Planning and Development Act 2000. The SCSI (2019) reports that rates vary dramatically across local authorities, from approximately EUR 6,000 per unit in some rural counties to over EUR 25,000 per unit in parts of Dublin. This variation reflects both the genuine differences in infrastructure costs across locations and the policy discretion exercised by each local authority.

Nelson et al. (2012) provide the comprehensive textbook treatment of development impact fees. Their key finding is that impact fees are capitalised into land values in competitive markets: when a fee is imposed, the price that developers are willing to pay for land decreases by approximately the present value of the fee, leaving the total development cost unchanged. This capitalisation argument suggests that development contributions do not increase housing costs in the long run -- they reduce land prices instead.

However, Mathur et al. (2004) find that capitalisation is only partial in practice: approximately 50-80% of impact fees are absorbed by land values, with the remainder passed through to housing consumers as higher prices. In Ireland, where land markets are thin and transaction volumes are low, capitalisation may be even less complete because landowners have stronger bargaining power relative to developers.

Yinger (1998) provides the foundational theoretical analysis of development charge incidence. He shows that the incidence depends on the relative elasticities of housing supply and demand: when supply is inelastic (as in tightly regulated Irish markets), more of the charge falls on landowners; when demand is inelastic (as in areas with strong population growth), more falls on housing consumers.

The Department of Housing (2022) published guidelines for local authority development contribution schemes, emphasising that rates should reflect actual infrastructure costs and should not be used as a general revenue source. The NESC (2018) report on land value capture argues that development contributions should be higher, not lower, and should capture more of the uplift in land value that results from public infrastructure investment and planning decisions.

McAllister et al. (2018) study the interaction between infrastructure levies and development viability in the English context and find that viability is most sensitive to development contributions in marginal locations -- precisely the areas where new housing supply is most needed. This finding translates directly to Ireland, where the gap between achievable sale prices and development costs is largest in the counties outside Dublin and the commuter belt.

The Local Infrastructure Housing Activation Fund (LIHAF, Dept of Housing, 2021) and Serviced Sites Fund (Dept of Housing, 2022) represent the government's recognition that infrastructure costs can be a binding constraint on development viability. These funds effectively subsidise the infrastructure component of development cost, reducing the effective development contribution rate for eligible schemes.

Evans (2004) and Dunne (2015) compare development charge systems across jurisdictions and find that Ireland's system is middling by international standards: lower than the English Community Infrastructure Levy (CIL) in high-value locations but higher than the typical US impact fee in low-value locations. The key structural difference is that CIL rates are set by an independent examiner based on viability evidence, while Irish development contribution rates are set by elected councillors with limited viability analysis.

Kehoe (2020) reviews development contribution practice in Ireland and identifies several anomalies: some local authorities charge identical rates for houses and apartments despite the different infrastructure requirements; some charge per unit regardless of dwelling size; and some have not updated their rates in over a decade, meaning that real rates have declined with inflation while nominal rates remain unchanged.

## Theme 4: BCAR Compliance Costs and Building Regulations

The Building Control (Amendment) Regulations 2014 (SI 9 of 2014, commonly called BCAR) introduced a mandatory certification system for new buildings in Ireland. Under BCAR, every new building requires an assigned certifier (typically an architect or building surveyor) who must inspect the construction at defined stages and certify compliance with building regulations. The DECLG (2014) impact assessment estimated that BCAR would add EUR 3,000-5,000 per dwelling in compliance costs.

The SCSI (2020) survey of actual BCAR compliance costs found that the true cost is higher than initially estimated: EUR 4,500-8,000 per dwelling, depending on the complexity of the building and the location. Apartment developments incur lower per-unit BCAR costs due to economies of scale in certification, while one-off houses incur higher costs because the assigned certifier must make the same number of site visits regardless of the number of units being certified.

Duffy (2019) at the RIAI (Royal Institute of the Architects of Ireland) argues that BCAR has improved building quality but at a significant cost to housing affordability. The pre-BCAR system relied on builder self-certification, which contributed to the defective-concrete-blocks crisis (Government of Ireland, 2022) and the pyrite crisis (PRB, 2023). The cost of remediation for these defects far exceeds the per-unit cost of BCAR compliance, suggesting that BCAR is cost-effective from a lifecycle perspective even if it increases upfront costs.

Ball (2011) studies the relationship between building regulations and construction costs across European countries and finds that regulatory compliance costs account for 3-8% of total construction costs, with the variation driven primarily by the complexity of the regulatory framework and the efficiency of the enforcement system. Ireland falls in the middle of this range.

Gyourko and Molloy (2015) in the NBER Handbook chapter on building regulation costs take a broader view, arguing that the total regulatory burden on housing includes not just direct compliance costs (such as BCAR) but also indirect costs: delays in obtaining permits, uncertainty about regulatory changes, and the cumulative effect of multiple overlapping regulations. They estimate that the total regulatory burden adds 15-25% to housing costs in heavily regulated US markets, though this includes zoning and land-use regulation as well as building codes.

Malpezzi (1996) provides the classic analysis of building standards and housing affordability, arguing that there is an optimal level of regulation that balances consumer protection (preventing defective buildings) against affordability (minimising compliance costs). Over-regulation reduces housing supply and increases prices; under-regulation leads to defective buildings and high remediation costs. The Irish experience with both pyrite (under-regulation) and BCAR (potentially over-regulation) illustrates both sides of this trade-off.

The NZEB (Nearly Zero Energy Building) standard, mandatory for all new dwellings from November 2019, adds further compliance costs. The SEAI (2019) estimates that NZEB compliance adds EUR 5,000-15,000 per dwelling in additional construction costs (better insulation, triple glazing, heat pumps, mechanical ventilation with heat recovery). Szalay and Zold (2014) find similar cost increases for NZEB compliance across European countries.

However, the predecessor project C-1 found that NZEB was NOT a significant driver of construction cost increases -- the 4% per annum cost growth was driven primarily by materials and labour inflation rather than regulatory compliance. This finding is consistent with the SEAI data showing that NZEB costs have declined as the supply chain has adapted: heat pump costs have fallen 20-30% since 2019, and NZEB-compliant building envelopes are now standard practice for Irish builders.

The PwC/DECLG (2017) cost-benefit analysis of BCAR found that the net present value of the regulation was positive over a 30-year horizon, primarily because the cost of remediating defective buildings (estimated at EUR 2.5 billion for the pyrite crisis alone) far exceeds the cumulative BCAR compliance costs. This finding does not change the fact that BCAR adds to upfront development costs, but it suggests that eliminating BCAR would not be a net benefit to society even if it reduced per-unit costs.

## Theme 5: Construction Costs and Productivity

The construction cost data for Ireland comes from three primary sources: the CSO Wholesale Price Index for Building Materials (WPM28), the CSO Earnings series for construction labour (EHQ03), and the Buildcost.ie Construction Cost Guide (H1 2025). Together, these paint a picture of an industry experiencing persistent above-inflation cost growth.

The WPM28 index shows that building material prices increased by approximately 35% between 2015 and 2024, with a sharp spike during 2021-2022 driven by supply chain disruptions and energy price increases. The current index value suggests that material price inflation has moderated to approximately 2-3% per annum, below the 4% annual rate identified in predecessor project C-1.

Labour costs, as measured by the EHQ03 earnings data, have grown more rapidly: approximately 4-5% per annum since 2019, driven by labour shortages in the construction sector. SOLAS (2023) estimates a shortfall of 27,000 construction workers relative to the Housing for All target of 33,000 completions per year. This labour shortage puts persistent upward pressure on wages, with skilled trades (electricians, plumbers, plasterers) commanding premiums of 15-25% above general construction labour rates.

The Buildcost.ie H1 2025 Construction Cost Guide provides the most granular data on current construction costs, broken down by dwelling type and region. The guide reports tender prices (the price a developer pays to a contractor to build a dwelling) ranging from EUR 2,432/sqm for a 3-bed terraced house in the North West to EUR 3,300/sqm for a 4-bed detached house in Dublin. These figures include the contractor's overhead and profit but exclude land, professional fees, VAT, and developer margin.

Turner and Townsend (2024) place Irish construction costs in the upper third of their international construction cost index, approximately 25% above the EU average. The AECOM (2024) European Construction Costs Monitor confirms this ranking. Deloitte (2023) finds that Irish residential construction costs per square metre are higher than in the UK, Germany, France, and Spain, but lower than in Switzerland, Norway, and Denmark.

The construction productivity literature is damning: the Farmer Review (2016) documents that construction productivity growth has been near-zero in most developed countries for the past 50 years, while manufacturing productivity has roughly tripled. The OECD (2020) Economic Survey of Ireland identified construction sector productivity as a binding constraint on housing supply.

McKinsey Global Institute (2017) estimates that widespread adoption of modular and off-site construction methods could reduce construction costs by 20-30% and construction time by 30-50%. However, the Irish modular construction sector remains small: Enterprise Ireland (2020) estimates that fewer than 5% of Irish housing completions use modular methods, compared to 10-15% in the UK and 20-30% in Scandinavian countries.

The Irish Timber Frame Manufacturers Association (ITFMA, 2023) reports that timber frame construction, which accounts for approximately 30% of Irish new-build houses, delivers a cost saving of 5-10% relative to traditional block-and-mortar construction, primarily through faster build times. However, timber frame apartments are less common due to fire safety concerns and the higher cost of fire-rated timber frame systems.

## Theme 6: Development Viability Assessment

The viability testing literature provides the analytical framework for this project's central question. Crosby et al. (2013) define development viability as the condition where "the value of a development is more than the cost of development, including a reasonable profit margin for the developer." The RICS (2019) formalises this as the residual land value method: development is viable when the residual land value (sale value minus all development costs minus developer profit) exceeds the existing use value of the land.

McAllister (2017) critiques the viability system in planning, arguing that viability assessments are inherently subjective because key inputs (construction costs, sale prices, developer margin, finance costs) are uncertain and their values depend on the assumptions made by the assessor. Different assumptions about the developer margin alone (15% vs 20% of gross development value) can flip a development from viable to unviable.

The SCSI (2021) Viability Study of apartment development in Dublin found that apartment development was unviable at prevailing sale prices in all Dublin postcodes except Dublin 2, 4, and 6, even before applying Part V and development contributions. The CIF/SCSI (2022) joint report recommended reducing the apartment design standards (minimum floor areas, dual aspect requirements, car parking ratios) to improve viability, some of which were subsequently adopted.

Bowie (2017) argues that the viability system has been captured by developers who use viability claims to reduce their affordable housing obligations. In the English context, viability assessments submitted by developers systematically overstate costs and understate revenues to justify lower Section 106 contributions. While Ireland does not have a formal viability assessment process for Part V, the underlying incentive structure is the same.

The residual land value method (Wyatt, 2013) is the standard valuation approach for development sites. Under this method, land value = gross development value minus construction costs minus developer profit minus planning obligations. The implication is that planning obligations (including Part V, development contributions, and compliance costs) reduce land values rather than increasing housing prices, at least in theory. The empirical evidence (Mathur et al., 2004) suggests partial capitalisation at best.

The predecessor project U-2 established that the viability frontier for Irish counties runs roughly along the commuter belt boundary: Dublin and the immediately adjacent counties (Kildare, Meath, Wicklow) are at or near the viability threshold, while counties further from Dublin are progressively more unviable. The U-2 finding that Dublin apartment development is viable only at +5.1% annual price growth highlights the extreme sensitivity of viability to relatively small changes in costs or revenues.

Henneberry et al. (2019) provide the theoretical framework for understanding viability as a dynamic rather than static concept. Viability depends not just on current costs and prices but on expected future price growth, which determines whether a developer will build now or wait. In a market with expected price growth, marginal developments become viable because the expected sale price exceeds the current cost. This is why Irish housing completions tend to be procyclical: when prices are rising, more developments cross the viability threshold.

The O'Devaney Gardens (DCC, 2020), Oscar Traynor Road (DCC, 2021), and Clonburris SDZ (SDCC, 2019) case studies illustrate the viability challenge in practice. Each required significant state intervention (either direct state funding, cross-subsidy from commercial development, or reduced planning obligations) to achieve viability. Without such intervention, these strategically important housing sites would not have been developed.

## Theme 7: International Comparisons and Cross-Domain Insights

The international comparison of housing policy costs provides crucial context for the Irish case. The UK system of zero-rating new-build residential for VAT purposes is the most frequently cited comparator (Barker Review, 2004). Under the UK system, a new house attracts zero percent VAT, while renovation attracts 20% VAT. This creates a perverse incentive to demolish and rebuild rather than renovate, but it also means that the VAT component of new housing costs is zero, a saving of 13.5 percentage points relative to Ireland.

The German system charges standard VAT (19%) on new construction but offsets this through a large social housing sector (Kofner, 2002) and strong tenant protections that maintain a large private rental sector. The Dutch model (Elsinga and Wassenberg, 2014) combines a large social housing sector (~30% of all housing) with moderate construction VAT and strong spatial planning. The Vienna model (Forster and Menking, 2016) provides social housing at scale through municipal ownership and construction, effectively bypassing the development viability question entirely.

The US Low-Income Housing Tax Credit (LIHTC) program (Eriksen and Lang, 2019) represents a fundamentally different approach: rather than imposing costs on market-rate development (as Part V does), the LIHTC provides a tax subsidy for affordable housing construction. This avoids the viability distortion of inclusionary requirements but requires ongoing fiscal expenditure.

The Canadian Housing Accelerator Fund (CMHC, 2023) and the Australian Housing Accord (Australian Government, 2023) represent recent policy innovations that address both the cost and supply sides of the housing equation. Both programs condition federal funding on local government reforms that reduce regulatory barriers to housing supply, in effect exchanging supply-side reform for direct cost subsidy.

The New Zealand experience (Johnson et al., 2018) is particularly relevant: New Zealand experienced a similar housing cost crisis in the 2010s and responded with a combination of zoning reform (enabling higher density), tax reform (extending the bright-line test for capital gains), and direct state construction (through Kainga Ora). The results have been mixed: Auckland housing completions increased significantly after the zoning reform, but prices continued to rise due to persistent demand-supply imbalance.

The international evidence supports several conclusions relevant to the Irish case. First, VAT on new construction is the single largest policy-set cost component in most countries, and countries that zero-rate new construction (UK) do deliver somewhat more housing per capita than countries that charge standard rates. Second, affordable housing obligations (Part V equivalents) are less important for overall viability than VAT but can be the marginal factor in borderline-viable developments. Third, the total regulatory burden (building regulations, planning processes, environmental requirements) is more important for housing supply than any single tax or levy. Fourth, direct state construction (as in Vienna or Singapore) sidesteps the viability question entirely but requires sustained fiscal commitment.

The CPO (Compulsory Purchase Order) mechanism (Law Reform Commission, 2019) and the Land Development Agency (LDA Act 2021, LDA 2022) represent potential game-changers for the land cost component. If the state can acquire development land at existing use value (agricultural price) rather than development value, the land cost component drops by 80-90%, fundamentally changing the viability equation. The NESC (2022) report on active land management supports this approach but notes the legal and political constraints on CPO use.

The Residential Zoned Land Tax (RZLT, Revenue Commissioners, 2024) takes a different approach to land costs: rather than reducing the price of land directly, it penalises landowners who hold zoned residential land without developing it, incentivising either development or sale. The expected effect is to increase land supply, which should reduce prices through competition. However, Coyne (2019) at NERI notes that land hoarding is only one factor constraining supply: even if all zoned land were brought to market, development viability would still require achievable sale prices to exceed total development costs.
