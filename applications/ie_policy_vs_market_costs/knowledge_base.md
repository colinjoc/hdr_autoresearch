# Knowledge Base: Policy vs Market Costs in Irish Residential Development

## Established Facts

1. Irish VAT on new residential construction = 13.5% (Revenue Commissioners)
2. UK zero-rates new-build residential for VAT (0%) -- the most favourable comparator
3. Part V obligation = 20% of units at cost price (Planning & Development Act 2000)
4. Development contributions vary EUR 6k-25k per unit across local authorities (SCSI 2019)
5. BCAR compliance costs = EUR 4,500-8,000 per dwelling (SCSI 2020)
6. Planning fees are trivially small: EUR 65 per house, ~EUR 1,500 all-in with fire/disability certs
7. Hard construction costs = ~53% of sale price nationally (SCSI benchmark)
8. Dublin apartment development is NOT viable without price growth of ~5.1%/yr (U-2 predecessor)
9. Materials inflation ~4%/yr, labour inflation ~4-5%/yr (C-1 predecessor, CSO data)
10. Ireland needs ~93,000 homes/yr vs ~30,000 completions (Davy 2024 estimate)
11. Construction productivity has been flat for 50 years (Farmer Review, OECD)
12. Modular construction could reduce costs 20-30% but accounts for <5% of Irish output
13. Developer margin benchmark = 15-20% of GDV (SCSI viability standard)
14. Finance costs = 7-10% of development cost (commercial bank rates 2024)
15. Land prices per hectare range from EUR 173k (Cavan) to EUR 1.59M (Dublin) (RZLPA02)

## Key Findings from Model

16. Policy-set costs account for 13-17% of total development cost across all locations
17. VAT is the single largest policy cost component (~8% of total cost)
18. Zero counties are viable for speculative 3-bed semi development at current costs
19. Zeroing ALL policy costs makes only 4/26 counties viable (Dublin, Wicklow, Kildare, Meath)
20. VAT zeroed alone achieves the same result as ALL policy halved (3 counties viable)
21. The viability gap ranges from EUR 23k (Wicklow) to EUR 226k (Longford) per unit
22. Market costs (materials, labour, land, finance, margin) constitute >83% of total cost
23. The housing viability crisis is fundamentally a MARKET cost problem, not a policy cost problem

## Known Pitfalls

- Tax capitalisation: VAT reduction may flow to landowners, not buyers (Crossley et al.)
- Partial capitalisation: development contributions only 50-80% capitalised into land (Mathur et al.)
- Viability assessments are subjective: 15% vs 20% margin flips viable to unviable (McAllister)
- BCAR costs have declined as industry adapts; initial estimates were high
- Modular construction savings are theoretical; Irish scale is insufficient for economies
- International comparisons misleading without controlling for quality standards and climate
- Land price data from RZLPA02 has high variance; county medians based on few transactions
