# Knowledge Base: Housing Policy Lever Interactions

## Established Facts

1. **Current completions ~35,000/yr** against HFA target of 50,500 — a gap of ~15,500 units/yr (CSO 2023, DHLGH 2021).
2. **Viability margin is negative** for Dublin houses (-3.1%, U-2) and deeply negative for commuter belt (-9 to -11%, U-2). Most sites are unviable at current costs.
3. **Viability-application correlation r=0.91** (U-1): viability is the dominant driver of new housing applications. Cost reductions translate almost linearly to application volume.
4. **Pipeline duration 962 days** (PL-1): nearly 3 years from application to completion. Finance carry costs over this period add ~11% to total cost.
5. **Build-yield 59.6%** (S-1): only ~60% of granted permissions result in completed dwellings. The 40% gap represents lapsed permissions, stalled sites, and partial completions.
6. **Capacity ceiling ~35,000/yr** (S-3): the construction industry cannot currently deliver more than ~35,000 units/yr regardless of demand.
7. **Hard costs = 53% of total** (SCSI/C-1): modular construction's cost-reduction potential is proportional to this share.
8. **VAT at 13.5%** adds ~12% to pre-VAT cost. UK zero-rates new build.
9. **Part V (20% social housing obligation)** adds ~3.9% to private development cost via cross-subsidy.
10. **Finance at 7% on 60% drawdown** over 2.6 years = ~11% of total cost.
11. **Developer margin 15%** (private) vs 6% (social housing).
12. **Land cost ~18% of total** in Dublin. CPO to agricultural value saves ~16%.
13. **Materials CAGR 3.99%, Labour CAGR 4.03%** (C-1): cost escalation partially offsets any cost reduction within ~2 years.

## Known Pitfalls

1. **Ceiling binding**: any analysis ignoring the capacity ceiling overstates the effect of demand-side levers.
2. **Additive vs multiplicative**: cost reductions from different levers applied to the same cost base are additive in euros but the viability effect is nonlinear (threshold at zero).
3. **Double-counting finance and duration**: duration reduction saves on finance carry, but a separate finance rate cut also saves. When both apply simultaneously, the interaction is sub-additive on the finance cost component.
4. **Absorption rate ignored**: the model assumes all completed units are sold/occupied. If supply exceeds 50,000+, absorption may constrain prices and margins.
5. **General-equilibrium effects**: large supply increases would reduce prices, compressing margins and potentially reducing viability gains. Not modeled in the base case.
6. **Political feasibility varies enormously**: VAT cut to 0% (€1.4B+ revenue loss) is politically difficult; BCAR halved is technically feasible but safety-sensitive.
7. **Scale effects uncertain**: higher volume could either reduce costs (factory investment, learning curve) or increase costs (demand-driven inflation in materials/labour). Net direction is unknown.

## Stylised Facts from Literature

- Supply elasticity varies 10x across regulatory regimes (Saiz 2010).
- Planning delay reduces starts by ~10% per year of additional delay (Ball 2011).
- Modular construction can reduce costs 10-30% and time 20-50% (McKinsey 2017, Bertram et al 2019).
- Fiscal multipliers are state-dependent: 3x in recession, ~1x in expansion (Romer & Romer 2010).
- Build-out rates on large sites average 15.5% of capacity per year (Letwin 2018).
- Relaxing supply constraints in most-regulated areas reduces prices 25-35% (Hilber & Vermeulen 2016).
- Irish construction capacity could expand 20-30% with targeted workforce investment (IGEES 2022).
