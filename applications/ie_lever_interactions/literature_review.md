# Literature Review: Housing Policy Lever Interactions and Multiplier Effects in Ireland

## Theme 1: Housing Supply Economics and the Elasticity of Supply Response

The theoretical foundations of housing supply economics rest on the observation that housing production responds to price signals with significant lags, constrained by land availability, planning regulations, and construction capacity (DiPasquale & Wheaton, 1996; Mayer & Somerville, 2000). The price elasticity of new housing supply varies dramatically across jurisdictions, ranging from near-zero in heavily regulated markets to above 2.0 in permissive ones (Saiz, 2010; Green, Malpezzi & Mayo, 2005). This elasticity is the fundamental parameter determining whether demand-side interventions (subsidies, tax credits) translate into additional units or merely into higher prices.

Malpezzi and Maclennan (2001) established that the long-run price elasticity of new residential construction depends critically on the regulatory environment, not just on physical constraints. Their cross-country comparison showed that restrictive planning regimes systematically reduce supply elasticity, creating a policy feedback loop: regulation reduces supply responsiveness, which amplifies price volatility, which generates political pressure for more regulation. Glaeser, Gyourko and Saks (2005) extended this finding to US metropolitan areas, showing that the gap between construction costs and house prices — the "regulatory tax" — had grown from near-zero in the 1960s to over 50% of house prices in constrained markets by 2000.

For Ireland specifically, the supply elasticity underwent a dramatic shift across the Celtic Tiger period. During the boom (1997-2007), Ireland's housing supply was among the most elastic in the OECD, with completions reaching 93,000 in 2006 — a per-capita rate roughly three times the EU average (Caldera & Johansson, 2013). The crash revealed that this elasticity was not structural but was driven by speculative demand and lax lending (Honohan, 2010). Post-crash, the supply system seized up: completions fell to 4,575 in 2013, and the subsequent recovery has been painfully slow despite sustained price growth (CSO, 2023). The current output of approximately 35,000 completions per year remains well below the Housing for All (HFA) target of 50,500 (DHLGH, 2021).

Hilber and Vermeulen (2016) provide the most directly relevant international evidence for Ireland's predicament, showing that in England, both geographic and regulatory constraints reduce the house-price elasticity of supply, with regulatory constraints being quantitatively more important. Their key finding — that relaxing supply constraints in the most regulated areas would reduce house prices by 25-35% — establishes an upper bound on the potential impact of planning reform. However, their model assumes a single constraint operates at a time, which misses the interaction effects that are central to this study.

Paciorek (2013) introduced a dynamic model of supply constraints that explicitly models the interaction between regulatory delay and construction capacity. His model predicts that the combination of slow permitting and limited construction capacity produces a larger supply constraint than either factor alone — an early hint at the super-additive interaction effects this study investigates. The Irish context provides a natural test case: pipeline duration of 962 days (PL-1) combined with a capacity ceiling of approximately 35,000 units per year (S-3) creates exactly the dual-constraint regime Paciorek's model predicts should generate the largest interaction effects.

The concept of viability margins — the gap between achievable revenue and total development costs — is central to understanding why housing supply responds to cost reductions in a highly nonlinear way (Crosby & Wyatt, 2016; McAllister, Wyatt & Coleman, 2013). When viability margins are negative (as they are for Dublin houses at -3.1%, per U-2), marginal cost reductions that merely reduce the loss are much less effective than reductions that flip the margin to positive. This threshold effect creates the potential for large interaction effects: two levers that individually leave viability negative but jointly flip it positive would show a super-additive combined effect on applications and completions.

Somerville (1999) and Topel and Rosen (1988) established the empirical relationship between construction costs and new housing supply, finding that a 10% reduction in real construction costs increases new starts by 5-15% in the long run. The wide range reflects variation in other binding constraints — a finding directly relevant to this study's core question about which constraints bind simultaneously and how relaxing multiple constraints interacts.

## Theme 2: Policy Interaction Effects in Housing and Urban Economics

The literature on housing policy interactions is surprisingly thin relative to the literature on individual policy effects. Most housing policy evaluations treat interventions in isolation: the effect of planning reform (Ball, 2011; Barker, 2004), the effect of tax changes (OECD, 2022), or the effect of building regulation (Gann, Wang & Hawkins, 1998). Few studies examine what happens when multiple policies change simultaneously, despite the fact that real policy packages always involve multiple levers.

The Barker Review (2004) and the Letwin Review (2018) represent the closest approximations to multi-lever analysis in the UK context. Barker argued that planning reform alone was insufficient without complementary investment in infrastructure and workforce capacity — an implicit recognition that policy levers interact. Letwin focused specifically on build-out rates, finding that developers restrict output to maintain prices (the "absorption rate" constraint), which creates a ceiling on completions independent of planning permissions. Letwin's finding that build-out rates averaged 15.5% of total site capacity per year implies that even with unlimited permissions, completions on any given site are constrained by market absorption. This introduces a third constraint (alongside planning and capacity) that may interact with the others.

Hsieh and Moretti (2019) provide the strongest theoretical case for policy interaction effects in housing. Their general-equilibrium spatial model shows that housing supply constraints in productive cities (San Francisco, New York) reduce aggregate US GDP by 36%. Critically, their model shows that relaxing constraints in a single city produces a much smaller effect than relaxing constraints in all constrained cities simultaneously — a macro-level interaction effect driven by labor mobility across cities. While their analysis is at the inter-city level rather than the intra-project level of this study, the underlying mechanism (complementary constraints that each partially neutralize the other's relaxation) is identical.

Glaeser and Gyourko (2018) synthesize the evidence on housing supply constraints and argue that the binding constraint varies across markets: in some, it is land; in others, regulation; in still others, construction costs. Their framework implies that the most effective policy package depends on which constraints are binding locally — a prediction that this study can test for Ireland by identifying which lever combinations produce the largest interaction terms.

The fiscal multiplier literature provides a methodological template for analyzing policy interactions (Blanchard & Perotti, 2002; Nakamura & Steinsson, 2014; Ramey, 2019). Romer and Romer (2010) showed that the output multiplier for tax changes depends critically on the economic environment: tax cuts during recessions have multipliers above 3, while those during expansions have multipliers near 1. This state-dependence is analogous to the viability-dependence in housing: cost reductions that push viability from negative to positive have a much larger multiplier than those that merely reduce an already-positive margin.

Moretti (2010) and Kline and Moretti (2014) established the "local multiplier" framework, showing that each additional job in the tradeable sector generates 1.6 additional jobs in the non-tradeable sector. The housing construction sector operates as a local multiplier on the supply side: each additional housing completion generates demand for local services, which generates further housing demand, creating a positive feedback loop. This third-order effect is modeled in this study as the optional "scale economies" loop.

The concept of policy complementarity from the development economics literature (Kline & Moretti, 2014, on "Big Push" multipliers) is directly applicable. A "big push" occurs when multiple complementary investments are made simultaneously, producing aggregate returns that exceed the sum of individual returns. The housing policy analogy is clear: reducing costs, speeding planning, expanding capacity, and lowering taxes simultaneously may produce a policy "big push" whose combined effect exceeds the sum of individual lever effects.

## Theme 3: Systems Dynamics Modeling of Housing Supply

Systems dynamics (SD) provides the natural modeling framework for housing supply feedback loops. Sterman's (2000) canonical textbook establishes the core methodology: identify stocks, flows, feedback loops, and delays; build a simulation model; validate against historical data; and use the model to test policy interventions. Meadows (2008) provides the conceptual foundation for identifying leverage points in complex systems — places where small policy interventions produce large system-wide effects.

Forrester's (1969) Urban Dynamics was the first application of SD to housing markets, modeling the interactions between housing, employment, population, and land use in a single urban area. Despite its age, the model's core insight remains valid: housing markets contain multiple reinforcing and balancing feedback loops whose interaction determines system behavior. The key reinforcing loop (more housing → more population → more demand → more housing) and the key balancing loop (more housing → lower prices → lower profitability → less construction) are both present in this study's model.

Eskinasi, Rouwette and Vennix (2009) applied SD specifically to housing policy, simulating urban transformation in the Haaglanden region of the Netherlands. Their model included explicit feedback loops between policy interventions, construction activity, housing quality, and demographic flows. They found that policy interventions that appeared effective in isolation could become ineffective when combined with other interventions due to feedback-loop interactions — precisely the phenomenon this study investigates.

Ghaffarzadegan, Lyneis and Richardson (2011) argued that small SD models (under 20 stocks) can be more policy-relevant than large ones because they are transparent enough for policymakers to understand and challenge. This study's model follows their recommendation: it has a single causal chain (cost → viability → applications → permissions → completions) with one capacity constraint and optional third-order feedback, making it interpretable while still capturing the essential dynamics.

Pruyt (2013) demonstrated that SD models are particularly well-suited to analyzing policy packages because they can represent the causal mechanisms through which individual policies interact. A purely statistical approach to policy interaction (e.g., estimating interaction terms in a regression) captures the net effect but not the mechanism. The SD approach reveals why interactions are positive (synergy) or negative (redundancy), which is essential for policy design.

The housing supply pipeline — from land acquisition through planning to construction to completion — is a natural stock-and-flow system with significant delays (Ball, 2011; Bramley, 2013). The 962-day median pipeline duration (PL-1) means that policy changes take approximately 2.6 years to flow through to completions, creating a dynamic system with long time constants. Duration-reduction levers work by shortening this delay, while other levers (cost reduction, capacity expansion) change the flow rates. The interaction between delay reduction and flow-rate increase is a classic SD phenomenon: faster flow through a shorter pipeline produces a multiplicative effect on throughput.

Barlas and Yasarcan (2006) established formal methods for analyzing goal-seeking behavior in SD models, which is directly relevant to the question of whether Ireland can reach the HFA target of 50,500 completions per year. Their framework distinguishes between achievable goals (where the system has sufficient capacity and responsiveness) and structurally unachievable goals (where feedback loops or capacity constraints prevent convergence). This study's capacity ceiling analysis directly tests whether 50,500 is structurally achievable.

## Theme 4: Construction Costs, Modular Methods, and Cost Reduction Levers

Construction costs in Ireland are among the highest in Europe, with a price level index (PLI) of 99.7 against the EU average of 100 (C-3, Eurostat). The SCSI (2022) cost breakdown for a typical Dublin 3-bed semi-detached house shows total development cost of approximately €592,000, of which hard construction costs account for 53% nationally. This cost structure determines the leverage available to each cost-reduction lever.

Modular and offsite construction represents the largest single lever for hard-cost reduction. McKinsey (2017) estimated that modular construction can reduce total project costs by 20% and project time by 50% in mature markets. Bertram et al. (2019) provided more conservative estimates of 10-20% cost reduction for current-generation modular systems, with potential for 30% or more as the industry scales. Pan, Gibb and Dainty (2007) surveyed UK housebuilders and found that the primary barriers to modular adoption were financial (higher upfront investment), supply-chain (limited manufacturer capacity), and cultural (industry resistance to change), not technical.

Steinhardt and Manley (2016) analyzed the adoption trajectory of prefabricated housing across multiple countries and found that successful adoption requires simultaneous action on multiple fronts: demand signals (government procurement), supply capacity (factory investment), and regulatory accommodation (performance-based rather than prescriptive building codes). This multi-front requirement is itself an interaction effect at the industry level: modular adoption is more effective when combined with regulatory and procurement reform than when pursued alone.

The NHBC (2019) survey of UK industry views on modern methods of construction (MMC) found that cost savings of 10-15% were achievable with current technology, with higher savings possible at scale. However, they noted that the savings are sensitive to project scale: modular construction requires a minimum order size to achieve economies of scale, meaning that the cost-reduction lever interacts with the volume lever. This creates a potential positive feedback loop: cost reduction increases volume, which enables more modular adoption, which further reduces costs.

The Farmer Review (2016) made the case that the UK construction industry's reliance on traditional site-based methods is structurally unsustainable given demographic trends in the workforce (aging, declining apprentice intake). Farmer argued that modular construction is not just a cost-reduction lever but a capacity-expansion lever: factory-based production requires fewer skilled tradespeople per unit, effectively expanding the capacity ceiling. This dual effect means the modular lever interacts with both the cost axis and the capacity axis — a compound interaction that purely additive models miss.

VAT on new housing is a significant cost component. At 13.5% in Ireland (compared to zero-rated in the UK for new-build), VAT adds approximately 12% to the pre-VAT cost (since 0.135/1.135 = 0.119). Copenhagen Economics (2007) studied the effects of reduced VAT rates on construction across EU member states and found that lower VAT rates were associated with higher construction output, but the elasticity varied by market conditions. In constrained markets (where demand exceeds supply), reduced VAT is more likely to translate into additional supply than into higher pre-VAT prices; in unconstrained markets, the benefit accrues partly to developers as higher margins. Ireland's current supply-constrained state suggests VAT reduction would primarily increase supply rather than margins.

Keen and Lockwood (2010) analyzed the general theory of VAT and its economic effects, finding that sectoral VAT exemptions or reductions create distortions but can be welfare-improving when applied to goods with positive externalities (housing arguably qualifies) or to sectors facing binding supply constraints. The Mirrlees Review (IFS, 2011) recommended broadening the VAT base rather than introducing reduced rates, but acknowledged that housing-specific reduced rates have a stronger case than most because the positive externalities of adequate housing supply are large and well-documented.

Developer margins in Ireland average 15% for private housing (C-2), compared to as low as 6% for social housing where the risk profile is different. Crosby and Wyatt (2016) analyzed the relationship between developer margins, risk, and viability, finding that margins below 15% are viable for projects with significant pre-sales or government guarantees. This suggests that the developer-margin lever is partly a risk-transfer lever: reducing margins to 6% is only feasible if risk is simultaneously reduced (e.g., through government guarantees or forward-purchase agreements). The interaction between margin reduction and risk-transfer mechanisms is a policy interaction that this study captures through the finance-rate lever.

## Theme 5: Planning Duration, Permitting, and the Pipeline Feedback Loop

The planning and permitting system is a critical component of the housing supply pipeline, with median duration of 962 days from application to completion in Ireland (PL-1). This duration includes local authority assessment, An Bord Pleanala appeals (mean 42 weeks in 2024, per PL-3), judicial review delays (105,000 unit-months of direct delay, per S-2), and various other regulatory steps (BCAR certification, fire safety, disability access).

Ball (2011) provided the foundational empirical evidence on planning delay and housing supply responsiveness in England, finding that a one-year increase in average planning delay reduced new housing starts by approximately 10%. His finding implies that duration reduction is a significant lever — but his model did not examine interactions with other levers. The question of whether duration reduction amplifies or substitutes for cost reduction is unanswered in Ball's framework.

The Irish planning system underwent a significant institutional experiment with the creation and subsequent abolition of An Bord Pleanala's Strategic Housing Development (SHD) fast-track process (2017-2022). SHDs bypassed local authority assessment, reducing the planning stage from an average of 52 weeks to approximately 25 weeks. However, the SHD process generated a high rate of judicial review challenges (S-2), partially offsetting the time savings. The net effect of SHDs on pipeline duration was approximately zero when JR delays are included — a policy interaction (fast-track planning × judicial review) that illustrates how lever effects can be neutralized by system responses.

Glaeser and Ward (2009) analyzed the causes of land-use regulation, finding that existing homeowners use the planning system to protect property values, creating a political economy feedback loop: high prices → incumbent opposition to new supply → restrictive planning → higher prices. This loop suggests that planning reform is politically feasible only when combined with other levers that mitigate the price impact on incumbents — another interaction effect at the political level.

Adams and Leishman (2008) studied the factors affecting the take-up of new housing supply (the conversion from planning permission to construction starts), finding that the primary determinant was viability at the point of decision. Their finding directly supports this study's feedback loop model: improved viability increases the application rate, which increases permissions, which increases starts. But they also found that take-up rates were sensitive to market confidence, which introduces a subjective element not captured by cost-based viability calculations.

The lapse rate — the fraction of planning permissions that expire unused — is 9.5% in Ireland (PL-4, with CI 4.4-15.6%). Lapsed permissions represent wasted system capacity: applications that consumed planning resources but produced no housing. The lapse rate is itself a function of viability: permissions lapse when market conditions deteriorate between grant and build. This creates a negative feedback loop in the pipeline model: improved viability today reduces lapse rates, increasing the effective yield of past permissions — a stock effect that purely flow-based models miss.

The relationship between planning volumes and completions is mediated by the approval rate (68% nationally) and the build-yield (59.6%, per S-1). The build-yield captures the combined effect of lapse, delay, and abandonment between permission grant and completion. A build-yield of 59.6% means that approximately 40% of granted permissions never result in completed dwellings — a massive system inefficiency that interacts with every upstream lever. Improving viability should increase build-yield (fewer lapses), creating a positive feedback through the pipeline.

## Theme 6: Capacity Constraints, Workforce, and Scale Economies

Construction capacity is a hard constraint on housing output that operates independently of planning permissions, viability, or demand. The current capacity ceiling in Ireland is approximately 35,000 units per year (S-3), which is already binding: gross completions under current conditions equal the ceiling. This means that any demand-side lever (cost reduction, VAT cut, margin compression) that increases the application-to-completion flow will hit the ceiling without producing additional completions unless the ceiling itself is raised.

The Farmer Review (2016) documented the structural capacity constraints in UK construction: an aging workforce (27% over 50, 15% over 60), declining apprentice intake, and low productivity growth. These trends apply with equal force to Ireland, where the construction workforce has not recovered to its pre-crash level despite a decade of demand growth (CIF, 2022; SOLAS, 2022).

IGEES (2022) analyzed Irish construction sector capacity and estimated that output could increase by 20-30% above current levels with targeted workforce investment (training, immigration, productivity improvement), but that increases above 50% would require fundamental changes to construction methods (i.e., modular). This estimate directly informs the workforce lever settings in this study: +20% represents achievable workforce expansion, +50% represents ambitious expansion combined with modular methods.

The McKinsey (2017) report on construction productivity found that the construction sector has experienced essentially zero productivity growth over the past 50 years in most OECD countries, in contrast to manufacturing (where productivity has grown 8× in the same period). They attributed this to fragmented industry structure, project-based production (no learning curve), and resistance to industrialization. Modular construction represents the primary pathway to productivity improvement, which is why this study treats it as both a cost lever and a capacity lever.

Scale economies in construction are well-documented for individual projects (larger projects have lower per-unit costs due to fixed-cost amortization) but are less clear at the industry level. DKM (2021) estimated that construction cost escalation in Ireland was running at approximately 4% per year (materials CAGR 3.99%, labour CAGR 4.03%, per C-1), which partially offsets cost-reduction levers over time. However, if construction volume increases significantly, the increased demand for materials and labour could accelerate cost escalation — a negative feedback loop (anti-scale economy) that moderates the multiplier effect.

Conversely, higher volume enables investment in manufacturing capacity (modular factories, prefabrication facilities) that could reduce per-unit costs — a positive scale economy. The net direction of scale effects at the industry level is ambiguous and represents a genuine uncertainty in the model. This study treats scale economies as a third-order optional effect, reflecting this uncertainty.

The absorption rate — the rate at which the market can absorb new housing stock — is a demand-side capacity constraint that Letwin (2018) identified as the binding constraint on build-out rates for large sites. If absorption is limited (because households cannot or will not buy/rent at current prices), then even with unlimited construction capacity and planning permissions, completions are capped by demand. However, the absorption constraint weakens as prices fall (which they would if supply increased significantly), creating a price-mediated feedback loop between supply and absorption.

The general-equilibrium effect of large supply increases is complex. Favilukis, Ludvigson and Van Nieuwerburgh (2017) showed that increasing housing supply in a general-equilibrium model reduces house prices, which reduces household wealth, which reduces consumption, which can have contractionary macroeconomic effects. However, the lower prices also improve affordability, which attracts population inflow, which generates new demand — a countervailing effect. The net welfare effect is positive (more housing at lower prices), but the GDP effect is ambiguous.

For Ireland specifically, the general-equilibrium feedback is likely positive because Ireland is a small open economy with significant latent demand for housing (170,000+ on social housing waiting lists, per DHLGH, 2021). Increased supply would primarily serve unmet demand rather than displacing existing demand, so the absorption constraint is unlikely to bind at volumes below 60,000-70,000 per year.

## Theme 7: Combinatorial Optimization and Factorial Design for Policy Analysis

The methodological challenge of this study is to identify the optimal policy package from a combinatorial space of 10 levers with multiple settings each. The full factorial design (Montgomery, 2017; Box, Hunter & Hunter, 2005) provides the gold standard for identifying interaction effects: by testing every combination, no interaction can be missed. The 104,976-combination full factorial used in this study is computationally feasible (the feedback-loop model evaluates in microseconds) but generates a data volume that requires systematic analysis methods.

Response surface methodology (RSM) (Myers, Montgomery & Anderson-Cook, 2016) provides the analytical framework for identifying optimal regions within the factorial design space. RSM fits a second-order polynomial surface to the response (completions) as a function of the lever settings, with the interaction terms directly estimating the pairwise synergies and redundancies. The fitted surface can be used to identify the optimal lever combination, the marginal contribution of each lever, and the sensitivity of the optimum to each lever's setting.

Taguchi's (1986) robust design methodology offers a complementary perspective: rather than optimizing for the highest expected completions, optimize for the lever combination that is least sensitive to parameter uncertainty. In a policy context, robustness is arguably more important than optimality because policy implementation is uncertain and parameter estimates carry confidence intervals.

Saltelli et al. (2008) provide the definitive guide to global sensitivity analysis, which this study uses to decompose the variance in completions across the lever space. Sobol indices (Sobol, 2001) measure the fraction of output variance attributable to each lever and each lever interaction, providing a rigorous decomposition of which levers and which interactions matter most.

Morris (1991) introduced factorial screening designs that efficiently identify which factors have nonlinear or interaction effects, which is useful when the full factorial is computationally expensive. While this study uses the full factorial, Morris's methodology informs the analysis strategy: start with main effects, then test for pairwise interactions, then test for higher-order interactions only if pairwise interactions are large.

Kleijnen (2015) specifically addressed the design and analysis of simulation experiments, noting that simulation models (unlike physical experiments) have no measurement noise, which simplifies the statistical analysis but also means that observed differences are real effects, not noise. This study's feedback-loop model is deterministic for a given parameter set, so all variation in the factorial is due to real lever effects and interactions. The Monte Carlo simulation introduces parameter uncertainty to generate honest confidence intervals.

Monte Carlo methods (Metropolis & Ulam, 1949; Robert & Casella, 2004; Rubinstein & Kroese, 2017) provide the natural framework for propagating parameter uncertainty through the feedback-loop model. By drawing from distributions for each uncertain parameter (build-yield, approval rate, viability elasticity, cost shares), the Monte Carlo simulation produces a distribution of completions for each lever combination, from which confidence intervals can be constructed. Vose (2008) and Morgan and Henrion (1990) provide practical guidance on specifying input distributions and interpreting output distributions.

The Pareto frontier (Deb, 2001; Ehrgott, 2005) addresses the multi-objective nature of policy design: maximizing completions may conflict with minimizing policy cost (lost tax revenue, increased public expenditure). Deb et al.'s (2002) NSGA-II algorithm is the standard method for identifying the Pareto-optimal set, but this study uses exhaustive enumeration over the full factorial, which is feasible given the small design space and provides an exact Pareto frontier.

Borgonovo and Plischke (2016) reviewed recent advances in sensitivity analysis, including moment-independent methods that go beyond variance decomposition to capture the full distribution of output conditional on each input. Their methods are applicable to this study's Monte Carlo output and can identify levers that affect not just the mean but also the variance of completions — a policy-relevant distinction because a lever that increases expected completions but also increases variance (risk) may be less desirable than one that increases completions with lower variance.

The multi-criteria decision analysis (MCDA) literature (Figueira, Greco & Ehrgott, 2005) provides formal methods for combining the Pareto frontier with policy preferences. In practice, policymakers face tradeoffs between completions, fiscal cost, political feasibility, and implementation complexity. This study focuses on the completions-vs-cost frontier but acknowledges that the final policy recommendation depends on these additional dimensions.
