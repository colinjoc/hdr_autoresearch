# Literature Review: Zoned Land to Planning Application Conversion in Ireland

## Theme 1: Urban Land Economics and the Development Decision

The fundamental economic theory of land development timing was established by Titman (1985), who showed that undeveloped urban land carries a real option value — the owner can choose when to develop, and this option is more valuable when future prices are uncertain. Capozza and Li (1994) extended this to show that development intensity (the density at which land is developed) and timing are jointly determined: higher uncertainty delays development but increases eventual density. Quigg (1993) provided the first large-scale empirical test, finding that vacant land in Seattle traded at a 6% premium over its intrinsic development value, consistent with option theory.

DiPasquale and Wheaton (1996) established the canonical four-quadrant model linking property markets (rents, prices) to asset markets (construction, stock adjustment). In this framework, zoned land that is not being developed represents a stock adjustment failure — either rents are insufficient to justify construction, or supply-side frictions prevent the market from clearing. Harvey and Jowsey (2004) and O'Sullivan (2012) provide textbook treatments of how land markets differ from other factor markets: land is spatially fixed, heterogeneous, and subject to regulatory constraints that create artificial scarcity.

Evans (2004) specifically addresses the economics of land supply, arguing that planning systems create a discontinuity between agricultural and development land values — the "planning premium" — that makes landowner behaviour fundamentally different from textbook competitive supply. Saiz (2010) demonstrated that geographic constraints (mountains, water) explain about half of the variation in US housing supply elasticity, with regulatory constraints explaining the remainder. Green, Malpezzi, and Mayo (2005) estimated metropolitan-specific supply elasticities, finding enormous variation (0.5 to 5.0) that is primarily explained by regulatory stringency.

For Ireland specifically, Kenny (1999) modeled housing demand and supply, finding that supply was relatively elastic during the 1990s expansion but subject to significant lags. Roche (2001) and Murphy (1998) documented the Celtic Tiger house price boom, which was driven by a combination of income growth, demographic change, and credit expansion — but was enabled by elastic land supply through the planning system. The subsequent crash (Honohan 2010; Kitchin et al. 2012) destroyed developer balance sheets and created a "lost decade" of low supply. Allen-Coghlan and McQuinn (2020) estimated that Ireland needed 30,000-35,000 homes per year by 2020 but was delivering roughly 20,000, a gap that has persisted.

The real options perspective is critical for understanding why zoned land sits undeveloped. Dixit and Pindyck (1994) established the general theory of irreversible investment under uncertainty, showing that the threshold for investment is substantially higher than the standard net-present-value rule would suggest. McDonald and Siegel (1986) derived the optimal investment timing rule: invest when the project value exceeds the investment cost by a factor that depends on uncertainty and the discount rate. For real estate, Bulan, Mayer, and Somerville (2009) showed empirically that land in US cities was developed more slowly when price uncertainty was higher — a direct test of the real options model. Cunningham (2006) found similar results in Seattle, with a one-standard-deviation increase in price uncertainty reducing development probability by 5.5 percentage points.

Williams (1991) and Grenadier (1996) modeled strategic interactions between developers, showing that the option to wait can create development cascades — long periods of inactivity followed by bursts of building when a threshold is crossed. Bar-Ilan and Strange (1996) incorporated construction lags, showing that the option value is higher when the lag between investment and completion is longer, which is directly relevant to Ireland's 3-year median pipeline (predecessor PL-5).

The theoretical prediction for Irish zoned land is therefore clear: at 7,911 hectares with a median land price of EUR 571,000 per hectare, the option to wait is substantial. If a developer expects prices to rise, waiting preserves the option; if prices might fall, the irreversibility of construction makes waiting even more attractive. The Residential Zoned Land Tax (RZLT) was explicitly designed to reduce this option value by imposing a holding cost on undeveloped land (DHLGH 2022).

## Theme 2: Zoning Theory and Regulatory Constraints

Glaeser and Gyourko (2003, 2018) established the modern framework for understanding how zoning and building restrictions affect housing affordability. Their key insight is that in well-functioning housing markets, the price of a home should approximate the marginal cost of construction plus the land cost. When prices substantially exceed construction costs — as they do in Ireland (SCSI 2023; viability ratio ~1.26) — the excess is attributable to regulatory constraints and natural land scarcity. Gyourko, Saiz, and Summers (2008) created the Wharton Residential Land Use Regulatory Index, demonstrating enormous cross-metropolitan variation in regulatory stringency.

The welfare economics of land use planning were analyzed by Cheshire and Sheppard (2002), who estimated that UK planning restrictions reduced welfare by creating artificial scarcity. Turner, Haughwout, and van der Klaauw (2014) developed a general equilibrium model finding that US land use regulations reduced welfare by 2% of income in regulated cities. However, Ahlfeldt and Pietrostefani (2019) found that density has both positive and negative externalities, and that some regulation is welfare-improving.

For Ireland, the planning system is discretionary rather than prescriptive — local authorities have significant latitude in interpreting development plans, which creates uncertainty for developers (Coffey et al. 2022). The Office of the Planning Regulator (2023) oversees consistency but cannot direct individual decisions. The Strategic Housing Development (SHD) fast-track process was introduced in 2017 to bypass local authority delays for large schemes (>100 units), but was abolished in 2022 after criticism (Croce 2019). The evidence from our data shows that large scheme applications (50+ units) rose dramatically from 16 per year in 2015 to 142 in 2018 (during the SHD era) but remained elevated post-abolition.

Ihlanfeldt (2007) found that regulatory stringency affects both housing and land prices, with the effect on land prices being larger — consistent with regulations acting as a supply constraint on the land market itself, not just the housing market. Mayer and Somerville (2000) showed that US cities with more regulatory steps in the approval process had 45% fewer housing starts. Paciorek (2013) built a dynamic model showing that supply constraints do not just raise the level of housing prices but increase their volatility, which feeds back into the real options channel (more uncertainty → more waiting).

Hsieh and Moretti (2019) estimated that US housing regulations reduced aggregate GDP by 36% by preventing workers from moving to the most productive cities. Ganong and Shoag (2017) showed that housing supply constraints explain the decline in US regional income convergence since the 1980s. These are not just housing problems — they are macroeconomic problems.

Fischel's (2001) "homevoter hypothesis" explains why existing homeowners support restrictive zoning: it protects their property values. In Ireland, local political dynamics similarly favour existing residents over potential new ones (MacLaran and Kelly 2014; Murphy 2016). The result is a system that zones land for residential use (to satisfy national planning requirements) but makes actual development difficult through conditions, objections, and infrastructure requirements.

Brueckner and Singh (2020) and Jackson (2016) provide modern empirical evidence that regulation stifles development, with Jackson finding that a one-standard-deviation increase in regulation reduces housing permits by 6%. Quigley and Raphael (2005) estimated that California's regulatory environment added $50,000-$90,000 to the cost of a median home. For Ireland, the equivalent analysis has not been done at scale, making the present study one of the first to quantify the zoned-land-to-application conversion rate directly.

## Theme 3: The Irish Planning and Housing System

Ireland's planning system is governed by the Planning and Development Act 2000 (as amended), with local authorities preparing development plans every six years that zone land for different uses (Government of Ireland 2018). The National Planning Framework (2018) sets the spatial strategy, directing 50% of growth to the five cities and 50% to regional centres and rural areas.

The housing crisis has been documented extensively. Housing for All (Government of Ireland 2021) set a target of 50,500 new homes per year, later revised upward. The Housing Commission (2022) recommended structural reforms including the RZLT. The ESRI has published numerous analyses (McQuinn and O'Connell 2024; Allen-Coghlan and McQuinn 2020; McQuinn 2021) consistently finding that supply constraints, not demand, are the binding issue.

The Residential Zoned Land Tax (RZLT), introduced in Finance Act 2021 and taking effect from 2024, imposes a 3% annual tax on the market value of land that is zoned for residential use and serviced but not developed (DHLGH 2022; Revenue Commissioners 2024; Oireachtas Library and Research Service 2022). The tax is designed to incentivize development by imposing a carrying cost on idle zoned land. Our data shows a 7.1% decline in applications post-RZLT announcement (2022-2024 vs 2018-2021), though the tax only became payable in 2024 and the effect may take time to materialize.

The Goodbody (2024) report provides the most comprehensive recent estimate of available residential zoned land: 7,911 hectares nationally, down from 17,434 hectares in the 2014 survey (Department of Environment 2014). This 54.6% decline over a decade reflects both genuine development (land converted to housing) and administrative changes (rezoning, disqualification from RZLT maps). The regional distribution is uneven: Northern+Western has 40% of the zoned land but lower demand, while East+Midlands has 33% with the highest demand.

The Land Development Agency (LDA, established 2018) was created to activate state-owned land for housing but has delivered primarily through acquisition (PL-3 found ~850 homes in 2023, mostly via Project Tosaigh). NAMA (2023) has been a more significant but declining source of land activation. Irish Water (2023, 2024) infrastructure capacity remains a binding constraint in some areas, with connection charges and capacity limitations affecting which zoned land can actually be developed.

Development contributions — levied by local authorities under Section 48/49 of the Planning Act — add EUR 5,000-30,000 per unit depending on the local authority (Foley, O'Callaghan, and Boyle 2020). These contributions fund local infrastructure but increase the cost of development, potentially deterring applications on marginal sites. The National Oversight and Audit Commission (2023) tracks local authority performance metrics but does not directly measure zoned land activation rates.

Our predecessor studies found: a 9.5% lapse rate on granted permissions (PL-4), a 59.6% build-yield (PL-5), and a need for ~85,000 permissions per year to meet Housing for All targets at that yield. The current study addresses the upstream question: why are only ~38,000 planning applications filed when there is physical land capacity for ~417,000 homes?

## Theme 4: Real Options and Development Timing

The real options framework provides the strongest theoretical explanation for the gap between zoned land availability and planning applications. The core idea, formalized by Dixit and Pindyck (1994) and applied to real estate by Titman (1985), is that development is an irreversible investment under uncertainty. Once land is built upon, the decision cannot be reversed without substantial cost. This irreversibility creates an option value in waiting.

Cunningham (2006) tested the theory directly using land parcel data in Seattle, finding that a one-standard-deviation increase in price uncertainty reduced the probability of development by 5.5 percentage points. The effect is economically large: it implies that during the 2008-2012 period of maximum uncertainty in Ireland, landowners' threshold for development would have been substantially above the net-present-value trigger.

Capozza and Li (1994) extended the framework to show that the option value depends on three factors: (a) the level of prices relative to development costs (the viability ratio), (b) the growth rate of prices (expected appreciation), and (c) the variance of prices (uncertainty). Our E05 result shows a national viability ratio of 1.26 (sale price / construction cost), which is barely above the ~1.2 threshold identified by Lyons (2021) for viable development. This suggests that much of the zoned land stock is on the margin of viability — a small change in costs or prices could make or break the development case.

Grenadier (1996) showed that when multiple developers hold options on similar land, strategic interactions can create "development cascades" — all developers wait until one breaks the equilibrium, then many develop simultaneously. This is consistent with the pattern observed in Irish housing data: long periods of low activity followed by sharp increases (the 2017 spike in applications visible in our data may be partly strategic cascade).

The RZLT can be understood as a mechanism to reduce the option value of waiting. By imposing a 3% annual holding cost on undeveloped zoned land, the tax shifts the optimal development trigger downward — landowners must develop sooner because waiting is costly. Abel et al. (1996) and Bar-Ilan and Strange (1996) analyzed how holding costs affect investment timing, finding that they accelerate investment but may also reduce the quality of development (landowners develop at suboptimal times to avoid the tax).

Clapp, Jou, and Lu (2012) examined density and intensity of development, finding that developers facing uncertain future prices build at lower density initially, preserving the option to add density later. This is relevant to Ireland's dominance of one-off housing (43.8% of applications): building a single house on a large zoned site preserves the option to develop the remainder later.

Kahneman and Tversky (1979) introduced prospect theory, which suggests that losses loom larger than gains. For Irish landowners who acquired zoned land during the Celtic Tiger at prices far above current values, the prospect of selling or developing at a loss may create an additional hold-out effect beyond what rational option pricing would predict. This "loss-aversion hold-out" is not captured in standard real options models but may be quantitatively important.

## Theme 5: Infrastructure, Viability, and Construction Costs

Development of zoned land requires not just a planning permission but viable economics: the sale price must cover land acquisition, construction costs, development contributions, financing costs, and a developer margin. The SCSI (2023) estimated that the cost of building an apartment in Dublin exceeds EUR 400,000 including all costs, while the median apartment sale price is only marginally above this. The viability constraint is more binding for apartments than houses, which partly explains the dominance of houses (81.6% of residential applications) over apartments (10.0%).

DKM Economic Consultants (2023) and Coyne (2022) analyzed delivery costs in detail, finding that "hard" construction costs account for only 40-50% of the total, with land (15-25%), development contributions (5-10%), professional fees (8-12%), VAT (12%), and financing (5-8%) comprising the remainder. National Competitiveness and Productivity Council (2023) benchmarked Irish construction costs against European peers, finding Ireland in the top quartile for cost per square metre.

Infrastructure is a binding constraint in specific locations. Irish Water (2023, 2024) has identified areas where wastewater treatment capacity is insufficient for the zoned land capacity — this means land is zoned and on the RZLT map but cannot physically be developed until infrastructure is upgraded. The National Transport Authority (2023) and TII (2023) similarly note transport capacity constraints in rapidly growing commuter zones (Meath, Kildare, Wicklow). Our E17 experiment used the one-off housing rate as an infrastructure proxy, finding no significant correlation with application intensity — suggesting infrastructure may be a local rather than systemic constraint.

Development contributions vary dramatically by local authority (Foley, O'Callaghan, and Boyle 2020). Dublin City Council charges among the highest rates, while rural counties charge less but also have less infrastructure to fund. The interaction between contribution rates and viability has been cited by the CIF (IHBA 2023) as a barrier to development on marginal sites. However, our E18 experiment found no significant correlation between refusal rates (a proxy for regulatory burden) and application intensity, suggesting that the barrier may be economic rather than regulatory.

Laird and Venables (2017) provide the theoretical framework for understanding how transport investment enables land development by improving accessibility and thus land values. In Ireland, the planned MetroLink and BusConnects programmes are expected to unlock development potential in specific corridors, but these are multi-decade investments. The immediate constraint is wastewater capacity, which is faster to address but requires significant capital expenditure.

## Theme 6: International Comparisons (UK, NZ, NL)

The United Kingdom provides the closest comparator. DLUHC (2023) reports approximately 400,000 planning applications per year in England alone, on a much larger urbanisable land base. Barker (2004) concluded that the UK's discretionary planning system was the primary barrier to housing supply, a finding echoed by Hilber and Vermeulen (2016) who estimated that regulatory constraints added 35% to house prices in England. Cheshire (2009) argued that the UK's "urban containment" policy created artificial scarcity, and Monk and Whitehead (1999) evaluated the economic impact of planning controls. White (2015) and DCLG (2017) documented the UK's attempts at reform.

The UK Section 106 system (Morrison and Burgess 2014; McAllister 2017; Crook et al. 2016) provides an instructive comparison with Ireland's development contributions. Under S106, developers negotiate affordable housing and infrastructure contributions with local authorities, creating uncertainty about the total cost of development. Whitehead (2007) found that S106 was effective at delivering affordable housing but reduced the overall volume of market housing. Gallent and Tewdwr-Jones (2007) provide a broader analysis of planning's role in housing provision.

New Zealand reformed its planning system through the Resource Management Act 1991, which was intended to be more permissive but created its own barriers (Productivity Commission NZ 2012). Grimes and Mitchell (2015) estimated that NZ planning rules added NZD 32,500-60,000 to the cost of a dwelling in Auckland. Lees (2017) quantified the costs of land use regulation nationally, finding significant effects on housing affordability. Greenaway-McGrevy and Phillips (2016) documented the rapid house price growth that resulted.

The Netherlands uses a fundamentally different approach: active land policy (actief grondbeleid) where municipalities purchase and service land before selling it to developers (Needham 2006; Buitelaar and Sorel 2010; Priemus and Louw 2003). This eliminates the land-hoarding problem because the municipality, not the landowner, controls the timing of development. Van der Krabben and Jacobs (2013) compared Dutch and US zoning, finding that the Dutch system produces more predictable outcomes but at higher fiscal cost to municipalities. Vermeulen and Van Ommeren (2009) found that Dutch land use planning shaped regional economic structure, with housing supply constraints directing growth to specific corridors.

The international comparison yields several insights for Ireland: (a) all systems face the fundamental tension between zoning land and actually developing it; (b) holding costs (like RZLT) are one mechanism to close the gap; (c) active land policy (the Dutch model, partially replicated by Ireland's LDA) is another; (d) the conversion rate varies enormously by institutional context.

Our E20 comparison found Ireland's application intensity (2.72 apps/ha/yr on RZLT-zoned land) is 13.6x the UK rate (~0.2 apps/ha/yr on urbanisable land), but this comparison is misleading because the denominators differ radically — Ireland's 7,911 hectares of specifically zoned, serviced, residentially designated land is a much narrower base than the UK's total urbanisable land. A fairer comparison would use UK "allocated residential sites" but this data is not available at the national level.

## Theme 7: Spatial Analysis and Regional Dynamics

Spatial patterns in housing development are well-documented in the literature. Anselin (1995) developed Local Indicators of Spatial Association (LISA), building on Moran's (1950) global test for spatial autocorrelation. These tools measure whether high-value (or low-value) observations cluster geographically. Our T05 experiment found significant spatial clustering of application intensity by local authority (Moran's I = 0.37, p = 0.004), indicating that high-intensity LAs are geographically proximate to other high-intensity LAs.

Cliff and Ord (1981) and Getis and Ord (1992) provide the theoretical foundations for spatial statistics, while Fotheringham, Brunsdon, and Charlton (2002) extend the analysis to geographically weighted regression — allowing coefficients to vary across space. In our context, the relationship between zoned land and applications may differ between Dublin (where demand is intense but land is scarce) and the West (where land is abundant but demand is lower).

Morgenroth (2014, 2018) analyzed regional dynamics in Ireland, finding persistent disparities in economic activity that map closely onto our regional application rate differences: East+Midlands (3.09 apps/ha/yr), Southern (3.65), Northern+Western (1.63). The National Planning Framework (2018) explicitly aims to redirect growth toward regional centres, but Murphy and Scott (2013) found that accessibility advantages strongly favour Dublin.

Williams and Shiels (2000) documented the acceleration of Dublin sprawl during the Celtic Tiger, with development leapfrogging to commuter counties (Meath, Kildare, Wicklow). Our E23 results show that Cork County, Meath, and Wicklow have the highest application intensity — consistent with continued peri-urban development pressure. Conversely, Fingal County has the lowest intensity (0.08 apps/ha/yr) despite having the largest zoned land stock (3,519 ha), suggesting that much of Fingal's zoned land is either not genuinely developable or is being held for strategic reasons.

The spatial dimension of the RZLT is important: the tax applies to land that is both zoned and serviced, but service capacity varies locally. Irish Water (2023) has identified specific constraints in the Greater Dublin Area, meaning that some RZLT-mapped land cannot be developed even if the landowner wishes to. EPA (2023) water quality requirements add another spatial dimension, with some areas restricted due to downstream environmental sensitivity. Caulfield (2015) and the National Transport Authority (2023) document the transport accessibility gradient that shapes development viability.

O'Callaghan et al. (2014) and Kitchin (2014) provide critical perspectives on the spatial politics of Irish development, arguing that planning decisions are shaped by power relationships as much as by economic logic. The concentration of planning applications in specific LAs (HHI = 0.049, equivalent to ~20 equal LAs) suggests that development activity is modestly concentrated but not dominated by a few authorities. The European Commission (2023) and IMF (2022) have both noted Ireland's spatial housing imbalance as a macroeconomic risk.
