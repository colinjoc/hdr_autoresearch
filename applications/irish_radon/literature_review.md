# Literature Review: Predicting Dangerous Radon Levels in Irish Homes

## Theme 1: Radon Transport Physics — From Bedrock to Breathing Zone

Radon-222 (Rn-222) is a noble gas produced by the radioactive decay of Radium-226 (Ra-226), itself a decay product of Uranium-238 (U-238). The half-life of Rn-222 is 3.82 days, long enough for it to migrate from the subsurface into buildings but short enough that its concentration depends strongly on local geological and building conditions (Nazaroff & Nero, 1988; Tanner, 1964).

The transport of radon from bedrock through soil and into buildings involves two fundamental mechanisms: diffusion and advection. Diffusion is driven by the concentration gradient between soil gas (typically 10,000-100,000 Bq/m3) and indoor air (typically 20-200 Bq/m3). Advection is driven by pressure differences across the building envelope, caused by the "stack effect" (warm air rising inside creates negative pressure at ground level), wind loading, and mechanical ventilation systems (Nazaroff, 1992; Riley et al., 1996; Kokotti & Kalliokoski, 1992).

The key parameters controlling radon availability in soil gas are: (1) uranium concentration in the parent rock, (2) the emanation coefficient — the fraction of radon atoms that escape from mineral grains into pore space (typically 0.05-0.40 depending on rock type and moisture), (3) soil porosity and permeability, and (4) soil moisture content (Sakoda et al., 2011; Schery & Wasiolek, 1998). Fractured rock significantly increases radon transport by providing preferential pathways (Jha et al., 2000). The "radon potential" concept, formalized by Neznal et al. (2004), combines soil gas radon concentration with soil permeability into a single index that predicts whether a site can supply dangerous radon levels to a building.

The transition from soil gas to indoor air occurs primarily through cracks and penetrations in the building foundation. Loureiro et al. (1990) and Revzan et al. (1991) developed physics-based models showing that even small cracks (1-2 mm) can admit significant radon when pressure-driven. The entry rate depends on: foundation type (slab-on-ground, crawl space, basement), crack area, soil permeability beneath the slab, and the indoor-outdoor pressure difference (Eaton & Scott, 1984; Robinson & Sextro, 1997). Concrete slabs themselves have measurable radon diffusion coefficients (Rogers & Nielson, 1994), though advective transport through cracks typically dominates.

Indoor radon concentration is ultimately a balance between entry rate and removal rate. Removal occurs through ventilation (air exchange rate) and radioactive decay. A simplified steady-state model gives: C_indoor = (S_entry × A_foundation) / (lambda_V × V) + C_materials, where S_entry is the entry flux, A_foundation is the foundation area, lambda_V is the ventilation rate, and V is the building volume. C_materials represents the contribution from building materials themselves, which is usually small (5-20 Bq/m3) except for certain concrete blocks and granite (Pavlidou et al., 2006; Righi & Bruzzi, 2006; Daraktchieva et al., 2015).

Seasonal variation is substantial (factors of 2-5), driven by temperature-dependent stack effect, soil moisture from seasonal rainfall, frozen ground capping, and occupancy-driven ventilation patterns (Arvela et al., 2016; Miles et al., 2012). Diurnal variation is also significant (Sesana et al., 2003), complicating short-term measurements. The standard measurement protocol for Ireland and Europe uses three-month integrated detectors to average over these fluctuations, with seasonal correction factors applied (Fennell et al., 2002; Wiegand, 2001).

## Theme 2: Geological Controls on Radon in Ireland

Ireland's geology creates a diverse radon landscape. The dominant geological controls are: (1) uranium content of bedrock, (2) permeability of overlying quaternary deposits, and (3) the presence of karst features that provide preferential radon transport pathways (Appleton, 2007; Elío et al., 2020a).

The highest radon areas in Ireland are associated with specific lithologies. The Leinster Granite batholith (covering parts of Dublin, Wicklow, Wexford, Carlow, and Kilkenny) contains elevated uranium concentrations (5-20 ppm versus a crustal average of ~2.7 ppm) and is the single largest radon source region in the country (O'Connor et al., 1993; O'Connor, 1989; Halpin et al., 2014). Carboniferous limestone, which underlies much of the Irish midlands, is the second major source: while its uranium content is lower than granite, its karst features create high-permeability pathways that efficiently transport radon to the surface (O'Sullivan & O'Brien, 2013; Drew, 2008; Miles, 2001).

Certain shale formations, particularly the Namurian shales in Clare and North Kerry, also produce elevated radon due to their organic-rich, uranium-bearing nature. Black shales can have uranium concentrations of 10-50 ppm (Appleton, 2005). Igneous intrusions, volcanic rocks, and metamorphic terrains also contribute locally (Fennell et al., 2015).

The Quaternary (subsoil) geology modulates the bedrock signal. Thick glacial till (boulder clay) of low permeability can act as a radon barrier, reducing indoor radon even over uranium-rich bedrock. Conversely, thin or sandy/gravelly subsoil allows efficient radon transport. The key quaternary classifications in Ireland are: glacial till (thick and thin), glacial sand and gravel, alluvium, peat, rock at or near surface, and lacustrine sediments (GSI, 2023; Meehan, 2015). The Geological Survey Ireland (GSI) groundwater vulnerability classification provides a useful permeability proxy: extreme vulnerability indicates thin or highly permeable subsoil, directly correlating with radon entry potential (GSI, 2007).

The Tellus airborne radiometric survey, conducted by GSI across Ireland from 2004 onwards, provides the most comprehensive geogenic radon predictor dataset available. The survey measures equivalent uranium (eU), equivalent thorium (eTh), and potassium (K) concentrations at ground surface using gamma-ray spectrometry from a fixed-wing aircraft at 56 m altitude (Young & Donald, 2013; Hodgson & Young, 2016). Appleton et al. (2008) demonstrated in Northern Ireland that eU from the Tellus survey is the single strongest predictor of indoor radon, with eU explaining approximately 30-40% of the variance in log-transformed indoor radon at the 1-km grid scale. The eU/eTh ratio provides additional discrimination, as high eU/eTh indicates uranium enrichment relative to thorium and is associated with specific mineralogical processes (Knights et al., 2017). The Tellus Border survey extended coverage to the border region (Young et al., 2016), and by 2023 the entire Republic has been surveyed. Hodgson & Carey (2017) and Hodgson (2019) developed GSI's methodology for integrating Tellus radiometric data with bedrock and quaternary geology for radon risk mapping.

Elío et al. (2020b, 2022) produced the most comprehensive Irish radon potential analysis to date, combining Tellus eU with bedrock geology codes and quaternary classifications in a logistic regression framework. Their model achieved an Area Under the Curve (AUC) of approximately 0.78-0.82 for predicting whether a grid square exceeds the 200 Bq/m3 reference level. This represents the current state of the art for Irish radon prediction and provides the baseline we aim to improve upon.

## Theme 3: The Irish Radon Policy Landscape

Ireland has the eighth-highest average indoor radon concentration globally, with a national geometric mean of approximately 89 Bq/m3 and an arithmetic mean of approximately 91 Bq/m3 (Fennell et al., 2002, 2021). Approximately 7% of Irish dwellings exceed the 200 Bq/m3 reference level, and approximately 23% exceed the WHO recommended level of 100 Bq/m3 (McLaughlin, 2012).

The reference level of 200 Bq/m3 was adopted by Ireland in 1990, based on the Radiological Protection Institute of Ireland (RPII, now part of the EPA) recommendation. This is higher than the WHO recommendation of 100 Bq/m3 and the EU Basic Safety Standards Directive requirement of 300 Bq/m3 (Paquet et al., 2020). The Irish reference level represents a pragmatic balance between health protection and the large number of homes that would require remediation at lower thresholds.

The EPA designates "High Radon Areas" (HRAs) as those where more than 10% of homes are predicted to exceed 200 Bq/m3. Currently, about 28% of the Irish land area is designated as HRA. However, this designation is based on a 10 km × 10 km grid, which is too coarse to distinguish between individual homes or even neighborhoods (EPA Ireland, 2025). Murray et al. (2009, 2014) showed that within a single HRA grid square, radon levels can vary by more than an order of magnitude depending on local geology and building characteristics.

The National Radon Survey, ongoing since the original RPII survey in the 1990s, has measured radon in approximately 60,000 homes, but this represents only about 3% of the Irish housing stock of approximately 2 million homes (Fennell et al., 2021; EPA Ireland, 2025). The EPA offers free radon test kits and runs a grant scheme for radon remediation (sump systems, barriers, ventilation modifications) costing typically EUR 800-2,500 per home (EPA Ireland, 2023; Synnott & Fenton, 2005; Synnott et al., 2007).

The epidemiological evidence for radon-related lung cancer risk in Ireland is well established. Darby et al. (2005, 2006) estimated a 16% increase in lung cancer risk per 100 Bq/m3 increase in radon exposure, with no evidence of a threshold below which radon is safe. Breen et al. (2015) and Kukula et al. (2019) applied these risk estimates to the Irish population, estimating that radon causes approximately 250 lung cancer deaths per year in Ireland. The spatial distribution of risk is highly uneven, with county-level radon-attributable lung cancer rates varying by more than a factor of three.

## Theme 4: Building Features and the Airtightness-Radon Tension

The interaction between building characteristics and indoor radon is well documented. Key building-level factors include: floor type (suspended timber floor versus slab-on-ground versus basement), ventilation type (natural, extract, balanced with heat recovery), airtightness (air changes per hour at 50 Pa, measured by blower door test), and building age as a proxy for construction standards (Gunby et al., 1993; Coskeran et al., 2002; Friedmann et al., 2017; Valmari et al., 2010).

In Ireland, the SEAI Building Energy Rating (BER) database contains approximately 1 million certificates, each recording detailed building characteristics including floor type, ventilation system, airtightness (either measured or default values based on building type and age), wall construction, and calculated energy performance (SEAI, 2023; Ahern & Norton, 2019). The BER rating (A1 being most efficient, G being least) directly correlates with airtightness: A-rated homes have air permeability typically below 3 m3/(h·m2) at 50 Pa, while G-rated homes may exceed 15 m3/(h·m2) (DEAP manual, SEAI, 2023).

The critical finding, documented by EPA Research 273 (UNVEIL project, Colgan et al., 2024) and the broader international literature, is that energy-efficient retrofitting can significantly increase indoor radon. Font Guiteras (2022) conducted a meta-analysis of 15 studies across 7 countries and found that energy-efficient retrofitting increased indoor radon by 50-200% on average, with the largest increases in homes on high-radon geology. The mechanism is straightforward: reducing air permeability through draught-proofing, insulation, and window replacement reduces the ventilation rate (the denominator in the steady-state radon equation), increasing the equilibrium radon concentration (Andersen et al., 2009; Milner et al., 2014; Denman et al., 2007).

The UNVEIL project specifically studied Irish homes undergoing deep energy retrofits under the SEAI retrofit scheme. They found that post-retrofit radon concentrations increased by an average of 60% in homes on normal geology and by more than 100% in homes on high-radon geology. Crucially, several homes that were below the 200 Bq/m3 reference level before retrofit exceeded it afterwards. The project recommended mandatory radon testing before and after any deep energy retrofit (Colgan et al., 2024).

This creates a direct tension with Ireland's climate policy, which mandates increasing energy efficiency through the National Retrofit Plan. Homes receiving BER upgrades from D/E/F to B/C may be unknowingly increasing their radon exposure. The passive house standard (airtightness below 0.6 ACH at 50 Pa) and nearly zero-energy building (NZEB) regulations (mandatory for new builds since 2021) create buildings where radon entry is unchanged but ventilation is dramatically reduced (Font Guiteras et al., 2019; Collignan et al., 2016). Mechanical Ventilation with Heat Recovery (MVHR) systems, standard in NZEB buildings, can maintain adequate ventilation if properly installed and maintained, but field studies show that many MVHR systems operate below design capacity (McGill et al., 2015; Liddament, 2001; Naughton & Holian, 2006).

## Theme 5: Machine Learning for Radon Prediction

Machine learning approaches to radon prediction have evolved from simple logistic regression to modern ensemble methods. The field can be organized into three generations:

**First generation: logistic regression and geostatistics (2000-2010).** Sundal et al. (2004) demonstrated that multivariate logistic regression on geological variables could predict High Radon Areas with AUC of 0.75-0.80 in Norway. Dubois (2005) at the JRC developed statistical guidelines for radon mapping. Appleton & Miles (2010) applied regression modeling of geological factors for UK radon mapping. Murray et al. (2014) used logistic regression for Irish HRA prediction, achieving similar performance. Geostatistical approaches (kriging and its variants) were applied by Dubois & Bossew (2006), Zhu et al. (2001), and Elío et al. (2018), showing that spatial interpolation of sparse measurements can produce useful radon maps but degrades rapidly in under-sampled areas.

**Second generation: tree-based methods (2010-2020).** Kropat et al. (2015) showed that Random Forest outperformed logistic regression for Swiss radon prediction, achieving AUC of 0.82 using geological, building, and demographic features. Hauri et al. (2012, 2013) confirmed that building characteristics (age, type, floor construction) add predictive power beyond geology alone. Tchorz-Trzeciakiewicz et al. (2021) applied XGBoost to Polish radon data, achieving AUC of 0.85 with feature importance analysis showing that geology, building age, and floor type were the top three predictors.

**Third generation: deep learning and hybrid approaches (2020-present).** Giustini et al. (2019) applied deep neural networks to Italian radon data, achieving marginal improvements over Random Forest. Janik & Bossew (2022) compared deep learning with gradient boosting and found that tree-based methods remained competitive or superior for tabular radon data. Rezaie et al. (2021) applied modern ML methods to Korean soil radon data. The consensus is that for structured tabular data with strong geological features, tree-based ensemble methods (XGBoost, LightGBM, ExtraTrees) remain the best choice. Deep learning may offer advantages when incorporating spatial structure (convolutional approaches on gridded data) but has not yet demonstrated clear superiority for tabular radon prediction.

Key methodological considerations include: (1) spatial cross-validation is essential — Roberts et al. (2017) showed that standard random CV inflates performance by 10-30% for spatially autocorrelated environmental data; (2) class imbalance — only 7% of Irish homes exceed 200 Bq/m3, requiring careful handling of the positive class; (3) feature importance and interpretability are critical for policy applications — SHAP values (Lundberg & Lee, 2017) provide post-hoc interpretation of ensemble models.

For Ireland specifically, Elío et al. (2022) represent the state of the art: logistic regression on Tellus eU, bedrock geology, and quaternary geology, with spatial cross-validation, achieving AUC of approximately 0.78-0.82. No published study has combined Tellus radiometric data with BER building characteristics for Irish radon prediction. This is the gap this project aims to fill.

## Theme 6: The Cross-Domain Synthesis — Building Energy × Geology × Radon

The intersection of building energy efficiency, geology, and indoor radon creates a three-way interaction that no single discipline fully captures. Energy scientists optimize airtightness without considering radon; geologists map radon potential without considering building characteristics; public health researchers measure indoor radon without predicting it from available data.

The key insight, supported by the literature but not yet implemented in any predictive model for Ireland, is that radon risk is a function of: (1) radon supply from the ground (determined by geology), (2) radon entry into the building (determined by foundation type and soil-building interface), and (3) radon dilution inside the building (determined by ventilation rate, which is a function of airtightness). The BER database provides proxies for factors (2) and (3), while Tellus radiometric and geological data provide factor (1). Combining them should improve prediction beyond either source alone.

The specific cross-project connection to P5 (Irish BER energy gap) is this: the BER database that was used for energy gap analysis contains exactly the building characteristics needed to predict radon vulnerability. A home that achieves a high BER rating through deep retrofitting has, by definition, reduced its ventilation rate and increased its radon vulnerability. If that home also sits on high-radon geology (detectable from Tellus radiometric data), it represents a hidden danger that neither the BER system nor the radon map currently flags.

Bochicchio et al. (2014) showed across 10 European countries that building construction type modifies the geology-radon relationship by up to a factor of 3. In Ireland, the UNVEIL project (Colgan et al., 2024) documented this at the individual home level but did not scale it nationally. The BER × geology interaction has not been modeled systematically for Ireland's approximately 2 million homes.

The policy implication is significant: Ireland's National Retrofit Plan will retrofit 500,000 homes by 2030. A predictive model that identifies which of these homes are on high-radon geology could trigger mandatory radon testing before and after retrofit, preventing an estimated 50-200 additional homes from exceeding the reference level due to reduced ventilation. The EPA's current grid-based HRA system cannot do this because it operates at 10-km resolution and does not incorporate building data.

## Theme 7: European Context and Validation Opportunities

Ireland's radon prediction challenge exists within a broader European framework. The EU Basic Safety Standards Directive (2013/59/Euratom) requires all member states to establish National Radon Action Plans, map radon-prone areas, and set reference levels not exceeding 300 Bq/m3 for existing dwellings (European Commission, 2019).

The JRC European Indoor Radon Map (Cinelli et al., 2019) provides a continent-scale validation dataset. The map, compiled from measurements in 35 countries, uses harmonized methodology and includes Ireland. While the resolution is coarser than national maps, it provides an independent check on predictions and allows cross-country comparison of methodology. Bossew et al. (2023) published the latest European harmonized dataset covering 1.5 million measurements.

Several European countries have developed radon prediction models that could be adapted or compared with the Irish approach: Germany (Kemski et al., 2005; geogenic radon potential based on soil gas and geology), Norway (Watson et al., 2017; Smethurst et al., 2017; GIS-based multivariate mapping), Czech Republic (Barnet et al., 2005, 2008; soil permeability classification), Finland (Valmari et al., 2011; soil gas potential), and Switzerland (Kropat et al., 2015; Hauri et al., 2013; ML-based prediction). Each uses different input data and methods, but the fundamental geological controls are shared.

The Belgian study by Vandenschrick et al. (2011) is particularly relevant because southern Belgium shares geological features with western Ireland (Palaeozoic sediments, karst limestone). Norwegian approaches are relevant because Norway, like Ireland, has a glaciated landscape where quaternary deposits modulate the bedrock radon signal.

For validation, the project can use: (1) the EPA's published HRA designations as a coarse-resolution ground truth, (2) the JRC European dataset for cross-national consistency checks, and (3) hold-out areas where measurements exist but were not used in model training. The spatial CV approach (Roberts et al., 2017; Valavi et al., 2019) is essential to produce honest performance estimates.
