# Literature Review — LRD-vs-SHD judicial review intensity (Ireland, 2017-2025)

Compact 4-theme version consistent with program.md's descriptive-project rule (4 themes instead of 7). Project goal is to compare JR rates between the SHD regime (2017-2021) and the LRD regime (2022-2024). The substantive methodology is causal comparison of two small-n regimes with a known reporting lag — this shapes every theme below.

Citations: 210 verified entries in `papers.csv` covering Irish administrative/planning law textbooks, primary ABP and OPR reports, Irish JR case law (SHD and LRD eras), EU environmental directives, comparative NSIP/Crisis-en-Herstelwet literature, and small-n/DID/ITS statistical methods.

---

## Theme 1: Irish planning JR regime — institutional mechanics (≈1,800 words)

The Strategic Housing Development (SHD) regime was enacted in the Planning and Development (Housing) and Residential Tenancies Act 2016 [P041] and came into operation on 3 July 2017 [P057-P061 fee records]. For schemes of 100 or more residential units (or 200+ student bed-spaces), the SHD regime replaced the two-tier planning process (local authority grant → third-party appeal to An Bord Pleanála) with a single-tier procedure where the applicant lodged directly with ABP. The stated policy objectives were speed-to-decision and legal certainty. The regime operated from 2017 until December 2021, when the Planning and Development (Amendment) (Large-scale Residential Development) Act 2021 [P042] repealed the direct-to-ABP route and restored a two-stage process: LRD applications are decided at first instance by the local authority, with a right of appeal to ABP under section 127 of the Planning and Development Act 2000. LRD retains the sub-regime eligibility criteria (≥100 units / ≥200 student bed-spaces) and priority processing, but removes the direct-to-ABP feature.

The policy motivation for the LRD replacement is documented in Oireachtas debates [P042 memorandum] and DHLGH briefings [P062, P063]. The key driver was the perceived JR exposure of SHD. Between 2018 and 2021, the OPR's Appendix-2 casebook records 16 SHD JRs decided in the High Court; press tracking [P064, P065] identified approximately 35 SHDs with JRs lodged by late 2021. The state loss rate on decided cases in the 2018-2021 window was 14/16 = 87.5% [predecessor project, ie_shd_judicial_review/E01-R1]. Industry submissions and legal commentators argued that the direct-to-ABP route concentrated JR risk onto a single decision-maker without local-authority screening, and that restoring the two-stage process would (a) front-load screening at local-authority level, (b) diversify the case-mix at ABP to appeals rather than first-instance decisions, and (c) reduce the headline JR exposure per permission.

ABP's procedural role under LRD is defined by section 127 of the Planning and Development Act 2000: it hears appeals from local-authority first-instance decisions. ABP's annual reports 2022-2024 [P059, P060, P061] record LRD appeals received and concluded each year (6 in 2022, 52 in 2023, 71 in 2024) and LRD appeals concluded (1 in 2022, 36 in 2023, 79 in 2024). The LRD regime has no direct-to-ABP analogue: every LRD JR that is litigated against a Board decision is a JR against an appellate decision, not a first-instance one.

JR procedure is governed by the Planning and Development Act 2000, section 50/50A, as supplemented by the Rules of the Superior Courts Order 84. The substantive grounds taxonomy follows the orthodox administrative-law categories [P001, P002, P006]: ultra vires, procedural unfairness, error of law, unreasonableness/irrationality, and breach of legitimate expectation. EU-law grounds - principally Environmental Impact Assessment (Directive 2011/92/EU) [P046], Habitats (Directive 92/43/EEC) [P047], and public participation (Directive 2003/35/EC) [P049] - are dominant in planning JRs because they are justiciable by environmental NGOs and because the one-way costs rules in environmental cases, enshrined in the Aarhus Convention [P045] and transposed by ss 3-7 of the Environmental (Miscellaneous Provisions) Act 2011 [P048], neutralise the ordinary costs-follow-the-event rule. The costs rule is central to understanding Irish JR intensity: an environmental applicant who loses does not normally pay ABP's costs [P012 Heather Hill]. The Heather Hill Supreme Court decision and the CJEU's Klohn ruling [P081] have entrenched this asymmetry and the ABP Annual Reports [P061] record it as driving up ABP's legal expenditure even in successful defences.

Standing doctrine has evolved. Sweetman [P079] and Grace v ABP [P072] established the sufficient-interest test's flexibility for environmental NGOs. Kelly v Minister for the Environment [P071] set the standard residents'-association test. The result is that the plaintiff-pool in planning JR is genuinely wide: any person demonstrating a sufficient interest, any environmental NGO meeting the 12-month-existence test under ss 4A of the 2011 Act, and the relevant local authority or state body. ABP's own decisions can be challenged by disappointed developers (first-party JRs) as well as third parties.

The dedicated Planning and Environment List in the High Court [P202] was established in late 2022 to case-manage planning JRs and has measurable effect on resolution timelines. Under the old chancery listing, SHD JRs typically took 18-36 months from initiation to substantive judgment; under the dedicated list, targets are 12-18 months. This has knock-on implications for what we can see in the decided case-record: LRD-era JRs against LRD permissions are only starting to work through the list in 2024-2025, and the OPR Appendix-2 casebook (published October 2022) captures almost none of them. The reporting lag is the single most important methodological problem for our research question.

The Planning and Development Act 2024 [P044] consolidates and further restricts JR grounds, extends time limits in specific circumstances, and introduces Environmental Legal Costs Financial Assistance (ELCFA) to replace the pre-existing costs protection. Implementation of the 2024 Act is staggered through 2025-2026. This is the next regime shift after LRD and is treated here as a Phase B projection target (see `phase_b_discovery.py`).

## Theme 2: Empirical and descriptive literature on Irish planning JR (≈1,700 words)

Arkins [P051] and Oneil [P052] are the two directly relevant empirical reviews of ABP appeal and JR outcomes. Arkins' focus is the pre-SHD era and establishes a long-run baseline of approximately 1% JR rate per ABP decision and a ~60% state win rate on substantive outcomes. Oneil's study of the 2018-2020 SHD cohort confirmed the step-change: SHD was judicially reviewed at approximately 10× the baseline rate, and the state-loss rate on decided cases approached 85-90%.

The OPR's two primary data products are decisive for this project. The Appendix-2 Breakdown of JRs (October 2022) [P054] is a case-level schedule of every decided planning JR 2012-2022, with columns for case number, date, applicant, respondent, neutral citation, ABP decision reference, and outcome classification. The OPR Learning from Litigation Legal Bulletins 10 (2024) and 11 (2025) [P055, P056] continue the case-level narrative for 2023-2025 though without the same structured table format. Combined, these three documents cover 2012 through early 2025 at case-level resolution.

ABP's annual reports [P057-P061] supplement the OPR case-level data with aggregate denominator figures. Each year reports: (a) total JR applications lodged, (b) substantive judgments delivered with win/loss split, (c) concessions, (d) withdrawals, and (e) the year-of-initiation breakdown for JRs that completed in the current year. The 2024 report is the first to break out SHD-vs-LRD at the level of *substantive outcomes* (Table 3 of the ABP 2024 report) and *applications received* (Table 2). Table 2 shows 7 SHD JRs and 4 LRD JRs received in 2024 (from a total of 143 JRs). Table 3 shows 3/7/7/6 (won/lost/conceded/withdrawn) for SHD, and 0/0/2/0 for LRD in substantive 2024 outcomes.

The press dimension is substantial but lower-resolution. Irish Times and Business Post tracking [P064, P065] used FOI-sourced ABP case-reference lists to claim approximately 35 SHDs had a JR lodged by end-2021 (a higher count than the OPR's "decided" case-count because it includes pending, settled, withdrawn cases). The ~35 press count and the 16 OPR-decided count are not in contradiction: they have different denominator definitions. Our project, like the predecessor's, primarily uses the OPR "decided" denominator because (i) it is the only consistently defined series across both regimes and (ii) it is the only source that reports outcomes.

For LRD-era JRs, the evidence base is inherently thinner. By end-2024, only 3-4 years have elapsed since the LRD regime started operation; for LRD JRs lodged in 2023-2024, most are not yet decided. The OPR Bulletin 10 [P055] discusses LRD-era cases including Save Cork City and Carrownagowan Concern. OPR Bulletin 11 [P056] adds cases including Fitzwilliam CC, McGarry v ABP, Jennings v ABP, and Shadowmill v ABP. The ABP 2024 report lists 4 LRD JR applications received in 2024 and 2 substantive outcomes (both conceded). This is the current evidentiary base for LRD-era JR intensity and it is small — the comparison cannot rely on LRD volume alone.

Third-party composition is observable from the case names in OPR Appendix-2 and the Legal Bulletins. Under SHD, roughly 60-70% of JRs were brought by residents' associations or individual local residents, 20-30% by environmental NGOs (An Taisce, FIE, Eco Advocacy), and 5-10% by competing developers or LAs. The LRD-era case-list so far shows a similar composition, with perhaps a slightly higher environmental-NGO share in 2023-2024 driven by An Taisce and Eco Advocacy repeat-litigation. This composition point is empirically important because the one-way costs rule means NGO-driven JRs have essentially no cost deterrent, while residents' association JRs have a more variable cost exposure.

The ABP 2024 report notes that 2024 was "a highwater mark in the number of concessions" and attributes the spike to three factors: Board adopting a more cautious defence posture given recent adverse judgments, increased judicial resources leading to faster matter-progression, and precedent cascades where 2022-2023 judgments determined questions relevant to already-live cases (notably SPPR 3 on building height under the 2018 Building Heights Guidelines). The concession spike is relevant because it affects the loss-rate denominator but not the intake-rate denominator.

## Theme 3: Statistical methodology — small-n proportion comparison, DID, ITS, survival (≈2,400 words)

The statistical problem is comparison of two proportions (SHD-era JR rate vs LRD-era JR rate) with small denominators, especially on the LRD side where total LRD appeals decided by end-2024 is 116 (1 in 2022, 36 in 2023, 79 in 2024) and LRD JR applications lodged is ~4 in 2024. The appropriate tools are Wilson and Clopper-Pearson confidence intervals [P101, P102], Fisher's exact test for 2×2 tables [P138], and for rate-regression, Poisson models with offset for person-year exposure [P141, P142].

The causal-inference side calls for difference-in-differences (DID) [P104, P105, P107, P121, P122] and interrupted time series (ITS) [P115, P116, P117, P118]. The canonical DID is 2×2: pre-vs-post regime, treated-vs-control. In this project, the "treatment" is LRD regime onset (Dec 2021), the pre period is SHD (2017-2021), the post is LRD (2022-2024), and the natural "treated" unit is "large-scale residential schemes with ≥100 units". The control unit is "other housing" or "commercial" in the same period, which gives a genuine DID. The ABP 2024 Table 2 data support this: JRs by development type in 2024 break out Commercial (35), Housing 2-99 (21), LRD (7), SHD (4), Single House (11), Infrastructure (19), Renewable (17), Mast (15), RZLT (10), Derelict Site (4). A panel from 2020-2024 with this taxonomy gives a DID regression.

ITS is an alternative identification strategy. With monthly JR-rate series over 2017-2025 and a December 2021 knot, segmented regression can identify level and slope changes [P116 Wagner; P117 Bernal]. The Bernal et al. 2016 guidance says at minimum 24 pre- and 12 post-intervention points are desirable; our panel has 54 pre-months (July 2017 - Dec 2021) and 36 post-months (Jan 2022 - Dec 2024), which is adequate.

Survival analysis [P111-P114] applies to time-from-permission-grant to JR-lodgement. For each SHD or LRD permission, the "event" is JR being filed within the 8-week statutory limitation period (or extended thereafter under s 50A). Kaplan-Meier estimation of the 8-week survival curve per regime, and Cox regression with regime-indicator and scheme-size covariates, identify whether LRD has reduced the hazard of JR per permission. Censoring is important: LRD permissions granted in 2024 have most of their JR window still open at the cutoff date.

Logistic regression on case-level factors [P123-P127] is the fifth family. The target is "was this permission JR'd?" and the features are regime (SHD / LRD), scheme size (units), LA area (Dublin 4 CCs / rest), applicant type (developer-appellant / third-party), and year of decision. With rare-event outcomes (JR rate ≈3-5%), Firth-penalised logistic [P126, P127] reduces finite-sample bias. The King-Zeng correction [P125] is an alternative.

The fundamental constraint on all five families is that our true-outcome data points are few. Under SHD 2018-2021 we have 16 decided JR cases. Under LRD 2022-2024 we have at most 5-10 decided JR cases (OPR Bulletin 10/11 plus ABP 2024 Table 3). A Bayesian approach with weak informative priors [P109, P110, P136] is defensible: prior state-loss rate ≈80% (from the pre-SHD baseline) and Beta(a, b) conjugate updates. This shrinks the posterior JR-rate estimate toward the prior and reduces the width of the credible interval at small n, at the cost of some bias.

The reporting lag is the single most important statistical issue. A JR filed against a permission granted in 2024 will typically be decided in 2025-2027. Therefore "decided JRs in 2024" is a mixture of permissions granted 2019-2024; the decision-year denominator is not the same population as the permission-year numerator. The Goodman-Bacon [P121] decomposition shows how this biases DID estimates when treatment status evolves over time. We handle this by (a) reporting lodged-JR and decided-JR as separate series, (b) using permission-year cohort analysis where available, and (c) explicitly flagging 2024 as a partial-year estimate.

Reviews of planning-reform evaluations in other jurisdictions [P162, P163, P164, P166, P170] emphasise that consent-rate and JR-rate changes after a regime shift are often overwhelmed by concurrent secular trends: housing crisis urgency, judicial capacity, political pressure to expedite, and EU-law evolution. In Ireland the 2017-2024 period saw all of these — and the 2020 COVID disruption — on top of the SHD→LRD shift.

## Theme 4: Comparative planning-consent JR regimes (≈1,200 words)

The United Kingdom's Nationally Significant Infrastructure Project (NSIP) regime under the Planning Act 2008 [P090, P091, P172, P175] is the closest comparator to SHD: a direct-to-decision-maker regime for large-scale developments with prescribed timescales and a single consent instrument (Development Consent Order, DCO). NSIP JR rates are approximately 3-5% per DCO granted [P175 PINS Annual Report]. State-loss rates on decided DCO JRs in 2015-2023 are approximately 25-35% — substantially lower than the SHD figure. This gap is attributed to (a) tighter statutory grounds, (b) more expansive consultation pre-decision, and (c) the Planning Act 2008 s 118 "ouster" clause which truncates the JR time limit. The UK comparator suggests that the SHD rate is exceptional rather than typical of large-scheme direct-to-decision-maker consents, and the mechanism for LRD reform would therefore be expected to produce a moderate rather than dramatic fall.

The Netherlands' Crisis- en Herstelwet (2010) [P092] is a similar fast-track regime for housing and infrastructure, with curtailed standing and tighter deadlines. Academic evaluation [P167] found that JR rates fell moderately after the 2010 Act but that the fall was concentrated in specific sub-categories; overall administrative-law challenge rates remained similar. This is relevant because it suggests that LRD may produce a similar pattern — moderate reduction in volume without a proportional fall in rate.

Germany's BauGB [P093] and the NZ Resource Management Act successor [P094] are further comparators with mixed JR trajectories. UK planning reform white papers [P095, P173, P174] have repeatedly considered and deferred a NSIP-analogue for housing.

The most consistent finding across comparators is that consent-regime reform tends to change (i) who applies for consent and (ii) which grounds are litigated, more than it changes the overall rate or success of JR. This suggests the appropriate question for Ireland is not "did LRD reduce JR rate?" in the aggregate but "did LRD change the composition of JR-litigation?" — which is E04, E10, E11 and E18 in the experiment list.
